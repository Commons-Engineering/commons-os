### Section 1: Context

In commons governance across all domains, a structural problem emerges: stewards—elected officials, resource managers, organizational leaders—hold information that constituencies need to verify trustworthiness, yet no mechanism forces disclosure. The O1 principle INCENTIVES & SELF-INTEREST tells us that stewards will exploit this gap unless transparency is designed into governance. Simultaneously, RECIPROCITY & FAIRNESS suggests that fairness in hierarchical systems is not equal access but asymmetric accountability: those asking others to trust their judgment must volunteer visibility that those without power cannot demand. The O1 principle POLYCENTRIC / FRACTAL ORGANISATION reveals that in multi-level systems, opacity at any tier creates a pocket of unaccountable power that spreads through cascading governance. This transversal responds to a universal governance failure: the collapse of legitimacy when stewards remain opaque while constituencies are expected to comply.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Transparency as equalizer vs. transparency as structural asymmetry; perfect information vs. cognitive accessibility.**

The problem manifests identically across domains: (1) INFORMATION ASYMMETRY AS POWER: Stewards exploit knowledge gaps—about budget allocation, decision criteria, actual authority distribution—to serve concentrated interests while appearing to serve the commons. (2) TRANSPARENCY PARADOX: Publishing all information equally (perfect transparency) either overwhelms constituencies with cognitive load or reveals nothing useful because it lacks interpretation. (3) ACCOUNTABILITY ASYMMETRY REVERSED: In many systems, transparency requirements are imposed on residents, nonprofits, and communities while government and corporate partners remain opaque, inverting legitimate power flow. (4) OPACITY-ENABLED CAPTURE: When civic organizations gain governance roles, they face pressure to protect their institutional positions; absence of disclosure mechanisms enables mission drift undetectable to members. (5) LEGITIMACY FAILURE: Constituencies cannot verify whether stewards are trustworthy, so they either disengageor demand heavy-handed control, creating resentment. The core tension: legitimate governance requires stewards to prove they deserve trust, but perfect transparency is paralyzing; the answer is not symmetrical access but ASYMMETRIC DISCLOSURE where power holders bear the burden of visibility.

---

### Section 3: Solution

The universal mechanism is ASYMMETRIC STEWARD TRANSPARENCY WITH ADAPTIVE DISCLOSURE.

**Structure:**
1. INVERSION OF BURDEN: Transparency is default; secrecy requires justification. Stewards (those holding power) must publish all decision rationale, resource flows, authority distribution, and performance metrics in accessible formats. The burden of proof for opacity rests on power holders, not on constituencies demanding openness. This creates a strong incentive alignment: stewards know their opacity will be noticed and questioned.

2. POWER-PROPORTIONAL DISCLOSURE: Transparency scales with power. Those holding more decision authority, resource control, or governance influence disclose more extensively than those without power. A municipal budget committee discloses spending in detailed line items; individual community members disclose involvement only if they hold formal roles. This asymmetry is fair because it matches disclosure burden to actual authority.

3. STAKEHOLDER-ADAPTIVE INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE: Information is published at multiple granularities for different constituencies. Technical specialists receive detailed data (meeting minutes with full rationale); general members receive simplified summaries (one-page decision briefs); affected populations receive narrative explanations (how this decision impacts you). This prevents information asymmetry by ensuring cognitive accessibility, not equal raw access.

4. MANDATORY RESPONSE & FEEDBACK LOOPS: Every formal query, public comment, or stakeholder challenge to steward decisions receives a written response explaining either incorporation or explicit rejection with reasoning. Responses are published, creating a public record of how stewards engage critique. This closes information gaps because constituencies can verify whether their concerns are taken seriously.

5. ACCOUNTABILITY LEDGERS & PERFORMANCE DISCLOSURE: Continuously updated public documents map actual power distribution (who can veto, who controls resources, who shapes agendas) versus formal governance structure. Performance against covenants—ecological, financial, social—is disclosed in accessible community language. Stewards cannot hide the gap between promised and actual outcomes.

6. CASCADING NESTED ACCOUNTABILITY: In multi-level systems (neighborhood → district → city), each governance tier publicly reports both upward (to those it serves) and downward (to those it represents), creating transparency that flows through the entire structure. Opacity at any level becomes visible to the tier above and below, preventing captured pockets.

7. RECALL & NO-CONFIDENCE MECHANISMS: Transparent, low-threshold procedures allow constituencies to remove stewards through no-confidence votes if they repeatedly hide information or violate transparency covenants. This makes the cost of opacity immediate and personal.

**Why This Works Across Domains:**

Business: Workers hold executives accountable because compensation, budget allocation, and strategic rationale are disclosed asymmetrically—workers know more about how executive decisions affect them than executives know about worker experience. Executives cannot exploit information gaps.

Ecology: Resource extraction stewards must disclose environmental impact data continuously; the communities absorbing that impact receive the information first and most detailed. Ecosystems cannot consent, so human stewards must be hypervisible to those representing ecosystem interests.

Life: Care coordinators disclose time allocation asymmetrically because those receiving care have the highest stakes in knowing whether they are being neglected. Hidden preferences become visible through structured disclosure.

Urban: Civic organizations, municipal government, and BIDs disclose budget, decision authority, and performance under binding transparency statutes. Citizens cannot demand equal access to information, but they can demand asymmetric accountability: stewards answer to constituencies, not the reverse.

**The Protective Intent:**

This is not radical transparency. It is PROTECTIVE TRANSPARENCY—designed specifically to prevent the most common governance failure: steward capture. By making power holders more visible than the powerless, by adapting disclosure to decision authority, and by inverting the burden of proof for secrecy, commons governance creates the conditions for legitimate authority. Constituencies can trust stewards not because stewards are trustworthy by nature, but because opacity is structurally costly and visibility is structurally rewarded. The result is a commons where governance is alive (adapting through feedback), shared (constituencies understand what is happening), just (power holders bear proportional transparency burden), and built to last (accountability mechanisms prevent capture from metastasizing).

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping power explicitly. Before you can make it visible, you need to know where it lives: Who decides resource allocation? Who controls information flows? Who can veto proposals? Who appoints others? Document this as an org chart of *actual* power, not formal titles. The most useful version names specific people and their specific authorities.

Next, establish a disclosure calendar. Stewards commit to publishing—on a regular, predictable schedule—five categories of information: (1) Decision rationale: *why* a choice was made, including rejected alternatives and reasoning; (2) Resource flows: budgets, spending, allocation methods, what was requested vs. granted; (3) Authority distribution: who can decide what, how decisions get made, where veto points exist; (4) Performance against commons goals: metrics that show whether decisions served shared benefit; (5) Conflicts of interest: relationships, financial stakes, or other incentives that shaped choices.

The key move is *adaptive disclosure*: format and depth adjust to audience. A detailed decision memo for governance bodies; a one-page summary with visual breakdown for general members; a FAQ addressing common questions; quarterly town halls where stewards explain and defend their choices live. The same information, different containers. This isn't dumbing down—it's cognitive justice. Accessibility is not less rigorous; it's more.

Create mechanisms for challenge. Transparency without contestation is performance. Build in processes where members can formally question steward decisions, demand justification, propose alternatives. These don't have to overturn decisions (though they might), but they must be heard and answered publicly. The act of having to defend choices creates real accountability teeth.

Finally, audit the system itself. Designate someone (ideally independent or rotated) to verify that stewards are actually disclosing on schedule, that disclosures are genuinely informative rather than technically transparent but meaningless, that adaptive formats are reaching their intended audiences. The pattern fails silently if no one checks whether it's working.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, three things flourish. First: *trust becomes earned rather than assumed*. Members can see the reasoning, spot bias, verify that power isn't being weaponized for private gain. Trust that survives scrutiny is durable. Second: *steward discipline improves*. Knowing you must explain yourself changes how you decide. You think harder about fairness, you seek broader input, you avoid shortcuts that can't be justified. Third: *legitimacy becomes resilient*. Even when decisions disappoint members, the transparency shows the reasoning was genuine, the process was fair. People accept loss better when they see it wasn't rigged.

The risks are real, though. **Transparency theater**: stewards publish enormous amounts of technically true but context-free data—a thousand-page budget with no narrative, decisions announced without rationale. Members can't extract signal from noise, so they disengage. Trust erodes because accessibility failed. **Surveillance rebound**: some stewards use detailed disclosure as surveillance—publishing member behavior, preferences, dissent—while keeping their own decision-making opaque. Asymmetry inverts in the wrong direction. **Decision paralysis**: stewards become so focused on justifying every choice that they slow down, consult endlessly, defer to the loudest voices. Transparency becomes an excuse for diffusion of responsibility.

The pattern only survives if the asymmetry stays real: stewards disclose much more than members need to. If members feel pressure to publish their reasoning equally, the mechanism breaks. Transparency flows upward from power, not downward equally.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989–present)**: The city published detailed breakdowns of how municipal budgets were allocated, held open assemblies where residents voted on spending priorities, and released results showing which neighborhoods received resources and why. Stewards had to explain allocation logic; residents could challenge it. The model spread globally because transparency was built into the process, not bolted on after. Decisions were visible, contestable, and traceable to community input.

**Savageau Forest Commons, Nova Scotia**: Indigenous stewards managing shared timber and hunting grounds maintain detailed records of harvest decisions, wildlife populations, and resource distribution among member families. Decisions are explained through monthly councils where any member can question allocations. The transparency is oral and relational, not bureaucratic—but equally rigorous. Stewards know they'll be asked to justify; accountability is woven into face-to-face governance. This has prevented the resource capture that destroyed similar commons elsewhere.

**Platform cooperatives like Stocksy United and Resonate**: Worker-owners have access to algorithms determining pay, content promotion, and fee structures. These platforms publish the logic (not just the code) that stewards use when making resource and visibility decisions. Members can't change algorithms directly, but they can see how they work and demand justification for changes. This asymmetry—stewards explain, workers verify—has created trust that venture-backed platforms routinely fail to build.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed intelligence and AI make this pattern simultaneously more fragile and more essential. The fragility: when decisions are made by algorithms or hybrid human-AI systems, explaining reasoning becomes mathematically complex. A neural network's decision-making is often opaque even to its designers. Stewards can claim "the AI decided" as a way to avoid accountability. The pattern breaks if "explaining your choice" becomes technically impossible.

The strength: automation makes comprehensive logging and disclosure *easier*. Every decision can be timestamped, the inputs recorded, the outputs traced. You can show exactly what data fed into a decision, what weights the system applied, what alternatives were considered. Transparency can become more granular, not less.

The real shift is this: in a cognitive-distributed era, transparency must account for *distributed causation*. A decision isn't "the steward decided" anymore; it's "the steward, the algorithm, the dataset, and the feedback loop together produced this outcome." Members need to see all those layers. This means disclosing not just the steward's reasoning but the system's training data, the incentive structures the algorithm optimizes for, the feedback it received that shaped its choices.

This also opens a new risk: stewards blame algorithms for choices that were actually parameterized by human values. The pattern survives only if transparency extends to the people who designed the system, set its goals, and chose which data to feed it. The asymmetry must reach back through the entire chain of decision-making power.

### Section 8: Vitality

A commons practicing this pattern well shows specific signs. **Stewards anticipate questions**. They disclose rationale without waiting to be asked, format it for real comprehension, invite challenge. They visibly adjust future decisions based on member feedback, showing that transparency fed back into governance. **Members engage critically**. They read disclosures, ask follow-up questions, propose alternatives, sometimes accept hard decisions because they see the reasoning. Engagement is informed and sustained, not reactive. **Trust is unevenly distributed but stable**: some members trust stewards consistently; others remain skeptical and scrutinize more heavily. Both groups perceive the system as fair because they can verify it. **Conflicts are managed predictably**: when decisions disappoint, disagreement is about substance (we'd choose differently), not about hidden agendas (you're secretly serving yourselves).

Signs of decay are equally clear. **Transparency becomes ornamental**: stewards publish extensive documents that no one reads or understands. Accessibility has failed; legitimacy is hollow. **Challenge mechanisms atrophy**: questioning steward decisions is technically possible but socially discouraged; members who ask are seen as complainers. Asymmetry starts reversing. **Information floods without signal**: stewards overwhelm constituencies with data as a form of obfuscation. Technical transparency masks actual opacity. **Members disengage**: fewer people read disclosures; participation in challenge mechanisms drops; trust erodes because the pattern feels performative.

The diagnostic question: *Can a member articulate, in their own words, why a recent steward decision was made the way it was, and what data or logic they'd need to contest it?* If yes, the pattern is alive. If no—if members can't reconstruct the reasoning or don't know how to challenge it—the pattern is decaying, no matter how much stewards publish.
