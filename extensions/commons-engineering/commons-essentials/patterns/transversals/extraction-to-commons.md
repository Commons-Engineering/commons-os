> Extraction is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is a friend who always takes and never gives. Sometimes it is a platform that profits from your content without sharing the revenue. Sometimes it is a city that sells public land to private developers. Sometimes it is an industry that takes fish from the ocean faster than they can reproduce. In every case, the value creation is being consumed rather than sustained. The commons transition begins when someone asks: who is taking, who is giving, and is the balance just?

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★★ (High)
> This pattern draws on Ostrom's governance principles, Bollier's commoning practice, Linebaugh's history of enclosure, and Raworth's regenerative economics. The pattern is observable across all domains: a person who sets boundaries against extraction becomes healthier; a platform that shares value with its contributors builds loyalty; a city that protects public assets builds civic trust; an ecosystem governed by sustainable harvest rules regenerates. The evidence is unambiguous: extraction eventually destroys its own source of value.

---

### Section 1: Context

You lend a neighbour your lawnmower. They return it. You lend it again. They return it. The third time, they do not return it. You notice, but you say nothing — it feels petty. A month later, they have not only kept the mower, they have lent it to someone else. Your shared arrangement has been enclosed. What was a commons — a tool shared between neighbours — has been captured by one participant.

Scale this up. A fishing village shares access to a bay. For generations, every family takes what they need, and the fish replenish. Then a company arrives with industrial trawlers. In five years, the fish are gone. The village's commons has been extracted — not by the villagers, who governed it sustainably, but by an external actor who took value without returning it and without being governed by the community's rules.

Scale it differently. A software developer contributes code to an open-source project for years. A large company takes that code, builds a proprietary product on it, earns billions, and contributes nothing back. The developer's labour was extracted. The commons — the shared code base — enriched a private actor who did not reciprocate. This pattern repeats everywhere: content creators on platforms, care workers in health systems, volunteers in community organisations, topsoil in industrial agriculture. Wherever value is created collectively but captured privately, extraction is happening. The question is not whether extraction exists — it is everywhere. The question is whether the value creation can recognise it and build governance that transforms extraction into stewardship.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core tension is Extraction vs. Stewardship.**

Three forces make extraction persistent and stewardship difficult:

**1. Extraction is invisible until it is advanced.** A person whose energy is being extracted by a demanding relationship does not feel it immediately — they feel it after months of depletion. A community whose commons is being enclosed does not notice the first fence — they notice when the path they always walked is suddenly blocked. An ecosystem being overharvested shows no stress until the population crashes. Extraction works precisely because it takes a little at a time, below the threshold of alarm. By the time the depletion is visible, the extractive pattern is deeply established — legally, culturally, and structurally.

The value creation appears to continue. But each cycle, a little more value flows out than flows in. The "alive" quality is slowly failing — the system is depleting rather than regenerating — and no one can point to the exact moment it began.

**2. Enclosure is framed as improvement.** Every act of enclosure comes with a narrative: "Private ownership is more efficient." "Professional management will improve the resource." "Development will create jobs." "Monetisation will fund better services." These narratives are not always wrong — private management sometimes is more efficient, development sometimes does create jobs. But the narrative hides what is lost: the shared access, the collective governance, the distributed benefit, the community knowledge that sustained the resource for generations.

When a city sells a public park to a developer who builds luxury flats with a small public plaza, the narrative says: "The city gained revenue, jobs, and housing." What the narrative omits: the city lost a commons — a space that belonged to everyone, governed by no one in particular, creating value that no balance sheet measured.

**3. Power asymmetry protects extractors.** Those who extract value from a commons typically have more power than those who sustain it. The industrial trawler company has more legal resources than the fishing village. The platform has more negotiating power than the content creator. The landlord has more political access than the tenant. This asymmetry means that even when extraction is recognised, the extractors can defend their position — through law, lobbying, public relations, or simple economic pressure. The transition from extraction to commons requires overcoming this power gap, which is why it so often requires collective action.

---

### Section 3: Solution

> **Therefore, recognise extraction where it occurs, name what was shared before it was enclosed, and build governance practices that make the value creation shared, self-renewing, and just.**

The transition has three phases, each producing a specific improvement in value creation quality:

**Phase 1: See the extraction.** (Improves "systematic")

Make the invisible visible. Map the value flows in your system: who contributes? Who benefits? Is the exchange reciprocal? If value consistently flows from many contributors to few beneficiaries without returning, extraction is occurring. This is not a moral judgement — it is a systems diagnosis. A doctor does not moralise about a blood clot; they diagnose it and treat it. Extraction is a circulation disorder in the value creation.

Practical diagnosis: follow the value. A content creator posts to a platform. The platform sells advertising around the content. The creator receives "exposure." Follow the money: the advertiser pays the platform. The platform pays its shareholders. The creator receives nothing. The value creation — content that attracts attention — is working. The value distribution — attention converts to money that flows to shareholders — is extractive.

**Phase 2: Name the commons.** (Improves "shared" and "just")

Before enclosure, there was a commons. Before the platform captured the content, creators shared freely among themselves. Before the trawler company arrived, the village governed the bay. Before the landlord raised rents, the neighbourhood was a community. Naming the commons is not nostalgia — it is precision. It identifies what was shared, who participated, how it was governed, and what made it work.

This naming matters because the commons transition is not about creating something from nothing. It is about restoring something that was taken. The governance already existed — it needs to be rediscovered, adapted, and strengthened, not invented from scratch.

**Phase 3: Build commoning practices.** (Improves "alive" and "built to last")

Commoning is not a state — it is a practice. It is the ongoing, active work of governing shared value creation. It includes: agreeing on rules for use, monitoring compliance, resolving disputes, adapting to changes, and ensuring that benefits flow fairly to all who contribute. Commoning is what distinguishes a true commons from merely shared property. Without commoning practices, any shared resource eventually gets enclosed again — because the forces of extraction never stop.

The practices must be matched to the scale: a household commons needs kitchen-table governance. A community commons needs regular assemblies. A digital commons needs transparent contribution tracking and benefit-sharing agreements. A global commons needs multilateral institutions with enforcement capability.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

**Step 1: Diagnose the extraction.** Ask three questions about your value creation: (1) Who contributes the most value? (2) Who captures the most benefit? (3) Are they the same people? If the answers to questions 1 and 2 are different groups, extraction is occurring. This is not a moral failing — it is a design problem. The value creation was not designed to return value to those who create it.

**Step 2: Identify the enclosure mechanism.** How does the extraction work, structurally? A contract that assigns intellectual property to the employer. A platform's terms of service that claim rights to user content. A zoning law that prevents community use of public land. A trade agreement that allows foreign companies to harvest local resources. The mechanism is always structural — it is built into rules, contracts, laws, or norms. To change the extraction, you must change the structure.

**Step 3: Convene the commoners.** Bring together the people who contribute to the value creation. Not the extractors — the contributors. The content creators, not the platform executives. The fishers, not the trawler company. The care workers, not the hospital administrators. The neighbours, not the developer. Ask them: What do we share? How should it be governed? What rules would make this fair? The answers are the foundation of the commons governance.

**Step 4: Establish commons governance.** Based on the convening, create rules for the shared value creation. Ostrom's design principles remain the best guide: clear boundaries (who is in, who is out), rules matched to local conditions, collective decision-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, accessible conflict resolution, and recognition by external authorities. The governance does not need to be complex — but it does need to exist, and it needs teeth. A commons without governance is just a resource waiting to be enclosed.

**Step 5: Create anti-enclosure protections.** Once commons governance is established, protect it against future enclosure. Legal structures: community land trusts, cooperative charters, open-source licences, conservation easements. Economic structures: benefit-sharing agreements, community investment vehicles, revenue-sharing models. Cultural structures: naming the commons publicly, celebrating commoning practices, teaching commons governance to the next generation. Each protection makes re-enclosure harder — but none makes it impossible. Anti-enclosure is an ongoing practice, not a one-time installation.

**Step 6: Iterate and adapt.** The commons transition is not a one-time event. It is a continuous practice of noticing extraction, naming it, and adjusting governance. What works today may not work tomorrow — technology changes, populations shift, power structures evolve. The value creation must have a regular practice of reviewing its own governance: is extraction creeping back? Are the rules still fair? Are new participants included? This iterative practice is what makes the commons "built to last" — not because it is rigid, but because it adapts.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

**What this pattern creates:**

Value creation that regenerates its own source. When contributors are fairly compensated, they contribute more and better. When shared resources are governed sustainably, they replenish. When communities control their own commons, they invest in them. The result is a value creation that gets stronger over time rather than weaker — the opposite of extraction's depletion trajectory. And a sense of ownership and dignity among participants: they are stewards, not labourers; co-creators, not inputs.

**What this pattern costs:**

The beneficiaries of extraction lose their privileged position. This is the most significant cost, and it is the reason the pattern faces resistance. The platform that shares revenue with creators earns less for shareholders. The company that recognises community resource rights cannot extract them freely. The landlord who accepts rent regulation earns less per unit. Each lost privilege is a real economic cost to the extractor — and they will resist it proportionally.

Governance overhead. A commons requires ongoing governance — meetings, monitoring, dispute resolution, adaptation. An extractive system does not need this overhead because it does not need consent. The trade-off: more democratic, more just, more sustainable — but also more effortful.

**What this pattern prevents:**

The collapse that always follows unchecked extraction. Every extractive system eventually depletes its source: the overfished sea, the exhausted workforce, the hollowed-out community, the burnt-out individual. The commons transition prevents this collapse by ensuring that value creation is regenerative — that more flows in than flows out. It also prevents the social damage of extraction: the resentment, the inequality, the loss of trust, the erosion of community that always accompanies the feeling of being taken from without being given to.

### Section 6: Known Applications

**A person and their boundaries.** He gave everything to his work — evenings, weekends, creative energy, emotional labour. In return, he received a salary and occasional praise. The value he created (client relationships, team mentoring, institutional knowledge) far exceeded the value he received. He was being extracted. Burnout forced the reckoning. He set boundaries: no email after 7pm, no weekend work, and a conversation with his manager about the value he actually created versus the compensation he received. The manager resisted — boundaries threatened the extraction. He held firm. Within a year, two things happened: he was healthier, and his work was better — because he brought energy and creativity instead of depletion and resentment. The value creation — his professional contribution — improved when extraction was replaced by fair exchange.

**A company and its platform contributors.** A marketplace platform built its value on small-business sellers. For years, the platform extracted: increasing fees, changing algorithms to favour paid placement, and using seller data to launch competing products. Sellers organised. They negotiated collectively for transparent fee structures, data ownership rights, and a seller advisory council with veto power over policy changes that affected them. The platform resisted, then conceded — because the sellers were the value creation, and their exit would destroy the platform. Two years later, seller satisfaction increased, seller retention improved, and the platform grew faster than before the negotiation — because contributors who are treated as partners create more value than contributors who are treated as inputs.

**A city and its public land.** A waterfront district had been public land for a century — accessible to all, used for markets, festivals, and daily recreation. A developer proposed privatising it: luxury apartments with a "public" promenade controlled by private security. The community organised a land trust. They negotiated with the city: the land would remain in public trust, governed by a community board. Development could occur — but it would be community-led, with mixed-income housing, genuine public space, and commercial rents reinvested in the district. The development took longer and produced less profit than the private alternative. But it produced a neighbourhood that belonged to its residents, rather than a neighbourhood that displaced them. Twenty years later, the land trust district was the most socially cohesive and economically stable area in the city.

**A fishery and its governance.** A coastal fishery was collapsing under industrial extraction. The government's response — quotas — was poorly enforced and widely evaded. Local fishers, who had governed the fishery informally for generations, proposed a co-management arrangement: they would monitor catch, enforce limits, and manage the fish stocks, with government providing legal backing and scientific support. The arrangement was established. Within a decade, fish populations recovered to pre-industrial levels. The local fishers earned more per catch (because the fish were larger and more valuable) with less effort (because the stocks were healthy). The commons governance — which cost the government less than enforcement — produced better ecological and economic outcomes than either pure regulation or unregulated extraction.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an era where AI agents participate in value creation, extraction takes forms that previous generations could not have imagined. Every click, every search, every conversation generates data that is captured, processed, and monetised — usually without the knowledge or consent of the person who generated it. AI models are trained on the collective output of millions of creators, workers, and thinkers — extracting patterns from their labour to create value that flows to a small number of companies.

Two implications:

**1. Data is the new enclosure.** What was once freely shared — conversation, creative expression, movement patterns, social relationships — has been enclosed by platforms that capture this data and extract value from it. The commons transition for the cognitive era requires recognising data as a commons resource: generated collectively, it should be governed collectively. This means data trusts, collective bargaining for data rights, transparent accounting of how data-derived value is distributed, and governance mechanisms that give data contributors meaningful control over how their data is used.

**2. AI can detect extraction that humans cannot see.** The same analytical power that enables extraction can be turned to detect it. AI can map value flows across complex systems — tracing where value is created, where it is captured, and where the gap between contribution and benefit is largest. This makes the "systematic" quality of the commons transition stronger: extraction that was previously invisible (because it was too complex, too distributed, or too slow to see) becomes diagnosable. But the diagnosis is only useful if the governance exists to act on it. AI that detects extraction but operates within an extractive system merely provides better information to the extractors.

The fundamental pattern does not change: extraction depletes, stewardship regenerates. But the scale, speed, and invisibility of digital extraction require that the commons transition be as technologically sophisticated as the extraction it seeks to transform.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

A value creation that has successfully transitioned from extraction to commons has a distinctive quality: contributors are visibly invested. They do not participate because they must, but because they benefit. They defend the commons — not out of obligation, but out of genuine ownership. Governance meetings are well-attended and substantive. Disputes are raised early and resolved fairly. New participants are welcomed because the commons is strong enough to share. And the resource itself is visibly regenerating — fish stocks growing, community energy increasing, creative output improving, soil health recovering.

The most reliable vitality sign: people contribute more than the minimum. In an extractive system, participants do the least they can — because every additional contribution is extracted. In a healthy commons, participants contribute generously — because they trust that the value returns.

**Signs of decay:**

The earliest sign is re-enclosure creep. Small exceptions to the commons rules — "just this once," "just for this partner," "just temporarily" — that gradually expand until the commons governance is hollow. Each exception is individually reasonable. Collectively, they are enclosure by increment.

The second sign is contributor withdrawal. The most committed contributors — the ones who cared most about the commons — begin to disengage. They do not leave dramatically. They simply stop volunteering for governance, stop contributing beyond the minimum, stop defending the commons in public. Their withdrawal is a signal that the extraction they fought to end is returning, and they no longer believe the governance can prevent it.

The third sign is benefit concentration. The value created by the commons begins flowing disproportionately to a few participants. The community land trust that was supposed to serve everyone begins favouring certain members. The cooperative that was supposed to share surpluses begins paying management more than workers. The open-source project that was supposed to benefit all contributors begins serving one company's roadmap. When benefits concentrate, the "just" quality has failed — and the commons is on its way back to extraction.
