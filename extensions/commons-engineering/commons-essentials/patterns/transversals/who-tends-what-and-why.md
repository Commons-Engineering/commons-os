> Assign care responsibilities based on who has the capacity, who benefits most, and who's closest to the problem. Make it

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This transversal pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of 4 domains.

---

### Section 1: Context

From O1: Every System Has Limits, Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation, Decide at the Lowest Level, Visibility as Commons Act. From O2: The Commons itself—systematic value creation that is shared and just. Stewardship is the practical mechanism by which a commons is tended. Yet stewardship is almost never equal. Some actors have more capacity (time, resources, expertise, proximity), more historical benefit (they inherited the resource), or clearer obligation (they created the problem they now must solve). The question is not whether to distribute stewardship unequally—it is inevitable—but whether that distribution is honest, proportional, formalized, and visible. Without differentiation and formalization, stewardship collapses: the most capable burn out carrying invisible burden, the least capable are silently exploited, the least responsible free-ride, and the commons degrades because no one is actually accountable for tending it.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Equal treatment vs. differentiated capacity | Invisible stewardship labor vs. formalized accountability.**

Across domains, stewardship work is treated as either invisible (Life: maintenance burden, care work; Ecology: monitoring, restoration) or diffuse (Urban: public goods like safety and trust; Business: equipment upkeep). When responsibility is not formally allocated based on capacity and benefit, several failures emerge: (1) Burnout—those with greatest capacity shoulder disproportionate burden; (2) Free-riding—those with least capacity and greatest benefit contribute least; (3) Collapse—when a critical steward leaves or exhausts their capacity, the commons degrads; (4) Injustice—the work of stewardship is undervalued and undercompensated, falling disproportionately on the poorest and most vulnerable. Additionally, stewardship obligations are often inherited unconsciously (through inheritance, legacy positions, or role assumption) without clarity about what was inherited or what is owed. The result: stewardship becomes a burden no one openly agreed to, rather than a role consciously claimed.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Differentiated Stewardship & Capacity-Calibrated Responsibility operates through three integrated mechanisms: AUDIT, FORMALIZE, and MAKE VISIBLE. First, AUDIT the stewardship landscape: inventory the resources, map who currently tends them, assess the real carrying capacity of each steward (their time, expertise, capital, proximity, historical relationship), and identify the true costs of stewardship (monitoring, maintenance, conflict resolution, adaptation). This audit answers: What needs tending? Who can realistically sustain that work? What are the hidden costs? Second, FORMALIZE differentiated responsibility based on that audit: allocate stewardship roles, rights, and obligations proportionally to capacity and historical benefit. This is not equal division—it is fair proportion. A large landholder bears more restoration burden; a legacy heir carries stewardship obligation proportional to their benefit; an equipment owner whose team has the expertise maintains that equipment. Formalize these allocations through documented agreements, customary law recognition, legal instruments (performance warrants, steward authority doctrines), or explicit family agreements (inheritance responsibility conversations, intergenerational wealth governance). Third, MAKE VISIBLE the true cost of stewardship: resource inventories, maintenance burden assessments, governance cost transparency, monitoring allocation frameworks, and quantification bias correction (counting invisible commons like trust and safety, not just easily measurable ones). This visibility serves two functions: it prevents people from acquiring stewardship obligations that exceed their capacity, and it prevents the system from exploiting stewards by treating their work as invisible. The mechanism is recursive and cyclical: audit creates the baseline; formalization allocates responsibility proportionally; visibility holds the system accountable; feedback adjusts allocations as capacity changes, resources degrade, or new stewards arrive. Across domains, this takes different forms—legal doctrine in urban commons, explicit family conversation in inherited wealth, ecological baseline surveys and monitoring protocols in land management, equipment maintenance scheduling in business—but the structure remains constant: capacity measured, responsibility allocated fairly, and stewardship made visible so it can be valued, sustained, and adjusted as conditions change.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming the work. Gather your stewards—whoever currently tends the resource, whether formally or not—and create a shared inventory of what actually needs doing. Don't estimate. Spend time observing: Who checks the water quality? Who fixes the fence after storms? Who hosts the difficult conversations when someone violates norms? Who remembers why decisions were made five years ago? Write it down. This audit is the first act of respect.

Next, assess capacity honestly. Capacity isn't just time available—it's expertise, proximity, social trust, and resources. A farmer living on the land has different capacity than a board member in the city. A young parent has different carrying limits than a retired engineer. An organization with budget has different constraints than volunteers. Create a simple matrix: for each stewardship task, list who currently does it, what it actually requires, and whether that person has genuine capacity or is already overextended.

Then formalize agreements—but lightly. Not bureaucratic contracts. Shared understandings. Document who tends what, when, and why. Include succession: who learns this work next? Who substitutes during absence? This formalization does three things: it makes invisible labor visible (often for the first time), it prevents the same person from inheriting every task by default, and it creates permission structures for others to step in.

Distribute tasks using three calibrations: *Historical benefit* (those who have gained most from the commons contribute proportionally), *Proximity* (those closest to the problem carry more weight, but not all), and *Genuine capacity* (never ask someone to steward beyond their real limits). A wealthy landowner who benefits from groundwater restoration contributes resources. A downstream farmer with less wealth contributes labor and local knowledge. A city agency contributes expertise. All are essential; none are equal, and that's fair.

Finally, rotate and document. Stewardship shouldn't calcify around one person. Build apprenticeship into the structure. Make it normal for responsibilities to shift as life changes. When someone steps back, someone steps forward with support.

### Section 5: Consequences

When differentiated stewardship is tended well, several things flourish. Burden distributes justly across real capacity, so burnout dissolves. Invisible work becomes visible, which means it's finally valued. People develop genuine relationships with the resource because they're tending it directly. Younger stewards learn from experienced ones. The commons doesn't depend on any single heroic person—it's resilient across generations and transitions.

But if this pattern is neglected, specific harms emerge. The most common: default stewards (often women, often those with most care orientation) absorb all work by proximity and empathy, become exhausted, and vanish. The commons then faces collapse because no one else learned the work. Alternatively, responsibility drifts so diffusely that nothing gets tended—the fence rots, conflicts simmer unresolved, knowledge dies with the person who held it.

There's also a risk of over-formalizing. Bureaucratic stewardship checklists can kill the gift relationship and sense of mutual care that make commons work sustainable. The goal isn't perfect accountability in an auditing sense; it's honest clarity about who's carrying what, why it matters, and whether that distribution is actually fair.

When differentiated stewardship works, people feel seen. When it fails, they feel either invisible or trapped.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Andean *ayni* and *minka* systems**: For centuries, Quechua communities have practiced reciprocal labor exchange where stewardship of irrigation channels, terraces, and communal grazing lands is assigned based on benefit received, land holdings, and seasonal capacity. Wealthier families contribute resources; younger able-bodied members contribute labor; elders contribute knowledge and conflict resolution. The system is formalized through community assemblies and rotates leadership annually. Work is visible, valued publicly, and explicitly linked to fairness.

**Japanese forest commons (*satoyama*)**: Village forests in regions like Nagano have sustained stewardship for centuries by differentiating roles: certain families hold harvesting rights and carry maintenance responsibilities; the village elder stewards dispute resolution and long-term planning; younger members handle physical restoration work. Benefits flow to those who tend (timber, fuel, medicinal plants), creating direct incentive. Capacity constraints are respected—elders don't climb trees; the strongest don't make strategic decisions alone.

**Bangladesh Water Management Associations**: Following major floods, communities formalized stewardship of embankments and drainage systems by assigning roles based on land elevation (those at greatest risk from flooding take active roles in maintenance), available time, and technical expertise. Farmers contribute labor proportional to benefit. Engineers oversee design. Wealthier landowners fund repairs. Women manage household water collection and reporting. The visible assignment prevents collapse when donors leave and ensures local capacity actually carries the work.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an era of distributed intelligence and AI assistance, the *audit* phase of this pattern becomes both easier and more urgent. AI can help map stewardship landscapes quickly—tracking who does what in complex systems (watersheds, digital commons, supply chains). But this same visibility creates new risk: the temptation to optimize stewardship into pure efficiency, removing the relational and gift dimensions that make commons work sustainable.

The real shift is this: differentiated stewardship becomes *transparent* in new ways. Algorithms can calculate who has capacity, what's needed, who benefits most. But humans must still decide: Is fairness calculated or relational? Do we use AI to automate the formalization (risky—it can kill trust) or to inform human negotiation (better)?

Distributed AI also enables new forms of stewardship. Sensors monitor water quality; humans interpret meaning. Blockchain can track contributions; community still decides if contribution is valued. The pattern's core—honest calibration of who tends what and why—becomes more possible and more necessary. Without it, AI-assisted systems can create invisible hierarchies faster than humans can notice.

The cognitive shift: stewardship becomes more legible, but *deciding how to value different forms of stewardship*—care work versus technical work, local knowledge versus expertise—remains irreducibly human.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: Stewardship tasks are named and written down somewhere, yet that document is used and revised, not archived. People can explain why they do their work and why others do theirs—not defensively, but matter-of-factly. Younger people are learning stewardship work from older ones. Someone steps back, someone steps forward; transitions happen without crisis. The work feels visible enough that it's valued, but light enough that it doesn't feel like surveillance. When tension arises about fairness, people can point to the shared agreement and renegotiate it.

**Signs of decay**: Stewardship work is either invisible (everyone assumes someone else is handling it) or completely dependent on one person. New people don't learn the work; knowledge dies with the steward. People are overextended and stop showing up. Arguments about fairness happen but there's no shared framework to resolve them. The agreement exists only in someone's head, or on a dusty document no one reads. Free-riding emerges because no one can see who's actually carrying what. The commons begins to fail not from external pressure but from internal stewardship collapse.

**Diagnostic question**: *If your key steward disappeared tomorrow, would the commons survive? Could someone else step in within a month? Could anyone explain why stewardship is distributed the way it is, and whether they think it's fair?*

If the answer to any of these is "no," the pattern is not yet alive.
