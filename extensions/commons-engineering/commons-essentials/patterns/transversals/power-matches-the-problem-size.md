### Section 1: Context

This transversal emerges from Orbit 1 principles of **Polycentric Organisation** (nested, semi-autonomous authority centres), **Subsidiarity** (decisions at lowest competent level), and **Carrying Capacity** (resource systems have natural functional boundaries). The 42 patterns in this cluster all diagnose the same failure mode: commons collapse when governance scale does not match functional scale. A civic association cannot manage a watershed. A city government cannot make daily decisions about a dog park. A household cannot preserve pollinator habitat in isolation. Yet naive solutions create opposite failures: attempt to centralise all governance at the 'right' ecological scale and you destroy local autonomy and adaptive capacity; keep all authority local and you create tragedy-of-the-commons across boundaries. The solution is neither centralisation nor fragmentation but **scalar coherence through nested governance with clear subsidiarity, veto rights at adjacent scales, and federated coordination protocols.** This requires designing the governance structure to match the actual nested scales of the resource system.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Local autonomy vs. cross-scale coordination; subsidiarity vs. collective action at appropriate ecological/social scale.**

**The Scalar Mismatch Dilemma.** Most commons operate within multiple overlapping functional scales simultaneously—a watershed is nested within a bioregion; a neighbourhood park serves both block-level users and city-level ecosystem function; a household's livelihood draws from local production and global supply chains. Yet governance institutions are often designed at arbitrary scales (city boundaries, administrative districts, property lines) that do not align with these functional realities. Three failure modes emerge: **(1) Scale Collapse:** Decision-making migrates upward to convenient centralised authority, destroying local autonomy and adaptive learning. Local knowledge is ignored; member participation atrophies. **(2) Fragmentation:** Each scale operates independently; coordination fails. Local overexploitation damages downstream commons. Neighbourhood decisions ignore city-scale ecological function. Individual choices compound into systemic harm. **(3) Tyranny of Majority or Paralysis:** A single governance scale tries to serve heterogeneous needs (some want quiet zones, others want intensive use). Majority rules suppress minority preferences, or consensus requirements paralyse all action. The underlying problem: **governance authority, decision-making granularity, and functional scale are decoupled.** Without intentional alignment, commons degrade whether through centralisation, fragmentation, or internal conflict.

---

### Section 3: Solution

**Nested Scale Coherence** requires four operational moves: **(1) Functional Scale Mapping.** Before designing governance, identify the actual nested scales at which the resource system operates. For ecology: household plot, village territory, watershed, bioregion—each with distinct ecological processes (nutrient cycling, water movement, species migration, climate regulation). For urban commons: micro-local (block), neighbourhood, district, city, region. For life/personal commons: daily/household, community, network, global. The scales are not arbitrary; they are determined by the carrying capacity, feedback loops, and externality patterns of the system itself. **(2) Subsidiarity with Scalar Authority Nesting.** Establish explicit decision-making authority at each scale: What decisions are made by households alone? What requires neighbourhood consensus? What is watershed-scale coordination? What is bioregional? Subsidiarity means: decisions affecting only that scale's function are made locally; decisions with cross-scale impact require coordination protocol (federation, cross-scale learning networks, or explicit veto rights). **(3) Protective Veto & Escalation Protocols.** Prevent upscale capture of downscale autonomy through mutual veto rights: adjacent scales can veto decisions that directly threaten their commons' health, but cannot veto purely local choices. Enable escalation without centralisation: if a neighbourhood cannot resolve conflict with a neighbouring commons, it escalates to federation; if federation cannot resolve, to region—but each step is explicit, reviewable, and has triggers for devolution back to lower scale. **(4) Heterogeneity Within Coherence.** Allow rules, preferences, and designs to vary significantly across local commons while maintaining overall coherence: different neighbourhoods have different quiet/active zoning; different farms practice different land stewardship; different households prioritise differently. The coherence comes not from uniform rules but from: (a) transparent boundary maintenance between zones, (b) reciprocal learning networks across commons, (c) shared monitoring and feedback at cross-scale level, (d) escalation protocols that clarify when local variance threatens adjacent commons. **Example: Watershed Commons.** Household-level decisions (how much water to use, what to plant) made by residents. Neighbourhood-level decisions (shared irrigation infrastructure, seasonal allocation protocols) made by neighbourhood assembly. Village-level decisions (inter-neighbourhood conflicts, emergency allocation) made by village council. Watershed-level decisions (total sustainable extraction, environmental flow requirements, cross-village allocation during drought) made by federated watershed authority with binding veto on extraction rates. Bioregional decisions (climate adaptation, species migration corridors, long-term sustainability planning) made by bioregional authority with veto rights on watershed-level decisions that threaten larger-scale function. Each scale has authority; each scale is accountable to adjacent scales through veto and feedback; decisions remain local when impact is local; coordination is federated not centralised. **Example: Urban Commons.** Dog park micro-commons governed by daily users (rules, maintenance, conflict resolution). Multiple dog parks federated at neighbourhood scale to share resources and resolve breed/size conflicts. Neighbourhood parks coordinated at district scale for city-wide access equity. District parks networked at city scale for ecological connectivity and resilience. City parks aligned at bioregional scale with watershed health and native habitat. Each level has authority over its scale's function; each has veto over upscale decisions that threaten its autonomy; heterogeneous rule-sets coexist (some parks dog-only, some mixed-use, some wilderness) while ecological coherence is maintained through cross-scale learning and monitoring.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping the functional scales where your specific commons actually operates. This isn't theory—it's observing where the real work happens. For a water system: trace where precipitation falls, where it flows, where it's used, where pollution enters, where flooding occurs. For a neighbourhood garden: identify who plants daily, who makes seasonal decisions, who handles conflicts, who connects to city policy. Each scale has a different rhythm and a different set of actors who understand it from the inside.

Once you see the scales, the work is **naming authority at each level**. This means: Who decides *what, when, and how* at the household scale? At the neighbourhood? At the watershed or district? The key move is subsidiarity with escalation: the smallest competent group decides, full stop—unless the decision affects a larger scale, in which case it surfaces upward with transparency, not permission-seeking.

Concretely: establish **clear decision rules for each scale**. A neighbourhood land trust might decide its own garden plots autonomously (local scale). But if a plot owner wants to use pesticides that run into the shared irrigation system, that touches watershed function—it escalates. The escalation isn't veto from above; it's a structured conversation where both scales are heard.

Create **linking mechanisms between scales**. Delegation flows downward (higher scales empower lower ones to act without constant approval). Information flows upward (lower scales report emerging needs or conflicts that need cross-scale attention). Representation flows both ways—lower-scale members sit on higher-scale bodies, so lived experience informs larger decisions.

Build in **veto rights and buffer zones**. Subsidiarity only works if smaller scales have real power to say no. In Iroquois governance, village councils could decline to send warriors to confederate wars. In Swiss cantons, communes retain substantive authority even within federal structures. Design explicit points where a lower scale can refuse upward pressure, with clear process for resolving deadlock.

Finally, **review and rebalance regularly**. Functional scales shift—a neighbourhood changes, a watershed faces new stresses, supply chains break. Every 2–3 years, ask: Are decisions still happening at the right scale? Where is power getting stuck? Where is it fragmenting?

### Section 5: Consequences

When power matches problem size, remarkable things flourish: decisions are faster (no unnecessary layers), more informed (people closest to the issue decide), and more legitimate (affected parties had voice). Trust grows because the scale of accountability matches the scale of impact. A neighbourhood with real authority over its commons builds social fabric; a watershed managed across its actual boundary protects ecosystems rather than treating symptoms.

Resource flow improves. Money, materials, and attention move toward where they're actually needed, not trapped in misaligned bureaucracies. Knowledge accumulates locally—a farmer knows her soil better than any distant expert. Communities develop adaptive capacity: when conditions shift, the scale that can respond fastest does.

But the risks are real if this pattern is neglected. **Fragmentation** is the most visible danger: if every local scale acts without cross-scale coordination, a river dies from a thousand small pollutions that no one scale can see. A city's housing crisis worsens when neighbourhoods hoard land and resist density, unable or unwilling to serve regional need.

**Capture at intermediate scales** is subtler and more insidious. The scale that coordinates between local and global—the regional authority, the watershed board, the supply chain hub—can become a bottleneck where power concentrates. It can serve local scales or exploit them, depending on how accountability is wired.

**Tyranny of the local** emerges when subsidiarity becomes insularity. A neighbourhood might decide to exclude newcomers, disable accessibility, or accept toxic industry nearby because outsiders bear the cost. Subsidiarity requires that lower scales make authentic decisions *within the constraint that they cannot export their harms to other scales*.

And there's the risk of **scale blindness**: mistaking the scale you inhabit for the only real scale. A household sees daily needs but not watershed limits. A city sees economic opportunity but not bioregional carrying capacity. This pattern requires intellectual humility about which scales matter and for which decisions.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Swiss Cantonal Federalism.** Cantons retain substantial authority over education, healthcare, and taxation. The federal government handles defence, currency, and cross-cantonal coordination. This distribution evolved from functional necessity—education serves communities, currency serves the entire economy. It's not perfect (urban cantons can dominate rural ones), but the principle of aligning power to scale has held for centuries. The system survives because it genuinely distributes decision-making, and because mechanisms exist for renegotiation.

**The Iroquois Confederacy (Haudenosaunee).** Villages were autonomous in daily governance. The Confederacy council handled inter-nation relations and collective defence. Crucially, villages could veto confederate war declarations—the scale making the sacrifice had veto power. Women held property and could withdraw support (food, shelter) from wars they opposed. The system aligned authority to the scale affected by decisions. It functioned for centuries until colonialism shattered it.

**The Mutuelles in Senegal and West Africa.** These are rotating savings and credit associations operating at neighbourhood scale. A group of 20–50 people makes all decisions about membership, rules, and disbursement. They're nested within informal city networks and sometimes linked to formal microfinance. The scale is small enough that everyone knows default risk; it's large enough to accumulate meaningful capital. Decisions happen where trust and information exist. The model has survived colonialism and economic restructuring because power and competence are genuinely aligned at the small scale, while loose federation allows resource flows across groups.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence change the **texture of scale matching** without eliminating the principle. Traditionally, subsidiarity worked because local people had information that distant bureaucrats couldn't access—a farmer knew microclimates, a neighbourhood knew its conflicts. AI can now process watershed data in real time, model housing needs across regions, forecast supply chain stress. This tempts centralization: "Now we can decide everything from the centre."

That's a trap. AI gives you *better information at each scale*, not a reason to collapse scales. A watershed manager can now see nutrient flows in real time—but the farmer still needs to decide what to plant next season, grounded in soil, weather, and livelihood. The farmer's decision is *more legitimate and effective* when informed by real-time data, not *replaced* by it.

What changes is **faster feedback loops and clearer consequence visibility**. A neighbourhood can see immediately how its water use affects the watershed. A city can see how its zoning affects regional housing. This transparency should push toward finer alignment, not centralization. It reveals where local decisions have cross-scale impact, triggering escalation protocols and renegotiation of boundaries.

The danger: **opacity of the algorithm becomes a new form of distant control**. If a machine-learning system recommends which decisions should escalate, or which scale should have authority, we've replaced visible bureaucrats with invisible ones. The pattern requires that escalation and boundary-drawing remain *interpretable and contestable*—humans (affected by the decision) must be able to understand and challenge why a decision is happening at this scale rather than that one.

Peer-to-peer networks and blockchain also enable **new linking mechanisms**: real-time coordination across scales without hierarchical intermediaries. A watershed can coordinate among dozens of municipalities without a central authority. But this only works if the underlying principle—subsidiarity with clear escalation—is embedded. The technology is neutral; it amplifies whatever governance logic you put into it.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Decisions are made visibly and close to those affected. People can name who decided and why. When something breaks, the relevant scale feels the consequence and acts to fix it. Feedback is swift—not months of reports but days. There's visible tension and negotiation between scales (this is healthy, not a sign of breakdown). Small-scale experiments happen, successes spread, failures are contained. Cross-scale representation is active—people from lower scales sit on higher-scale bodies and bring lived experience. Subsidiarity is real: lower scales routinely make decisions without asking permission upward.

**Signs of decay:** Decisions are made distant from those affected. "That's not our scale to decide" becomes an excuse for inaction at the scale that could act. Escalation becomes one-way—upward power flows freely, but downward empowerment is weak. Representatives from lower scales sit on higher bodies but are ignored or co-opted. Subsidiarity becomes rhetorical while real power concentrates. Cross-scale impacts are invisible or denied—a city doesn't see the watershed cost of its growth; a region doesn't see the household burden of its labour policies. Feedback loops are broken—it takes months or years to know if a decision worked. Smaller scales fragment into isolated islands.

**The diagnostic question:** *When the last significant decision was made that affected this commons, at what scale did it happen, and was that the smallest scale competent to make it?* If the answer is "I don't know who decided," or "a distant authority decided," or "we were never asked"—power and problem are mismatched. If the answer is "we decided locally, with information about cross-scale impact and clear escalation if needed"—the pattern is alive.
