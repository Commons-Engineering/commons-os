> When a shared system creates extra value—profit, resources, benefits—you need clear rules for who gets what and why. Don

### Section 1: Context

This transversal orbits four principles that converge on a single force: how to govern the distribution of value in systems that create it collectively. Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation demands that contributions be matched by benefits over time. Cooperation Creates Surplus asks whether agents stay committed to shared objectives—and surplus allocation is the primary signal of commitment. Channel Self-Interest acknowledges that agents respond to how rewards are structured; invisible or inequitable allocation undermines participation. The Challenge of Cooperation recognizes that surplus distribution is itself a collective action problem: without rules, the strongest capture benefits; with rules, the system can regenerate. Across business, ecology, life, and urban domains, we see the same pattern repeating: systems that endure make surplus visible, distribute it transparently, and treat allocation as a governance question, not a technical afterthought. Systems that hide surplus or allow elite capture degrade rapidly as trust erodes and marginal contributors withdraw.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Accumulation by stewards vs. Fair distribution to contributors and beneficiaries.**

In every domain, surplus emerges from collective value creation—but surplus is politically contested and structurally invisible. In business, profit from worker labor flows primarily to shareholders unless governance actively redirects it. In ecology, benefits from ecosystem services or conservation efforts flow to distant investors and governments while stewards bear costs. In urban commons, value created by steward labor (cleaning, organizing, monitoring) is extracted by property owners while stewards remain underpaid. In personal and organizational life, privilege and resources accumulate invisibly to the already-advantaged unless deliberately redistributed. The tension is acute: (1) Stewards and contributors lose motivation when surplus flows to non-contributors. (2) Marginalized members subsidize affluent ones, deepening inequality. (3) The system loses legitimacy when distribution is perceived as unfair. (4) Without reinvestment of surplus, commons infrastructure degrades. (5) External actors (investors, governments, markets) extract value that should remain in the system. The problem deepens because surplus allocation is rarely treated as a governance question at all—it defaults to the path of least resistance (shareholder capture, rent extraction, elite accumulation) unless deliberately designed otherwise.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Surplus Allocation Governance is a composite governance practice with three integrated components:

**1. VISIBILITY & QUANTIFICATION**
Make surplus explicit. In business, calculate actual profit, distinguish operating costs from surplus, and track where it goes. In ecology, quantify ecosystem service values (carbon sequestration, water filtration, pollination), measure commons revenues, and track benefit flows. In urban commons, calculate service worker compensation against value created, measure external demand for commons resources. In personal life and organizations, audit how resources, opportunities, and privilege flow. Visibility is not neutral—it reveals structural inequities that remain hidden when surplus is implicit.

**2. ALLOCATION FORMULA DESIGN**
Establish explicit rules for how surplus is distributed. These rules should be (a) transparent—everyone understands the logic, (b) values-based—reflecting what the system actually prioritizes, not what shareholders default to, and (c) multi-stakeholder—specifying claims for stewards, contributors, reinvestment, marginalized members, and future regeneration. Common allocation logics include: equal per-capita (every member receives equal share), contribution-weighted (proportional to labor or resources invested), need-based (prioritizing those with greatest need), or commons-reinvestment (prioritizing system capacity). Hybrid approaches are typical: subsistence needs for stewards first, then infrastructure reinvestment, then proportional distribution, then external benefit. The key is that the formula is **explicit, democratically decided, and regularly reviewed**—not hidden in accounting structures.

**3. FEEDBACK & EQUITY AUDITING**
Regularly assess whether actual distribution aligns with intended allocation formula and whether distribution is equitable across gender, wealth, age, ethnicity, and other axes. Benefit-sharing equity audits reveal who captures benefits and who bears costs. Is surplus actually reaching stewards or being siphoned to administrators? Are marginalized members receiving proportional benefit? Is reinvestment actually strengthening commons infrastructure, or is surplus being extracted? Feedback loops trigger course correction: if distribution is inequitable, governance processes adjust the formula.

**Cross-Domain Integration**: The transversal works across domains because it applies the same three-part structure to different kinds of surplus. In business, surplus is financial profit. In ecology, it is ecosystem service value and commons revenue. In urban commons, it is the difference between service costs and property value generated. In personal systems, it is unearned privilege and resource flows. The mechanism—visibility, formula, audit, feedback—is universal. The application varies.

**Why This Matters for the Commons**: Surplus allocation governance directly strengthens the five qualities. It makes value **alive** by creating feedback loops that activate stewardship—stewards see benefits flowing and commitment deepens. It ensures value is **shared**—surplus becomes visible and distributed, not hidden. It builds **justice**—marginalized contributors receive fair benefit, external extraction is prevented, accumulation is bounded. It makes the system **built to last** because reciprocity is honored, trust is maintained, and commons infrastructure is reinvested in. It is **systematic**—allocation is governed by rules, not ad-hoc extraction. Without this governance, commons accumulate by elite capture. With it, commons regenerate.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making surplus visible—this is foundational. Gather your community and ask: What value are we creating together? For a worker cooperative, this means transparent accounting: total revenue minus genuine operating costs equals surplus. For a land trust, it's ecosystem services quantified (carbon sequestrated, water cleaned, habitat restored) plus financial revenue. For a shared garden or neighborhood commons, it's the amenity value created (property enhancement, food production, gathering space) and volunteer labor hours.

Once surplus is named, design allocation rules *before* the surplus appears. This prevents fights over windfall. Create a simple decision framework: What percentage goes to reinvestment in commons infrastructure? What percentage returns to active contributors? What percentage supports those burdened by the commons (noise from a shared workshop, wildlife impacts on adjacent lands)? What percentage seeds new commons or supports the broader movement?

Make these rules visible and participatory. A worker cooperative might allocate: 40% reinvestment, 35% distributed to workers by contribution metric, 15% to the broader solidarity economy, 10% to a community benefit fund. A community land trust might allocate: 50% land stewardship, 30% affordable housing reserves, 15% community programs, 5% network support.

Document *why* each rule exists. The reasoning builds legitimacy. A high reinvestment percentage isn't theft—it's survival for commons under extraction pressure. A contributor-based distribution isn't favoritism—it's reciprocal honoring of effort.

Create accountability mechanisms: annual transparent reports showing actual surplus, how it was allocated, and why. Let members question the math and propose adjustments. This isn't bureaucracy—it's the listening that keeps stewardship honest.

Most importantly, test allocation rules on small surpluses first. A cooperative's first modest profit is the moment to prove fairness works before larger sums test your commitment. Start with clarity, adjust with humility, rebuild trust through consistency.

### Section 5: Consequences

When allocation is transparent and fair, several things flourish: Contributors feel reciprocity—their effort is visibly returned. This isn't just financial; it's the deeper reciprocal trust that holds commons together across seasons of hardship. Stewards lose the burden of hidden judgment about who deserves what. Members stay engaged because they see themselves in the system's rewards.

The commons themselves strengthen. Reinvested surplus funds the unglamorous work: maintenance, monitoring, conflict resolution, documentation. Without visible reinvestment, commons decay silently. With it, they become more resilient, more beautiful, more capable of serving members across time.

But risks emerge if this pattern atrophies. Hidden allocation creates the suspicion that stewards are extracting value. Even if they aren't, the appearance alone fractures trust. Contributors feel cheated; members stop showing up. The commons becomes a hollow institution, drained of the reciprocal energy that made it work.

Allocation rules can also become rigid, losing responsiveness to genuine need. A strict formula might distribute surplus to active contributors while a member faces hardship. Or reinvestment rules might calcify around outdated infrastructure while members need something new. The pattern requires both structure and flexibility.

There's also the risk of performative fairness: elaborate allocation systems that look good on paper while actual power remains concentrated. A worker cooperative might distribute surplus equitably while key decisions remain with a board. The pattern only works if allocation rules align with genuine decision-making power.

Finally, poorly designed allocation can accidentally incentivize the wrong behaviors: rewarding visible busyness over impact, or accumulation over sharing. The structure must reinforce the values you actually want to cultivate.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragón Cooperatives (Spain).** Since 1956, this federation of worker cooperatives has distributed surplus through a transparent formula: portion to reinvestment, portion to workers (differentiated by role and seniority but with a capped ratio—highest earner makes no more than 6 times the lowest), portion to the cooperative fund for education and mutual aid, portion to community. This visible fairness has kept Mondragón resilient through economic crises while maintaining cooperative culture across 80+ enterprises and 80,000 members.

**Indigenous land trusts in Australia and Canada.** Communities managing traditional lands often allocate benefits from carbon credits, conservation payments, and tourism revenue through decision-making processes tied to kinship and stewardship responsibility rather than market ownership. The Daintree Rainforest Aboriginal Corporation, for instance, allocates revenues to environmental restoration, youth employment, cultural programs, and elder support—visibly centering reciprocal obligation rather than profit extraction.

**Platform cooperatives like Stocksy and Fairbnb.** These reimagine digital commons by allocating surplus differently than extractive platforms. Stocksy (photographer cooperative) keeps ownership and profit-sharing with contributors; Fairbnb allocates a percentage of fees to local community development funds in neighborhoods where hosts operate. Both are explicit: you see where money flows and why. This transparency, though lower profit overall, builds loyalty because members recognize themselves in the system's rewards.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence will make surplus allocation simultaneously easier and more contested. Easier because transparent accounting becomes trivial—algorithmic tracking of value creation, contribution metrics, and benefit flows can operate at scale with unprecedented clarity. A digital commons can know in real time who contributed what, what was created, and where surplus flowed.

But this precision becomes a trap. Quantification can obscure what matters. Not all contribution is measurable: emotional labor, institutional memory, presence during crisis, the wisdom that prevents mistakes. An AI-optimized allocation system might fairly distribute financial surplus while completely missing the invisible contributions that hold a commons together.

The deeper shift is toward participatory surplus governance at scale. Distributed intelligence can help communities run sophisticated allocation experiments: modeling different formulas, simulating their effects, and learning what maintains fairness while preserving trust. This democratizes allocation design away from specialist boards and toward member deliberation.

But distributed systems also create new extraction risks. Value can flow upward through algorithmic feeds, recommendation systems, and data harvesting before allocation systems even see it. A platform cooperative must govern not just the visible surplus it distributes, but the invisible value extraction happening through its infrastructure. Sharing gains fairly now means auditing where value flows before it reaches the allocation table.

### Section 8: Vitality

A commons with vital surplus allocation shows clear signs: Members can articulate the allocation formula and the reasoning behind it. They don't all agree with it, but they know it. There's visible evidence of reinvestment—maintained infrastructure, improved tools, documented learning. Contributors receive some reciprocal return, however modest. There's an annual moment of transparency where actual allocation is discussed and adjusted.

You see contestation, not consensus. Healthy allocation governance includes disagreement about fairness. Members argue about whether reinvestment is high enough, or whether hidden contributors are being seen. This friction, managed respectfully, keeps the pattern honest.

The diagnostic question: *Can a new member, within a month, explain where surplus goes and why?* If they can't, allocation has become invisible again—and invisibility is where extraction hides.

Signs of decay appear first as silence. Nobody talks about surplus anymore because nobody knows where it goes. Reinvestment becomes sporadic; infrastructure deteriorates. Contributors feel unrecognized—their effort isn't visibly reciprocated. Trust shifts from reciprocal to transactional: people participate only for direct payment, not for the commons itself.

Then comes the faster decay: whistleblowers appear. Someone finally names the hidden allocation; members discover stewards kept surplus for themselves or gave it to favored groups. The commons fractures. Recovery requires not just new rules but genuine accountability and repair.

The pattern asks something continuous of stewards: the willingness to be transparent about power, to submit allocation decisions to member scrutiny, to adjust when fairness falters. This is cultivation work. It has no end state. The gain is not a perfect system but a commons where reciprocal trust is visibly, repeatedly honored.
