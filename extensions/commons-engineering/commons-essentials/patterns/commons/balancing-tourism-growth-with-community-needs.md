### Section 1: Context

Tourism in cities and towns operates as a dual commons problem: a shared resource (public space, cultural heritage, neighbourhoods, housing stock, ecosystems) is simultaneously an extraction mechanism for outside capital. In overtourism contexts—Barcelona, Venice, Amsterdam, Dharavi, Khayelitsha—tourism systematically converts community assets into commodities: housing becomes Airbnb inventory, culture becomes performed spectacle, labour becomes precarious hospitality work, and carrying capacity is ignored until collapse. The 28 Orbit 1 principles reveal the root failure: tourism governance treats growth as axiom rather than as a design choice bounded by resident wellbeing, ecological limits, and cultural integrity. When communities lose decision-making authority over tourism in their own neighbourhoods, extraction accelerates.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Tourism as extractive capital (maximizing visitor volume, investor profit, cultural commodification) vs. Tourism as stewarded commonwealth (bounded by resident wellbeing, ecological limits, cultural integrity, housing protection).**

Four intersecting tensions define the tourism commons crisis:

1) **Housing Colonisation**: Tourism capital (investors, platforms, speculators) competes for residential housing stock, driving displacement. Residents cannot afford neighbourhoods tourism makes valuable.

2) **Cultural Erosion under Commodification**: When tourism operators control cultural representation and revenue, living cultural practice gets compressed into performative product. Sacred practices commodify, intergenerational transmission stops, authenticity becomes inventory.

3) **Labour Invisibility & Precarity**: Tourism workers (guides, hospitality, care-mediators) absorb visitor volume without protection, wage floors, or voice in tourism decisions. Care work becomes free emotional labour rather than valued commons work.

4) **Carrying Capacity Collapse**: Tourism grows until ecological, infrastructural, and social systems break. Residents experience crowding, noise, rising prices, ecosystem degradation—costs borne locally while revenue leaks elsewhere. No one has authority to say "stop."

---

### Section 3: Solution

**Resident-Steered Tourism Commons** inverts decision-making authority from external operators to resident assemblies and implements four integrated mechanisms:

**Mechanism 1: Resident Governance with Binding Veto**
Neighbourhood assemblies (quarterly convenings) hold binding veto power over new tourism development, accommodation licenses, and infrastructure changes. Assemblies are composed of permanent residents only; tourists and outside investors cannot vote. Residents define what tourism is permitted, at what scale, during which seasons, and in which zones. This mechanism instantiates Subsidiarity—decisions stay with those who bear consequences.

**Mechanism 2: Carrying Capacity as Living Threshold**
Communities establish transparent numerical limits on visitor-night capacity per neighbourhood, derived from resident-to-visitor ratios and infrastructure absorption. Limits are not fixed dogma but adaptive: monitored quarterly via resident feedback, ecological indicators (biodiversity, water quality, noise), and social vitality metrics (public space accessibility, price stability, cultural event attendance). When thresholds breach, automatic visitor restrictions or seasonal closures trigger—no economic exemptions. This embeds Carrying Capacity & Feedback into governance.

**Mechanism 3: Revenue Capture & Local Circulation**
Tourism revenue (visitor taxes, accommodation levies, heritage fees, business contributions) flows into community-controlled commons pools, not municipal budgets or private operators. Mandatory allocation: (a) formalized paid positions for care workers—elders, youth, disabled people as cultural interpreters and conflict mediators, funded at living wage; (b) affordable housing protection (acquisition of tourism-commodified properties, conversion to permanent housing); (c) ecological restoration obligations (proportional to visitor footprint, operator-executed and community-verified); (d) worker security fund (0.5-1% payroll tax supporting emergency hardship, paid leave, training). Revenue stays local and visible; residents see how tourism funds their care economy. This enacts Reciprocity & Economic Constraint—tourism extracts resource; extraction must be repaid to those bearing costs.

**Mechanism 4: Housing Decoupling & Cultural Sovereignty**
Residential housing is legally and fiscally decoupled from tourism accommodation. Permanent owner-occupancy or long-term tenancy is required; short-term rental prohibition or heavy tax in residential zones. Residents hold binding right-of-first-refusal when tourism-commodified housing returns to market. For culture: communities explicitly define what cultural practices are available for tourism representation and what remains sacred, family-only, or off-limits. This boundary is protected by covenant and community monitoring, not market force. Ceremonial integrity is non-negotiable.

**Mechanism 5: Seasonal Reclamation Cycles**
Public spaces and cultural amenities operate on seasonal cycles: during high tourism seasons, zones are managed for visitor access but with resident-priority booking windows (1-2 weeks ahead). In low seasons, public spaces are fully reclaimed by residents—plazas become resident markets, cultural venues host resident festivals, streets belong to neighbourhoods again. This pattern, grounded in Adaptive Cycles, ensures public commons never become permanently colonized; residents experience their own place as home, not museum backdrop.

**Integration**: These five mechanisms operate as a polycentric system. Assemblies (Mechanism 1) set carrying capacity targets (Mechanism 2), which trigger revenue obligations (Mechanism 3) and housing protections (Mechanism 4). Seasonal cycles (Mechanism 5) regenerate resident time and space. Feedback from residents continuously adjusts all parameters. Culture and housing are decoupled from tourism commodity logic. Care work becomes visible and compensated. No single actor—operator, investor, municipality—can override resident stewardship. Tourism becomes a stewarded commonwealth, not a growth machine.

## Section 4: Implementation

Resident-steered tourism commons works through deliberate cultivation of three nested structures: **governance capacity, economic redistribution, and cultural authentication**.

**Build neighbourhood assembly infrastructure first.** This isn't a suggestion box. Convene resident assemblies quarterly in accessible times and languages. Start small—20-30 people committed—and grow. Assemblies need stipends for organizers, childcare, translation. Their first act: map what matters. What housing is at risk? Which cultural practices are living (not museum pieces)? Where is public space degrading? This mapping becomes the baseline for veto decisions.

**Implement binding permits tied to resident welfare.** New accommodation licenses, tourism businesses, events, infrastructure—all require assembly approval. Design the veto with clear criteria: Does this displace residents? Does it extract cultural value without benefit-sharing? Does it damage ecosystems or public space? Make criteria visible, debatable, revisable. Veto isn't permanent; it's a pause that demands reckoning.

**Redistribute tourism revenue directly to residents and cultural stewards.** Tax visitor stays at 8-12% and direct funds to a community benefit fund. This fund is governed by residents, not city hall. Use it for: (1) below-market housing preservation and acquisition by community land trusts; (2) cultural steward salaries—people who maintain traditions, teach heritage, shape how tourism encounters culture; (3) public space maintenance and greening; (4) small business grants for resident-owned tourism enterprises (walking tours led by neighbours, homestays, craft sales).

**Create cultural authentication protocols.** Residents decide what cultural practices are available for tourism experience and under what conditions. Some practices remain private. Others become teachable if tourism operators partner with cultural stewards (who earn and control the narrative). No commodification without consent and benefit-sharing.

**Implement housing protection simultaneously.** Restrict short-term rentals to owner-occupied units or community-controlled properties. Acquire buildings at market prices through community land trusts before speculators do. Set caps on tourist beds per neighbourhood—hard numerical limits that assemblies can adjust based on experience.

This takes 18-36 months to stabilize. It requires legal scaffolding (city ordinances, trust deeds, revenue agreements). It requires patience with conflict—property owners will resist, platforms will litigate. But the pattern works because it treats the commons as non-negotiable and treats residents as the primary stakeholders, not the economy.

## Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:**

Neighbourhoods stabilize. When housing speculation is bounded, rents stabilize. Residents stay put. Schools and clinics serve familiar faces. Local networks deepen. Tourism becomes seasonal and bounded rather than all-consuming. Residents begin to see tourism as *their* asset rather than something happening *to* them.

Cultural stewards earn dignified income. Traditions don't disappear into Instagram feeds; they're maintained and transmitted by people who carry them. Younger people see possibility in cultural knowledge. Authenticity replaces performativity.

Public space recovers. When resident assemblies control infrastructure, they invest in what residents actually need: parks, plazas, libraries, cooling stations. These spaces become less crowded with merchandise and more alive with everyday life.

Small resident-owned businesses thrive. A neighbourhood bakery, a guided walk by an elder, a craft cooperative—these enterprises capture tourism spending because they're embedded in trust and authenticity.

**What risks emerge:**

**Governance capture by factions.** If assemblies aren't truly inclusive (if they exclude migrants, renters, non-property owners), they can become extractive tools for one group against others. Veto power in unprincipled hands becomes coercive.

**Tourism decline and economic hardship.** If caps are set too restrictively, tourism revenue drops. Workers in tourism sectors lose income. This is real hardship and requires transition support: retraining funds, income supplements, job creation in other sectors.

**Protectionism masquerading as community protection.** Some neighbourhoods may use veto to exclude outsiders, migrants, or particular cultural groups. Resident stewardship can harden into exclusion if not paired with explicit anti-discrimination accountability.

**Slow transformation of mindsets.** Residents accustomed to seeing tourism as inevitable may resist governance responsibility. Some prefer extraction (landlord rentals) to stewardship.

Tend these risks through transparency, conflict mediation, external audit, and willingness to revise protocols when harms emerge.

## Section 6: Known Uses

**Barcelona's Superblocks + Housing Resistance (Spain).** In the Poblenou neighbourhood and others, resident assemblies have slowed tourism accommodation licensing and redirected hostel taxes to affordable housing acquisition. Barcelona's 2019 tourism plan explicitly capped cruise ships and new hotels. The model isn't perfect—gentrification pressure continues—but assemblies now have negotiating power they lacked before. Residents describe the shift as moving from *subjects of tourism* to *decision-makers*.

**Palermo's Ballarò Co-operative Markets (Sicily, Italy).** The historic markets faced erasure as food tourism commodified street culture. Resident market vendors (many immigrant families) formed governance councils with binding input on food tour licensing and pricing. They set rules: no more than 3 tour groups per hour, guides must be trained by vendors, 30% of revenue goes to market upkeep. Tourism exists, but it's stewarded. Vendors now earn more and control representation of their work.

**Oaxaca's Indigenous Tourism Collectives (Mexico).** In rural communities around Oaxaca city, indigenous assemblies control all tourism access through community-run cooperatives. Each village decides: Do we host tourism? What practices remain sacred and private? Collective models mean families benefit together; individual exploitation is harder. Cultural stewards are compensated by the community for teaching. This model predates the "sustainable tourism" trend—it's grounded in indigenous sovereignty practices where the community, not the market, is the primary unit.

These examples share a pattern: resident or community control replaces investor control. Revenue stays local. Cultural stewards are recognized and paid. Limits exist, and they're enforced.

## Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence reshape this pattern in crucial ways.

**Data transparency becomes a weapon and a risk.** Real-time sensors can track tourist density, housing market dynamics, cultural commodification patterns. Resident assemblies can demand algorithmic transparency: *Why is Airbnb promoting this neighbourhood?* *How are algorithms pricing rentals?* Platforms can no longer hide extraction behind opacity. This is good for resident power.

But the inverse risk: surveillance. If data collection serves not residents but city planners or platforms, it becomes a tool for controlling which neighbourhoods are "optimized" for tourism. Assemblies must own their own data infrastructure—not rent it from tech platforms.

**Algorithmic matching of cultural stewards to tourism experiences** can distribute income more equitably—connecting visitors directly to cultural practitioners—or it can further commodify cultural knowledge. The difference is who controls the algorithm: stewards and assemblies, or platforms.

**Distributed autonomous organizations (DAOs) or blockchain-based governance** might enable transparency in revenue-sharing and transparent voting for dispersed residents. Or they might create technical complexity that excludes residents without digital literacy. The tool is neutral; intention and care matter.

**AI-assisted urban simulation** allows assemblies to model tourism scenarios before implementation: *If we cap at 500 visitors daily, what happens to local businesses? Housing prices?* This shifts governance from intuition to evidence, giving residents stronger arguments.

The deeper shift: AI makes *extraction visible*. It can surface the hidden flows of money, data, and cultural value leaving neighbourhoods. Resident-steered commons becomes more feasible when residents can see, in real time, what tourism takes and leaves behind.

## Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life in this pattern:**

- Resident assemblies meet regularly, draw diverse attendance, make binding decisions that stick.
- Housing costs stabilize or decline; residents can afford to stay.
- Cultural stewards are earning and teaching; younger people are learning traditions from elders.
- Public space shows signs of everyday use—children playing, neighbours gathering, not just tourists passing through.
- Tourism revenue is visible in neighbourhood infrastructure: parks maintained, schools funded, libraries open.
- Resident-owned tourism businesses exist and thrive.
- Conflict occurs openly (not hidden resentment) and gets resolved.

**Signs of decay:**

- Assemblies become rubber stamps; real decisions happen elsewhere.
- Housing still rises; evictions continue; assembly veto gets ignored or litigated away.
- Cultural performances become more touristy, less authentic; stewards earn less over time.
- Public space degrades, feels crowded and commercialized.
- Tourism revenue disappears into municipal budgets or investor pockets; residents see no benefit.
- Resident-owned businesses can't compete; tourism consolidates into chains and platforms.
- Conflict becomes silent or explosive; people stop showing up.
- Youth leave because there's no economic future in stewarding culture.

**Diagnostic question:** 

*When a resident who has lived in this neighbourhood for 20 years is offered triple their apartment's rent by a tourism investor, can they afford to stay? And if they choose to, does the neighbourhood still feel like theirs, or like a place being performed for visitors?*

If the answer is "they can't afford to stay" or "it no longer feels theirs"—the pattern is decaying, no matter what the assembly voted. Vitality requires that residents remain *materially and psychologically rooted* in their place, not displaced by the wealth their place generates.
