### Section 1: Context

In mature civic systems, proposals often arrive at deliberation disconnected from their genealogy—stripped of prior iterations, stakeholder conflicts, rejected alternatives, and communities of origin. They appear as autonomous ideas rather than as responses to lived conditions. Simultaneously, proposals from under-resourced communities lack the sophistication and coalition-breadth of institutional submissions, creating structural advantage for elite proponents. Decisions affecting commons (shared infrastructure, environmental thresholds, public space) are made sector-by-sector without visibility into cross-sector trade-offs. Approved proposals often diverge from implementation without public accountability. These patterns fragment collective memory, concentrate deliberative voice, and erode trust in governance as responsive to shared flourishing rather than institutional convenience. This pattern draws from Genealogy Requirement, Cross-Sector Deliberation Linking, Dignity Covenant, Capacity Building Cohort, and Escalation Ladder principles—treating proposal stewardship as a commons unto itself, alive and self-maintaining.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Proposal atomization & opacity vs. genealogical accountability & cross-scale coherence.**

Three living tensions operate in proposal lifecycle governance:

**Genealogical Erasure vs. Accountability**: Proposals stripped of lineage appear neutral but encode invisible interests. Without knowing who initiated, what communities shaped it, and which alternatives were rejected, deliberators cannot assess whose values and needs are embedded. This opacity favours institutional actors who can afford opaque advocacy.

**Proposal Atomization vs. Coherence**: Unrelated proposals addressing the same problem domain create redundant effort, obscure shared concerns, and prevent commons-scale coordination. Conversely, forced consolidation erases credit and local agency.

**Implementation Divergence vs. Trust**: Approved proposals often drift during implementation without public visibility or accountability. Communities that invested in deliberation experience broken covenant, degrading faith in civic participation itself.

The deeper tension: proposals are treated as objects to be processed rather than as expressions of community intelligence that deserve dignified stewardship across their full lifecycle—from genealogy through implementation.

---

### Section 3: Solution

**Genealogy-Rooted Proposal Stewardship** operates as a nested, binding governance structure with five interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Genealogy Binding & Archive (Truth-Seeking as Commons Act)**
Every proposal submitted to deliberation must include visible genealogy: original framers, prior iterations, explicitly rejected alternatives, stakeholder conflicts that shaped design, and communities of origin. This genealogy is not advisory narrative but binding disclosure—platforms cannot accept proposals without it. A commons pool archive makes this genealogy searchable and contestable, enabling future stewards to trace how decisions evolved and to revive proposals when circumstances change. The archive is maintained collectively; platform vendors are stewards, not owners.

**2. Capacity-Building Cohorts & Diversity Requirements (Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation)**
Proposals must include sponsors from at least three distinct demographic or geographic cohorts, preventing elite capture before deliberation begins. Underrepresented communities receive resourced cohorts for proposal development—research support, mentorship, collaborative refinement—ensuring proposals from all communities reach deliberation with equal sophistication. This is not charity; it is correction for structural asymmetry. Experimentation budget allocations (reduced thresholds, rapid iteration) create safe space for emergent voices to test ideas at smaller scale before committing to deliberative resources.

**3. Cross-Sector Impact Disclosure & Escalation (Polycentric Governance)**
When a proposal affects commons (public space, shared infrastructure, environmental thresholds) or intersects multiple sectors (housing + transit, economy + environment), it automatically triggers cross-sector deliberation linking. Affected stakeholders across sectors are notified; interdependencies surface; joint deliberation occurs before approval. This prevents siloed decisions that create downstream harm. Proposals affecting commons automatically escalate through nested governance scales—from neighbourhood to district to city—with deliberative authority distributed according to impact radius and subsidiarity.

**4. Proposal Pooling & Commons Coordination (Cooperative Surplus)**
When multiple unconnected proposals address the same problem domain, a commons escalation mechanism surfaces them together. Originators are invited into facilitated negotiation to craft a joint proposal, with credit and benefit distributed across all originators. This prevents redundancy, surfaces shared concerns, and distributes power rather than concentrating it in a single proponent. Communities retain agency; pooling is negotiated, not imposed.

**5. Implementation Covenant & Public Accountability (Built to Last)**
Approved proposals trigger binding implementation timelines with transparent accountability. Implementing authorities publicly commit to timeline and trade-offs; delays escalate through governance channels; divergences from approved proposal require deliberative re-engagement. When implemented proposals fail to deliver promised outcomes, the genealogical record enables assessment of whether the proposal itself was flawed or whether implementation eroded it—preserving institutional learning.

**Stewardship as Ongoing Cycle**: This pattern treats proposal lifecycle as a continuous cycle of proposal-deliberation-implementation-evaluation-revival, not a linear process. Dormant proposals can be revived if circumstances change, bringing them back through genealogy-disclosure and cross-sector checking. Rejected proposals remain in the archive as learning for future stewards. Implementation divergences are documented in genealogical record, enabling next-generation refinement.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by establishing a **Proposal Genealogy Template** that becomes non-negotiable entry infrastructure. When someone files a proposal—whether for land use, budget allocation, program design, or policy—they complete a structured form: Who first named this problem? What communities experience it? What versions were tried before? What did we learn from failure? What alternatives did we reject, and why? Who disagreed, and what would their version look like?

This isn't bureaucratic busywork. It's *cartography*. You're mapping thought-territory so others can see where you've walked.

Next, **cultivate genealogy stewards**—people trained to help proposers excavate their own lineage. These aren't gatekeepers; they're midwives. They ask clarifying questions, help trace funding sources, surface buried stakeholder input, and ensure rejected alternatives are documented with integrity, not dismissal. Stewards might be neighborhood historians, longtime organizers, or people from affected communities paid to do this work.

Create a **living archive**—digital or hybrid—where genealogies are searchable and transparent. Not a filing cabinet. A *commons*. When someone searches "affordable housing," they see not just active proposals but the genealogy of housing conversations in that place over years. They see whose voices shaped earlier versions. They see what was tried and learned.

Establish **cross-proposal coherence meetings** where stewards and proposers review new submissions against existing genealogies. Does this proposal contradict something we committed to last year? Does it ignore a community that was central to an earlier iteration? Does it resurrect a rejected alternative without acknowledging why it was rejected? These meetings aren't rejection gates—they're *learning conversations*.

Finally, **link deliberation to genealogy**. When a proposal reaches decision-makers, the genealogy becomes the opening text. Not an appendix. The main document. Deliberators must engage the lineage before debating merits. This shifts the question from "Is this a good idea?" to "Is this responsive to where we actually are, and do we understand who shaped it?"

### Section 5: Consequences

**What Flourishes**

When genealogies are visible, underrepresented voices gain *narrative power*. A proposal that emerged from a South Side tenant association's ten-year fight carries different weight than one generated in a consultant's workshop. Genealogy doesn't guarantee adoption—but it ensures the origin story gets told by those who lived it. Trust increases because people see themselves in the proposal's history.

Cross-sector coherence improves. When a housing proposal can be traced back to a health department conversation about lead exposure, and both reference a food-justice initiative from three years prior, people begin seeing the *system* rather than isolated problems. Proposals start reinforcing each other instead of contradicting.

Innovation becomes rooted rather than extractive. New ideas are tested against what communities have already learned, reducing the wasteful cycle of rediscovering old solutions.

**What Risks Emerge**

The genealogy process can become another burden on already-exhausted community organizers if steward roles aren't properly resourced. Genealogy work requires time and sometimes payment. Without it, the pattern reproduces inequality by making invisible the labor of tracing.

There's a risk of *genealogical gatekeeping*—where stewards or early proposers use lineage narratives to exclude newcomers. "You weren't part of the original coalition, so your voice doesn't carry weight here." Genealogy must remain open to newcomers who genuinely engage the history, not closed to protect original authority.

Documentation can calcify. What was learned from a rejected alternative can be forgotten if the archive isn't tended. Genealogies need periodic review and update, or they become monuments rather than living maps.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Medellín's Participatory Budget & Genealogy Tracking (Colombia, 2004–present)**

Medellín's Sistema de Participación Ciudadana doesn't just ask for spending proposals; it requires proposers to document where ideas originated—in neighborhood assemblies, previous budget cycles, or community organizations. Successful proposals are traced back to their genealogy in annual reports, making visible which neighborhoods' ideas actually got funded and which got repeatedly proposed but ignored. This transparency has shifted power: underrepresented eastern communes can now point to patterns of rejection and demand reasons.

**Whose Knowledge Counts? Indigenous Land Proposals in Aotearoa New Zealand (1990s–present)**

When Māori communities propose land management or conservation approaches, the *Te Ao Māori* (Māori worldview) genealogy—spanning generations of ecological knowledge and cultural practice—is now legally required to be documented alongside Western scientific genealogy. This pattern emerged from decades of Māori organizers insisting that their proposals not be treated as novel ideas but as expressions of continuity. The effect: proposals grounded in multi-generational knowledge now carry authority they previously lacked.

**Participatory Budgeting in New York City Districts (2009–present)**

Though less formal than Medellín, successful PB efforts now track proposal genealogy informally through community meetings and documentation. When a public school proposal for youth programs resurfaces, facilitators trace it back: "This was proposed in 2015, triaged out, reframed in 2018, and is now back because the conditions that made us reject it have changed." This genealogical honesty prevents the exhaustion of re-pitching the same idea infinitely.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence reshape how genealogies are traced and held. Large language models can now rapidly scan archives, identify proposal patterns across years and sectors, and surface genealogical connections humans might miss—showing how a housing proposal echoes (or contradicts) earlier environmental justice work. This is powerful *if* genealogy stewards remain human decision-makers who interpret what the AI surface finds.

The risk is outsourcing genealogy judgment to algorithms. An AI might surface "this proposal contradicts an earlier one" without understanding the legitimate reasons why a community might deliberately revise or reject an earlier approach. Genealogy is interpretive work, not pattern-matching.

Distributed intelligence—networks of community historians, organizers, and data stewards across neighborhoods—can now maintain genealogies collaboratively in real-time, creating living archives that evolve as proposals evolve. This is closer to how genealogy actually works: iteratively, in conversation, not as a static record.

The key shift: genealogy moves from *archive* (a place you consult) to *protocol* (a way you think together). AI tools help maintain the protocol, but humans decide what genealogy *means* for this moment's decision.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life**

Genealogies are actively used in deliberation. When decision-makers reference the history of a proposal, its rejected alternatives, and the communities that shaped it—even to disagree with it—the pattern is alive. You see underrepresented voices gaining authority over time: the same organizer's name appears in multiple proposal genealogies, and decision-makers now anticipate their input.

The archive is genuinely searchable and used. People from new communities can trace back and see where similar problems were tackled before, reducing reinvention of flat tires.

Cross-sector proposals emerge that explicitly reference genealogies from different domains. A housing proposal mentions lessons from health initiatives. This shows that genealogy is becoming a *connective tissue*, not just documentation.

**Signs of Decay**

Genealogies become routine boxes to check, stripped of real content. Forms are filled out with minimal information; no one actually reads them in deliberation. The practice becomes visible but powerless.

Archives go unmaintained. Old genealogies are filed but never updated or referenced. New proposers don't know they exist or can't find them. The archive becomes a tomb.

Genealogy work falls entirely on underfunded community organizers. Steward roles aren't resourced, making the pattern another unpaid burden on the already exhausted.

**The Diagnostic Question**

When a proposal is deliberately rejected or significantly revised in deliberation, does the community that originated it understand *why*? Can they trace the genealogy of that rejection—the reasoning, the competing values, the decision-makers' concerns? If yes, the pattern is alive. If the rejection feels opaque, genealogy is failing.
