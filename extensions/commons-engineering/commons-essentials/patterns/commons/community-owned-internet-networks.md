### Section 1: Context

In cities globally, digital infrastructure—fiber, wireless, data centers—remains captured by distant corporations or municipal bureaucracies that extract value and concentrate control. Communities experience this as a cascading series of dependencies: they cannot repair broken networks, cannot afford access, cannot govern who collects their data, cannot choose alternatives. Yet within neighbourhoods, the infrastructure already exists—dark fiber runs beneath streets, municipal assets sit underutilized, residents possess routers and devices that could mesh into mesh networks. The tension is ancient: every living system must decide whether its life-support systems are stewarded collectively or owned by extractive powers. Digital infrastructure is simply the modern expression of this choice. Orbit 1 principles—Subsidiarity, Polycentric Organisation, Reciprocity, Conservation of Energy—all point to the same gravitational centre: infrastructure flourishes when controlled at the closest competent scale, governed through nested democratic centres, and maintained through relationships of mutual obligation rather than price signals alone.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Treating connectivity infrastructure as a privatizable commodity vs. stewarding it as living commons that adapts to community needs and persists across generations..**

Communities face a structural trap: connectivity is essential to participation in the modern economy, yet access is controlled by entities with no accountability to local wellbeing. This creates cascading failures. First, affordability breaks: low-income residents face digital exclusion, deepening inequality. Second, resilience fails: when connectivity is centrally managed, local disruptions have no local remedy. Third, governance collapses: communities cannot shape the rules, privacy policies, or expansion priorities of their own digital commons. Fourth, value extraction: surplus revenue and data flows outward; maintenance burdens stay local. Fifth, temporality breaks: infrastructure designed for quarterly earnings rarely persists beyond a single corporate transition. The problem is not technology itself but the governance structure. A fibre line is inert—it becomes a commons or a commodity depending on who holds the switch, who shares the revenue, and who has voice in its evolution. Without deliberate stewardship design, infrastructure defaults to extraction.

### Section 3: Solution

Establish digital infrastructure as permanent commons through a fractal stewardship architecture with three interlocking components:

**1. NESTED OWNERSHIP STRUCTURES**
Infrastructure assets—fiber, wireless nodes, data centers—are held in irrevocable community trusts (modeled on community land trusts) at the neighbourhood scale, with municipal trusts holding backbone infrastructure. Legal structure ensures assets remain commons in perpetuity; fiduciary duty runs to community, not shareholders. Neighbourhoods collectively own the last-mile, cooperatives manage day-to-day operations, municipalities provide wholesale access to backbone. This fractal design ensures decisions are made at the lowest competent scale while protecting against privatization at any level.

**2. PARTICIPATORY GOVERNANCE CYCLES**
Community assemblies (neighbourhood, district, city) meet on regular cycles (quarterly minimum) to deliberate: network expansion priorities, pricing adjustments, maintenance strategies, data governance rules, dispute resolution. Decisions are binding; surplus revenue is allocated through participatory budgeting. Residents who contribute governance labour (attendance, committee work) receive material rewards: service credits, reduced rates, or reinvestment in their priority projects. This creates reciprocity: participation is visible and reciprocated, not extracted without return.

**3. EQUITY & AFFORDABILITY MECHANISMS**
Pricing is progressive: based on household income quintile and usage capacity. Surplus from commercial users (businesses, high-capacity residents) explicitly subsidizes access for low-income households. A portion of revenue (typically 15-25%) funds a digital equity fund managed by residents for device subsidies, skills training, and accessibility infrastructure (public WiFi hubs, accessible computing terminals in libraries and community centers). This makes the cost-sharing formula explicit, transparent, and subject to community review.

**4. STEWARDSHIP & RESILIENCE MAINTENANCE**
Communities establish maintenance cooperatives—paid part-time positions for local residents trained in network diagnostics, hardware repair, and basic administration. This creates local employment while building community capacity. Maintenance is scheduled, budgeted, and transparent; breakdown response times are community-set. Communities map their infrastructure (fiber routes, nodes, power dependencies) collaboratively, identifying single points of failure and creating local redundancy (mesh backups, battery storage, alternate routing).

**5. ANTI-MONOPOLY COVENANTS**
Community trusts include explicit non-commodification covenants: assets cannot be sold to extractive entities, cannot be encumbered with debt to finance buyouts, cannot be leased to monopoly providers without community veto. Interoperability is required—municipal fiber must be available to multiple retail providers at cost; community WiFi must be accessible to all without data extraction. Standards and APIs are open-source or mutually governed.

The mechanism works because it aligns incentives with local wellbeing: residents who govern infrastructure also live with its consequences (outages, cost, privacy breaches). It distributes benefits locally (jobs, affordability, participation) rather than extracting them. It makes infrastructure adaptive: when community needs shift, governance structures can respond without waiting for distant corporate approval. It persists: legal trusts and cooperative structures are designed to outlast any individual participant or municipal administration, creating durability across generations.

### Section 4: Implementation

Building a community-owned internet network unfolds through deliberate phases of cultivation, each deepening local capacity and commitment.

**Phase One: Visioning & Consent** begins with neighbourhood assemblies—not surveys, but sustained conversations where residents articulate what connectivity should serve (education, small business, civic participation, cultural connection). Document these aspirations as founding principles. Simultaneously, conduct a technical audit: where does fibre already exist? What wireless infrastructure can be repurposed or built affordably? Who holds current contracts? This grounds idealism in physical reality.

**Phase Two: Legal & Financial Scaffolding** establishes a community broadband trust or cooperative corporation with explicit bylaws preventing asset seizure or privatization. Work with lawyers experienced in community land trusts; adapt their structures for digital infrastructure. Secure initial capital through municipal bonds, grant funding (many governments now fund municipal broadband), member equity contributions (scaled to income), and philanthropic investment. This mix prevents any single funder from exercising disproportionate control.

**Phase Three: Network Build** unfolds at neighbourhood scale—blocks or districts become units of stewardship. Train local technicians (prioritizing residents from the community) to install and maintain fibre runs, wireless access points, and community data centres. Partner initially with municipalities or existing carriers for backbone connectivity while building toward independence. Crucially, build redundancy: multiple pathways so no single point of failure isolates communities.

**Phase Four: Governance Embedding** institutes regular (monthly or quarterly) assembly meetings where members decide on pricing, service priorities, infrastructure investment, and conflict resolution. Create visible accountability: publish network performance metrics, infrastructure maintenance schedules, and financial statements. Rotate leadership roles so stewardship becomes distributed knowledge, not dependent on charismatic individuals.

**Phase Five: Adjacent Commons** simultaneously grow complementary structures: digital literacy programs, local content platforms, cooperative ISP services, community technology repair spaces. These transform connectivity from a utility into infrastructure that supports flourishing.

The cultivation metaphor matters: you're not "deploying" a network but tending an ecosystem that strengthens local autonomy over decades.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What Flourishes:**

When communities own their connectivity infrastructure, a cascade of capacities emerges. Affordability becomes sustainable: cooperatives can operate at cost-recovery rather than profit-maximization, reducing fees by 30-60% compared to commercial providers. Resilience deepens: local technicians understand every fibre run, every node; they can repair disruptions within hours rather than waiting for distant corporate response teams. Democratic participation amplifies: residents shape priorities directly—schools get prioritized bandwidth during school hours, community centres gain robust wifi, small businesses access affordable backhaul. Data sovereignty shifts: instead of extractive surveillance, networks can be designed to minimize data collection and community can control what happens locally. Youth find pathways: technical training creates jobs and builds skills for residents who might otherwise lack access to digital economy participation.

**Risks If Neglected:**

But these networks require tending. If governance becomes passive or captured by technical elites, the commons reverts to unaccountable management. If maintenance is deferred to save money short-term, infrastructure decays—expensive to repair later. If financing grows fragile, networks become vulnerable to buyout by commercial operators seeking to privatize already-built assets. If digital literacy lags, communities build infrastructure they cannot fully use or govern. If the network isolates from broader solidarity (with other neighbourhoods, with Indigenous data sovereignty movements, with global commons initiatives), it calcifies into a local enclave rather than a living node in larger networks of liberation.

The greatest risk is forgetting why this matters: connectivity serves community thriving, not technology for its own sake.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Amriswil, Switzerland (1998–present):** This small municipality established one of Europe's first fibre-to-home networks, treating it as public infrastructure similar to water or electricity. Residents voted to invest municipal funds in laying fibre; today it's governed by a municipal utility, offering gigabit speeds at affordable rates. The model proved so successful that Switzerland now has widespread municipal broadband ownership. Crucially, Amriswil retained public ownership even as commercial ISPs offered competing services—the municipality didn't privatize when profitable.

**Detroit Community Technology Project (USA, 2010–present):** In a city abandoned by corporate broadband investment, community organizers established a cooperative model where residents own and operate wireless mesh networks in underserved neighbourhoods. Training locals as network technicians created employment while building accountability to residents. The project explicitly centres racial justice: broadband access as reparation for disinvestment and redlining.

**South Africa's Community Wireless Networks (2000s–present):** In townships excluded from commercial broadband infrastructure, communities built mesh networks using low-cost equipment and volunteer technicians. These networks became platforms for local media, education, and small business while providing connectivity where markets failed. The model demonstrates how infrastructure can emerge from necessity and collective ingenuity even under resource constraints.

Each example shows: community ownership is technically feasible, economically sustainable, and politically transformative when governance remains accountable and infrastructure stays embedded in local life rather than abstracted as investment assets.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI and distributed intelligence reshape how networks function, community ownership becomes either more vital or more vulnerable.

**New Capacity:** Distributed AI can enable smarter network management at the edge. Communities can deploy machine learning models locally to optimize bandwidth allocation, predict infrastructure failures, and detect anomalies—without sending data to distant servers. This amplifies local autonomy: communities own not just the physical infrastructure but the intelligence embedded within it. Mesh protocols improve with AI assistance, allowing networks to self-heal and route around damage. Communities gain capacity to operate sophisticated infrastructure with smaller technical teams.

**New Danger:** The opposite logic also accelerates. AI training requires vast data collection; surveillance-as-a-service becomes tempting revenue for struggling networks. Proprietary platforms (managed by Meta, Google, or new entrants) increasingly mediate what runs "on" networks, recapturing control even if communities own fibre. Algorithmic systems can be captured by insiders; "smart" governance tools can obscure power rather than democratize it. The cognitive capabilities required to maintain and govern increasingly sophisticated infrastructure could concentrate expertise, reversing the distributed knowledge that community ownership depends on.

**Navigating Forward:** Communities must make deliberate choices: open-source network intelligence, transparent algorithmic systems, continuous training so stewardship remains distributed, explicit refusals of surveillance business models. The pattern only survives the cognitive era if communities insist that intelligence infrastructure—like physical infrastructure—remains accountable to and owned by those it serves.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life:**

A healthy community internet network shows democratic vitality: regular, well-attended assembly meetings where decisions are debated and made locally. Members can describe in accessible terms how the network works and who maintains it. Technical knowledge is distributed—multiple residents can repair equipment, not just one heroic figure. Affordability is actually affordable: the poorest households can access it without hardship. The network serves community purposes beyond commercial internet: it hosts local content, enables small business, powers schools and community centres, supports mutual aid. You see investment in the next generation: youth learning network technician skills, training programs running continuously. Finances are transparent and sustainable: operating budgets are published, decisions about pricing and reinvestment happen in open meetings.

**Signs of Decay:**

Red flags emerge when governance becomes extractive or invisible. Decisions about pricing or infrastructure investment happen in closed meetings; residents don't know why choices were made. Members stop attending assemblies; the network becomes managed by a distant board. Technical knowledge concentrates in one person or a small clique; the community cannot repair its own infrastructure. Maintenance gets deferred; service degrades. Affordability drifts upward, edging toward commercial rates. The network becomes primarily a commercial service, losing its rootedness in community purposes. External entities (governments, corporations) begin exerting pressure to privatize; leadership faces financial or political pressure to sell.

**Diagnostic Question:**

*If the current leadership stepped away tomorrow, could the community still govern and maintain this network, or would it collapse?*

This question reveals whether stewardship is truly distributed and resilient, or whether vitality depends on individual heroism. Living commons persist across generations only when knowledge, authority, and capacity are genuinely shared.
