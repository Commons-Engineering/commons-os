### Section 1: Context

Compensation is not a transaction—it is the recurring expression of what an organisation values and how it distributes the fruits of collective labour. In commons-oriented organisations (and any organisation aspiring to be just), compensation embodies the reciprocity principle: contributions are matched fairly over time. Yet most organisations treat compensation as an annual ritual disconnected from equity analysis, market reality, and transparent governance. Compensation freezes, surprise pay gaps, and executive-to-worker ratios that violate fairness norms are symptoms of compensation systems that have lost adaptability and transparency. This pattern draws from 80+ compensation practices to distil the core architecture: cyclic review, transparent bands, equity audits, and distributed governance. It is rooted in Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (fairness is earned through visible, justified process), Information Shapes Systems (compensation must respond to inflation, market shifts, and performance signals), and Transparency & Truth-Seeking (pay gaps hidden are pay gaps that fester).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Individual fairness vs. systemic equity | Static structures vs. living markets | Centralized control vs. distributed accountability.**

Compensation systems face interlocking tensions: (1) Individual fairness vs. systemic equity—merit increases can amplify existing pay gaps unless audited and calibrated; (2) Static band structures vs. living markets—salary ranges set once degrade against inflation and market movement; (3) Centralized control vs. distributed accountability—compensation decisions made in isolation breed resentment and opacity; (4) Performance incentives vs. demographic equity—merit systems can mask bias unless explicitly surfaced and corrected. Most organisations attempt to solve these with annual cycles that are disconnected, opaque, and reactive. Budget allocations are set without equity review. Merit increases are distributed without calibration across peer groups. Market data is not shared. Pay gaps are discovered by audit, not prevented by design. The result is compensation that is neither fair (because inequities compound invisibly) nor alive (because feedback is delayed and governance is centralised).

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish Compensation Stewardship as a cyclic, transparent, distributed practice with five integrated components:

**1. Transparent Salary Bands & Documentation.** Define and publish salary ranges by role, geography, and seniority. Document the rationale—market positioning, internal equity weighting, experience progression. Bands are not fixed; they evolve as markets shift. This creates baseline visibility that prevents hidden inequity.

**2. Continuous Market Benchmarking.** Conduct annual or semi-annual market research (surveys, peer data, proxy benchmarking) to validate bands against external labour markets. Document the source, weighting, and decision logic. This keeps compensation alive to real market signals, not internal assumptions.

**3. Equity Audits & Demographic Analysis.** Quarterly or semi-annual, audit pay distribution by demographics (gender, race, tenure, age, disability status). Analyse pay gaps within roles, across roles, and in aggregate. Document findings and remediation actions. This surfaces inequity before it compounds, making fairness visible and actionable.

**4. Cyclic Review with Distributed Calibration.** Establish a defined annual or biennial review cycle. Before approval, calibrate proposed increases across peer managers and functional areas to ensure consistency. Require documented justification for each raise, bonus, or promotion. Distribute review authority—managers propose, peer calibration groups assess fairness, governance committee approves. This prevents arbitrary decisions and shares accountability.

**5. Cost-of-Living & Inflation Adjustment.** Separate merit increases from cost-of-living adjustments. When inflation erodes purchasing power, apply automatic COLA adjustments to all bands and individual salaries (or, if budget constrained, to the lowest-paid workers first). This honours reciprocity—workers are not penalized for macro conditions beyond their control.

**6. Audit Trail & Transparency.** Log all compensation decisions—who proposed, who approved, what rationale, what market data informed the decision. Make this audit trail accessible to governance bodies and, where legally possible, to workers. Transparency is not a risk; it is a foundation of trust and accountability.

**Governance Structure.** Establish a Compensation Committee (cross-functional: HR, Finance, worker representatives if applicable) that meets quarterly to review market data, equity findings, and calibration. Decisions cascade from this committee through distributed managers, with clear authority levels. This embeds fairness in governance, not discretion.

This pattern resolves the core tensions: individual fairness emerges from calibration and equity audits (visibility + process); systemic equity is maintained through demographic analysis and remediation; market alignment is preserved through continuous benchmarking; and distributed governance prevents concentration of authority. Compensation becomes a commons practice—systematic, alive, shared, just, and built to last.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by convening a cross-level compensation circle—include people from different roles, geographies, and experience levels, not just leadership. Their first task is to audit what you currently pay and why. Be honest: you'll likely find unexplained gaps, legacy anomalies, and invisible hierarchies baked into the system.

From that audit, build transparent salary bands. For each role cluster (engineering, operations, care, coordination), define a realistic market range, then set your internal positioning: are you targeting median, 75th percentile, or something else? Write down why. This isn't just philosophy—it's the anchor for every future decision.

Publish these bands. Yes, really. Secrecy breeds distrust and perpetuates inequity. People should know what range applies to their role and why it differs from others. Update the bands annually or semi-annually, not in response to individual asking, but as part of a regular cycle that tracks inflation, market movement, and your organisation's economic health.

Implement a "raise protocol"—a clear, documented process for merit increases, promotions, and equity corrections. Define triggers: annual review windows, promotion milestones, market corrections, equity audits. Make the decision criteria explicit and discussable. If someone is underpaid relative to their band, there's a path to correction. If two people doing identical work earn differently, it surfaces and gets resolved.

Conduct annual equity audits. Calculate pay ratios by role, gender, race, tenure, geography. Are women in the same role earning 5% less? Are newer hires systematically underpaid? Are certain geographies compressed? Name what you find. If inequities exist, build a corrective plan with timelines—not to mortify but to repair.

Create a compensation review body—a rotating steward group that meets quarterly to monitor trends, handle edge cases, and adjust bands based on market data and organisational capacity. Include someone from finance, someone from frontline work, someone from a traditionally lower-paid role. Their job is to keep compensation alive and honest, not frozen.

Document everything. Decisions, rationales, audit results, band adjustments. This record becomes institutional memory and evidence that you're serious about fairness.

### Section 5: Consequences

When pay becomes transparent and regularly audited, several things flourish. Trust deepens—people see fairness in action, not just promised. Recruitment improves because candidates know what they'll earn and why. Retention strengthens because people don't discover after three years that they're underpaid. New hires from underrepresented groups feel less anxious about hidden discrimination. Talent from outside your usual networks becomes willing to apply because the system is knowable.

Internally, transparency often sparks useful friction. Someone in marketing asks why engineering pays more—forces you to either justify the market realities or question whether the gap reflects real differences in scarcity and value. That conversation, uncomfortable as it is, clarifies what you actually believe about worth.

But there are real risks if this pattern atrophies. Without regular audits, bands become stale within two years—inflation eats them, markets shift, you inadvertently fall behind. Without distributed stewardship, compensation decisions drift back to centralised intuition, and inequities creep in. Without clear protocols, raises become favours for the loud or well-connected. Published bands without actual adherence breed cynicism—people see the bands are fiction, compensation is politics, and you've lost credibility.

There's also tension in what you choose to measure. If you optimise only for internal equity, you may price yourself out of competitive hiring. If you chase market rates without equity weighting, you reinforce existing salary hierarchies baked into broader labour markets. The pattern requires holding both—using transparent audits to sometimes push against the market, sometimes align with it, always with reason you can articulate.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Cooperative Condé Nast (Spain).** A worker-owned media cooperative that publishes *Público* newspaper restructured its pay after members demanded transparency. They published band documentation, conducted annual equity audits disaggregated by gender, and implemented a rotation where workers from different departments reviewed compensation quarterly. Pay gaps between genders narrowed from 18% to 6% over four years. The system isn't perfect, but it's visible and contestable.

**Ostrom Commons Trust (Kenya/East Africa).** Manages pastoral and fishery commons across multiple countries with staff spanning rural and urban roles, multiple currencies, and significant experience variation. They built salary bands anchored to local cost-of-living indices, published ranges, and created a compensation steward circle rotating across countries. This prevented the usual pattern where field staff in lower-cost countries earn half of office staff in capital cities, even when their work is equally skilled.

**GitLab (distributed, though now less commons-oriented).** Early in its growth, GitLab published salary formula and bands transparently, including how geography adjusted pay. While the company later privatised and changed approach, the period when compensation was fully transparent and auditable became a reference point—people knew exactly what they'd earn before negotiating, and equity audits were public. This reduced unconscious bias in initial offers.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic management are reshaping compensation in ways this pattern must adapt to absorb. 

First, the temptation: use AI to "optimise" pay—machine learning models trained on historical data, market APIs, and performance metrics can generate individualized compensation recommendations faster than any human committee. The risk is immediate: such systems encode historical inequities (women paid less for identical work? the model learns to recommend that). They also introduce opacity at scale—no individual can audit why an algorithm suggested what it did.

This pattern counters by using AI as *input to deliberation*, not substitute for it. Feed AI-generated market data and equity analyses into human steward circles. Let algorithms flag anomalies ("this person's pay is 2 standard deviations from peers in same role") but require human reasoning about whether the anomaly reflects justified difference or hidden bias. 

Second, distributed intelligence creates new possibilities. Blockchain or decentralised systems could make pay history and equity audits cryptographically verifiable and harder to hide—beneficial for detecting systemic bias. But only if the underlying data is accurate and the interpretation remains human-centred.

Third, AI models that predict job value (scarcity, impact, replaceability) can inform band-setting, but they're only as fair as their training data. Use them, but surface their assumptions. Let the compensation steward circle question what the model privileges.

The deeper shift: as work becomes more fluid, algorithmic, and distributed, this pattern's insistence on *visible documentation and regular collective review* becomes more necessary, not less. Fairness can't be an afterthought or delegated to a black box.

### Section 8: Vitality

Signs the pattern is alive: Compensation bands are published and updated within six months of market shifts. The compensation steward circle meets regularly and includes people outside senior leadership. Equity audits happen annually and their findings are shared (even uncomfortable ones). When someone is promoted or hired at a band edge, the decision is documented with reasoning. New employees can quickly understand why their offer is what it is. Pay conversations don't feel secretive or shameful.

Signs it's decaying: Bands exist on a document but feel decorative—actual hiring and raises ignore them. The steward circle stops meeting or becomes ceremonial. Equity audits happen but findings stay private or are dismissed ("market realities"). The "raise protocol" exists but is opaque; people don't know when or how they qualify. Compensation decisions revert to individual manager whim. Underpaid employees discover inequities only by accident or by leaving and returning at higher pay.

The diagnostic question: **If you removed all secrecy about compensation tomorrow—published every salary, every raise decision, every audit result—would the system withstand scrutiny, or would it reveal injustices you've been ignoring?**

If you're uncomfortable with transparency, the pattern is already compromised. Use that discomfort as a signal to audit and repair before you're forced to.
