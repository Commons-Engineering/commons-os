> Show when promises are made, when work gets done, and when money actually changes hands—so you see your real cash rhythm

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Business domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every commons moves resources and fulfills commitments across time. A customer pays upfront for a year of service. A cooperative receives a grant for equipment that will be used over five years. A member prepays for harvest share. An organization commits to future debt service. These time-shifted flows—where cash, goods, or labor are received at one moment but value is delivered across many—are the living arteries of any sustained system.

Without visibility into these flows, a commons can appear solvent while actually overcommitted. It can mistake cash received for revenue earned, or defer expenses so far that they become invisible. The result is a system that loses track of its own heartbeat. This pattern descends from Conservation of Energy (obligations are real debts, not wishes), Information Shapes Systems (time-shifted flows create critical feedback loops about capacity), and Time-Sliced Planning (the only way to manage commitments is to see them clearly in their sequential unfolding).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Cash flows now vs. value flows later — between the moment money arrives and the moment commitment is fulfilled.**

Time-shifted obligations create three living tensions:

**Visibility vs. Illusion.** A commons can collect cash and feel flush while actually mortgaging its future. A SaaS company with $1M in prepaid annual contracts looks strong until the moment it must deliver service and lacks capacity. A cooperative with a deferred harvest loan appears to have resources until harvest fails.

**Reciprocity vs. Asymmetry.** When prepayment arrives, the receiver has cash but the giver has only a promise. Without transparent tracking, that promise becomes nebulous—delayed, reinterpreted, or forgotten. Trust erodes.

**Capacity vs. Commitment.** Every obligation consumes future capacity. A commons can accept prepayments, grants, or commitments that exceed what it can actually deliver. The system then becomes trapped: it cannot return the resources without collapse, and it cannot deliver without overextending. This is the carrying capacity problem in time: not spatial limit, but temporal overcommitment.

The core conflict: How do we hold time-shifted obligations in a way that keeps them alive, visible, and retrievable—so the commons can see whether it is promising more than it can deliver, whether value flows match cash flows, and whether the system can actually sustain what it has committed to?

---

### Section 3: Solution

**The Commitment Ledger: Making Time-Shifted Flows Visible**

Create a shared, continuously updated register of every obligation the commons has made—organized by commitment date, due date, and the parties to whom it is owed. This is not an accounting trick; it is a commons' nervous system.

The register tracks three dimensions simultaneously:

**1. Inbound Promises (What the commons has received in advance)**
Every prepayment, advance deposit, or grant is logged with: (a) when cash arrived, (b) when and how the corresponding value will be delivered, (c) the capacity required to fulfill it, (d) the current status (pending, in-progress, completed, at-risk). A $10K prepaid service contract becomes visible as a $10K liability that consumes 200 hours of labor in Q3. If Q3 capacity is only 150 hours, the mismatch surfaces now—not in month 9 when the customer demands service.

**2. Outbound Commitments (What the commons owes to others)**
Every lease payment, debt obligation, equipment purchase, or staffing commitment is tracked with due dates and required cash outflows. This reveals the commons' minimum cash burn and the timing of resource demands. It answers: "What must we deliver, regardless of income, just to stay alive?"

**3. Performance & Reconciliation (How reality diverges from promise)**
As value is delivered—service rendered, goods shipped, work completed—the register is updated. Completed obligations are marked done. Overdue ones flag risks. This creates feedback: Are we delivering on time? Are prepayments arriving faster than we can fulfill them? Are outbound commitments eating up our capacity?

**The Governance Function**

The Commitment Ledger becomes the basis for shared decision-making. Before the commons accepts a new prepayment, it asks: "Does this fit in our available future capacity?" Before it commits to a new project, it checks: "Will this create a mismatch between when we're paid and when we can deliver?" This shifts governance from abstract budgeting to concrete stewardship of time itself.

The ledger is not locked behind finance—it is visible to all contributors. Everyone who depends on the commons knows what's been promised and what's due. This builds reciprocity: promises are kept transparent. It also creates accountability: if the commons consistently promises more than it delivers, the mismatch becomes visible to everyone, and course correction becomes necessary before crisis.

**The Adaptive Loop**

Monthly or quarterly, the commons reviews the ledger in conversation. What's on track? What's at risk? What new capacity do we need? This is not a report filed away; it is a conversation that shapes the next cycle of commitments. Over time, the commons learns its own rhythm: how much prepaid revenue it can safely accept, how long it realistically takes to deliver, when it has capacity for new work. The pattern evolves from accounting rule to lived governance.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping your actual cash rhythm—not your hope for it, but what really moves.

**First act: Inventory your commitments.** Sit with your finance keeper and list every obligation across time. When did money arrive? When must it be spent? When must the corresponding work be complete? When do you owe repayment or delivery? Create a simple three-column table: *Date money arrived / Date commitment due / What must happen in between.* Include prepaid subscriptions, grant milestones, loan schedules, harvest shares, membership dues held for future events—everything.

**Second act: Make the Commitment Ledger visible.** Post this table where decisions happen. A Google Sheet works. A whiteboard in the weekly meeting works. What matters is that every person who commits the commons' time or resources can see what's already promised. This transforms abstract budget from something the finance person knows into something everyone feels.

**Third act: Create a decision rule.** Before accepting new money or committing new work, check the ledger. Ask: *If we take this on now, can we actually deliver on what we've already promised?* This sounds obvious until you notice how often it doesn't happen. A cooperative accepts a new bulk order because the immediate cash looks good, then discovers it's already committed its labor to fulfilling last month's harvest shares.

**Fourth act: Track the gap.** The real diagnostic: the space between when money arrives and when work completes. If you're six months out on every commitment, you're running on borrowed time. If commitments cluster in one season, you'll have cash desert elsewhere. Name this pattern. Make it discussable.

**Fifth act: Close the loop.** When a commitment is fulfilled—goods delivered, service completed, debt repaid—mark it closed. This teaches the commons its own rhythm. You'll see: we were anxious about the spring expansion, but we always deliver by June. We struggle with Q3 cash despite strong Q2 sales. Our three-year grants create feast-famine cycles.

The Commitment Ledger isn't accounting—it's perception. It makes visible what was already true but invisible.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes when you track honestly:**

The commons develops a true sense of its own capacity. Not wishful thinking—actual knowledge of how much commitment it can carry and still deliver well. Members stop overcommitting from enthusiasm or scarcity mindset. You say no to good opportunities because you see clearly that you're already at capacity. This feels like loss until you notice it's actually integrity.

Trust deepens because everyone sees the same information. The finance keeper isn't hiding a crisis. The leadership isn't pretending resources exist. Decisions made together at the ledger table hold differently than decisions made from incomplete knowledge.

You catch the slow-motion crises before they hit. The grant that funds Year 1 but requires Year 2 self-funding becomes visible in Month 3, not Month 13. The subscription revenue that looks strong until 40% churn hits in summer is flagged as a risk you can plan for.

**What risks emerge if left untended:**

The Commitment Ledger becomes a theater of false precision—accurate numbers that mislead because they miss something crucial. You track when money arrives and work is due, but not whether the work is actually *happening* or just *scheduled*. The ledger says "Q2 expansion complete by June 30" but no one's actually monitoring whether June 15 work is on track.

The commons becomes rigid. Every commitment locked in, no flexibility, no room for crisis or opportunity. The ledger becomes a cage instead of a map.

Worst: the ledger is kept but not consulted. It sits in the spreadsheet while decisions happen elsewhere, unchanged. It becomes a compliance artifact rather than a living tool.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Cooperatives (Basque Region, Spain):** The federation maintains a multi-year commitment register for each cooperative member—both inbound (shared earnings due, member equity obligations) and outbound (jobs promised, service commitments). When Mondragon considered expansion into new sectors, they explicitly used the ledger to assess whether member cooperatives had capacity to support it. The visibility prevented overextension that had historically weakened weaker cooperatives.

**Sumac Cooperative (Toronto, Canada):** This worker-owned bookstore uses a physical whiteboard ledger visible in their kitchen, updated weekly. It tracks prepaid book orders, rent obligations, and labor commitments across the 12-person collective. Members report the practice transformed how they negotiate new wholesale agreements—they can now say with confidence, "We have six weeks of labor already committed; we can absorb X new titles but not Y."

**Gram Vikas (rural Odisha, India):** This NGO supporting village commons manages a commitment ledger across multiple village water and sanitation projects. The ledger tracks when funds arrive from government, when community labor is promised, and when infrastructure must be complete. It surfaces the mismatch between government disbursement cycles and monsoon seasons—money arrives in July, but earth work must happen in March. The visible gap led them to advocate for changed disbursement schedules, not just complain about impossible timelines.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world of distributed AI and algorithmic decision-making, the Commitment Ledger becomes both more powerful and more fragile.

**More powerful:** AI can now flag patterns humans miss. An algorithm watching your ledger over two years notices you always overcommit in Q2 by 30%, or that you consistently underestimate implementation time for grant-funded work. Predictive models can show you: if you accept this contract, what's the probability you'll miss your existing commitments? This moves the ledger from historical record to foresight tool.

But this creates a new risk: you outsource judgment to the algorithm. The machine says "capacity remaining: 120 hours" and you take on 115-hour projects because the number said you could, missing the human reality that 40 of those hours should be unallocated buffer.

**More fragile:** When commitments are tracked in distributed ledgers (blockchain for supply chain promises, automated APIs connecting commons to multiple funding sources), the single-point truth of your commitment register breaks apart. You have visibility into commitments across multiple systems, but they're no longer *your* ledger—they're scattered across platforms you don't fully control.

The antidote: keep a *human-readable, human-governed* Commitment Ledger even as you connect to automated systems. The ledger is not just a database; it's a place where humans gather to see what we've promised and to know ourselves.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

The ledger is used in real meetings, not consulted afterward. People cite it during discussions. "Wait, we're already committed to the March event—can we take on the May client?" The ledger informs decisions as they're made, not ex post facto.

Members develop fluency with the commons' rhythm. They know: we're tight in Q2, generous in Q4. They feel it in their bones, not just their heads. New members ask the ledger keeper to teach them how to read it, like learning the weather patterns of a place you've just moved.

There's honest talk about missed commitments. When something doesn't complete on time, you discuss it at the ledger—why did we miss? Was it a prediction error? A capacity error? A priority shift that wasn't explicit? You adjust both the ledger and your practices.

**Signs of decay:**

The ledger is outdated. Last updated two months ago. New commitments are made verbally, not entered. The finance keeper updates it alone; no one else reads it. It becomes a legal artifact rather than a living tool.

Commitments are never marked complete. The ledger grows longer but never shorter. It loses credibility because it doesn't reflect reality—work was done, but the ledger still shows it as pending.

The commons ignores the ledger and keeps overcommitting anyway, hitting the same crises again and again. The ledger becomes a silent witness to a problem that leadership won't address.

**The diagnostic question:**

*If you lost your accounting software tomorrow, could you reconstruct your real cash rhythm from your Commitment Ledger?* If yes, you're tracking what matters. If no—if your ledger would be discovered to be incomplete or divorced from reality—you're tracking theater.
