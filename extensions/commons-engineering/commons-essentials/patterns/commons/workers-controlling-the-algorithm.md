### Section 1: Context

Platform workers face a structural governance void: they supply labour but control neither the algorithmic systems that determine task allocation, speed, earnings, nor the legal status that determines whether they qualify for protection. Platforms extract value through opacity — workers cannot see why they were deactivated, how their earnings were calculated, or what algorithmic factors influence their access to work. This creates what Srnicek calls "data asymmetry" but more fundamentally, it is a commons problem: the algorithms, earnings data, and working conditions that determine worker survival are treated as private platform property when they are actually shared resources shaped by collective behaviour. The 28 Orbit 1 principles — especially The Challenge of Cooperation, Visibility as Commons Act, and Subsidiarity — establish that when multiple agents depend on a shared system, that system must be governed collectively or it degrades into extraction. This cluster of 93 patterns represents the emergence of worker-controlled alternative infrastructures: data trusts, cooperatives, insurance pools, arbitration bodies, and transparency audits that treat platform systems as contestable, auditable, and subject to worker governance.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Algorithmic opacity & unilateral control vs. worker visibility, contestation & collective governance.**

Platform workers face three interlocking control mechanisms that fragment collective power: (1) Algorithmic opacity — task assignment, earnings calculation, deactivation decisions operate as black boxes, preventing workers from understanding or contesting the rules governing their work; (2) Earnings volatility engineered by algorithms — speed requirements, surge pricing, and task scarcity are algorithmically manipulated to maximize platform extraction, making individual worker income unstable and unpredictable; (3) Structural precarity through legal classification — independent contractor status strips workers of employment protections while algorithms function as invisible management, creating the worst of both worlds. Each mechanism isolates: workers cannot see what happened to others (no transparency), cannot collectively predict earnings (volatility prevents coordination), and have no legal standing to contest conditions (classification void). The result is that workers experience algorithmic governance without algorithmic transparency, collective control without collective power. This violates core commons principles: Reciprocity (no fair exchange when one party controls all information), Incentives (individual survival incentives work against collective interests), and Subsidiarity (decisions affecting workers' livelihoods are made at the highest level — the platform algorithm — rather than by those affected).

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish distributed worker-controlled infrastructure that inverts platform opacity into collective visibility and transforms individual precarity into shared stewardship of working conditions. This operates across four nested governance layers:

**Layer 1: Algorithmic Transparency & Contestation** — Workers collectively audit and document algorithmic systems through structured observation, data analysis, and testing. Transparency audits expose task allocation bias, wage-setting parameters, and deactivation logic. Arbitration bodies (distinct from platform appeals) hear deactivation cases with mandatory algorithmic disclosure and power to impose remedies. This makes algorithms visible and contestable rather than immutable.

**Layer 2: Earnings Governance as Shared Resource** — Replace individual earnings volatility with collective pooling mechanisms: income floors guaranteed through escrow accounts, volatility redistribution systems where high-earning periods fund low-earning periods, portable benefits accumulation across platforms, and wage floor bargaining where workers collectively set minimum hourly thresholds enforceable through code. Treat earnings not as individual market outcomes but as shared resource subject to fairness standards.

**Layer 3: Worker-Controlled Data Commons** — Pool transactional data (task pricing, earnings, algorithmic ratings, deactivation patterns) into independent data trusts governed by worker representatives. These become collective bargaining assets: workers negotiate platform terms as a unified entity with leverage over their own information. Cross-platform collectives document comparative earnings and algorithmic discrimination patterns, enabling informed choice and coordinated action.

**Layer 4: Cooperative Exit & Alternative Platforms** — Establish worker-owned platform cooperatives that operate algorithms designed for worker wellbeing rather than extraction, with workers as co-owners governing algorithmic design. Create legal classification shields protecting cooperative workers from reclassification. Build portable certification standards and skill advancement commons so workers can transition between platforms or reduce platform dependency without losing accumulated benefits.

These layers are fractal: they operate from local worker networks (informal slowdown campaigns, dark pattern resistance guides) through sectoral organizations (benefit pooling, legal defense funds) to multi-platform infrastructure (data cooperatives, portable pensions, cooperative alternatives). Governance is subsidiarity-based: decisions about speed, scheduling, quality standards, and earnings floors are made by workers closest to the work, not imposed by central algorithms. The pattern creates feedback loops: worker audits reveal algorithmic bias, triggering collective campaigns, generating data for class action litigation, which forces platform transparency, which enables better audits. The system is alive because it continuously adapts to new algorithmic mechanisms and evolving platform control tactics. It is built to last because the infrastructure (data trusts, arbitration bodies, cooperatives, benefit funds) persists beyond any single campaign or worker cohort, and because it generates its own governance capacity as workers develop expertise in auditing, bargaining, and cooperative operations.

### Section 4: Implementation

Workers controlling the algorithm unfolds through deliberate acts of collective cultivation. Start where visibility is weakest: establish a worker data cooperative that systematically documents what the algorithm actually does. This means structured observation — workers recording task assignments, earnings calculations, deactivation triggers — then pooling that data to reverse-engineer algorithmic patterns. This is not passive data collection; it's active archaeology of the rules you live under.

Next, build contestation infrastructure. Create a worker tribunal or dispute resolution body that examines algorithmic decisions in real time. When someone is deactivated, when earnings drop inexplicably, when task allocation seems retaliatory — these cases become collective learning. The tribunal doesn't replace the platform; it creates a parallel knowledge system where workers can challenge opacity with evidence drawn from their own experience.

Simultaneously, develop alternative algorithmic capacity within the worker collective. This might mean training worker-technologists to audit code, hiring external auditors accountable to workers rather than platforms, or designing open-source task allocation systems that workers could migrate toward. The goal is not to become technologists yourselves, but to fragment the platform's monopoly on algorithmic interpretation.

Governance requires rotating leadership and decision-making power through working committees: an algorithms committee that interprets data, a standards committee that proposes new working condition rules, a dispute committee that arbitrates. Rotate these roles quarterly or annually so knowledge doesn't concentrate. Keep meetings accessible — written summaries, video explanations, translation — so participation isn't gatekept by technical fluency.

Crucially, link data documentation to collective action. Every algorithmic insight should connect to a concrete demand: "We've documented that task allocation clusters deactivations on Fridays — we demand algorithmic adjustment or compensation." Make transparency a tool for leverage, not just information.

### Section 5: Consequences

When workers gain visibility into algorithmic systems, several things flourish: dignity returns. Workers stop internalizing platform narratives that deactivation is merit-based or inevitable. Collective consciousness emerges — "the algorithm is manipulating us" becomes "we understand how it manipulates us and we're changing it together." Deactivations become grievances with evidence rather than mysteries accepted as fate.

Earnings stabilization becomes negotiable. When workers collectively document that algorithms artificially engineer scarcity or speed requirements, wage suppression becomes visible as a choice, not a law of nature. Collective bargaining gains material ground.

But real risks emerge if this pattern is not tended carefully. Platforms may respond with opacity escalation — encrypting code further, fragmenting worker data collection through terms-of-service enforcement, accelerating replacement of human workers with automation to eliminate the organizing base. Worker auditing capacity can become a technical elite separate from the broader worker base, hoarding knowledge rather than distributing it. The tribunal can become performative — workers document injustice meticulously while the platform ignores it.

There is also the risk of cooptation: platforms may adopt "algorithmic transparency" as PR while maintaining actual control, showing workers a sanitized version of their algorithm that masks real decision-making. Workers must stay suspicious of transparency theater.

If this pattern decays, workers revert to isolated data experiences, algorithmic decision-making returns to pure opacity, and collective power fragments back into individual precarity.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Deliveroo Workers Collective (UK, Italy, Spain)**: Couriers began systematically documenting algorithmic task assignment patterns, discovering the algorithm penalized workers who declined low-pay deliveries and rewarded speed compliance. They published findings openly and demanded algorithmic audits as part of collective negotiation. The pattern didn't achieve full worker control, but shifted opacity into partial transparency and made algorithmic harm undeniable to regulators.

**Turkopticon (Global)**: An Amazon Mechanical Turk worker-built platform that inverts platform surveillance. Workers rate employers, document algorithmic rejection patterns, and maintain collective memory of how algorithms screen tasks and workers. Turkopticon became a distributed tribunal where algorithmic decisions could be contested with peer evidence. No formal governance body, but collectively authored rules about what constitutes fair algorithmic treatment.

**Jottings Cooperative (China, delivery sector)**: A worker-owned logistics coordination platform where delivery workers collectively control task allocation algorithms. Workers vote on algorithmic rule changes — if surge pricing rules harm earnings equity, workers propose new formulas and vote. The algorithm is transparent code; workers can see the decision trees. Governance rotates monthly across five worker committees. Still small, but demonstrates that algorithmic ownership is technically achievable.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI systems become more opaque and decisions more distributed across multiple algorithmic layers, worker-controlled transparency becomes more essential and more complex. Workers cannot simply read code anymore; they must develop collective hermeneutic capacity — the ability to interpret black-box AI systems through behavioral observation rather than technical inspection.

This pattern evolves toward worker-controlled AI governance: worker collectives training their own machine learning models on documented algorithmic behavior, creating counter-models that predict platform decisions and test algorithmic bias before it harms workers. Think of it as algorithmic immune systems — worker-built AI that detects when platform AI is behaving unfairly.

Distributed intelligence changes the game in another way: workers can now integrate external AI tools into their contestation infrastructure without platform permission. A worker collective might deploy open-source large language models to analyze thousands of algorithmic decisions, finding patterns human auditors would miss. The power asymmetry narrows slightly — workers gain algorithmic sight-lines.

But the risk deepens too. Platforms may use generative AI to personalize opacity — showing each worker a different algorithmic explanation tailored to pacify them individually, fragmenting collective learning. Workers must remain vigilant about centralizing their analytical capacity, building collective interpretation systems rather than allowing platforms to talk to workers one-on-one through AI interfaces.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: Workers can articulate *why* they were deactivated or why earnings shifted — not because the platform told them, but because they collectively figured it out. Algorithmic audits happen regularly and are circulated widely, not just among technologists. When the algorithm changes, workers know because they noticed it together, not because the platform announced it. Younger workers learn algorithmic literacy from experienced ones. Disputes with the platform cite worker-documented evidence. The collective has trained at least one worker deeply enough in code or data analysis that they can challenge external auditors' claims. Turnover decreases because workers feel less randomly harmed.

**Signs of decay**: Algorithmic documentation becomes sporadic, falling to a small group who burns out. Workers stop checking tribunal decisions because nothing changes. Platform opacity deepens and workers accept it as inevitable again. Technical knowledge concentrates in one person or small cell; if they leave, the capacity disappears. Workers start asking "why bother?" because collective action hasn't visibly shifted platform behavior. Deactivations feel mysterious again. Workers stop gathering data.

**The diagnostic question**: *Can a randomly selected worker explain what the algorithm actually does to their earnings and task allocation, using evidence the collective gathered, without waiting for permission from the platform or a technical expert?* If yes, the pattern is alive. If no, it's decaying.
