### Section 1: Context

Disabled people navigate systems designed without them—buildings, workplaces, governance processes, and economic structures that treat accessibility as an afterthought, personal expense, or charity. This forces disabled people into isolation, economic precarity, and dependence on non-disabled gatekeepers for basic participation. Yet disabled communities have always practiced collective care, peer mentorship, and mutual aid. The shift from individual accommodation to collective stewardship is grounded in disability justice: the recognition that disabled people's knowledge is expertise, that accessibility benefits everyone, and that disabled people must lead in designing the systems they inhabit. This pattern draws from Cooperation Creates Surplus—disabled workers generate surplus when hired by disabled employers who understand their needs—and from Subsidiarity, where disabled residents lead accessibility decisions because they possess irreplaceable local knowledge. It reflects Reciprocity in care economies where disabled people support other disabled people, and Polycentric Organisation where nested cooperatives (housing, employment, arts, peer navigation) operate at multiple scales while sharing accessibility standards.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Individual accommodation burden vs. collective accessibility stewardship.**

Accessibility framed as individual accommodation creates fragmentation: each disabled person negotiates separately with employers, landlords, and institutions, bearing hidden costs and emotional labour. Systems deny accommodation episodically—when disabled people lack documentation, change jobs, or face gatekeeping. Accessibility improvements remain siloed: one workplace upgrades, another doesn't. Disabled people are excluded from designing solutions, so systems reflect non-disabled assumptions. Economic structures treat disability as cost-drain rather than recognizing disabled workers' distinctive contributions. Care work is underpaid and undervalued even though disabled people often deliver superior peer care. Non-disabled people make accessibility decisions affecting disabled lives. This scatters collective power and prevents disabled economic autonomy. The commons faces a similar problem: without accessibility embedded in governance design and disabled leadership in authority roles, commons reproduce the ableism of broader systems. Accessibility remains reactive and incomplete.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Accessibility Stewardship Commons embeds disability justice into three interlocking systems: (1) **Governance Mandate**: Every commons, cooperative, and institution establishes a Disability Justice Stewardship Committee with majority representation from disabled members, holding decision-making authority over accessibility audits, design changes, budget allocation for access supports, and workplace accommodation. This is not consultation—disabled members lead. (2) **Collective Resource Pools**: Cooperatives and commons establish Accessibility & Care Commons Funds—budget lines ensuring accommodation costs (equipment, personal assistance, home modification, accessibility upgrades) are mutualized rather than individualized. Disabled-led collectives analyze and redistribute disability-related economic costs (medical gatekeeping, equipment expenses, lost wages from inaccessible systems) across the community, rejecting charity framing. (3) **Disabled-Led Employment & Peer Infrastructure**: Disabled people are hired as Peer Navigators, Accessibility Auditors, Care Workers, and Mentors—paid hourly roles designed around episodic disability, with flexible scheduling and sensory-aware workplaces. Revenue from accessibility auditing, consulting, and peer support returns to disabled worker-members. Neurodivergent and chronically ill people design their own work roles, recognizing their care approaches and communication styles as distinctive expertise, not deficits. (4) **Accessibility as Shared Infrastructure**: Physical, sensory, cognitive, and communication accessibility is treated like water or electricity—commons-managed infrastructure maintained through collective standards, regular auditing, and cross-subsidy. Curb cuts, accessible housing, clear signage, accessible governance processes, and accommodation protocols are embedded into cooperative operations and design mandates, not added as exceptions. (5) **Fractal Knowledge Sharing**: Accessibility audits, accommodation solutions, and peer mentorship patterns are documented and shared across nested cooperatives and neighbourhoods. Disabled expertise circulates as commons knowledge rather than remaining isolated in individual workplaces. (6) **Universal Design as Standard**: All design, renovation, and governance decisions undergo mandatory accessibility impact assessment led by disabled residents. Accessibility is treated as a core commons value equivalent to affordability or democratic participation, not a compliance burden.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming it: convene disabled people already doing this work in your city—peer counsellors, community organisers, access consultants, disabled workers. They're here. Listen first. They know the specific terrain: which transit routes fail, which employers quietly fire disabled workers, where housing is actually affordable, which neighbourhoods have informal support networks.

Establish a Disability Justice Stewardship Committee with decision-making authority—not advisory. Majority disabled members, paid. Give them real budget to conduct accessibility audits of city infrastructure, workplaces, and housing stock. This is employment. This is also knowledge generation that no non-disabled consultant can produce.

Create physical accessibility maps collaboratively. Disabled people navigate daily; they know the inaccessible cafés, the transit stops where no one stops, the sidewalk patterns that disable people with pain conditions. Document this. Make it public and updatable. Hire disabled people to maintain and expand these maps.

Establish accessibility standards as procurement requirements: any contractor bidding for city work, housing development, or service provision must meet accessibility thresholds *and* employ disabled people in implementation and oversight. This makes accessibility work economically visible and creates jobs.

Build housing cooperatives and land trusts explicitly for disabled people. Combine universal design principles with support services: shared attendant care networks, peer support meals, accessible common spaces where disabled residents co-plan maintenance and governance. Price housing for people on disability income. Hire disabled residents as cooperatively employed staff.

Create sectoral stewardship networks: disabled workers in healthcare, education, transit, food systems organising together across employers. They share accommodation solutions, spot exploitative patterns, bargain collectively for better access terms. This distributes knowledge and power.

Fund disabled artists, educators, and knowledge-keepers to teach accessibility design to planners, architects, and engineers. Make it mandatory for licensure. Disabled people should teach what accessibility requires.

### Section 5: Consequences

When disabled people lead accessibility design, cities become more livable for everyone: parents with strollers navigate better, elders move with less strain, neurodivergent people find sensory-friendly spaces, people with chronic illness find rest places. Universal design doesn't diminish anything; it expands ease.

Economic power shifts. Disabled people move from isolated supplicants to collective agents. Employment in accessibility auditing, mapping, design oversight, and support services creates jobs controlled by disabled communities. Cooperative housing means disabled people build equity collectively rather than enriching landlords. This is slow—cooperatives take years to stabilize—but it consolidates power.

Knowledge deepens. Cities learn what disabled people already know: accessibility isn't a technical problem to solve for outsiders. It's a governance question: who decides what counts as livable? Who bears costs? Disabled people's epistemology—embodied, collective, survival-tested—becomes authoritative.

Risks emerge if this work isn't sustained. Disabled committee members can burn out under pressure to "prove" accessibility works. Tokenism spreads: cities appoint disabled people to committees, ignore their recommendations, then claim consultation happened. Gentrification follows accessibility investment—improved neighbourhoods price out the disabled people who made them livable. Housing cooperatives face funding crises; support networks collapse if paid work dries up.

Backlash happens. Non-disabled business owners resist hiring disabled workers. Planners resent accessibility mandates. Cities defund stewardship work. Disabled people internalize messages that they're too expensive, too slow, too complicated.

Sustaining this requires recognizing accessibility work as perpetual commons stewardship, not one-time retrofit. It requires stable funding, rotating leadership to prevent burnout, and disabled people maintaining control.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Vancouver's Disability Justice Network** (ongoing): Disabled organisers, artists, and service workers formed collectives that audit city planning, advocate for transit accessibility, and operate peer support housing. They've influenced municipal procurement policies and trained city staff on disability justice frameworks. Power remains contested—funding is precarious, influence fluctuates—but the network sustains because disabled people lead and employ each other.

**Uruguay's Cooperative Movement & Disability**: Over decades, disabled people in Montevideo have built housing cooperatives and worker cooperatives in care, food production, and craft work. Disability-led cooperatives operate with universal design principles baked in from inception. Members have stable housing, employment, and collective voice in governance. Funding comes through social economy frameworks and state support for cooperative development.

**Nairobi's Disabled Persons Organization & Infrastructure Advocacy**: Disabled Kenyans have mapped informal settlement accessibility, documented how inaccessible city design excludes disabled people from work and services, and advocated for inclusive informal-economy policies. They've established peer support networks and pushed local government to hire disabled people in planning roles. Work is under-resourced and political pressure is intense, but disabled people's leadership in defining what accessibility means has shifted conversations.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI accelerates certain aspects of this pattern while threatening others.

Accessibility mapping becomes faster: computer vision can identify barriers (missing curb cuts, inaccessible signage, broken elevators), processing spatial data across cities. But disabled people must define what "accessible" means and set priorities. An algorithm doesn't know that a particular disabled person can navigate steps but not crowds, or vice versa. Disabled people remain the epistemologists; AI becomes a tool they direct.

Distributed decision-making becomes viable at scale. Disabled Justice Stewardship Committees across cities can share audits, solutions, and governance experiments through networks. AI can help coordinate across groups—flagging common patterns, connecting isolated workers—but decision-making stays local and disabled-led.

Risk: cities deploy AI to "solve" accessibility without disabled people, claiming efficiency. Automated accessibility solutions (self-driving transit, voice interfaces) can displace disabled workers and workers in support services if not embedded in cooperatives with job guarantees. Disabled communities must control AI deployment in accessibility work.

Opportunity: disabled people use AI as a commons tool. Disabled tech workers build systems that disabled communities own and govern collectively. Peer support networks use encrypted platforms for care coordination. Disabled artists and educators use generative tools to create accessible teaching materials. The cognitive shift is from "AI solves accessibility" to "disabled people use distributed intelligence to strengthen collective stewardship."

### Section 8: Vitality

A **living** Disabled People Building Accessible Cities pattern shows:

- Disabled people occupy paid, decision-making roles in governance bodies. Committees meet; decisions are implemented.
- Disabled workers are employed in accessibility auditing, design, and maintenance—not as volunteers or interns, but as salaried stewards.
- Disabled people's spatial knowledge circulates publicly: maps are updated, stories are told, newcomers learn the terrain from disabled guides.
- Housing is affordable to people on disability income. Residents participate in cooperative governance. Support networks function across households.
- Non-disabled people encounter disabled people as colleagues, designers, authorities—not as exceptions to accommodate.
- Disabled artists and educators are commissioned and paid to teach accessibility.

**Signs of decay:**

- Disabled committee members rotate constantly (burnout). Decisions aren't implemented.
- Accessibility work is unpaid or precarious—volunteer-dependent. Disabled people can't sustain it.
- Maps and knowledge exist but aren't shared. Disabled people navigate separately, repeatedly.
- Accessible housing becomes gentrified. Original disabled residents are priced out.
- Cities revert to individual accommodation frameworks. Disabled people return to negotiating alone.
- Disabled people are employed but in tokenized roles—present but powerless.

**Diagnostic question:**

*When a disabled person who didn't previously live here arrives in your city, can they learn the accessible geography from disabled people already here, find affordable housing designed for their access needs, and find paid work in collective accessibility stewardship? If not, the pattern is fragile.*
