> How communities can govern together around competing histories, reparations, and belonging—holding different truths at o

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)

---

### Section 1: Context

Cities built on colonial extraction, slavery, displacement, and violence carry layered, irreconcilable histories in their streets, monuments, and institutions. Official heritage discourse has traditionally consecrated a single authorized narrative—the conqueror's, the state's, the museum's—while rendering invisible the experiences of the colonized, enslaved, dispossessed, and resistant. This legacy persists in urban form: monuments glorifying extraction, place names erasing indigenous presence, archives controlled by descendant institutions, and memorial commons governed by authorities with no accountability to affected communities. Yet the material heritage itself—forts, trade routes, colonial architecture, institutional buildings—remains as evidence that demands reckoning. The tension is not between preservation and destruction, but between whose stewardship, whose narratives, whose claims to belonging get institutionalized and whose remain temporary, vulnerable, erasable.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Heritage preservation as erasure vs. heritage as active decolonial commons; singular authorized history vs. legitimate coexistence of conflicting memories; institutional control vs. community stewardship; temporary contestation vs. permanent counter-narrative infrastructure..**

Cities face a double bind: erasing material heritage silences the history embedded in it; preserving it unchanged perpetuates the original violence by consecrating dominant narratives. Contested heritage sites become sites of ongoing harm—communities see monuments celebrating their oppression, memorials that omit their suffering, interpretation controlled by descendant institutions, and repatriation claims rejected as 'not our decision to make.' Meanwhile, counter-narratives—created by grassroots movements, community historians, and resistance practitioners—emerge in cracks, exist temporally, disappear, and must be recreated. This exhausts community stewardship capacity and leaves power asymmetry intact: the official narrative is permanent, funded, institutional; the counter-narrative is marginal, volunteer-driven, removable. The deeper problem: heritage governance remains centred in institutions rather than distributed among those most affected. Decisions about whose history is memorialized, how sites are interpreted, whether repatriation occurs, and what belonging means are made without meaningful participation from descendants of harm or communities claiming stake.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish permanent Contested Heritage Commons Stewardship Structures—multi-stakeholder governance bodies holding decision-making authority over heritage designation, interpretation, repatriation, and memorial contestation at the neighbourhood or city scale. The structure has five integrated dimensions:

**1. Narrative Legitimacy Protocols.** Create formal infrastructure that presents competing historical narratives about the same site simultaneously—not seeking false consensus but making visible the legitimate coexistence of conflicting interpretations. Use narrative cartography to map which communities claim which histories, when those claims emerged, and on what evidence. Design memorial commons and interpretation sites (plaques, digital archives, temporary installations, counter-monuments) that hold multiplicity without hierarchizing. The goal is not resolution but civic capacity to sit with ambiguity.

**2. Distributed Decision Authority.** Transfer governance of heritage interpretation, naming, conservation priorities, and memorial design from municipal or institutional bodies to neighbourhood circles including community historians, descendants of harm, indigenous practitioners, long-term residents, and cultural stewards. Use subsidiarity principle: decisions made at lowest competent level. Reserve veto or co-governance rights for communities most affected by the heritage in question.

**3. Counter-Narrative Permanence Infrastructure.** Invest in permanent, well-resourced, institutionally-backed infrastructure for suppressed histories—not symbolic gestures but enduring capacity: dedicated curatorial positions, archival space, annual interpretation budgets, memorial sites with maintenance commitments, and counter-inscription frameworks (plaques added to existing monuments, projections, recontextualized namings). Make counter-narratives as durable as official monuments.

**4. Heritage Debt & Repatriation Commons.** Establish transparent accounting mechanisms for heritage removed, displaced, commandeered, or damaged during colonialism and state violence. Create binding settlement processes for repatriation, financial reparations, and epistemic restitution (acknowledgment of stolen knowledge, validation of community expertise). Treat repatriation not as one-time transfer but as ongoing governance of the commons—communities and institutions negotiating shared stewardship or full return based on legitimate claims.

**5. Living Heritage Cycle.** Institutionalize regular (annual or biennial) neighbourhood heritage visioning circles where interpretation narratives are actively revisited, new histories incorporated, and memorial frameworks updated. Heritage is not fixed but alive—new communities arrive, new archives surface, new understandings emerge. The commons adapts through structured contestation rather than top-down revision.

**Implementation**: Site a City Heritage Commons Stewardship Circle at contested sites (colonial forts, slave trading posts, displacement memorials, institutional buildings housing colonial collections). Charter members from affected communities, academic institutions, municipal heritage bodies, and cultural practitioners. Give decision authority over interpretation, naming, conservation standards, repatriation claims, and memorial design. Establish 3-year rolling governance cycles with transparent conflict mapping and narrative legitimacy protocols. Fund permanent curatorial and community stewardship capacity. Link neighbourhood circles through polycentric network—each site semi-autonomous but sharing standards for narrative transparency and counter-memorial permanence. Make the governance structure itself visible (show who decides, how conflicts are resolved, which narratives are recognized) as part of the decolonial act.

This pattern transforms contested heritage from a site of unresolved harm into a living commons where competing claims are permanently stewarded, counter-narratives are institutionally protected, and belonging itself is recognized as contested but legitimate.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by convening—not the usual suspects, but the people whose ancestors are embedded in the contested site itself. This means descendant communities of the enslaved, the displaced, the colonized; historians and knowledge-keepers from those communities; young people from the neighborhood; artists and cultural practitioners; and yes, some institutional people—but as guests, not chairs.

The first act is **mapping all the stories already present**. What do elders remember? What do archives hold? What do oral traditions say? What do the streets themselves tell people who walk them daily? Resist the urge to reconcile these into a single narrative. Instead, create a **living archive structure**—physical and digital—that holds multiple accounts side by side. A memorial might have plaques in different languages telling different versions of what happened at that location. A museum room might present competing interpretations without trying to adjudicate between them. A walking tour might include licensed community guides who tell the story from their own lineage's perspective.

Second, **shift decision-making power**. The commons stewardship body makes binding decisions about what gets preserved, what gets removed, what gets added, what interpretive texts accompany heritage. This isn't advisory. Communities don't ask permission; they hold authority. This requires real resource transfer—budget lines, hiring power, curatorial control.

Third, **create material infrastructure for counter-narratives**. This isn't just adding plaques to existing monuments. It's commissioning new artworks, sponsoring oral history projects, supporting descendant communities in creating their own heritage interpretations and institutions. It's repatriating objects to community custody. It's creating spaces—public plazas, gathering halls, gardens—where multiple ways of remembering can coexist and be tended.

Fourth, **establish protocols for ongoing contestation**. Heritage isn't settled. New evidence emerges. Generational perspectives shift. Build in formal review cycles. Include mechanisms for communities to challenge interpretations, propose additions, request removals. Make the contestation itself visible and valued, not hidden.

Fifth, **resource this sustainably**. Allocate permanent funding—not project grants that evaporate. Pay community historians and guides as professionals. Support descendant organizations doing their own heritage work independently of city institutions.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern flourishes, something shifts in how a city holds itself. People begin to see contested sites not as problems to solve but as places where their full humanity—including the painful parts—is finally recognized. Descendant communities experience something like restoration of dignity, though not healing (these aren't the same). Young people from those communities see themselves reflected in official heritage, which changes what they believe is possible to claim and create. The city becomes legible as a place of real history, not sanitized mythology.

Intellectually, the pattern cultivates **epistemological humility**—the understanding that no single institution or methodology holds all truth. This spills over into how people think about other domains: governance, science, knowledge itself.

The risks, if this pattern weakens: **Contested Heritage Commons can calcify into new orthodoxies**. One descendant community's narrative can become as authoritative and erasive as the original one was. Without genuine power-sharing, "multi-stakeholder" becomes theater while institutional gatekeeping continues. **Perpetual contestation can become weaponized**—used to delay justice indefinitely, to exhaust communities fighting for recognition. Heritage work can become professionalized and gentrified, so that only certain educated voices get heard as "legitimate." **Communities can face co-optation**—their narratives repackaged as diversity content for tourism, robbing them of meaning.

If stewardship structures lack real authority, they become advisory bodies that institutions ignore, breeding cynicism. If resources don't flow to descendant communities directly, the pattern becomes costly cultural theater without material consequence.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Ballymurphy, Belfast**: After British soldiers killed eleven civilians in 1971, the neighborhood blocked official memory for decades. In the 2010s, community organizations led oral history projects and created their own memorial infrastructure independent of state sanction. The *Ballymurphy Precedent* now shapes how contested sites across Ireland are governed—with community stewardship recognized as legitimate authority.

**Zanzibar's Stone Town**: Multiple communities—Arab, Indian, African, descendant of enslaved people—share heritage sites layered with conflicting histories of trade, slavery, colonialism. Rather than a single museum narrative, the town has developed a **multi-sited, community-guided interpretation system** where different guides from different communities lead tours that present competing perspectives on the same spaces. The Sultan's Palace tells a story; the slave market chamber tells another; descendant communities' oral histories tell a third.

**La Montaña, Guatemala**: Indigenous Q'eqchi' communities recovered and stewarded pre-Columbian and colonial sites in their territory after displacement and violence. They created their own archaeology—not Western-credentialed but deeply rooted in lineage knowledge—and established **community heritage committees with binding authority over site access, interpretation, and use**. This model has influenced heritage governance across Central America, showing how decolonial stewardship looks in practice.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI systems begin to narrate history and interpret heritage through pattern-matching, this pattern becomes both more crucial and more precarious. AI can generate plausible but false narratives very convincingly. It can homogenize contested histories into probabilistic consensus—exactly what this pattern resists.

The cognitive era demands that **Contested Heritage Commons explicitly train and deploy community knowledge-keepers as curators of AI systems**—not as data subjects, but as authorities who audit, reject, and direct how their histories get represented. A community-stewarded heritage commons might use AI to surface archival connections humans miss, to translate oral histories into searchable forms, or to create immersive multi-perspective experiences. But the interpretation remains human, rooted, contested.

Distributed intelligence also creates opportunity: **decentralized ledgers can document provenance and repatriation chains**; **networked communities can share contested heritage governance models**; **multimedia platforms can present parallel narratives without hierarchy**. But only if communities control the infrastructure itself.

The risk: heritage becomes a data domain where algorithms optimize for engagement, flattening nuance. Stories become training data. AI systems, trained on dominant historical narratives, reproduce colonial erasure at scale. The pattern only survives if communities have technological sovereignty—control over the systems interpreting their pasts.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Descendant communities are making binding decisions about heritage without seeking permission first
- Young people from contested communities see themselves in official narratives and create new ones
- The city tolerates permanent interpretive disagreement without collapsing into relativism
- Resources flow directly to community heritage organizations, not through institutional intermediaries
- Repatriation is happening—objects and remains actually move from museums to community custody
- Walking through the city, you encounter multiple stories in the same place, held together by design, not erased

**Signs of decay:**
- Community representation on stewardship bodies becomes tokenistic; institutions still control interpretation
- Descendant communities are exhausted from perpetual contestation without material change
- Heritage work gets professionalized and gentrified; only credentialed voices are heard as legitimate
- Repatriation becomes bureaucratic stalling—objects stay in institutional custody indefinitely
- New monuments go up without community input; the pattern is forgotten
- The city "resolves" its contested history prematurely, settling into a new false consensus

**Diagnostic question:** *If a descendant community proposed removing a beloved monument that carries their community's history of resistance, would the governance structure honor that authority—or would it say the monument is "too important to the city" and override the decision?* That answer tells you whether the pattern is genuinely alive or merely decorative.
