### Section 1: Context

Urban commons in the Global South and homeless-visible Northern cities face a deepening contradiction: parks and public thresholds are simultaneously spaces of essential survival (shelter, gathering, informal economy) and sites of elite enclosure (gating, surveillance, restricted hours). This tension is governed by lineage to three Orbit 1 principles: The Challenge of Cooperation (who decides occupancy?), Subsidiarity (decisions made by residents, not distant enforcement), and Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (what makes a homeless person's shelter claim legitimate?). Current practice oscillates between criminalization (removal, fines) and invisibility (toleration without protocol), both of which deny commons status. The emerging pattern recognizes that urban thresholds—park edges, semi-public courtyards, buffer zones—are inherently shared spaces where multiple legitimate uses collide: ecological integrity, informal dwelling, gathering, play, rest. These thresholds require explicit negotiation, not hidden conflict.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Exclusion (gates, enforcement, criminalization) vs. Inhabitation (shelter rights, dignified presence, negotiated access).**

Gating and enclosure fragment urban commons while claiming to provide security. Residents in enclosed neighbourhoods gain perceived safety but lose informal surveillance networks that actually protect cities; homeless encampments are criminalized rather than governed; park edges become battlegrounds between conservation, property defense, and survival. The deeper conflict: who has the right to inhabit public space, and who decides? Current law defaults to exclusion—police remove shelters, gates restrict access, bylaws criminalize temporary structures. This inverts justice: those with resources can defend territory; those without are pushed into invisibility or violation. Residents who might be natural stewards are disempowered (told "enforcement is not your job"); homeless people are denied voice in decisions about spaces they occupy; ecological and social needs are treated as opposed rather than negotiable. The system is neither alive nor just because it suppresses information (informal settlements made invisible) and concentrates authority (police, property owners) rather than distributing it.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Inhabitation as Commons Right operates at three nested levels, each distributing agency and making conflict visible for ongoing negotiation.

**Level 1: Invert the Default Rule.** Statutory presumption: all urban commons (parks, plazas, thresholds, public edges) permit occupancy rights—sleeping, gathering, temporary shelter, informal structures—unless explicitly restricted by a commons charter negotiated with all affected parties. This shifts burden of proof from homeless people (justify your presence) to the community (justify exclusion). Inclusion becomes the baseline; restrictions require transparent covenant.

**Level 2: Resident Gatekeeping as Stewardship.** Car-free districts, park edges, and semi-public thresholds are not enforced by police but by residents as distributed gatekeepers. Residents collectively decide access permissions—for other residents, deliveries, disabled persons, temporary shelter users—through regular commons meetings, not centralized rules. This cultivates what residents actually want: livable neighbourhoods where informal surveillance and collective care replace both gating (which isolates) and abandonment (which invites harm). Residents become stewards because they have real authority; they develop nuance about who belongs, when, and under what conditions.

**Level 3: Explicit Negotiated Protocols for Park Edges & Thresholds.** Design park perimeters, shelter zones, and semi-public courtyards with graduated infrastructure: water, sanitation, storage, shade, seating. Establish transparent governance protocols (seasonal timeframes, material standards, maintenance responsibilities) co-authored by housed and unhoused residents, ecologists, and adjacent property holders. These protocols are not fixed—they adapt annually as conditions change (climate, migration, housing availability). Protocols acknowledge that inhabitation is commons practice deserving dignity and service access (medical, housing navigation) rather than criminalization.

The pattern resolves the tension by treating thresholds not as zones to defend but as living edges where multiple legitimate claims can coexist through explicit negotiation. It distributes authority (gatekeeping to residents), makes conflict visible (protocols recorded, not hidden), and remains adaptive (covenants reviewed regularly). Justice emerges when the person sleeping in a park edge has voice in the rules governing that space.

## Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming the edge. Walk the city's actual threshold—where formal disappears into informal, where settlement already happens beneath notice. Talk to people already there: shelter users, informal vendors, kids, elders. What do they need? What are they already protecting? This isn't consultation theater. It's recognition.

**Establish a commons charter.** Bring together residents, unhoused people, shopkeepers, city workers—the ones actually stewarding that edge. Write down: Who can occupy this space and how? What uses are protected? What governance matters? The charter isn't perfect law; it's a living agreement that surfaces conflict before enforcement creates it. Update it annually. Pay people for this work.

**Create visible stewardship structures.** Not surveillance cameras. Rotating caretaker roles: residents who tend the space, know neighbors, handle disputes before they harden. Offer small stipends. A caretaker might be someone formerly unhoused, hired to keep a shelter commons safe and dignified. They have standing that police don't.

**Negotiate with power holders explicitly.** If a city wants to restrict the space, the charter makes that negotiation public. If a developer eyes the edge, residents have documented occupancy claims. This doesn't guarantee victory—but it moves conflict from hidden criminalization into visible governance.

**Formalize temporary structures.** If people are building shelter, work with them to do it safely—materials, siting, drainage—rather than raiding tents. Some cities have permitted tiny home villages, temporary pavilions, seasonal markets. The point: structures exist by right and design, not toleration.

**Establish dispute resolution that includes residents.** When conflict emerges (noise, boundaries, resource pressure), the caretaker circle meets first. Only escalate if residents can't hold the agreement. This keeps power local and teachable.

The cultivation is slow. You're building trust where criminalization was the default. Expect pushback from those who profit from enclosure.

## Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Cities become safer because informal surveillance networks rebuild—neighbors know each other, intervene early, care for vulnerable people. Informal economy thrives: vendors, repair people, day laborers can work openly and negotiate terms. Unhoused people access shelter with dignity, medicine, community. Public health improves: infectious disease spreads less in criminalized camps than in governed ones with access to water, sanitation, services. Young people and migrants find footing. The city reads as alive, not policed.

**What risks emerge:** If caretaker roles aren't paid or protected, they burn out or reproduce hierarchies (powerful neighbors dominating, marginalized people excluded). If the charter lacks teeth—no enforcement against developers or police who ignore it—residents lose faith and revert to informal resistance. If the space becomes too visible and successful, surrounding property owners may pressure for closure or "cleanup." If the city uses the charter to evict people while claiming to honor it, the pattern becomes a tool of displacement.

**The deeper risk:** Shared space at the edge can become a pressure valve—a place where inequality is made visible and slightly more humane, but where systemic poverty and housing shortage continue. The pattern works best paired with housing rights, land reform, and income floors. Otherwise it's beautiful management of structural violence.

## Section 6: Known Uses

**Kampung Kolaborasi, Jakarta.** Residents of informal settlements worked with city officials and NGOs to establish a commons charter for five adjacent kampungs. Rather than eviction, residents gained legal occupancy rights in exchange for improving water, sanitation, and fire safety themselves. Governance circles meet monthly. Unhoused newcomers are integrated through orientation by existing residents. The space has stayed intact for seven years, and adjacent formal neighborhoods have gained economic vitality from informal vendor networks.

**Dignity Village, Portland, Oregon.** Founded by unhoused residents themselves in 2000, this 0.5-acre site permits residents to build and govern a tiny-home village. Residents elect councils, manage finances, enforce behavioral agreements. Police don't enter unless invited. It operates as a commons under a long-term lease. Residents transition to housing or stay; dignity is unconditional. The village has survived threats of closure through documented self-governance and community support.

**Parque da Esperança, São Paulo.** A former dumping ground at the city's edge was occupied by squatters in the 1990s. Rather than evict, a coalition of residents, academics, and sympathetic city workers formalized a commons agreement. The space now combines shelter structures, community gardens, a small school, and informal commercial space. Governance rotates among resident groups quarterly. Police enforcement is rare because conflicts are resolved locally.

## Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an age of ubiquitous sensors and AI prediction, the pattern faces new pressures and new possibilities.

**The danger:** AI systems trained on historical crime data will flag informal settlements for enforcement. Predictive policing will concentrate surveillance on spaces where residents already lack privacy. The boundary between care and control blurs—a system that "helps" unhoused people by predicting their location and sending services can become a system that predicts and preemptively excludes them. Algorithms that optimize "neighborhood safety" will find shared edge-spaces inefficient and risky.

**The possibility:** Distributed intelligence—held by residents, not extracted to central servers—can make governance transparent and participatory at scale. A commons app built by and for residents, showing who's stewarding when, what agreements are live, what disputes are pending, can make informal governance visible without exposing people to surveillance. AI can help residents analyze patterns: which agreements work, where conflict clusters, what support is needed. Used this way, technology amplifies resident voice rather than replacing it.

**The shift:** The pattern depends more than ever on residents controlling the data about their own spaces. Who owns the sensors? Who trains the models? If outsiders (city, police, developers) control AI that learns the edge-space, inhabitance becomes precarious. If residents co-design the intelligence systems, they can use them to document their own claims and negotiate from evidence.

## Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People linger in the space not from necessity alone but from choice. Children play. Elders sit and watch. Conversations happen across difference—vendor and neighbor, newcomer and longtime resident. The caretaker is known and trusted. When small conflicts arise, they're resolved locally without escalation. The space changes with seasons and needs but holds coherence. Informal structures are maintained with care. People speak of "we" and "ours." The commons charter is referenced in disputes; people appeal to it rather than to outside authority.

**Signs of decay:** The caretaker role is vacant or held by someone outside the community. Agreements are ignored or enforced only against some people. Police or private security begin showing up routinely. The space becomes visibly divided—some areas kept clean, others neglected. Conversations go silent. People hurry through. Informal structures fall into disrepair. Conflict escalates without resolution. Outside media discovers the space and frames it as a problem. Property owners nearby begin pushing for "cleanup." The charter exists but isn't referenced; governance happens by whisper and exclusion.

**The diagnostic question:** If an outsider (journalist, official, developer, investor) walked into this space tomorrow, would residents show them around with pride, explain the agreements, invite them to help—or would they hide, scatter, stay silent?

If the first, the commons is alive. If the second, it's already being criminalized again, and the pattern is failing.
