> Participatory budgeting often overlooks care work and shared community assets, leaving women and caregivers out of spend

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Urban domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Participatory budgeting emerged as a commons response: direct community voice in resource allocation, bypassing concentrated power, distributing governance. Yet across cities globally—Porto Alegre to Nairobi, New York to Lima—a consistent pattern emerges: the same invisible hierarchies that structure mainstream budgets reproduce themselves within participatory processes. Care work (childcare, elder support, community health), prevention (water sanitation, disease surveillance, mental health commons), and maintenance (park stewardship, library operations, community space upkeep) are systematically underfunded relative to visible capital projects. This is not random. It emerges from how participation is designed: what counts as a 'project,' who feels authorized to propose care work, how benefits are measured, and whose time is valued. Participatory budgets that ignore these design choices amplify rather than interrupt gender, class, and care-work erasure—creating the appearance of justice while deepening the invisibility of those who sustain collective life.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Visibility bias (capital projects, individual benefit, tangible output) vs. Stewardship work (care, prevention, maintenance, collective thriving).**

Participatory budgeting faces a structural visibility problem. Participants—shaped by economic logic that measures value as tangible, individual, consumable—vote for what they can see: roads, markets, water points, health clinics, sports facilities. These are real needs. But the process renders certain work categorically ineligible or unmeasurable: childcare (who counts its health impact?), elder care (diffuse benefit, hard to photograph), community health prevention (benefit is absence of disease, invisible), library operations (maintenance, not capital). The invisibility compounds. Women, those responsible for care work, have lower participation rates or see their priorities systematically underfunded. Communities dependent on care labor face pressure to contribute 'sweat equity'—unpaid work that masks exploitation. Care infrastructure gets framed as 'wants' (community choice) rather than 'essential' (government responsibility), creating chronic underfunding. The result: participatory budgets reproduce the very hierarchies they were designed to interrupt, justifying them as 'community voice.' The commons—the living system of mutual care—becomes the victim of its own democratization.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Redesign participation to make stewardship work visible and valued. This requires five integrated shifts:

**1. REFRAME WHAT COUNTS AS A PROJECT.** Stewardship work—care, prevention, maintenance—must be eligible for budgeting at parity with capital projects. A community health surveillance system, childcare cooperative, elder companion network, or park stewardship program are projects with measurable outcomes. Assign them equal status with roads and clinics. Measure their benefits: health equity gains, time freed for other work, social cohesion, resilience.

**2. MAKE INVISIBLE LABOR VISIBLE.** Before voting, surface the care work that sustains community life. Map who provides childcare, elder support, food provisioning, health knowledge. Quantify time, cost, impact. Present this alongside infrastructure needs. This shifts the problem from 'what do people want?' to 'what does collective life actually require?' and 'who pays the cost?'

**3. RESTRUCTURE PARTICIPATION TO CENTER CARE WORKERS.** Participatory processes must actively solicit priorities from those doing invisible work—parents, elder caregivers, community health workers, food provisioners. Hold separate budget assemblies if necessary. Ensure their expertise shapes what gets proposed, measured, and funded. Reciprocity demands that those bearing invisible costs have explicit voice in allocation.

**4. ASSIGN ECONOMIC VALUE TO STEWARDSHIP.** Budget processes must assign time-value to care and maintenance work equivalent to other labor. If building a road costs X in labor, funding a community childcare program costs Y in actual wages, not volunteer assumptions. This makes opportunity costs real: funding care work means other projects are deferred—a choice the community makes consciously, not by default invisibility.

**5. MEASURE COMMONS HEALTH, NOT JUST PROJECT COMPLETION.** Design accountability around collective thriving, not individual benefit. Track: health equity shifts, time freed in households, participation in collective life, care burden distribution across gender/class. A participatory budget succeeds not when everyone gets what they voted for, but when the community's capacity to sustain itself—to care for itself—visibly increases.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping what's already happening. Before any budget cycle, send community members—especially women, caregivers, elderly people—to document care work in their neighborhoods: who watches children while parents work, who checks on isolated elders, who tends shared spaces, who resolves conflicts, who holds knowledge about seasonal needs. This is archaeology, not judgment. Write it down. Photograph it. Record stories. Make the invisible visible first.

Then redesign the participation process itself. Create three parallel budgeting tracks:

**Care and Prevention Track:** Dedicated funding for childcare networks, elder support, community health work, conflict resolution, knowledge-keeping. Frame these as infrastructure—as essential as pipes and roads. Budget in three-year cycles so care work can be sustained, not reinvented annually.

**Maintenance and Stewardship Track:** Fund the unglamorous work that keeps commons alive—park care, water system maintenance, school grounds, market cleaning, seed libraries. Make this work visible by hiring community members to do it, not volunteers. Pay reflects value.

**Community Priorities Track:** Keep the original participatory budgeting for community-chosen projects, but ensure the first two tracks are funded before this one.

Crucially: change how you measure and present results. Don't ask "How many people benefit?" Ask "What relationships deepen? What capacities grow? What becomes more resilient?" Track care work outputs alongside capital projects: number of children in safe, affordable care; elders with regular contact; maintenance hours completed; knowledge documented and shared.

Shift who facilitates. Bring in community members—especially those doing care work—as paid budget facilitators and decision-makers, not just participants. They understand what matters and why.

Finally, build feedback loops. After each cycle, ask those most affected by invisibility: Did this process change what got funded? Did care work get treated as a real priority, or as charity? Adjust ruthlessly based on their answers.

### Section 5: Consequences

When care work becomes visible and fundable, several things flourish: childcare becomes stable and affordable, reducing economic barriers to participation for parents. Elder isolation decreases—they become part of community life, not isolated in homes or institutions. Community members develop new capacities: they learn to organize childcare networks, train as health workers, manage commons spaces. The work itself becomes dignified, recognized, paid when appropriate.

Neighborhoods develop thicker networks of relationship and mutual obligation. People know each other across generations. Knowledge about seasonal patterns, local medicines, conflict resolution practices gets documented and shared rather than lost. Care becomes collective rather than isolated—distributed across a community, not concentrated on individual family units (usually women).

Budgets begin to reflect actual community values rather than economic logic alone. This shifts what gets built next: fewer monuments, more functional commons. More prevention, less emergency response.

But risks emerge if this isn't tended carefully: Care work funding can become paternalistic—outside experts designing "care solutions" rather than communities naming their own. Paid care work can become exploitative if wages aren't genuinely fair or if it recreates dependency relationships. The danger of co-option is real: governments may fund visible care work to appear responsive while continuing to ignore systemic inequities.

There's also the risk of burden-shifting: communities fund care because the state withdraws, leaving care workers more precarious, not less. Without attention to power, documenting care work can expose those doing it to surveillance or taxation without benefit.

The deepest risk: if care track funding comes from diverting money from elsewhere rather than genuine reallocation, you've simply made one group's invisibility visible while rendering another group invisible.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Vigo, Spain (Participatory Budgets with Care Focus):** For five years, Vigo's participatory budgeting reserved 30% of funds for what they called "care and social cohesion projects"—childcare spaces, elder companion networks, neighborhood gathering places. Results: documented increase in women's participation in subsequent budget cycles, measurable growth in formal childcare capacity, creation of paid elder companion positions held by community members.

**Shashamane, Ethiopia (Community Care Mapping):** A cooperative in this Oromia region developed a participatory budgeting process that began with community members mapping all care work (childcare, water collection, grain processing, elder care, healing knowledge). They then budgeted to strengthen existing care networks rather than replace them with new services. Funding went to tools, training, and modest payment for coordinated care work. The process elevated the economic status of care work within the community and slowed rural-to-urban migration of young women.

**Jackson, Mississippi (Black Feminist Budgeting Initiative):** Beginning in 2019, this grassroots participatory budgeting effort centered on what organizers called "care infrastructure"—childcare cooperatives, community health work, neighborhood commons maintenance. They explicitly rejected the "visibility bias" of standard participatory budgeting, creating dedicated funding streams for prevention and relationship-building work. Women who had been invisible in civic processes became budget decision-makers, shifting what the city funded and how.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems introduce both opportunity and danger here. The opportunity: algorithms can help make care work visible at scale. Machine learning trained on community-generated data can identify patterns in care work—who cares for whom, what's needed—that humans might miss. Participatory budgeting platforms powered by AI can automatically flag when proposals exclude care work or undervalue stewardship.

But the danger is real: AI can accelerate the invisibility problem. Algorithmic budgeting systems trained on historical spending data will reproduce the same visibility biases—they'll learn that capital projects "matter" more, that care work is harder to quantify, that certain people's votes don't count. AI risk here is crystallizing bias into code.

The shift required: make care work legible to algorithms by changing what we measure and feed into systems. Document care work in data form—hours, relationships, preventative outcomes, knowledge preserved. Build participatory budgeting systems where community members (especially care workers) help design what data matters and how algorithms should interpret value.

In distributed intelligence contexts—where decisions happen across networks rather than centralized budgets—the pattern becomes even more critical. Decentralized systems can either amplify invisibility (each node reproduces bias) or enable it (communities define their own care frameworks and fund them directly). The choice depends on conscious design: Do we build systems that make stewardship work visible and fundable at every node?

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Care work appears in budget proposals with detailed descriptions of what it accomplishes. Community members doing care work participate as decision-makers, not just beneficiaries. Budget documents include care-track funding with multi-year timelines. Conversations about budgeting include questions like "What care will this enable?" or "Who becomes more isolated if we choose this?" Caregivers describe feeling recognized, not invisible. Children have affordable, stable childcare. Elders report regular meaningful contact with community members.

**Signs of decay:** Care work remains eligibility-ambiguous: people unsure if they can propose it. The budget process still centers capital projects, with care work as an afterthought. Participation remains dominated by employed, non-caregiving residents. Care work proposals lose to sports facilities or market improvements. No data on care work outcomes. Caregivers describe the process as unchanged—still invisible, still unpaid. Feedback loops don't happen; no one asks whether the process actually shifted power.

**A diagnostic question:** After your first participatory budgeting cycle, ask the community members doing most care work in your neighborhood: *Did this process change what your community decides is worth paying for? Are you more visible, or just documented?* Their answer tells you whether you've actually redesigned invisibility or simply named it.
