### Section 1: Context

Every enterprise that must survive over decades faces a structural paradox: current operations generate the cash that funds tomorrow's innovations, yet the very focus required to run efficient operations blinds leadership to signals of market change. This tension is not new, but its stakes have risen. Market half-lives shorten. Capital once invested in 5-year product cycles now demands constant reinvention. Yet the attempt to innovate everywhere—to make every team a startup—destabilises the core operations that fund the system. The lineage runs through Christensen's Innovation Dilemma, Mazzucato's Entrepreneurial State, and the multi-horizon strategy frameworks (Govindarajan, McGrath). But most portfolio approaches treat allocation as a fixed percentage game ('70-20-10') applied uniformly, ignoring that carrying capacity, maturity stage, and competitive threat vary by context. The commons engineering challenge: How do we design a governance structure that treats innovation as a living portfolio—one that learns, adapts, and maintains carrying capacity across all three horizons?

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Operational stability (exploiting current value) vs. exploratory renewal (discovering future value).**

Organizations face three interlocking failures in innovation portfolio stewardship. First, false certainty: fixed percentage allocations (70% core, 20% adjacent, 10% transformational) ignore the reality that exploration generates uncertainty, not predictable output. When a transformational bet fails, rigid allocation leaves no way to reallocate responsibly. Second, visibility collapse: core operations and moonshots are typically governed through separate P&Ls with different success metrics, languages, and cultures. Leadership cannot see whether the portfolio as a whole is overloaded, underfunded, or generating actual option value. Third, carrying capacity blindness: organizations increase innovation spending without asking whether the system can absorb the complexity, conflict, and learning cost. The result: projects compete for scarce senior attention, experimentation teams operate in isolation, and the core business slows under the weight of innovation governance overhead. The underlying conflict: How do you allocate capital and governance authority across horizons whose success criteria, timescales, and risk profiles are fundamentally incommensurable?

---

### Section 3: Solution

Portfolio stewardship operates as a fractal governance system with three nested cycles, each with transparent allocation thresholds and adaptive feedback loops.

**HORIZON DEFINITION**: Map innovation work into three horizons—Core (protecting and extending current value streams), Adjacent (expanding into adjacent markets or capabilities), and Transformational (exploring fundamentally new value creation). Horizons are not business units but decision domains. A single team may work across all three.

**STEWARDSHIP COUNCILS**: Establish councils at three scales—Portfolio (enterprise-wide), Horizon (core/adjacent/transformational), and Project (individual initiative). Councils are NOT approval hierarchies but sense-making bodies. Each council meets on a short cycle (monthly for portfolio, quarterly for horizon) and operates by explicit decision rules: What signals indicate this horizon is at or near carrying capacity? What forward indicators tell us whether exploration is yielding discovery, or whether core operations are becoming brittle?

**ADAPTIVE ALLOCATION BANDS** (not fixed percentages): Set allocation bands—minimum and maximum—for each horizon, calibrated to your industry velocity and competitive threat. Example: Core 50-65%, Adjacent 20-35%, Transformational 5-20%. Bands replace fixed targets. Within bands, councils reallocate based on signal. If core operations show fragility (customer churn, talent exodus, capability gaps), shift resources up. If transformational bets are failing to generate learning, redirect funding to adjacent exploration.

**TRANSPARENT THRESHOLD LOGIC**: Make reallocation triggers public. Define what signals warrant moving capital: e.g., "If three sequential core initiatives miss their hypothesis targets, we rebalance adjacent allocation downward," or "If no transformational bet generates a proof-of-concept within 18 months, we reassess the bet portfolio." This is not rigidity—it is honesty. Thresholds can be revised quarterly, but they must be visible.

**DUAL METRICS ECOSYSTEM**: Track both backward and forward indicators. Backward: revenue, margin, customer satisfaction (core); unit economics, adoption rate (adjacent); capital deployed, cost per learning (transformational). Forward: capability gaps emerging, lead-user signals, patent velocity, internal startup graduation rates. The portfolio is healthy when forward indicators show learning flow across all horizons, not when any single metric optimizes.

**CROSS-HORIZON KNOWLEDGE FLOWS**: Establish rituals that connect horizon teams—not for approval but for pattern sharing. Core teams learn from adjacent teams' rapid prototyping. Transformational teams access core teams' customer relationships. This breaks the isolation that kills portfolio coherence.

**CARRYING CAPACITY AUDIT**: Quarterly, councils assess whether the total innovation load—number of concurrent initiatives, governance meetings, learning burden on shared platforms—is sustainable. When capacity is exceeded, the response is not "work faster" but "descope or sequence initiatives." This prevents the organizational entropy that kills both innovation and operations.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping your actual work—not the work you think you do, but the work your organization funds and deploys people toward. Create three pools: Core (the revenue engine), Adjacent (experiments within arm's reach of existing capabilities), and Transformational (genuine unknowns). This taxonomy matters less than ruthless honesty about which bucket each initiative inhabits.

**Establish transparent thresholds, not fixed percentages.** Core work should generate sufficient cash flow to cover its own operations plus a baseline innovation fund—typically 15–25% of operating profit, depending on your sector's velocity. This becomes your adaptive pool. Rather than allocating to Transformational work first, let Adjacent initiatives prove themselves. When an Adjacent bet shows traction (defined metrics: customer engagement, repeatable unit economics, or clear capability transfer), it graduates toward Core. When it stalls, it sunsets.

**Create decision gates at natural moments**—quarterly reviews, not arbitrary cycles. At each gate, ask three questions: (1) Does this work still fit its horizon? A Core initiative showing exponential potential should migrate toward Adjacent. (2) What changed in the environment? New competitor signals, regulatory shifts, or customer signals may justify reallocating from one horizon to another. (3) What can we learn and kill fast? Transformational work should have explicit kill criteria—not just financial targets, but learning thresholds. If you've learned what you need and the bet isn't scaling, harvest the insight and close it.

**Protect Adjacent work from organizational antibodies.** Core teams often view Adjacent initiatives as resource thieves. Create role clarity: Core leadership owns efficiency; Adjacent leadership owns learning. They report to different stakeholders, use different metrics, and have different success measures. This reduces friction.

**Fund Transformational work through conviction, not allocation.** Pick 1–3 Transformational bets at a time. Give them 18–24 months, a small team (3–8 people), and genuine autonomy. Their metric isn't profit—it's: "Does this could become a $50M+ revenue stream?" If yes at 18 months, migrate toward Adjacent. If no clear signal, close it.

The gardener's instinct: Core work needs tending (pruning, feeding); Adjacent needs patient observation; Transformational needs permission to fail visibly.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern works, you see three flourishings. First, cash generation becomes predictable enough to fund genuine exploration without panic. Core teams stabilize—fewer reorganizations, less context-switching, clearer long-term investment. Second, Adjacent initiatives graduate to Core at a meaningful cadence. The organization's revenue base shifts, slowly, toward emerging customer needs. Third, Transformational work generates failures that teach cheaply—failed bets become case studies that protect future bets.

But the risks run deep if this pattern is performed half-heartedly. **The most dangerous risk: bureaucratic theater.** You name three horizons but don't actually separate them. Adjacent teams get Core-style efficiency pressure. Transformational teams get asked for quarterly profits. Everything becomes Core work in disguise, and breakthrough capacity atrophies. Your organization becomes a slow-moving competitor in a fast-moving world.

**Second risk: Core starvation.** In times of crisis (recession, supply shock, lost customer), organizations slash Transformational and even Adjacent work, yanking resources to Core. This is sometimes necessary. But if this pattern repeats every cycle, your core infrastructure becomes dated. Customers sense the decay before financials show it. You optimize for survival, not renewal.

**Third risk: Adjacent work becoming a graveyard.** Initiative launches with fanfare, consumes resources for 18 months, generates interesting learnings, then sits—neither scaling nor closing. It becomes zombie work, draining resources without generating value or clarity. This happens when decision gates aren't enforced or when learning feels too valuable to abandon, even when market signals say the bet won't scale.

The antidote: clear decision dates, visible kill criteria, and permission from leadership to close things that work intellectually but don't work commercially.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Amazon's Kindle strategy (2004–2010)** exemplifies this pattern at scale. Kindle was Adjacent work—expanding beyond books into hardware and digital distribution, leveraging existing logistics and publisher relationships. It received patient funding through AWS revenues (Core work generating surplus). When Kindle showed traction, it wasn't immediately spun into a separate P&L; it migrated into Core operations. Amazon simultaneously funded Transformational bets: AWS (which eventually became more valuable than retail), the Echo (exploratory), Fresh (exploratory). They explicitly killed Kindle Fire as a tablet competitor when signals suggested it wouldn't scale. Core work (retail) funded exploration; learning informed resource allocation.

**Hindustan Unilever's Project Shakti (rural distribution network)** inverted the usual pattern. Their Core business was urban, middle-class FMCG. Shakti (Adjacent, then Core) rebuilt rural economics by training women entrepreneurs as distribution nodes. It required patience; rural margins were thin. But by treating it as genuinely Adjacent—separate team, different metrics, 3–5 year runway—they discovered a market worth billions and a capability (hyperlocal last-mile distribution) that became defensible. It graduated to Core without losing its innovative DNA because the separation was real.

**Patagonia's 1% for the Planet initiative** functions as Transformational work funded by Core operations. Founded in 1985, it remains exploratory—always questioning whether environmental grantmaking works, evolving methods, accepting non-financial metrics (policy change, acres protected). It's never been asked to scale to 50% of the company or to generate profit. That clarity of horizon preserves its exploratory character. When climate work shifted from fringe to mainstream, Patagonia had already cultivated expertise and partnerships that became strategic.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed intelligence and AI change the shape of this pattern without erasing it. The tension remains—you still need stable operations and exploratory capacity. But the cadence accelerates.

AI makes Adjacent work faster to validate. A marketing hypothesis that once required 6 months of A/B testing can now be prototyped and tested in 6 weeks. This means your decision gates should tighten. Adjacent initiatives that don't show traction within 9–12 months should be reevaluated faster; the option value of "wait and see" has decreased.

Transformational work becomes both harder and easier. Easier because AI can explore design spaces humans couldn't. Harder because everyone can. The advantage shifts from "we tried something different" to "we learned something others missed." This means your kill criteria for Transformational work should emphasize learning velocity and insight, not just eventual commercialization. Did this team discover something unique about customer behavior, capability, or market structure?

The bigger shift: AI's ability to accelerate routine Core work. As automation handles more operational work, the real scarcity becomes *strategic imagination*—the capacity to see what customers will need before they feel the ache. This argues for *increasing* allocation to Adjacent and Transformational work, not decreasing it. The Core may generate less surplus cash if it's increasingly automated, but it will require less human leadership attention, freeing cognitive space for exploration.

One caution: AI can make false certainty seductive. You can model thousands of scenarios and convince yourself the Transformational bet will work. Build in explicit uncertainty buffers; the map is never the territory.

### Section 8: Vitality

A healthy portfolio stewardship pattern shows several vital signs.

**Signs of life**: Core teams speak about their work with stability and pride, not defensive language. ("We serve 2 million customers reliably" rather than "No one cares about this anymore.") Adjacent initiatives have visible champions and clear graduation criteria; people can articulate when they'll know to move or kill the work. Transformational bets are openly discussed, including why they might fail. Leadership discusses resource allocation trade-offs in the open; debates about funding aren't hidden in private memos.

**Signs of decay**: Core work becomes the only "safe" career path. Adjacent work is discussed with skepticism ("nice to explore, but..."). Transformational bets are funded as executive vanity projects, not genuine strategic questions. Resource allocation happens through politics, not transparent thresholds. Failed Transformational work gets hidden rather than harvested for learning. The organization defaults to Core work in times of uncertainty, tightening the grip on current value.

**The diagnostic question**: *When was the last time an Adjacent initiative genuinely graduated to Core, or a Transformational bet was closed with visible learning shared across the organization?*

If you can't answer, your allocation is probably theater. If you can point to three examples in the last 24 months, the pattern is alive.
