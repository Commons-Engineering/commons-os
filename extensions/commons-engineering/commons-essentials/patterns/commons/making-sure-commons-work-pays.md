### Section 1: Context

Urban commons—community gardens, shared facilities, cooperative housing, neighborhood stewardship networks—depend entirely on continuous human labor: governance meetings, maintenance rounds, conflict resolution, mentorship, safety presence, knowledge transmission. Yet this labor is systematically rendered invisible, uncompensated, and disproportionately borne by women, youth, migrants, and the economically precarious. When stewardship is unpaid, only the affluent can afford to participate in governance; when participation requirements exist but are enforced unequally, commons reproduce the very poverty they claim to address. This pattern draws from South Asian mutual aid traditions, feminist commons scholarship, and recursive stewardship models where distributed labor creates both resilience and shared competence. It recognizes that sustainable commons require people who can survive through commons participation itself—not subsidizing their subsistence through external wage work.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Stewardship sustainability vs. commons affordability; invisible labor recognition vs. exploitation; livelihood security vs. free-riding prevention.**

Commons face a systemic contradiction: they require ongoing stewardship labor (governance, maintenance, dispute resolution, care) to remain alive, but this labor is treated as voluntary, invisible, or poorly compensated. Three forces collide. First, economic: stewardship attracts only the economically secure (those with outside income or wealth) unless guaranteed livelihood makes it economically viable. Second, justice: participation requirements (mandatory labor rotation, assembly attendance) disproportionately burden the economically precarious, who cannot absorb opportunity costs. Third, sustainability: invisible, uncompensated labor is unsustainable—stewards burn out, knowledge is lost, governance becomes brittle or captured by the few who remain. Simultaneously, commons must avoid becoming exclusive to the affluent through high participation fees or inaccessible governance structures. The pattern must reconcile livelihood security for stewards with affordability for low-income members, and distribute both visible and invisible labor equitably without enabling free-riding.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish a **Stewardship Subsistence Covenant**—a binding legal agreement that specifies four interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Steward Livelihood Floor.** Designate core steward roles (governance facilitators, maintenance specialists, dispute-resolution keepers, knowledge stewards) as compensated positions guaranteed minimum annual income from commons surplus or member dues. This income is unconditional on political outcomes—stewards cannot be defunded for unpopular decisions. Compensation levels are set through participatory valuation, transparent to all members, and benchmarked to local subsistence cost, not market wages. This ensures stewardship attracts economically diverse people and prevents burnout.

**2. Labor Valuation & Transparency Mapping.** Systematically identify and value all commons labor—governance (decision-making), maintenance (physical care), invisible work (emotional labor, mentorship, safety presence, knowledge transmission). Create visual maps showing: who does what labor, how much time it requires, what compensation it receives, and what invisible subsidies (e.g., childcare borne by family, opportunity costs absorbed by the precarious) are embedded. These maps are reviewed annually and updated as commons evolve. This makes exploitation visible and enables renegotiation.

**3. Mandatory Participation with Subsidy Pathways.** Require all members to contribute minimum annual stewardship labor (attendance, governance, maintenance, care work) on rotating schedules. But simultaneously create explicit accommodation and substitution pathways: members facing disability, caregiving, precarity, or crisis can substitute labor with modest fee payments, commons childcare provision, or skill-sharing arrangements. Participation requirements distribute burden equitably while building collective competence; subsidy pathways prevent the requirements from reproducing poverty. The goal is distributed shared responsibility, not extraction from the vulnerable.

**4. Subsistence Reserve & Anti-Poverty Covenant.** Legally reserve a fixed percentage of commons resources or revenue as an inalienable floor—dedicated to supporting members unable to contribute due to age, disability, illness, crisis, or extreme poverty. This reserve is protected even during scarcity; no member faces displacement or exclusion for inability to pay commons fees or meet participation standards due to genuine hardship. Eligibility for subsistence support is determined through peer recognition (not bureaucratic means-testing), and amounts are sufficient for basic needs.

**Adaptation Cycle.** These mechanisms are reviewed annually in commons assembly. Compensation levels, participation expectations, and reserve allocations adjust based on: actual commons revenues, member feedback on burden and fairness, labor market changes, and crisis events. The pattern remains alive through this continuous renegotiation, not through static rules.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping invisible labor. Spend two months documenting who does what: meeting facilitation, maintenance schedules, conflict mediation, onboarding new members, institutional memory. Don't assume—ask. Listen especially to those whose work goes unnamed. This becomes your stewardship inventory.

Next, audit commons revenue. What money flows through—membership dues, facility fees, service charges, grants, earned income? Build a simple ledger. You're not looking for perfection; you're looking for what's actually there. Many urban commons surprise themselves with hidden resources.

Now design the covenant together. This isn't top-down. Convene stewards, members, and a financial facilitator (often a sympathetic accountant or cooperative development worker) to answer three questions honestly: *Which roles are essential to keep this commons alive?* *What is livelihood in your bioregion—what's the actual cost of rent, food, healthcare?* *How do we fund this without pricing out members?*

The covenant typically takes shape as: (1) role definition and time commitment, (2) minimum annual compensation (pegged to regional living costs, not below), (3) funding sources (tiered member contributions, earned income, grants explicitly designated for steward wages), (4) term length (usually 2-3 years renewable), and (5) accountability (transparent reporting, steward voice in budgets). Write it plainly. Make it legal but human.

Implement through stages. Don't compensate everyone simultaneously if your commons is fragile. Start with 1-2 roles. Watch what shifts. Many commons begin with one part-time steward coordinator—the person who holds calendars, reminds people, troubleshoots. That role, done well, unburdens everyone else. Expand as capacity allows.

Create a steward development fund. Set aside 5-10% of compensation budget for training, rest, skill-building. Stewardship burns people out. Invest in their renewal. This is cultivation, not just extraction.

### Section 5: Consequences

When stewardship pays and gets named, three things tend to flourish. First, *continuity*: knowledge and relationships don't evaporate when a burned-out volunteer disappears. The commons develops institutional memory beyond any one person. Second, *accessibility*: stewardship becomes possible for people without outside wealth or flexible schedules—parents, people with disabilities, those without safety nets. The commons becomes truly multi-class. Third, *quality*: compensated stewards can respond thoughtfully to conflict, mentor newcomers properly, and maintain infrastructure before it fails. The work becomes skillful.

The risks demand attention. *Dependency* can emerge: if one steward holds too much power and compensation, the commons becomes fragile and that person becomes exhausted. Distribute roles and compensation across multiple people. *Professionalization drift*: paid stewards can begin to separate from the community, becoming managers instead of members. Counter this with mandatory participation in broader commons work and regular reflection with the whole membership. *Funding brittleness*: if one grant ends or one revenue stream disappears, stewards lose livelihood. Diversify funding ruthlessly.

A harder consequence: *naming the true cost*. When you stop hiding stewardship labor, members sometimes resist the dues increase or fee structure needed to fund it. This is actually healthy friction. It forces the honest question: *Do we actually value this commons enough to pay for its care?* Some communities discover they don't. Better to know that early.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Detroit Community Gardens Coalition (USA).** After years of volunteer burnout, they established paid Garden Steward roles—neighborhood coordinators who hold organizing meetings, coordinate maintenance schedules, and mentor new gardeners. Compensation comes from a blend of member dues, municipal contracts (cities pay for green infrastructure), and foundation grants. Stewards earn $18,000-$24,000 annually. The result: gardens stayed open through pandemic disruptions when purely volunteer networks collapsed elsewhere. Knowledge transfer accelerated. More parents and working-class people stepped into stewardship.

**Cohousing communities in Scandinavia (Denmark, Sweden).** Cooperative housing clusters (typically 15-30 households) formalize "community coordinator" roles paid from resident fees. The coordinator manages common meals, maintenance scheduling, guest policies, conflict resolution. Annual compensation ranges from $8,000-$15,000 depending on cluster size and complexity. This is embedded in their legal governing documents. The effect: cohousing survives its first five years (the vulnerable phase) instead of dissolving into isolated nuclear families. Intergenerational connection deepens because someone is paid to tend it.

**Bangalore Slum Sanitation Collective (India).** Community toilets in informal settlements required someone to monitor, clean, collect micro-fees, and handle disputes daily. Rather than leaving this to unpaid "volunteers," the collective established a wage for Sanitation Stewards—roughly $4-6 daily, paid from a small per-use fee. This recognizes that care work is work. Facility use increased; maintenance standards rose; women's safety improved because the steward was a trusted community presence. The model has scaled to 15+ sites.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence systems change stewardship in subtle ways. Maintenance monitoring can now be partly automated—sensors alert stewards to infrastructure problems, reducing reactive crisis-response. This shifts steward work upstream: less firefighting, more anticipation and relationship-building.

But here's the tension: *as operational tasks become more automated, stewardship becomes more relational and political.* A sensor tells you the pipes are leaking. A steward decides how to communicate it, who should fix it, whether equipment needs replacing, and how to involve community in the decision. These are irreducibly human calls rooted in judgment, trust, and accountability.

This makes the case for compensation *stronger*, not weaker. Stewards become more valuable as knowledge-holders and judgment-makers, not less. Their role shifts from maintenance technician toward what might be called "relational infrastructure"—they hold the commons together in its social dimension while technical systems handle sensing and monitoring.

Distributed intelligence also enables new forms of commons governance documentation. Decision-making can be recorded, tracked, and made transparent across larger, more complex commons networks. But someone must curate that infrastructure, teach people to use it, decide what transparency looks like. That's stewardship work too—now spanning digital and physical space.

The risk: *as technical systems handle more operations, commons organizations can be tempted to cut steward compensation.* Don't. The work doesn't disappear; it transforms. Pay for the new skills you need.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

- Stewards can name their compensation without shame or apology. ("I'm paid $20,000 a year to coordinate governance.")
- New people join stewardship roles who wouldn't have (parents, non-wealthy people, people of color who've been excluded from "volunteer" culture).
- Stewards report feeling trusted to do the work thoughtfully rather than frantically.
- Conflict gets addressed before it hardens into resentment because someone is paid to notice and respond.
- The commons survives staff transition—knowledge is preserved even when individuals move on.

**Signs of decay:**

- Stewards speak in hushed tones about their compensation or apologize for accepting it.
- Stewardship roles cluster around people with outside wealth or privilege.
- Stewards report chronic stress, time poverty, or feeling blamed when things go wrong.
- Compensation shrinks whenever finances tighten (stewards are the first cut).
- Leadership turns over frequently; knowledge walks out the door with each departing person.
- New members ask "Who actually runs this?" and no one answers clearly.

**Diagnostic question:** *Could a person with no other income, living alone at regional living wage standards, sustain themselves in a stewardship role for three years while developing real competence?*

If the answer is no, your stewardship is still built on hidden extraction. If yes—you've begun to honor the commons as a livelihood, not just a cause.
