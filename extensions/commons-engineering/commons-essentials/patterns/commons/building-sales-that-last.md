### Section 1: Context

The dominant sales paradigm treats buyers as conversion targets to be moved through a funnel. Beneath this lies a gravitational pull toward extraction: maximize deal size, shorten sales cycle, increase close rate, optimize each funnel stage. Yet this creates misalignment. Buyers who feel manipulated churn. Deals closed under pressure generate poor implementations and low retention. Salespeople burn out chasing quotas disconnected from actual customer fit. This cluster of 81 patterns—from funnel optimization to buyer personas to commission structures—all orbit the same magnetic core: how to move prospects into revenue. The Orbit 1 principles reveal a deeper possibility: what if sales were redesigned as reciprocal flow, where transparency about constraints creates fairness, and fairness creates durable value?

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Extraction (converting prospects into revenue) vs. Stewardship (building durable relationships where both parties thrive).**

The core tension: Sales exists to generate revenue, yet revenue extracted through misalignment or manipulation becomes fragile. Buyers feel they were pressured into a poor decision; sellers hit quota but generate churn; the relationship deteriorates. Simultaneously, sales teams face real incentive misalignment—commission structures reward closing deals regardless of fit; pipelines hide actual buyer readiness; personas reduce humans to stereotypes; negotiation becomes positional rather than integrative. The system optimizes for conversion rate and deal velocity at the cost of relationship health, customer lifetime value, and seller integrity. Yet every agent in this system—buyer, seller, manager, organisation—faces genuine constraints: real budget limits, real timeline pressures, real competitive threats. The forces are not evil; they are systemically misaligned.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement Transparent Threshold Negotiation, a pattern that replaces funnel optimization with reciprocal inquiry and explicit boundary-setting. The mechanism operates in four interlocking practices:

1. **Mutual Discovery, Not Extraction**: Before any pitch, both buyer and seller answer together: What is the actual problem? What does success look like? What constraints are non-negotiable (budget, timeline, internal politics, risk tolerance)? The seller's role shifts from persuasion to diagnosis—asking questions that help the buyer think clearly, even if that reveals the deal is not a fit. This embodies Truth-Seeking: visibility about misalignment early saves everyone time and trust.

2. **Fair Negotiation Framework**: Rather than positional haggling, establish integrative negotiation grounded in stated constraints. If the buyer has a hard budget ceiling, explore scope, timeline, or implementation approach instead of fighting over price. If the seller has margin thresholds, make them explicit. Commission structures should reward deals that stick (retention, expansion) not just close rate. This aligns incentives toward Cooperative Surplus: both parties design for outcomes where both win.

3. **Explicit Reciprocity Covenants**: Formalize expectations in writing—not just contract terms, but relational ones. "You will get X value if you invest Y effort in implementation." "We will proactively flag if we cannot deliver." "You agree to give feedback even if you don't renew." These are not legal weapons but transparency anchors—making the fairness agreement visible, renegotiable, and testable. This embodies Reciprocity: contributions are matched over time because both parties can see the exchange.

4. **Adaptive Cycle Review Points**: Build in scheduled renegotiation moments (quarterly or at defined milestones) where both parties assess: Is the value we predicted actually arriving? Do we need to adjust scope, support, or terms? Have circumstances changed that require new agreements? This creates Information Shapes Systems: the relationship evolves based on shared reality, not fixed assumptions.

The result: Revenue that persists because the relationship is built on fairness, not desperation. Customer lifetime value that compounds because retention is designed in. Sales teams whose compensation rewards deals that stick, not deals that break. Buyers who feel respected rather than manipulated. This pattern does not eliminate negotiation or self-interest; it channels them toward Alignment where both parties' incentives point toward sustainable value creation.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

The shift from extraction to stewardship begins with a single behavioral change: *ask before you solve*. Here's how to cultivate it:

**Start with shared diagnosis.** In your first real conversation, spend 60% of the time listening. Map the buyer's actual constraints—not their stated budget, but their real ability to act. Ask: "If we do nothing, what happens in six months?" Listen for the gap between their stated need and their actual urgency. Most sales cycles fail because sellers never confirm this gap is real for the buyer.

**Name the elephant.** Early in the conversation, say something like: "I want to be direct. I make money when you buy. You want to avoid a bad decision. So let's both put that on the table. My job is to tell you honestly whether we're a fit, not to convince you we are." This single act—naming the misaligned incentive—often dissolves it.

**Co-create the decision framework.** Don't present a proposal and wait for objections. Instead, ask: "What would you need to see to confidently say yes? What would make you say no?" Write these down together. Now you're not arguing about your solution; you're both testing it against shared criteria. When you present, frame it against *their* framework, not your value prop.

**Set explicit stop-gates.** Before moving forward, agree on the signal that means "this isn't working." Something like: "If by next Friday we can't align on timeline, we pause." This removes the pressure to keep hoping and lets you both exit cleanly if it's not a fit.

**Build implementation partnership into the deal.** The close isn't when they sign. It's when they see the first real result. Include in your proposal a 30-day "success threshold"—what has to happen for them to feel the deal was worth it. Make your commission contingent partly on them hitting this, not just on signature.

These practices feel slower upfront. They are. But they compress the actual selling cycle because you're not cycling through objections or buyers having buyer's remorse.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, several things begin to flourish:

**Buyer confidence compounds.** Prospects who feel genuinely understood—and who see you willing to walk away from bad fits—begin to trust you faster. Referrals increase because they know you won't sell their colleagues something that doesn't fit. Your reputation shifts from "closer" to "honest broker." This becomes a moat competitors can't replicate with a better pitch.

**Implementation actually works.** Buyers who chose you because it solved a real problem, with clear eyes on the cost and timeline, execute differently. They budget for adoption. They staff the project. They hold themselves accountable. Your success rates climb, not because your product improved, but because you sold to the right people for the right reasons.

**Your team's rhythm improves.** Salespeople who operate this way report lower burnout. You're not constantly fighting objections or managing buyer remorse. You're building on honesty. The quota pressure doesn't disappear, but it's paired with a clearer sense of when you're genuinely helping versus when you should step back.

**The risks, if not tended:** If you use this pattern as a *tactic*—if you ask about constraints but then ignore them to hit quota—trust collapses faster than before. Transparency can become manipulation if misaligned incentives are still in place (e.g., you're still paid only on signature, not on customer success). Without reshaping how sales is measured, the pattern inverts into a smoother extraction machine. The other risk: you may close fewer deals in the short term as you qualify out poor fits. If your business model depends on volume over value, this pattern may not be viable without restructuring your entire go-to-market.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Salesforce and enterprise software adoption, 2010s:** When Salesforce moved from a licensing model to a customer success model, top sellers began asking buyers "What problem are you trying to solve that you can't solve now?" instead of leading with feature tours. This simple shift—rooted in the pattern above—increased adoption rates and reduced churn by 30%. The sales conversations became shorter but stickier.

**Aga Khan Development Network (health systems in rural East Africa):** When rolling out maternal health programs across multiple countries, the network's local teams discovered that selling the program to ministry officials required a reversal: instead of presenting the solution, they asked, "What gaps do you see in maternal outcomes? What would you need to believe this could work here?" This co-creation approach meant governments owned the implementation, not the network. Programs stuck because they were locally designed, not imposed. The "sale" took longer, but sustainability doubled.

**Patagonia's retail model:** Patagonia sales staff are trained to ask customers, "Do you really need this?" and to suggest cheaper or secondhand alternatives when appropriate. This sounds like anti-sales. Instead, it builds fierce loyalty. Customers who bought because Patagonia was honest about their actual needs become repeat buyers and advocates. The pattern treats the sale as the beginning of stewardship, not the end of a transaction.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an AI-era sales environment, this pattern becomes both more necessary and more powerful.

AI systems are becoming your competitors in one direction: they can identify buyer intent at scale, personalize messaging, and optimize for conversion with inhuman speed. Extraction-based sales—the funnel, the playbook, the objection-handler script—are increasingly automated. Buyers know they're being targeted by algorithms.

What AI cannot do well is *genuine reciprocal inquiry*. It can mimic it, but buyers sense the difference. The sales function that survives and thrives will be the one that leans into what humans do better: honest sense-making under uncertainty, admitting what you don't know, and building trust through transparency.

This pattern also scales differently now. Instead of relying on individual salesperson skill, you can embed the framework into CRM logic, deal structures, and team processes. A shared diagnostic template, a success-threshold field, and a go/no-go gate become part of the workflow. AI can help surface the buyer's real constraints from conversation data; the salesperson uses that intelligence to ask better questions, not to close more aggressively.

The pattern also implies a shift in sales metrics. In a cognitive era where information flows freely, you optimize for *buyer fit* and *implementation velocity*, not pipeline volume. This makes the role more strategic and, paradoxically, more human.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

- Your salespeople can articulate the buyer's problem back to them in the buyer's own language, often before the pitch.
- Deals that close have a clear "why" statement: why the buyer chose you, why now, why it matters. Not just "they signed the contract."
- Buyer remorse or implementation friction is rare. When it happens, it's acknowledged early and resolved quickly.
- Your sales cycle has a clear "point of decision"—a moment when buyer and seller jointly say "go" or "no-go." Not endless stalling.
- Referrals increase because past buyers trust you enough to send peers.
- Your churn rate declines, especially early-stage churn (first 90 days).
- Salespeople talk about their "toughest no" as proudly as their biggest yes.

**Signs of decay:**

- Deals linger in late pipeline stages. Buyers are "interested" but not moving. (Often a sign you never confirmed a real problem.)
- Salespeople are cycling through the same objections repeatedly with the same buyer. (Usually means you missed a constraint.)
- Buyer remorse or post-sale friction is common. Buyers feel they were sold something, not that they chose it.
- Your sales team's language shifts back to "moving" prospects, "overcoming resistance," "staying in the deal."
- Referrals dry up; past customers are quiet.
- Quota pressure returns as the primary motivation; honesty is abandoned during crunch time.

**A diagnostic question:** If you walked away from a deal today because it wasn't a fit, could you explain why to both the prospect and your own leadership without defensiveness? If the answer is no, the pattern has decayed. Extraction pressure is back.
