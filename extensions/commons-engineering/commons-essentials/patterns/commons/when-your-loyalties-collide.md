### Section 1: Context

Every commons steward carries multiple memberships: family, institution, conviction, livelihood, community. These memberships are not corruptions of each other—they are the texture of human existence. The problem emerges when a steward pretends they have no stake in a decision while their hidden loyalties shape outcomes. In commons-based governance, where distributed authority replaces central command, the relational field is hyper-sensitive: subtle bias contaminates faster than in hierarchies because trust is the only legitimacy. Lineage to Conflict Evolves Systems (all systems generate conflict; the response is structured acknowledgment, not denial), Truth-Seeking Sustains Systems (systems that hide their own state degrade faster), and Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (cooperative systems depend on the perception that rules are applied consistently, not hidden).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Personal interest vs. collective integrity.**

Conflicts of interest are universal and inevitable. A board member's sibling works for a vendor. A community leader receives benefits from a decision they're voting on. A staff member's financial survival depends on a grant that would privilege certain community members. The living tension: suppressing these conflicts creates shadow governance (decisions made based on unstated interests), while ignoring them invites capture (the person with hidden stakes becomes an invisible arbiter). The deeper problem: pretending to neutrality when you have stakes destroys reciprocity. When others discover your unstated interest—and they will—trust doesn't recover. The competing loyalties themselves are not the poison; the dishonesty about them is. Without a structured way to name and navigate competing commitments, stewards either become corrupted in silence or burn out trying to be superhuman.

---

### Section 3: Solution

This pattern creates a three-layer governance practice:

**Layer 1: Systematic Disclosure.** Before participating in any significant decision, each steward (board member, coordinator, elder, facilitator) conducts a personal conflict inventory: What financial interests do I have? What family or relational stakes? What ideological commitments that might bias judgment? What competing organizational roles? This inventory is not a one-time confession but a living practice—revisited quarterly or when roles shift. Disclose findings to the relevant decision body before participation, not after consequences emerge.

**Layer 2: Structural Recusal.** Once conflicts are named, the steward does not pretend neutrality through heroic self-control. Instead, they step back: from that vote, that conversation, that budget decision. Recusal is not punishment—it is stewardship. The person with the stake leaves the room. This preserves two things: (1) the integrity of the decision (made by those without that particular interest), and (2) the integrity of the relationship (no hidden scorekeeping about whose interests were actually served).

**Layer 3: Transparent Management Structures.** Some conflicts cannot be resolved by stepping back—a founder still loves the organization, a parent still cares about their child's future. In these cases, create explicit structural buffers: external decision-makers for certain categories, rotating authority, clear rules about information access, public documentation of who decided what and why. The conflict doesn't disappear; it becomes visible and managed, not hidden.

The pattern works because it treats competing loyalties as inevitable design features, not moral failings. It asks: How do we govern well *knowing* people have stakes? Not: How do we find people with no stakes (impossible) or how do we hide the stakes we have (corrosive).

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with a personal audit—not once, but seasonally. Create a simple template: financial stakes, relational ties, ideological commitments, competing organizational roles, future opportunities that might be affected. The honesty here matters more than perfection. You're not trying to be neutral (impossible) but rather visible.

Then build the disclosure practice into your rhythm. Before major decisions, stewards name their interests aloud. Not apologetically. Not as disqualification. Simply: "I have a stake here. My cousin uses this service. I benefit if we choose this path. Here's what I see, and here's what I might be blind to." This transparency takes three minutes and transforms how others listen.

Create a recusal protocol—clear enough that stepping aside doesn't feel like shame. Sometimes you recuse yourself entirely from a decision. Sometimes you participate in discussion but not voting. Sometimes you stay present but flag your bias explicitly. The key: the community decides what's appropriate, not just the conflicted person.

For trickier cases, establish a "conflict committee"—trusted stewards who help navigate gray zones. They're not judges but counselors. They ask: *Is this conflict manageable through disclosure? Does it require recusal? Should we restructure the decision to avoid it altogether?*

Document patterns. After six months, review what conflicts appeared, how they were handled, what you learned. This isn't punishment; it's pattern recognition. You'll notice that the same tensions recur (budget allocation, hiring, vendor selection). Use that data to redesign those decisions so conflicts are less likely to arise.

Finally, normalize the awkwardness. Conflicts of interest are not scandals waiting to happen—they're ordinary friction in any living system. When stewards speak them regularly and unselfconsciously, the whole commons breathes easier. People can trust what's being decided because they understand the full landscape of interests shaping it.

### Section 5: Consequences

When you name loyalties clearly, trust deepens in ways that seem counterintuitive. People don't trust perfect neutrality (they know it's false); they trust transparency. A steward who says "I'm affected by this, and here's how" becomes more trustworthy, not less, because others can factor that bias into their listening.

The commons flourishes when decisions are made with full knowledge of who benefits and why. Communities become more resilient because they're not relying on stewards to be selfless—they're building systems where good decisions emerge even when people have real stakes. Energy that used to go into hidden maneuvering can go toward actual work.

But the risks are real. Without careful practice, disclosure becomes performative—people name a conflict and then push their preferred outcome anyway, hiding behind transparency theater. The practice must be paired with genuine willingness to step aside. Without that muscle, you get cynicism.

There's also the risk of paralysis. If stewards become hypervigilant about conflicts, afraid of any bias, they withdraw from participation entirely. That's not the goal. The goal is *visible participation*—you're here, you matter, and here's the full picture.

Relationship strain can emerge too. Naming loyalties publicly sometimes surfaces ruptures people wanted to keep private. If disclosure becomes a form of judgment ("You're too invested in this"), the practice collapses. You need enough maturity in your community to distinguish between "I have a stake" and "I'm corrupt."

The deepest risk: if your commons is embedded in an extractive system, transparency about conflicts of interest can actually put stewards at risk. In those contexts, you need protection structures first.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Mondragon Cooperatives (Basque region, Spain).** For decades, worker-owners have practiced systematic disclosure in governance councils. Before cooperative-wide decisions about strategy or investment, members declare their departmental interests, family ties, and ideological positions. The practice is so embedded that it's almost invisible—but it's why these organizations have navigated radical transitions (shift to digital manufacturing, expansion across continents) without the corruption and feuding that typically accompany such changes. Transparency isn't optional; it's foundational.

**The Karanga Traditional Council (Zimbabwe).** In this system of land governance, elders practice "mbira disclosure"—a rhythmic naming of interests and commitments before meetings. An elder might say: "I have three sons who depend on the grazing lands we're discussing. I also have a vow to the ancestors to protect those lands. I will participate in this council, and I ask you to hold me accountable to both loyalties." The practice acknowledges that interests aren't problems to hide but realities to navigate collectively.

**The Quaker Decision-Making Circle (ongoing, multiple countries).** Quakers have long practiced "conflict of interest minuting"—recording not just decisions but the disclosed stakes that shaped them. When someone brings a proposal, others ask: "What's your interest in this outcome?" The asker answers plainly. This creates a record that future communities can read and learn from. It assumes that good decisions aren't made by people without stakes but by people who own their stakes clearly.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

When intelligence is distributed—across teams, across AI systems, across geographies—the old model of central oversight collapses. You can't have one person verify everyone's conflicts. Instead, the system itself has to be transparent about where bias might live.

AI systems have their own "conflicts of interest"—they're trained on data that embeds preferences, they're designed by humans with stakes, they're deployed in contexts where their outputs benefit some people more than others. The pattern inverts: instead of people disclosing their stakes to AI systems, we need AI systems to disclose their design biases and training premises to the humans using them. "This model was trained on data from wealthy countries. Here's where it might fail in yours."

In commons that use distributed ledgers or public data systems, every transaction can theoretically be transparent. This creates a new temptation: the tyranny of perfect visibility, where every interest is exposed and people lose privacy. The pattern needs to evolve: *strategic* transparency, where we disclose what matters for collective decisions but preserve intimacy where it belongs.

The cognitive shift: from "Who do we trust?" to "How do we build systems where trust doesn't depend on anyone being selfless?" With AI as co-steward, that becomes even more urgent. A biased algorithm that hides its bias is worse than a biased human who admits it. The pattern becomes essential infrastructure for hybrid human-machine commons.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

Stewards name their conflicts without defensiveness. A board member says, "I own stock in that company" the way they'd say "I'm from that neighborhood"—as information, not confession. Discomfort has given way to matter-of-factness.

After disclosure, decisions noticeably *shift*. Not always—sometimes the conflicted steward's view was right all along—but sometimes the group's judgment improves because they're seeing the full picture. You can track this: "We disclosed X interest, and it changed how we decided."

Recusals happen without shame. Someone steps away from a vote, and nobody treats it as scandal. It's treated as stewardship—someone protecting the commons's integrity.

People feel safer telling the truth about future loyalties too. "I'm considering a job that might affect my judgment on this" becomes something you can say early, not hide until it explodes.

**Signs of decay:**

Disclosure becomes ritual without substance. People list conflicts mechanically, then ignore them. The steward with a clear financial stake votes exactly as you'd predict, and no one remarks on it.

Conflicts get weaponized. Someone raises a conflict not to improve decisions but to disqualify a rival: "You can't speak to this; you're too invested." The practice becomes a way to silence.

Stewards stop being honest. They sense that naming conflicts might cost them influence, so they keep quiet, and shadow governance returns.

Recusal becomes punishment. If stepping aside means you lose voice in the community, people stop doing it. The practice becomes too costly.

**Diagnostic question:**

If a steward with a clear conflict of interest proposed an unpopular decision that served the commons better than the popular alternative, would they be able to advocate for it? Or would their conflict make their voice unheard? If the latter, your practice has become purity theater, not governance.
