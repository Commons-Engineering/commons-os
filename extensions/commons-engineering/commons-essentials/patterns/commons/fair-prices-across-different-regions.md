### Section 1: Context

Multi-region enterprises face a permanent tension: markets differ in purchasing power, regulation, competition, and cost structures. Responding to these differences requires local pricing autonomy. Yet unchecked variance creates two commons failures — arbitrary unfairness (same customer, different price) and fragmentation (regional offices pursuing incompatible strategies). This pattern lives in the lineage of Decide at the Lowest Level (decisions made at the lowest competent level), Polycentric Organisation (authority distributed across nested centres), and Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (the perception that exchange rules are just). The commons here is transparent, stewarded pricing that serves regional adaptation without eroding system coherence.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Local autonomy & market responsiveness vs. system coherence & fair access.**

The living conflict: regional teams need authority to set prices responsive to local conditions. Yet without visibility and governance, variance drifts into three corrosive forms: (1) Unjustifiable arbitrage — price differences that serve no legitimate regional need, eroding customer trust; (2) Hidden variance — pricing decisions made in isolation, fragmenting brand coherence and creating compliance risk; (3) Centralized rigidity — pricing set at head office, blind to regional realities, forcing teams into workarounds or margin collapse. Each response without the others fails. Local autonomy without governance becomes anarchic. Central governance without local input becomes brittle. Transparency without approval authority becomes process theatre. The challenge is stewarding variance — making it visible, justifying it, distributing approval authority by decision scale, and auditing it as ongoing feedback.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish a Pricing Governance Stewardship system with three nested layers of authority and visibility:

**Layer 1: Transparent Documentation (All Decisions).** Every regional price variance must be documented with three elements: (1) the regional condition triggering it (purchasing power, regulatory requirement, cost structure, competitive pressure), (2) the decision rule (e.g., 'prices scale to 60% of purchasing power parity; regulatory minimums enforced separately'), (3) the approval authority and date. Documentation is not confidential — it is shared across regions to surface patterns and learning. This satisfies Truth-Seeking Sustains Systems and treats pricing variance as information about the system.

**Layer 2: Subsidiarity-Scaled Approval.** Establish clear thresholds: (a) routine variance within documented regional rules requires no escalation (Subsidiarity); (b) variance exceeding the rule or introducing a new justification requires regional leadership approval; (c) variance creating price parity violations or regulatory exposure requires governance board approval. This distributes authority to the competent level — local teams decide within their scope, escalation occurs only when coherence or compliance is at risk.

**Layer 3: Continuous Audit Cycles.** Quarterly, audit regional pricing against three standards: (1) **Justification coherence** — does each variance align with its documented reason, or has the reason changed without updating the rule? (2) **Fairness consistency** — do customers in similar regional conditions receive similar treatment? (3) **Arbitrage detection** — are price gaps creating unintended incentives for resale or channel-shifting? Audit findings feed back into governance board review, updating decision rules and thresholds. This embodies Information Shapes Systems.

The pattern does not eliminate variance — it stewards it. Regional autonomy is preserved because teams make decisions within their competence. System coherence is preserved because variance is visible, justified, and subject to feedback. Fairness is preserved because the rules themselves, not arbitrary assertion, determine pricing.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by creating a **Regional Pricing Registry**—a living document (spreadsheet, database, or wiki) that maps every product or service tier against each region, paired with the documented reason for any variance. This isn't bureaucracy; it's transparency made visible. Each entry answers: *Why does this cost that here?*

The registry becomes the spine of your system. Assign a **Pricing Steward** (or small council) with rotating regional representation—someone who understands both the enterprise and the local market. Their job: review new pricing proposals before they go live, asking three questions: (1) Does the justification match our principles? (2) Would a customer in another region understand why they pay differently? (3) Does this decision fragment our brand or strengthen it?

Build a **quarterly pricing audit cycle**. Gather regional teams, surface variance patterns, and ask collectively: which differences serve real needs, and which have drifted into opacity? This isn't about eliminating all difference—it's about making difference legible and defensible.

Create **clear escalation rules**. Small adjustments (under 5% variance from baseline) might be approved by regional leads. Larger shifts or category-wide changes go to the stewardship council. The threshold matters less than consistency: everyone knows the bar.

Document the **decision rules themselves**—not just individual decisions. For instance: "In low-purchasing-power regions, we scale prices to 60% of headquarters baseline, proportional to median household income." Publish these rules (at least internally, often to customers). Rules-based pricing feels fair because it removes the appearance of arbitrary judgment.

Finally, **close the feedback loop**. Create a channel for customers and frontline staff to flag pricing that feels unjust. Not every complaint changes policy, but every complaint gets a documented response. This accountability transforms the registry from an internal document into a living covenant with your market.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What Flourishes:** Pricing governance done well creates three forms of vitality. Trust deepens because customers see consistency and can understand price differences as legitimate adaptations, not hidden extraction. Regional teams gain real autonomy—they respond to local markets, not through shadow deals, but through transparent, defensible decisions. And the enterprise itself becomes more coherent: brand promise doesn't shatter across geographies; instead, it bends intelligently to local conditions while maintaining a recognizable core.

**Risks That Emerge:** If not tended, three pathologies can grow. *Rigidity*: governance systems can harden into bureaucratic gates that slow legitimate local response. Stewards focused on control rather than cultivation will kill the flexibility that made local autonomy valuable in the first place. *Window dressing*: documented reasons that are hollow—"competitive pressure" without specifics—create the appearance of governance without its substance, breeding cynicism. *Invisibility collapse*: if the registry becomes internal-only and never reaches customers, variance still erodes trust, just slower. Customers discover through social channels that their neighbor paid less, and transparency becomes retroactive damage control.

The deepest risk: forgetting that pricing governance serves people, not systems. Perfecting the registry while regional teams feel disempowered, or while customer anger about price differences goes unheard, means you've optimized process at the expense of the commons you meant to protect.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Pharmaceutical pricing across GTIN-tracked markets:** Novartis and similar enterprises use tiered pricing in high-income versus middle-income regions, documented publicly through regulatory filings and internal governance boards. Variance reflects genuine cost structures and willingness-to-pay, published so both customers and regulators understand the logic. This prevents the rage that emerges when patients discover identical drugs cost 10x more in one country than another with no justification given.

**Cooperative grocery networks in the US and Scandinavia:** The Coop Italia network and Swedish Coop Federation manage pricing across regions with documented cost-pass-throughs (transport, local supplier relationships, labor costs). Quarterly member assemblies review regional pricing decisions; variance that can't be explained in simple terms gets flagged. Members see pricing not as opaque corporate extraction but as a collective decision negotiated transparently.

**Kopernik Indonesia's water pricing model:** In rural and semi-urban Indonesia, Kopernik manages affordable water access through a sliding-scale model tied to documented local capacity to pay and infrastructure costs. Pricing governance councils include community members, ensuring that variance serves equity, not profit maximization. The transparency is crucial: communities accept price differences because they helped design them and can verify the costs they reflect.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed ledgers change this pattern's possibilities and hazards. **Possibility:** machine learning can surface pricing variance patterns humans miss, flag unjustifiable inconsistencies in real-time, and simulate how proposed changes ripple across regions and customer segments. A smart pricing registry can learn from outcomes—which variances actually matched local conditions, which ones didn't—and refine decision rules autonomously.

**Hazard:** algorithmic pricing can obscure responsibility. When an AI system sets prices based on learned patterns, the "reason" becomes opaque even to the stewardship council. A neural net optimizes for margin and customer acquisition simultaneously across regions; the variance emerges, but no human can articulate why. This is opacity dressed in objectivity—worse than the original problem.

**The shift:** governance must evolve from documenting human decisions to auditing algorithmic ones. You need explainability tools that make machine-generated pricing legible to stewards, regional teams, and eventually customers. The registry becomes a space where humans and algorithms negotiate together: here's what the algorithm suggests based on patterns; here's what our principles require; here's the decision we make as humans, overriding the pattern where needed.

Distributed pricing (each region running its own pricing engine within guardrails) becomes more feasible with better transparency infrastructure, but requires *stronger* collective governance to prevent fragmentation.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of health:** The registry is actively used and regularly updated—not a dust-gathering artifact. Regional teams reference it when justifying new pricing; stewards cite it when approving changes. Customers encounter variance, ask why, and receive clear, consistent explanations grounded in documented reasoning. No major pricing decisions happen in darkness; even contentious decisions are transparent and defensible. The system bends to legitimate local needs without fragmenting brand coherence.

**Signs of decay:** The registry exists but goes unread; variance happens and no one can explain it. When customers ask why they pay differently, teams offer vague justifications or silence. Regional offices make pricing decisions without stewardship oversight; governance exists only in theory. Audit cycles happen but reveal nothing changes; the document has become theater. Trust erodes slowly as customers discover hidden variance through social channels.

**Diagnostic question:** If a customer from one region contacted someone in another and asked, "Why does this cost 30% more where I am?" could someone point to a specific, documented, honest reason that would make sense to them? If not, your governance isn't working—it's hiding. If yes, even if they disagree with the reason, you've preserved the commons of trust.
