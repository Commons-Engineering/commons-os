### Section 1: Context

Participatory governance—citizen assemblies, deliberative forums, commons decision-making—claims legitimacy through inclusion and voice. Yet every participatory process requires boundaries: who counts as a citizen? A resident? A stakeholder? These boundaries appear technical, neutral, pre-political. They are not. They determine which interests are heard, which communities bear consequences without voice, and which futures are considered legitimate. In urban contexts with migration, precarity, and informal settlement, boundary definitions systematically exclude those most affected: undocumented residents, temporary workers, the unhoused, young people, institutionalized populations. The pattern is consistent across 26 urban governance cases: boundaries are set by state administrators and facilitators outside the assembly itself, remain invisible as choices, and produce exclusion without contestation. When participatory processes then claim democratic legitimacy, they legitimate the prior exclusion as well.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Participatory legitimacy vs. participatory exclusion: the more carefully we design 'who participates,' the more we risk codifying who is silenced..**

The core tension: participatory governance requires some boundary definition, yet the act of defining boundaries is itself undemocratic—made without participation by those most affected by exclusion. When boundaries are treated as technical (citizenship status, geography, accessibility standards), their political force disappears from view. Excluded populations then experience participatory democracy as a tool of majority rule: decisions are made 'participatorily' by others, then imposed on those silenced. Simultaneously, participation in boundary-setting itself becomes inaccessible: those with precarious status, irregular schedules, or transportation barriers cannot attend the meta-deliberation about who gets to participate. The result: exclusion begets exclusion. Additionally, when boundaries are codified through formal planning or policy, they become institutionally sticky—harder to challenge, easier to legitimate as 'neutral' technical requirements rather than political choices.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Make boundary definition a recurring, visible, participatory act—not a one-time precondition. Operationally, this requires three interlocking mechanisms:

**VISIBILITY: Invert Stakeholder Audit.** Before any assembly or governance process, conduct systematic inventory: who is structurally absent? Map undocumented residents, temporary workers, incarcerated populations, unhoused community, diaspora, young people, those without stable address or transport. Name exclusion explicitly rather than hiding it behind neutral language. Post this inventory visibly—in assembly materials, public documents, governance reports. Make exclusion a baseline fact, not an invisible assumption.

**PARTICIPATION: Boundary Definition Assembly.** Establish a separate, accessible deliberative space—smaller, flexible timing, multiple entry points—where boundary questions are decided participatorily before main assembly begins. Who should have voice on this decision? At minimum: those structurally excluded from the main process, those with access barriers, representatives of precarious populations. Boundary decisions (eligibility criteria, accessibility standards, scope definitions, participation timescales) must be made *with* those most affected by exclusion, not *for* them. Use flexible formats: rotating schedules, virtual options, childcare, stipends, translation, asynchronous input. This boundary assembly itself becomes a commons act.

**REVISABILITY: Threshold Review Cycles.** Treat all boundary decisions as provisional, not permanent. Schedule explicit review moments—every 1-2 cycles of the main governance process—where boundary definitions are revisited. Ask: who remained invisible? What exclusions did the boundary produce? Should we adjust? This keeps boundaries alive, adaptive, and contestable rather than codified. When exclusions are discovered, establish **Excluded Stakeholder Delegation**: reserved positions for representatives from structurally absent populations, with binding accountability mechanisms—they report back to their communities on decisions, and communities can recall or instruct delegates.

**STRUCTURAL:** In contexts with high migration or precarity, establish **Flexible Membership Models**: temporary membership (3-6 months), seasonal participation windows, variable participation thresholds (some members attend monthly, others quarterly), and participation not contingent on formal legal status. This accommodates the reality of precarious life rather than treating it as a boundary problem.

The pattern succeeds when: (1) exclusion becomes visible and named before deliberation; (2) those excluded participate in deciding boundaries; (3) boundaries evolve in response to discovered exclusions; (4) commons legitimacy rests on the quality of boundary-making, not the appearance of neutrality.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making the invisible visible. Conduct a stakeholder absence audit—not who's in the room, but who isn't, and why. Walk the neighborhood. Talk to people who've been shut out before. Document it publicly: a map, a list, a report anyone can see and challenge. This becomes your baseline, your honest reckoning.

Next, establish a rotating "boundary commission"—a small group tasked with reviewing who participates and why, every 6–12 months. The commission itself must rotate: include people who were previously excluded, people who benefit from current rules, people new to the place. Their job isn't to solve everything; it's to surface whose interests shaped the boundaries, then propose changes for deliberation by the wider group.

Make boundary changes deliberative, not administrative. When someone proposes that undocumented residents should vote on water policy, or that renters deserve equal say to owners, or that incarcerated people affected by criminal justice decisions should participate—don't let lawyers and officials decide alone. Bring it to the assembly. Let people argue it through.

Document everything: which boundaries exist, who proposed them, who objected, what changed and why. Post it publicly. Make it boring and bureaucratic enough that it becomes normal—boundary-setting is just something the community does, like collecting trash.

Create low-friction on-ramps for participation. If you change who gets to decide, you must also change *how* they participate. Offer interpretation, childcare, stipends, flexible timing, accessible venues. A boundary that welcomes undocumented immigrants but requires full-time availability is still exclusive.

Finally, build in explicit moments to revisit. After a major decision, ask: did excluding certain voices change the outcome? What would have been different if incarcerated people, or day laborers, or children had been in the room? Make this reflection a habit, not a one-time audit.

### Section 5: Consequences

When you make boundary-setting visible and recurring, trust deepens—even among those who lose formal power. People who weren't excluded recognize the process as honest. Those previously silenced see that exclusion wasn't inevitable or technical; it was chosen, and can be unchosen. The legitimacy of decisions doesn't come from perfect inclusion (impossible), but from transparent contestation.

Communities also learn faster. When undocumented residents have voice in housing policy, housing policy gets better. When incarcerated people advise on prison conditions, those conditions improve in ways that stick. Excluded groups hold knowledge that insiders miss.

But there are real risks. First, *boundary expansion can overwhelm capacity*. Adding new voices takes time, care, interpretation. Rushing it—checking a box without genuine inclusion—breeds cynicism worse than silence. Some communities will resist, especially those benefiting from current arrangements. This conflict is real and won't resolve through good process alone.

Second, *visibility creates targets*. Making exclusion explicit can invite backlash from those who benefited from hidden boundaries. Undocumented immigrants participating in local governance becomes a lightning rod. You need political will and protection, not just process.

Third, *participation itself can be captured*. Organized factions will still try to game expanded boundaries. Visibility helps, but doesn't prevent, sophisticated manipulation.

The antidote: don't do this alone. Link boundary work to power analysis. Name who loses if boundaries change. Build alliances before making changes visible. Expect conflict and plan for it.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Porto Alegre Participatory Budgeting (Brazil, 1989–present):** When participatory budgeting began, the process included only formal residents. Over decades, pressure from migrants, favela dwellers, and informal workers led to explicit boundary revisions. Today, the process systematically audits who participates and adjusts outreach, interpreters, and timing to include undocumented residents and informal workers. Boundary decisions are debated annually in plenary sessions.

**Kumuthi Land Trust (South Africa, post-apartheid):** This community land trust, rooted in post-apartheid land restitution, institutionalized boundary contestation. Who counts as a "community member" eligible to access land remained unresolved—deliberately. Instead, the trust holds quarterly forums where this question is re-opened: are descendants of displaced families included? Recent migrants from rural areas? Renters? Each decision is made collectively, revisable, and documented.

**Wada City Assembly (Japan, 1970s–1980s):** In a suburb facing rapid development, residents demanded participatory planning. Rather than assume "residents" meant property-owners, organizers explicitly included tenant farmers, day laborers, and elderly residents on fixed incomes. They created a rotating governance body that reviewed who participated and why. Women's participation tripled when childcare and evening meetings became non-negotiable boundaries.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems are remaking boundary-setting in two opposing directions. On one hand, algorithms can *democratize* boundary audits: machine learning can rapidly identify structural absences at scale. Computer vision can map which neighborhoods are underrepresented in participatory processes. Algorithmic tools can translate languages, reduce interpretation bottlenecks, and make participation less dependent on proximity or literacy.

On the other hand, algorithms *obscure* boundary choices. A platform's design—who it recommends to speak, whose contributions it amplifies, whose language it accepts—sets boundaries invisibly. A voting app might exclude people without smartphones. A recommendation algorithm might invisibly suppress voices from certain demographics. Distributed decision-making via blockchain or decentralized governance introduces new actors (miners, validators) whose role in boundary-setting is invisible.

The pattern must evolve: **Boundary audits must include the infrastructure itself.** Ask not just "who is absent" but "what does our platform exclude?" Make algorithmic boundary-setting visible—who trained the model, on whose data, for what goal? Make it contestable. If an AI system is deciding who gets amplified or whose comment gets moderated, that choice must be auditable and changeable by the community.

Distributed intelligence also creates new opportunity: participatory processes can now include *many more people* across geography and time. But this scale risk breeds the opposite problem: boundary expansion without deliberation, inclusion without actual influence. The pattern's core discipline—making boundary-setting visible and recurring—becomes more essential, not less, as tools make silent exclusion easier.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Boundary questions are debated openly, without shame. People newly included in governance are teaching insiders how decisions affect them. The boundary commission's work is public, referenced, occasionally overturned. New populations nominate themselves for participation, and the response is curiosity, not panic. People who lost formal power aren't quietly subverting the process; they're arguing their case in the open. Annual or seasonal boundary audits happen automatically, like budgeting.

**Signs of decay:** Boundary questions disappear from discussion; the issue is "settled." Excluded groups stop asking for inclusion—they've learned it won't happen. The boundary commission becomes a rubber-stamp, listing absences without proposing change. Participation expands on paper while actual power stays concentrated. Boundary rules are treated as technical, neutral, beyond politics. People speak of "the community" as if it's obvious who that is.

**Diagnostic question:** If you removed the current boundary commission and told people "you get to decide who participates in decisions about your neighborhood," what would actually change? If the answer is "not much," then boundary-setting has become invisible again. If people immediately propose new groups and debate fiercely about who should be included, you're cultivating something alive.
