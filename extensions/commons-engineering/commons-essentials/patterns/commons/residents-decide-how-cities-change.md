### Section 1: Context

Urban commons—parks, streets, water systems, digital services—are designed and governed in conditions of asymmetric information and power. Residents experience these systems daily but are excluded from their design; officials and technologists design without lived knowledge of place. When digital infrastructure arrives in cities, it typically appears as fait accompli: algorithmic systems for housing allocation, smart sensors in parks, data collection architectures determined by engineers. Meanwhile, participatory design has become a familiar language in progressive cities, yet many efforts remain extractive—consuming resident time without shifting actual authority, or conducting design in wealthy neighbourhoods while underestimating capacity in under-resourced communities. The tension is not between participation and expertise, but between top-down imposition and power-hoarding disguised as consultation.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Technocratic systems imposed from above vs. extractive co-design that consumes community time without shifting power.**

Three forces collide in urban governance: (1) Technical complexity that favours expert decision-making; (2) Democratic legitimacy that requires resident voice; (3) Scarcity of time and attention in communities already managing survival. Most efforts resolve this by adding a consultation layer—surveys, workshops, public comments—that consumes community time without distributing decision authority. Co-design in wealthy neighbourhoods generates sophisticated local knowledge; the same practice in low-income areas assumes capacity building must precede participation, delaying power-sharing indefinitely. Digital infrastructure amplifies the problem: algorithms that allocate housing or optimize traffic are invisible to those affected, yet participatory input arrives after design is crystallized. Trust is treated as a prerequisite for engagement, yet trust cannot be built without demonstrated power transfer. The deeper conflict: can residents and officials share epistemic and governance authority when structural incentives reward speed, certainty, and centralized control?

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish Participatory Stewardship Cycles—nested, recurring processes where residents co-design and govern public space and digital systems at the neighbourhood scale, with binding veto power and compensated time. The pattern operates across four interlocking movements:

**1. Trust Thresholds Before Co-Design.** Before inviting participation, establish measurable transparency signals: publish how previous decisions were made, show fulfilled commitments to prior feedback, acknowledge past harms. This is not consensus-building but honest accounting—residents assess whether officials actually share authority or merely consult. Trust is demonstrated through consistent action, not rhetoric.

**2. Participatory Lifecycle Governance.** For any urban system—digital or physical—conduct co-design at every stage: needs assessment (what problem are we solving?), procurement (who builds it?), implementation, operation, maintenance, upgrade, and eventual decommissioning. Residents hold binding veto power at each threshold. This prevents "participation theatre" at the front end followed by technocratic operation. It embeds ongoing feedback loops.

**3. Temporal Equity & Capacity Building.** Design participation explicitly addresses time poverty through: paid citizen designer roles; scheduled sessions accounting for work, care, school; provision of childcare, translation, and accessibility support; compensation for cognitive and emotional labour. Simultaneous with participation, embed learning—residents develop spatial literacy, systems thinking, and design fluency through doing real work on real briefs (not hypothetical exercises). This builds stewardship capacity that persists across cycles.

**4. Intercultural & Power-Aware Design.** Participation must actively address power asymmetries—historical mistrust of institutions, language barriers, cultural differences in how communities use space. Deploy value-sensitive design paired with explicit power redistribution: resident veto authority is non-negotiable; community organizations (not just individuals) hold decision seats; algorithmic systems are co-designed with those affected before deployment, not explained after.

**Implementation at Neighbourhood Scale.** Establish permanent Participatory Stewardship Ateliers—university-based or community-anchored studios where residents, design professionals, technologists, and officials meet regularly (not episodically) around shared briefs from the neighbourhood. These ateliers become institutional containers for iterative cycles: a park redesign becomes a vehicle for spatial literacy and trust-building; a digital mobility tool becomes a site for algorithmic transparency and collective decision-making. Results are bound into neighbourhood governance—through micro-jurisdictional experiment zones, binding design recommendations, and formal feedback loops to city-scale decision-making.

**Polycentric Scaling.** Neighbourhood cycles generate learning that flows upward: successful stewardship patterns are documented as replicable protocols (not recipes), tested in other neighbourhoods, and inform district and city-scale governance. This prevents both localism (each neighbourhood reinventing) and centralization (lessons locked in one place). The system is fractal—decision authority remains at the scale closest to the ground, but shared learning moves across scales.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start small and slow. Choose one neighbourhood block or a single city system—a park corner, a street's pedestrian safety, a housing allocation algorithm. Don't wait for perfect conditions; begin where residents already gather and where stakes are visible.

**Phase One: Transparency as Foundation (4-6 weeks).** Publish the thing as it works now. If it's a street, map who uses it, when, and for what. If it's an algorithm, show the rules it follows, the data it uses, who it helps and harms. Make this visible in the neighbourhood—not in PDFs, but on lampposts, in apps residents use, in formats elders and children can read. Answer questions in person. The goal isn't to persuade residents to love the current system; it's to create a shared fact-base so co-design conversations aren't starting from different planets.

**Phase Two: Resident Stewards as Decision-Makers (8-12 weeks).** Recruit 12-20 residents as paid stewards—$25-40/hour, meeting 4-6 hours weekly. Prioritize people who've lived through poor planning decisions: renters, disabled people, undocumented workers, families with kids, elders. Hire community organisers as facilitators—people with trust in the neighbourhood already. Give stewards authority: they approve or veto design proposals. They write the criteria others must meet. They decide what gets tried.

**Phase Three: Design in Cycles (12-16 weeks per cycle).** Present one design option at a time, built by stewards or co-designed with technicians/architects who answer to stewards. Stewards live with prototypes: a painted street redesign, a housing application process tested on phones, a park layout mocked up with rope and tarps. They gather feedback from residents in their networks. They iterate or reject. No "final" design—design as ongoing stewardship.

**Phase Four: Governance Through Operation (ongoing).** Stewards don't hand off when the thing launches. They monitor it monthly, adjust it, handle complaints, teach new people how it works. They have budget to make small changes without asking permission. They meet publicly so accountability stays visible. Rotate leadership so power doesn't concentrate.

The pattern works because it bends time toward understanding instead of extracting it. Stewards are paid to learn. They become fluent in the system's complexities. They build relationships with technicians and officials across the divide. And they have real veto power—the asymmetry doesn't disappear, but it flattens enough that trust can form.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What Flourishes.** When residents design the systems they live with, several things shift. First, solutions become context-specific and adaptive. A street redesign in a neighbourhood where many people are visually impaired looks different than one designed by someone who walks past. Housing algorithms built with residents caught in them capture real constraints that default systems miss. Second, residents develop what researchers call "institutional literacy"—they learn how systems actually work, where pressure points exist, what's possible. This literacy spreads. People become better advocates for themselves and others. Third, the system gains early-warning sensors. Stewards catch problems neighbours report before they become crises. Fourth, there's a dignity shift. Being asked to decide, being paid for your time, having veto power—these aren't small. People report feeling heard and capable.

**What Risks Emerge.** The pattern can become performative if stewards are selected to rubber-stamp decisions already made. It can burn out the same activists if rotation doesn't happen. Power can reconcentrate around stewards who become gatekeepers to officials. If resources are meagre—if the park gets $50,000 instead of $500,000—residents absorb the scarcity and blame each other rather than institutions. The process can slow real needs; a community spending three months deciding on street lighting is time a community with broken lights can't wait. If residents who don't speak the dominant language, or who distrust institutions deeply, aren't actively recruited and supported, the steward group reproduces the same exclusions it was meant to undo. Without binding authority and real budget, stewardship becomes theatre.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgeting in Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989–present).** Residents in neighbourhoods directly decide how to allocate a portion of the municipal budget—now reaching millions of dollars across hundreds of public investments. Stewards attend assemblies, propose projects, vote. The process has spread to hundreds of cities globally. What matters: residents vote on real money, and decisions are binding. What wobbles: in some cities it's become consultant-driven spectacle; in others, like parts of Porto Alegre, neighbourhood organisations have kept power rooted in residents.

**Kampung Improvement Programme, Jakarta (1969–present).** Residents in informal settlements co-designed infrastructure upgrades—drainage, pathways, community spaces—working with engineers. Residents decided where things went, contributed labour and local materials, and maintained them afterward. It kept housing people in place rather than displacing them. The pattern shows that stewardship works across contexts of extreme scarcity; the constraint wasn't money but honesty about it.

**Nighttime Commission, Auckland, New Zealand (2015–present).** A paid, representative group of residents meets monthly to advise on how the city changes after dark—from street lighting to late-night transport to night markets. They have veto over major decisions. What's telling: the Commission is multiethnic by design, includes night-shift workers and night-time precariat, and has real budget authority. It's small-scale but it shows the pattern holding in a wealthy Anglo context.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems make this pattern more urgent and more possible. More urgent because algorithmic decisions—about who gets housing, where police patrol, who sees what job postings—now shape city life invisibly. Residents can't co-design what they can't see. Residents Decide becomes necessary.

More possible because large language models can translate. A resident without technical fluency can describe a problem in plain language; a model trained on policy documents and code can translate it to technical requirements and back again. Stewards can interrogate algorithms in accessible language. "Show me three examples where your housing algorithm rejected someone who should have qualified." "What would change if we weighted rent burden differently?" The cognitive gulf narrows.

The risk: AI becomes the pretext for even faster extraction. Platforms deploy participatory tools that use resident input to train models, then use those models to make decisions residents never approved. The stewardship cycle breaks.

The pattern evolves: Residents Decide means **algorithmic transparency as default**—showing not just rules but the training data, the edge cases, the failures. It means residents own the weights: which variables matter most is a choice communities make, not technologists. It means slower deployment of systems, with stewards testing them long before launch. And it means distributed intelligence—residents and algorithms together, with residents holding veto.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life.** Stewards show up unprompted—not because they're paid, but because they care and they know their voice changes things. The waiting list for stewardship roles is longer than openings. Residents who aren't stewards reference decisions in casual conversation: "Remember when we voted for the street to be one-way?" New people ask how to join. The system handles surprises—a new problem emerges and residents solve it without waiting for officials. There's laughter in meetings. Stewards disagree publicly and stay in relationship. Budget gets spent on things residents asked for, not what consultants guessed they wanted. Mistakes happen and are fixed quickly, without blame-shifting.

**Signs of Decay.** The same 15 people show up every meeting and new people stop trying to join. Stewards are thanked but overruled on major decisions. A decision gets made, stewards find out from a news article. The process slows everything down and officials grow impatient. Meetings feel like performance—the chair summarises what stewards said but clearly it won't matter. Stewards burn out and don't get replaced. The role becomes a path to job offers or status for a few, breeding resentment. Participation drops during hard months when stewards are juggling survival. The system works beautifully for parks but officials ignore resident input on housing or policing—the high-stakes systems.

**Diagnostic Question:** If a steward proposed something that surprised or inconvenienced officials, would it actually happen, or would it disappear into a committee? If the answer is "it would actually happen," the pattern is alive. If it's "probably it would get discussed to death," the pattern is becoming theatre.
