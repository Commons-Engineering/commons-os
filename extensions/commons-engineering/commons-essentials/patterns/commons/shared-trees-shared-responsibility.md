### Section 1: Context

Urban trees are systemically stewarded by nobody and claimed by everybody. Municipal authorities manage street trees bureaucratically. Property owners treat trees as assets or liabilities. Residents develop informal attachments but lack standing. Infrastructure operators see roots as obstacles. Indigenous and cultural stewards are excluded from governance. Meanwhile, trees require continuous, distributed labour across decades—watering, pruning, conflict resolution, documentation, ceremonial care—that no single actor can sustain alone.

This pattern emerges from centuries of commons forest management (usufruct hierarchies, agroforestry tenure stacking) translated into urban contexts where competing claims (subsurface utilities, private property, public benefit, cultural significance, ecological regeneration) all stake legitimate interest in the same organism. The tension is not between ownership and stewardship but between exclusive control and nested, non-exclusive rights held by multiple parties simultaneously.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Exclusive ownership vs. distributed stewardship; short-term asset extraction vs. multi-generational care; top-down uniformity vs. nested community authority.**

Urban trees face three simultaneous crises of governance: (1) Fragmented authority—no actor has full responsibility for long-term care, creating maintenance deserts in low-income neighbourhoods while generating unequal service burdens on private owners. (2) Erasure of cultural and indigenous stewardship—trees are managed as ecological resources or aesthetic assets, stripping away ceremonial knowledge, medicinal use, and place meaning that sustained them for generations. (3) Subsurface conflict without commons process—utilities, roots, pavement, and building foundations all compete for underground space with no participatory mechanism to adjudicate claims, resulting in either tree removal or hidden infrastructure damage.

The deeper problem: tree governance treats ownership as monolithic (municipal, private, or contested) rather than recognizing that a single mature tree simultaneously serves ecological, cultural, infrastructural, and social functions requiring different expertise, time horizons, and authority structures. Exclusive ownership models collapse under this multiplicity.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish Layered Tree Stewardship Governance—a nested, non-exclusive rights framework where multiple parties hold defined, non-competing usufruct rights in the same tree, each with explicit responsibilities, benefit-sharing, and succession rules.

**Rights Bundling Architecture:**
Define at least four overlapping but non-exclusive stewardship roles: (1) Custodial steward (daily care—watering, pruning, monitoring health), typically a resident or neighbourhood organisation; (2) Governance steward (decision-making on species, placement, removal), typically a municipal representative or neighbourhood commons assembly; (3) Cultural steward (protection of ceremonial, medicinal, or cultural use), held by indigenous communities, cultural practitioners, or knowledge keepers; (4) Ecological steward (monitoring canopy contribution to cooling, water retention, habitat), held by environmental monitors or municipal staff.

**Subsurface Rights Allocation Protocol:**
Treat the underground volume around tree roots as a designed commons, not residual space. Establish formal protocols allocating subsurface zones: tree root zone (protected from new utility placement), utility corridor (if necessary, designed to coexist with roots), and water/nutrient flow pathways. Utilities, property owners, and tree stewards collectively permit new subsurface work through commons adjudication, not unilateral decisions. This shifts from root-vs.-infrastructure conflict to collaborative infrastructure design.

**Burden and Benefit Distribution:**
Maintenance labour and costs are explicitly shared: municipal provision of mulch, water, initial planting; resident stewards provide weekly monitoring and care; community volunteers conduct seasonal pruning; professional arborists intervene for complex health issues. Benefit flows are distributed: residents receive cooling and stormwater management; cultural practitioners gain access for ceremonies and harvest; municipalities reduce stormwater infrastructure costs; property owners receive property value appreciation without bearing full maintenance burden.

**Knowledge Commons Integration:**
Document tree-specific knowledge (species history, cultural significance, medicinal use, care protocols, local growing conditions) in community-accessible commons. Multilingual accessibility ensures language justice. Establish knowledge transfer mechanisms (mentorship, seasonal ceremonies, written guides) so stewardship survives practitioner turnover.

**Conflict Resolution Tribunal:**
Establish a nested commons tribunal for resolving disputes over placement, removal, care standards, or subsurface access. Include residents, utilities, municipal staff, cultural representatives, and neighbourhood commons delegates. Use deliberative methods (not top-down decree) to reach binding decisions. This formalizes tree governance as inherently political while keeping authority at the most local competent level.

**Anti-Commodification Covenant:**
Protect trees from speculative enclosure—no tree can be converted to private asset, carbon credit, or development offset without explicit commons consent. Heritage and mature trees gain legal personhood or guardianship protection, giving them standing against harmful actions even on private property when community interest is demonstrated.

**Succession Planning:**
Define explicit protocols for stewardship succession when actors age out, move, or change roles. Nested governance ensures trees persist beyond any single steward's tenure—the commons institutional structure carries forward even as individuals rotate through roles.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping existing trees and stewardship actors. Walk your neighbourhood. Identify which trees are orphaned—neglected by municipalities, ignored by property owners, untended by anyone. These are your entry points. Simultaneously, listen for who already cares: elders with memory of what grew there, gardeners maintaining adjacent lots, Indigenous residents with ancestral ties, property owners frustrated by code violations.

Next, design your rights bundle for a specific tree or grove. This isn't abstract—it's practical negotiation. A street tree might have a municipal custodian (infrastructure), a property owner (liability), a neighbourhood watering collective (daily care), and a cultural keeper (ceremonial access). Write these down. What does each party do? What do they gain? Crucially: how does responsibility transfer when someone steps back?

Formalize through lightweight governance: a tree stewardship agreement (not a legal document—a shared understanding, perhaps registered with the municipality). Include: seasonal tasks (who waters in drought), conflict resolution (what happens when roots threaten foundations), succession planning (who takes over if the property owner moves), and benefit-sharing (fruit goes to the community, shade belongs to all).

Start small—five to ten trees, not a hundred. Work with existing organizations: schools, community gardens, religious institutions, neighbourhood associations. They provide infrastructure you need: tools, water access, communication networks, continuity when individuals drop away.

Celebrate publicly. Plant ceremonies matter. Invite the cultural keeper to speak. Let the property owner plant alongside the water-tender. Document it—photos, names, stories. This transforms the tree from infrastructure into a *relationship*, witnessed and remembered.

Most importantly: build redundancy. If one steward disappears, the tree doesn't die. At least two people know how to prune it. At least three understand why it matters.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, several things flourish.

Trees receive consistent care because responsibility is distributed and visible—no longer hidden in municipal budgets or private obligation. Maintenance deserts shrink; low-income neighbourhoods stop being written off. Cultural stewardship re-enters the city; Indigenous knowledge about native trees, seasonal timing, and ceremonial use becomes *governance*, not nostalgia. Communities develop what researchers call "relational agency"—not just attachment to place, but recognized standing to shape it.

Economically, this shifts value: trees become productive (fruit, shade, nesting sites) rather than merely aesthetic. Younger people develop horticultural literacy—they learn to read a tree's needs. Social cohesion deepens; strangers meet regularly around shared work.

But risks emerge if not tended carefully.

Stewardship can become burden—care-work concentrated on already-marginalized groups (women, racialized communities, elders). Without explicit compensation or rotation, burnout kills the pattern. Conflict arises when stewards disagree: the property owner wants pruning aggressive; the cultural keeper wants minimal intervention. Lack of clear dispute resolution turns neighbours into enemies.

There's also a risk of *false inclusion*—tokenizing cultural keepers while municipal authorities retain real decision power. Or the pattern becomes gentrification vector: revitalized green spaces drive up property values, displacing the very communities who stewarded them back to life.

The deepest risk: assuming stewardship is voluntarily renewable. Real continuity requires succession planning, institutional memory, and often compensation for care-work over years. Without this, the pattern collapses when founders age out.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Seoul's Citizen Forest Governance (South Korea).** Beginning in the 1990s, Seoul established neighbourhood tree councils where residents, municipal staff, and sometimes Buddhist temples jointly steward urban forests. Each council holds rights to specific groves: they decide thinning, approve new plantings, organize seasonal festivals, and maintain detailed care calendars. The system explicitly incorporates Buddhist monastery knowledge of old-growth forest dynamics. Over thirty years, this has restored canopy in historically denuded districts and created intergenerational participation—grandmothers teaching grandchildren which trees fruit in which seasons.

**Karachi's Green Space Cooperatives (Pakistan).** Community organizations in Karachi have negotiated with the municipal authority to take custodial responsibility for street trees in designated neighbourhoods. Women-led water collectives manage irrigation during dry seasons; local shopkeepers become informal security against vandalism. Cultural keepers from Sindhi and Baloch communities reintroduced native neem and jujube trees, reconnecting urban residents to pre-colonial botanies. Benefits flow directly: fruit is sold by the water collective; shade attracts customers to neighbourhood shops.

**Musqueam and City of Vancouver Co-Stewardship (Canada).** The Musqueam Nation and Vancouver Parks Board negotiated shared governance of cedar groves in city parks. The Nation holds harvesting rights for cedar bark (used in traditional crafts) while the city manages public access and ecosystem health. This required learning a completely different stewardship calendar—Indigenous seasonal timing rather than municipal maintenance cycles—and recognizing that "protection" sometimes means *harvesting and tending*, not hands-off preservation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed stewardship becomes more navigable, and more necessary, in an age of AI and networked intelligence.

AI can do the surveillance work that once required expensive municipal audits: monitoring tree health via satellite and ground sensors, tracking maintenance gaps, predicting which trees need intervention. This shifts human attention away from *data-gathering* toward *meaning-making*—the conversations about which trees matter culturally, what harvesting timing honors, how to resolve competing stewardship visions.

Blockchain or distributed ledgers can formalize rights bundles and succession in ways that survive organizational turnover. A smart contract can automatically notify a backup steward if the primary caretaker hasn't logged watering records in two weeks—not to police, but to enable distributed oversight.

But the cognitive shift is deeper. In an era of algorithmic decision-making, distributed stewardship *reasserts human presence* as governance. It says: trees aren't resources optimized by models; they're beings stewarded by particular communities with particular histories. AI becomes the servant of this, not the arbiter.

Networks also enable cross-site learning: a water collective in Seoul learning from Karachi's dry-season strategies, or vice versa. Indigenous knowledge—once spatially isolated—becomes shareable without being extracted. The steward in one neighbourhood documents seasonal timing; another steward, hundreds of miles away, adapts it to local conditions.

The risk: that data transparency becomes surveillance, and "optimization" replaces cultural judgment. Only if stewards retain final authority—to say "no, we're not harvesting this year, the tree needs rest"—does this remain genuinely distributed.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** The tree has multiple known caretakers, and at least two can describe its needs without hesitation. New people—especially young people—are being brought into stewardship. The care calendar shifts seasonally; there's visible adaptation to weather. Conflict happens, but it's *addressed*—stewards negotiate, sometimes publicly. The tree produces (fruit, flowers, nesting sites), and that production is distributed—not hoarded. Stories attach to it; people remember why this tree matters. Succession has happened at least once: an original steward stepped back, and the tree didn't fall silent.

**Signs of decay:** Only one person knows the tree deeply; if they move, care lapses. Stewardship becomes visibly tokenized—a cultural keeper invited annually to bless the tree, but excluded from decisions. Maintenance follows municipal cycles regardless of weather or the tree's condition. Conflict arises and goes unresolved; stewards stop showing up. The tree stops producing, or produces only for markets (harvested, sold, the commons depleted). Stories fade; younger people don't know why the tree exists. Succession never happens; the original steward is indispensable.

**Diagnostic question:** *Can you name three people, from three different backgrounds or roles, who could step into primary stewardship of this tree right now—and would they say yes?* If not, the pattern is fragile. If yes, and if they've actually practiced together, it's alive.
