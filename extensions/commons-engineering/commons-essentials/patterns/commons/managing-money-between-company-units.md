### Section 1: Context

Multi-entity organisations — holding companies, consolidated groups, cooperatives with member entities, franchises, or regional hub structures — face a design problem unique to polycentric systems: how to move resources, share costs, and allocate profits between legal entities while maintaining both internal fairness and external legitimacy. Each entity is semi-autonomous (it has its own books, tax obligations, governance rights) but also interdependent (it shares services, transfers goods, receives financing from other entities). The interior economy of the group — all the invoices, loans, cost allocations, and profit flows between entities — must flow in ways that are simultaneously fair to each entity and defensible to tax authorities and external auditors. The 28 O1 principles apply here: Reciprocity ensures participants perceive terms as just; Polycentric Organisation recognises that each entity is a decision centre; Subsidiarity demands each entity controls its own accounting; Transparency allows the whole system to be audited; Economic Constraint means every transfer has a real cost or benefit.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Internal coherence vs. external legitimacy — the need to move resources, costs, and profits between legal entities while keeping the whole system coherent for consolidated reporting and tax authorities..**

In multi-entity systems, three interlocking tensions arise. First, the fairness tension: when one entity invoices another for shared services, allocates corporate overhead, or sets interest on internal loans, how can the price be 'fair' — not hidden, not arbitrary, not exploitative? Second, the visibility tension: interior flows are invisible to external stakeholders; if they are not documented and reconciled, they become points of hidden leverage, cross-subsidization, and tax risk. Third, the consolidation tension: for external reporting, all interior flows must be systematically eliminated — but elimination presupposes that they were first recorded, priced, and reconciled with internal fairness. If interior pricing is opaque or unfair, the consolidation process becomes an exercise in hiding problems, not surfacing them. Tax authorities apply scrutiny (transfer pricing audits, arm's-length testing) that forces organisations to defend interior prices retrospectively. The result: unprepared groups face unexpected tax exposure, restatements, and loss of stakeholder trust.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Interior Flows & Reciprocal Accounting is a governance system for managing all transfers of goods, services, costs, and financing between legal entities within a multi-entity whole. It operates in three integrated cycles:

**CYCLE 1 — PRICING STEWARDSHIP (Establishing Reciprocal Terms):** Every interior transaction — service charges, royalties, cost allocations, interest on loans — is priced using an arm's-length principle: the price is set as if the entities were independent. This is not a tax fiction; it is a reciprocity statement. The principle embeds fairness into structure: no entity pays hidden premiums or receives hidden subsidies. Prices are documented in an Intercompany Pricing Stewardship Framework that specifies the method (comparable uncontrolled price, cost-plus, profit-split) and the justification for each type of transaction. This framework is reviewed annually and adjusted if the underlying economic relationships change. The framework becomes the source of truth for both internal management reporting and tax compliance.

**CYCLE 2 — TRANSACTION VISIBILITY (Recording & Reconciling Flows):** Every interior transaction is recorded immediately in both entities' books — as an expense or revenue for one, as an income or asset for the other. At defined intervals (monthly, quarterly), all interior transactions are reconciled: Are the invoices received matching the services billed? Are loan interest accruals consistent? Are cost allocations transparent? A Reconciliation Schedule, owned by a neutral stewardship role (shared services coordinator, internal audit, or shared governance body), surfaces mismatches and ensures both entities agree on the facts. This visibility is not about control; it is about mutual accountability. Each entity can verify it is paying (or receiving) what was agreed.

**CYCLE 3 — CONSOLIDATION INTEGRITY (Eliminating Without Hiding):** For consolidated reporting, all interior balances, transactions, and intra-group gains are systematically eliminated — but only after they have been recorded, priced, and reconciled. The elimination process uses a Consolidation Elimination Protocol that is rule-based and auditable: for every interior invoice, the revenue is reversed; for every intra-group gain, the profit is removed; for every internal loan, the liability is cancelled. Because interior pricing has been transparent and arm's-length throughout the period, the eliminations reflect economic reality. The consolidated statements show the group's true external position, not a disguised version of internal leverage or hidden transfers.

**GOVERNANCE LAYER:** A Polycentric Pricing Council (representatives from each entity or a rotating governance body) owns the Pricing Framework and reviews the annual Reconciliation Schedules. Disputes over pricing fairness are resolved through a graduated process: first, local resolution between entities; second, escalation to the Council; third, external arbitration (benchmarking firm or tax advisor) if needed. This embeds Conflict Resolution into the system — disagreements are anticipated, surfaced early, and resolved transparently.

**OUTCOME:** Interior flows become a source of system integrity rather than hidden risk. Each entity can see it is treated fairly. Tax authorities can verify pricing is defensible. Consolidated statements reflect economic truth. The system is alive because pricing terms adapt annually; shared because governance is polycentric; just because arm's-length principles prevent exploitation; and built to last because it survives external scrutiny.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping your interior flows. Before you write a single policy, spend a week tracking what actually moves between entities: service hours, shared facilities, overhead allocations, loans, royalties, data licenses. Draw it. Who serves whom? What's implicit? What's been done the same way for five years without question? This map becomes your shared language.

Next, establish your pricing council — a small, rotating group from finance, operations, and each major entity. Their job is not to set prices from on high, but to make pricing logic *visible and debatable*. For each major flow, they answer: What's the actual cost of delivery? What's fair compensation for the entity providing it? What does the market charge for this? What does our mission require? Document the reasoning, not just the number. This reasoning becomes your policy.

Create tiered documentation. For high-volume, low-sensitivity flows (shared IT support, facilities), use a simple formula agreed once and reviewed annually. For complex or contentious flows (shared intellectual property, overhead allocation), document the methodology openly and get entity leadership sign-off. For novel situations, convene the pricing council explicitly.

Build in review cycles. Every transaction over a threshold gets a quarterly audit — not to catch fraud, but to catch drift. Does the pricing council's logic still hold? Has the underlying cost changed? Has market reality shifted? Monthly consolidated reports should show both consolidated performance and entity-level impact of interior pricing. Make it visible.

Institute a "fairness question" at every budget cycle: *If we were setting these prices fresh today, knowing what we now know, would we choose differently?* This keeps pricing alive, not calcified.

Finally, separate the record-keeper from the pricer. The person who calculates transfer prices should not be the person who negotiates them. This simple separation prevents the interior flow from becoming a hidden tool of manipulation.

### Section 5: Consequences

When interior flows are priced with care and transparency, trust deepens between entities. Leadership stops seeing the corporate center as a hidden tax. Shared services become genuinely valued because their cost is visible and justified. Entities can make real trade-off decisions: *Do we buy this corporate service, or build it ourselves, or partner differently?* Without that clarity, choice is impossible.

Consolidated reporting becomes honest. When you add up entity results, you're not hiding cross-subsidies or arbitrary allocations that mask real performance. Tax authorities see coherent logic, not creative accounting. Auditors find less to question. The system builds credibility.

But attention: if your pricing becomes *too rigid*, entities treat interior services as tax to avoid. They build parallel capacity, fragment operations, and destroy economies of scale. The system hardens into bureaucracy. You must remain willing to revisit prices when conditions change.

There's also a risk of false precision. You'll be tempted to allocate every overhead dollar perfectly — but perfect allocation is impossible and expensive to achieve. Better to allocate 80% of overhead clearly and accept that 20% lives in a shared pool. Some ambiguity, acknowledged, is healthier than false certainty.

In cooperatives or mission-driven groups, transparent pricing can expose uncomfortable truths: that one entity is genuinely subsidizing another, or that a shared service is unaffordable. This is not a failure of the pattern — it's the pattern *working*, surfacing reality so you can make conscious choices about cross-subsidy instead of hiding it.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Cooperative Corporation (Spain).** With over 200 worker cooperatives across manufacturing, retail, and finance, Mondragon uses an explicit "inter-cooperative pricing framework" that sets transfer prices based on fair cost-plus-contribution. Each cooperative's financial statements show how interior pricing affects net results. The system allows member cooperatives to stay autonomous while sharing infrastructure and capital. It's been refined over 60 years; new cooperatives are required to understand and accept the pricing logic before joining.

**Decathlon (France/Global).** The retail-and-manufacturing company operates through regional entities and brand-specific entities that buy from shared factories and distribution centers. Decathlon publishes its "inter-company pricing policy" internally and reviews it annually. They use market benchmarking to ensure that internal prices stay competitive with external suppliers. When they found their internal factory pricing was higher than external alternatives, they restructured it rather than hide the difference. This transparency has prevented entity resentment and supported their growth model.

**Farmers' Union Cooperatives in Kenya.** Regional grain-buying cooperatives that feed into a national union use explicit pricing formulas for internal grain purchases, transport, and milling services. The formula is simple — cost plus 8% — and is set at their annual general meeting where all members vote. By keeping the formula public and debated, they've avoided the historical problem of member cooperatives feeling exploited by the center. The system works because the rule is known, not arbitrary.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As organizations grow distributed and data-driven, interior flows become more complex and more exposed. Real-time dashboards now show consolidated performance *and* entity-level impact simultaneously; hidden cross-subsidies are harder to maintain. AI tools can model transfer pricing scenarios in seconds, surfacing unintended consequences quickly.

This creates both risk and opportunity. The risk: algorithmic pricing systems can optimize transfer prices to minimize consolidated tax burden while appearing logical. This is the old game played faster and less visibly. The opportunity: distributed intelligence allows each entity to see and challenge pricing in real time, not in annual reviews.

The pattern adapts by emphasizing *algorithmic transparency*. If an AI system recommends transfer prices, the logic must be explainable to human councils, not just optimal to the system. Entities need the ability to query why — to understand the weighting, the assumptions, the trade-offs. This keeps the council's role essential: not to calculate, but to interpret and choose.

In truly decentralized systems (blockchain-based cooperatives, distributed networks), interior flows can be priced by smart contracts with embedded fairness rules. But this only works if the rules themselves were chosen transparently, not coded by hidden architects. The pattern's requirement — that pricing logic be visible and debatable — becomes even more critical when the execution is automated.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Entities openly discuss pricing disagreements in meetings. The pricing council convenes regularly and makes changes. Entity leaders can articulate *why* they pay what they pay to the center. Consolidated reports are reviewed without defensiveness. When a major cost or service changes, repricing happens within months, not years. New entities joining the system can study the pricing logic and choose whether to accept it.

**Signs of decay:** Pricing hasn't changed in three years, even though costs have. Entities negotiate transfer prices in side conversations rather than in the council. The finance team guards pricing methodology as proprietary knowledge. Entity leaders complain about "overhead" but can't say what they're actually paying for. Consolidated reports focus on top-line numbers; entity-level detail is buried or obscured. When asked how interior pricing works, different leaders give different answers.

**Diagnostic question:** *Can a mid-level manager in any entity, right now, explain to an external stakeholder (a lender, an investor, a regulator) why their entity pays what it pays for services from the center — and would that explanation satisfy?*

If the answer is no, the system has drifted into opacity. If yes, the pattern is alive.
