### Section 1: Context

Urban communities—especially those facing disinvestment, displacement, cultural erasure, or deindustrialisation—are routinely planned *for* rather than *by*. External consultants, government agencies, and development interests arrive with predetermined visions, data sets that document only deficits, and solutions designed elsewhere. Meanwhile, the community itself holds vast, largely invisible assets: informal knowledge networks, cultural sites and practices, social relationships, existing infrastructure, skills, tools, land stewardship, and distributed power sources. These assets remain unmapped, unclaimed, and unavailable for collective decision-making. This pattern responds to that erasure by systematising the surfacing, documentation, and collective stewardship of what communities already possess—grounded in principles of local visibility, subsidiarity, truth-seeking, and distributed governance.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Deficit-focused external planning vs. asset-rooted community self-determination.**

Communities face two interconnected crises of invisibility. First, the *asset blindness* crisis: dominant planning systems document only deficits—poverty, blight, dysfunction—while rendering invisible the skills, relationships, cultural meaning, and infrastructure that communities maintain daily. This narrative shapes funding, policy, and regeneration priorities toward imported solutions rather than community-rooted ones. Second, the *power opacity* crisis: formal power holders (developers, officials, institutions) operate visibly and strategically, while community power sources—organizing capacity, cultural authority, relational networks, collective knowledge—remain unmapped and uncoordinated. Communities cannot easily deploy power they cannot see. Together, these create a governance vacuum: decisions about community futures are made by those who lack ground-level knowledge, while those with knowledge lack visibility or legitimacy in decision spaces. The result is misaligned regeneration, cultural loss, and eroded community agency.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement a *Community Asset, Relationship & Power Mapping System*—a structured, participatory process that systematically surfaces, documents, and sustains visibility of what communities possess and control.

**Core Mechanism:**

1. **Multi-layered Asset Surfacing**: Design participatory mapping exercises that document assets across overlapping domains—physical infrastructure (buildings, green space, informal gathering sites), cultural assets (murals, performance venues, ritual spaces, heritage sites), social relationships (networks, informal institutions, trust patterns), economic assets (cooperative enterprises, informal trade, skill distribution), knowledge systems (local and indigenous expertise, oral histories), and relational capacity (organizing ability, convening power, media platforms). Use visual, cartographic, and narrative methods; honour knowledge held outside professional frameworks.

2. **Distributed Documentation & Stewardship Claims**: Move beyond one-time mapping to ongoing, collectively maintained asset inventories—physical documents, digital dashboards, or hybrid systems—that remain visible and updateable by community members. Pair each asset with explicit stewardship relationships: who cares for this? who decides about it? what knowledge does it hold? This converts abstract assets into relational responsibilities.

3. **Power Visibility & Strategic Clarity**: Conduct parallel power mapping—identifying who holds what kind of influence (capital, political access, cultural authority, organizing capacity), what relationships bind or divide actors, where resources flow, and where leverage points exist. Make this visible to the community, not as gossip but as strategic intelligence for collective action.

4. **Counter-Narrative & Accountability**: Publish community-defined wealth indices, abundance maps, and capacity dashboards that displace deficit-focused municipal data with asset-rooted narratives. Use this visibility as accountability infrastructure: communities set targets for their own regeneration; institutions explain how they support community-defined priorities.

5. **Nested Governance Integration**: Link asset and power maps to formal decision-making processes—neighbourhood committees, development review boards, resource allocation bodies. Ensure that community-surfaced assets and priorities shape what gets funded, protected, or stewarded; ensure that power mapping informs who gets seat and voice in decisions.

**Why This Holds:** The pattern is systematic because it repeats across communities and time; it is alive because communities update it as context shifts; it is shared because residents control the mapping, the narrative, and the stewardship claims; it is just because it begins with what communities possess and value, not external deficits; it endures because it builds regeneration on existing relationships and infrastructure rather than importing solutions that decay when external actors leave.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start small and visible. Convene a core mapping team—10–15 people who represent different corners of your community: long-term residents, shop owners, elders, young people, artists, service workers, faith leaders, anyone with deep relational roots. This isn't about expertise; it's about coverage and trust.

Design a series of participatory mapping sessions (3–4 over 2–3 months). In each session, focus on one asset layer: physical places and infrastructure; relationships and networks; skills and knowledge; cultural and creative capacity; economic activity (formal and informal); governance and decision-making power.

Use large maps, photographs, sticky notes, markers, conversation. Ask simple, open questions: *Where do people actually gather?* *Who fixes things when they break?* *Who knows how to grow food, repair cars, teach kids, navigate systems?* *What informal economies exist?* *Who makes decisions, and how?* *What stories does this place tell about itself?*

Document everything—not in sterile data tables, but in stories, photos, hand-drawn maps, and recorded conversations. Create a visual asset inventory that's beautiful enough to live on a wall, simple enough for a child to understand, specific enough to be useful.

Crucially: *circulate it back*. Share drafts with the mapping team and wider community. Let people correct it, add to it, argue about it. This isn't external documentation—it's a community seeing itself.

Once mapped, the asset inventory becomes a living reference. Use it to inform local decisions about resource allocation, partnership priorities, and regeneration plans. Connect it to budgets and planning processes so it shapes what actually gets funded and built.

Maintain and update it seasonally. As people come and go, relationships shift, and new capacities emerge, the map evolves. It's not a finished artifact; it's a practice.

### Section 5: Consequences

What flourishes when you do this well: Communities develop what might be called "asset literacy"—a clear-eyed, dignified understanding of their own power. This shifts how people talk about their neighbourhood, how they relate to one another, and what they're willing to attempt together. External partners arrive with a different question: *How do we strengthen what's already working?* rather than *What do we fix?* Funding flows toward local leadership and existing infrastructure instead of imported solutions. Young people see futures that don't require leaving. Elders are sought out rather than invisibilised.

But there are real risks if this work isn't tended carefully. Asset mapping can become *commodification*—local culture and relationships packaged for external consumption or creative-class attraction, accelerating displacement. Mapping without power analysis can obscure which assets people actually control versus which are controlled by external interests. If the process doesn't connect to real decision-making authority and resources, it becomes empty—a feel-good exercise that documents community vitality while nothing changes. The map can also create false consensus, papering over real conflict about priorities and values that need to surface and be worked through, not smoothed over.

Risk is highest when external institutions use mapping as legitimation—conducting it, publishing findings, then implementing predetermined plans unchanged. The antidote is clear: the community owns the map, controls its use, and has veto power over decisions that affect their assets.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Southside Chicago, USA (Resident-Led Mapping):** In the Woodlawn neighbourhood, residents have run their own asset mapping process for over a decade, documenting informal economies, kinship networks, cultural institutions, and community-controlled land. Rather than waiting for external development plans, they've used the map to prioritize their own investments—supporting existing Black-owned businesses, protecting community land, and building intergenerational wealth. The map lives online and on physical displays in community spaces.

**Kampala, Uganda (Informal Settlement Asset Mapping):** In several informal settlements, resident associations conducted participatory mapping that documented not deficits but the extensive social support systems, water and sanitation networks, and economic activity that sustain 50,000+ people with minimal external support. The process shifted how NGOs and municipal government engaged—from viewing settlements as problems to be cleared to recognizing them as densely networked communities managing remarkable complexity. Residents used the map to negotiate for service provision based on existing infrastructure rather than replacement schemes.

**Catalonia, Spain (Network Mapping for Solidarity Economy):** Community organizations mapped relationships, skills, and economic capacity across several Barcelona neighbourhoods, creating a hyperlocal solidarity economy directory. They documented tool libraries, time banks, repair networks, urban gardens, cooking collectives, and skill-sharing. The map became infrastructure for mutual aid and reduced dependence on commercial services. It also made visible the care work (childcare, elder support, cooking) that sustains the economy but is usually invisible.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Asset mapping is entering a new phase. Distributed sensors, mobile devices, and participatory data platforms can make ongoing asset documentation easier—residents photograph and geolocate local assets; networks form around shared interests; real-time data surfaces what's happening now, not what was true in a consultant's report six months ago.

But this also introduces new risks. AI-enabled analysis can quickly convert asset maps into predictive models for gentrification, targeting, or resource extraction. External platforms can absorb community-generated data and use it for purposes communities never intended. The cognitive shift required is this: *Communities need to control their own data infrastructure.* This means locally hosted platforms, clear agreements about data use, and community authority over what gets shared and with whom.

AI can also help by amplifying signal from distributed knowledge—connecting skills across networks, matching needs with resources, making informal economies visible without requiring people to sit through long meetings. But only if communities build and own those systems.

The deeper change: Asset mapping moves from episodic documentation to continuous sensing. Communities become more aware, in real time, of what they have, what's changing, and where intervention is needed. This shifts power toward those who see clearly and can act quickly—which should be the community itself.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** The asset map circulates in daily conversation—people reference it when making decisions, planning investments, or imagining futures. Young people can articulate what their community actually has and what they're building on it. External partners ask to see the map before proposing anything. Community members initiate their own updates, adding new assets and relationships. Resources (funding, partnerships, permits) flow toward mapped assets and community priorities rather than contradicting them. Intergenerational mentorship deepens because elders' knowledge is named as essential, not sidelined.

**Signs of decay:** The map sits gathering dust—printed once, never updated. Nobody outside the mapping team knows it exists. External plans proceed unchanged, ignoring what the map documented. Community members are cynical ("We did the mapping exercise, nothing changed"). Assets are mapped but people have no power to protect or strengthen them. The map becomes a tool for outsiders—property developers, researchers, tourists—while residents see no return. Conflict and difference get smoothed over instead of worked through. Displacement accelerates despite the visibility of community assets.

**Diagnostic question:** *If we stopped updating the map tomorrow, how many people in this community would actively miss it? How many decisions—by residents, funders, partners, and officials—actually reflect what the map shows?*

If the answers are weak, the map has become documentation rather than infrastructure for power. Return it to the community to own and use.
