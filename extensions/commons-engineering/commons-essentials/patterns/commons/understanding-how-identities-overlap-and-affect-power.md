> Recognize how different parts of your identity intersect and create unique experiences within systems of power. Use this

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)

### Section 1: Context

Commons work that ignores intersectionality inevitably reproduces the power structures it claims to challenge. A policy designed to serve 'women' may benefit only privileged women while deepening harm for Black, disabled, or immigrant women. A movement that centers single issues—housing justice without racial analysis, or gender equity without class analysis—builds coalitions that fracture when members with multiply marginalized identities discover their needs were never the true target. The Commons Engineer must develop intersectional literacy not as an ethical add-on but as a design requirement. This lineage runs through Collective Action (whose rules must account for unequal starting positions), Truth-Seeking (whose blindspots hide where power compounds), Subsidiarity (whose 'local competence' must include those most affected), and Reciprocity (whose fairness looks different across differently situated people).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Single-issue analysis & design vs. compound lived experience across intersecting systems of power.**

The core tension: Designing for justice while remaining blind to how power compounds. When you treat identity dimensions separately—addressing 'gender' without 'race' or 'class'—you design for the least-burdened members of each category and exclude those holding multiply marginalized identities. When you design 'impact' without intersectional analysis, your success metrics measure benefit to privileged members of your target group, obscuring how your intervention may harm the most vulnerable. When you conduct power analysis that misses intersectionality, you allocate power and voice to people positioned to 'represent' a single identity, marginalizing those whose lived experience sits at multiple intersections. The person you're trying to serve isn't 'a woman' or 'disabled' in isolation—they're a disabled Latina single mother, and each dimension shapes how systems affect her differently than they affect you. Missing that, you miss the system entirely.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Intersectional Mapping & Power-Aware Design moves through three nested practices:

**1. MAP YOUR OWN COMPOUND POSITION.** Before designing for others, locate yourself at the intersection of your own identities: race, class, gender, sexuality, ability, immigration status, language, neurodiversity, religion, and others. For each, mark where you hold unearned advantage (privilege) and where you face systemic disadvantage (oppression). The crucial insight: you hold both simultaneously. You may be privileged by race but marginalized by disability; privileged by class but marginalized by sexuality. This isn't abstract self-knowledge—it's essential data. Your blindspots (where you hold privilege) are the places where your analysis will fail and your interventions will cause harm. Make these visible to your collaborators.

**2. ANALYZE COMPOUNDING EFFECTS IN YOUR CONTEXT.** Don't ask 'How does this policy affect women?' Ask 'How does this policy affect women at the intersection of race, class, ability, immigration status?' Trace how systems of power interact: How does surveillance capitalism affect Black trans women differently than white cis women? How does housing policy affect disabled immigrants differently than disabled citizens? How does your movement's communication channels exclude people with multiple marginalized identities? Map the load—the compounded burden that those holding multiple marginalized identities carry—and recognize it as a source of systems insight, not a problem to be managed.

**3. DESIGN WITH THE MOST MARGINALIZED AS THE CENTRE.** A commons principle: if your design serves those most burdened by compounding oppressions, it will serve everyone. If your design serves only the privileged within your target group, it will harm the marginalized. Reverse your design logic: Ask 'Who in this system faces the most compounded marginalization?' and 'What do they need?' Then test your intervention against that standard. Include people holding those compound identities in governance—not as token representation but as decision-makers whose lived experience is essential systems knowledge. When the most marginalized can shape the rules, reciprocity and fairness become structural, not aspirational.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping yourself—not as self-flagellation, but as radical honesty. Name the identities you carry: race, class, gender, sexuality, disability status, language, religion, citizenship, neurodiversity. For each, ask: Where do I move through the world with unearned ease? Where do I encounter friction, skepticism, denial of resources? This isn't about shame. It's about clarity. You can't see the water you're swimming in until you name it.

Then map the people you're designing *with* or *for*. Don't ask "What do women need?" Ask "What does a disabled trans Latina mother of three need from housing policy?" The specificity matters. Multiply marginalized people have already done this analysis in their own lives—they're navigating compound systems daily. Your job is to listen for the texture of their lived experience, not impose your single-issue framework.

Create a "power matrix" for any solution you're considering: How does this affect people at different intersections? Who benefits most? Who gets left behind? Who might this harm, even accidentally? Test your answer with people who hold the most marginalized positions within the system you're addressing. Not tokenistically. Structurally—in decision-making, resource allocation, leadership.

Document what you learn as you go. Intersectional analysis isn't a one-time audit. It's a living practice. When you discover your solution missed something, that's not failure—that's the pattern working. Adjust. Include people whose lives are most affected in the adjustment process.

Finally, name the trade-offs. You cannot design for everyone equally. When you choose to prioritize the needs of the most burdened, you may reduce short-term benefits to the privileged. This is intentional. This is just.

### Section 5: Consequences

When intersectional analysis shapes design, solutions actually reach people who need them most. Housing policies that account for how racism, sexism, and class operate together create homes for families previously locked out entirely. Organizing that centers Black trans disabled people's leadership strengthens the whole movement because these people navigate more systems and see more clearly. You build power that holds.

But there are real tensions. Centering the most marginalized takes time. It slows early wins. Stakeholders accustomed to single-issue framing may resist—they lose their comfortable narrative. And intersectional work demands intellectual humility: You will get it wrong. You will miss something. The risk is that people depending on your solutions bear the cost of your learning.

There's also the risk of analysis paralysis: mapping so many intersections that nothing moves. Or of false representation—including marginalized people in meetings without actually shifting power or resources. These are forms of extraction.

The deeper risk: If intersectional mapping becomes an exercise only for designers and leaders, it can become performative. The antidote is structural: marginalized people need decision-making power, not just participation in consultation. They need resources to lead, not volunteer their expertise.

When tended carefully, intersectionality prevents the cruel irony of liberation movements that liberate only some. It's the difference between a housing policy that works for everyone it claims to serve—and one that accidentally locks deeper doors for those facing multiple systems of exclusion.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Combahee River Collective (1970s, USA).** Black queer women articulated intersectional analysis while doing political work—recognizing that their liberation couldn't be achieved through single-issue organizing around race, gender, or sexuality separately. They didn't just theorize; they structured their organizing around this insight, creating political space that centered the most marginalized within Black liberation movements. Their work became foundational to how we now understand compound oppression.

**Disabled Women's Network (DAWN, Canada, ongoing).** This organization centers disabled women of color, particularly Indigenous and immigrant disabled women. Every program is designed with explicit attention to how disability, gender, race, and immigration status compound. Their policy advocacy specifically documents how healthcare, housing, and employment policies harm multiply marginalized disabled women—and proposes solutions grounded in that analysis.

**Land Back organizing (contemporary, USA).** Indigenous-led land reclamation explicitly works at the intersection of colonialism, environmental destruction, and contemporary capitalism. When designing return of land or co-stewardship agreements, organizers account for how Indigenous women, queer, and disabled people were disproportionately displaced—and center their leadership in land governance. This isn't additive (Indigenous + feminist analysis); it's synthetic, rooted in how Indigenous feminism already understands these systems as interconnected.

Each of these works because it doesn't treat intersectionality as a checklist applied to existing plans. It grounds the entire logic of action in compound reality.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a time of AI systems and algorithmic decision-making, intersectional thinking becomes both more urgent and more possible.

Urgent: because machine learning systems trained on historical data replicate and amplify existing inequalities. An algorithm trained on hiring records will learn to screen out women and people of color—not because anyone programmed bias, but because the training data holds bias. Without intersectional analysis, you won't catch that a housing algorithm systematically denies credit to Black women, or that a predictive policing tool disproportionately targets Indigenous trans people.

More possible: because distributed intelligence—human + machine—can map complexity that single humans can't hold. An AI system can track how policies affect people across dozens of identity intersections simultaneously, generating visualizations that make compound effects visible. It can surface patterns in how systems harm the most marginalized people faster than manual analysis.

But here's the catch: AI systems will only do this if humans design them with intersectional intent. The technology doesn't generate justice on its own. You have to build intersectional thinking into the prompts, the training data, the evaluation metrics, the governance.

In this cognitive era, intersectional analysis becomes a design requirement for any system claiming to serve justice. Not an add-on. A foundation.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Marginalized people are genuinely leading—in rooms where decisions happen, with resources and authority. Designs are specific to compound identities, not generic. When you ask "How does this affect disabled mothers?" you get detailed answers grounded in lived experience. Solutions catch unintended harms before they're deployed at scale. Mistakes are treated as data, not shame. People come back.

**Signs of decay:** Intersectionality becomes language without practice—workshops on "centering voices" where decision-making never shifts. Marginalized people are consulted but not resourced; they volunteer their time while privileged people are paid for analysis. Solutions are generic ("one size fits all" with a diversity filter). Mistakes blame the marginalized people for not "communicating clearly enough." People stop showing up.

**The diagnostic question:** *Who holds power to change the design when you discover it's not working for multiply marginalized people—and do they have the resources and incentive to actually change it?*

If the answer is "the same people who designed it in the first place" or "the marginalized people who discovered the problem, unpaid," your system is not intersectional. It's extractive.
