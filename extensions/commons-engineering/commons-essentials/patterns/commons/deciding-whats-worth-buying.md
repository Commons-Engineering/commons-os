### Section 1: Context

Every commons must distinguish between flows that sustain current operations (the heartbeat of daily life) and flows that build or renew the asset base (the bones and sinews). This distinction is especially alive in organizations managing physical infrastructure, equipment, or accumulated intellectual property—but it applies everywhere resources are finite and time horizons extend beyond a single cycle. The problem is not the distinction itself but how it is governed. Historically, capital authorization has been treated as a accounting compliance burden or a top-down control mechanism. But in a living commons, it becomes a stewardship practice: the collective act of deciding what investments serve the shared future, what maintenance preserves present capacity, and where the system is at risk of overcommitment. This lineage traces through Subsidiarity (decisions made closest to knowledge), Carrying Capacity (respect for what the system can sustain), Polycentric Organisation (nested circles of authority), Reciprocity (clear, fair rules that all stewards understand), and Feedback (making flows visible so the commons can sense and adapt).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Empowering local stewards to move resources quickly vs. preserving systemic oversight so capital allocations align with shared carrying capacity and just distribution across the whole..**

Three living tensions surface here. First: speed versus sight. Local stewards need authority to move capital quickly—to seize opportunities, respond to urgent maintenance, adapt to changing conditions. But without visibility into total capital commitment, the commons can drift into overextension. Projects pile up; future carrying capacity is mortgaged; maintenance is deferred; the asset base decays. Second: empowerment versus equity. If authorization is too decentralized, richer or louder constituencies capture disproportionate capital. Maintenance is underfunded everywhere while showcase projects flourish elsewhere. Reciprocity erodes. Third: classification versus clarity. The boundary between capital and operational spending is genuinely ambiguous—a repair that extends life, a training investment, a software upgrade. Without shared rules, decisions are made inconsistently, comparisons become meaningless, and stewards lose trust in fairness. The commons cannot sense its own state accurately.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish a nested threshold architecture with three integrated elements: (1) Classification Principles, (2) Tiered Authority Circles, and (3) Feedback Visibility.

**Classification Principles:** Define clear, learnable rules for what constitutes capital versus operational spending. Capital: expenditures that create or renew an asset whose benefit extends beyond the current cycle (typically 12+ months). Operational: expenditures that sustain current function within the cycle. Create a simple Decision Tree (used by all stewards) that asks: Does this extend the useful life or capacity of an existing asset? Does the benefit span multiple years? Is the amount material to the commons' total asset base? These rules apply consistently across all departments, creating the shared language necessary for fair comparison. Review and adapt these rules annually based on what the commons has learned about its own carrying capacity.

**Tiered Authority Circles:** Establish nested spending thresholds, each with explicit approval circles. Example structure (adapt to your scale): Tier 1 ($0–$5K): Single steward authority, logged for visibility. Tier 2 ($5K–$50K): Peer review (2–3 stewards in the functional area). Tier 3 ($50K–$250K): Cross-functional governance circle (operations, finance, strategy). Tier 4 ($250K+): Full commons assembly or delegated board. Each tier requires documented submission: what is being acquired, why it serves shared carrying capacity, what alternatives were considered, what maintenance or deferred spending it may displace. This is not bureaucracy—it is collective sense-making at escalating scales. The authority rises not because approval is harder, but because the choice affects more of the commons' future.

**Feedback Visibility:** Maintain a rolling, visible Capital Flow Map showing: (a) all approved projects by tier and stage, (b) actual spending versus approved budget, (c) the ratio of capital to operational spending over time, (d) maintenance versus growth capital (keeping what you have versus building new capacity). Review this map quarterly in cross-functional steward circles. When spending patterns emerge that misalign with shared values—e.g., growth capital captures 80% of resources while critical infrastructure decays—the pattern surfaces the tension explicitly. This triggers collective rethinking: Are our thresholds wrong? Is our classification rule missing something? Have circumstances changed? Stewards adapt the architecture in real time, maintaining fit between the governance structure and the commons' evolving needs.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making the distinction real, not abstract. Gather your stewards—the people who actually spend money—and ask them: *What breaks? What lasts? What do we need next year that we don't have today?* From their answers, build your classification together. Don't impose it from above. A capital purchase for one organization (a truck) might be operational for another (a delivery service). The principle matters more than the label.

Once you have classification language everyone recognizes, map your approval authority like ripples in water. A $500 repair? One person decides. A $5,000 equipment upgrade? Two signatures. A $50,000 asset purchase? The full circle convenes. The thresholds should reflect your actual carrying capacity—what can one steward decide without risking systemic strain? This isn't bureaucracy for its own sake; it's trust proportional to consequence.

Now tend the visibility. Create a simple, living artifact—a spreadsheet, a dashboard, even a physical board—where every approved purchase appears with its category, cost, and steward. Update it monthly. This isn't surveillance; it's memory. It lets the whole system see what's flowing out and feel whether the total load is sustainable.

The hardest part: *resist the urge to optimize too early.* Run your system for two or three cycles before you tweak it. You'll learn where the friction actually lives—where good stewards feel blocked, where blind spots opened up. Then adjust thresholds, clarify language, reshape authority circles.

Train new stewards explicitly. Don't assume they'll intuit the thresholds. Walk them through three real past purchases: "This was capital because…" "This was operational because…" "This one was borderline—here's how we decided." Narrative sticks better than rules.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Stewards move with confidence, knowing their authority. They don't second-guess small decisions or hide them. Capital flows where it's supposed to—toward genuine renewal rather than crisis patches. The organization develops institutional memory about what it owns and why. Over time, you can actually plan ahead because you're not perpetually shocked by what's been committed.

A healthy spending culture emerges: people feel trusted, but they also feel the weight of shared resource limits. They become more thoughtful about what they ask for. They collaborate to combine purchases, share equipment, defer the nice-to-have in favor of the necessary. The commons becomes visible to itself.

**What risks emerge:** If thresholds are set too low, bureaucracy calcifies. Good stewards burn out asking for permission on routine needs. They start hiding spending, working around the system, or leaving. If thresholds are too high, the whole burden lands on a few decision-makers who become bottlenecks. The system loses its distributed character.

Another risk: classification creep. A $10,000 purchase fits the rule, so it gets approved. Then another. Then a bundle. Before long, capital commitments have grown beyond what the organization can sustain, and maintenance is quietly deferred. The system becomes a permission-granting machine rather than a carrying-capacity guardian.

Worst: the artifacts (the dashboards, the records) become lies. Purchases happen off-books. People game the categories. Authority circles learn to stamp approvals without real deliberation. The pattern dies from the inside out, and you're left with the illusion of control.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Corporation** (Basque region, Spain) operates a network of worker cooperatives with explicit capital spending protocols. Each cooperative maintains tiered authority: operational spending flows through local management, but any asset purchase above a threshold goes to the worker assembly. Thresholds are adjusted annually based on the cooperative's financial health. This has allowed Mondragon to sustain growth for 70+ years while keeping capital decisions rooted in the communities that live with them.

**Mennonite Disaster Service** (North America) uses a nested approval structure for equipment purchases and facility renovation across 80+ volunteer chapters. Local chapters handle tools and supplies under $1,000; regional councils oversee vehicle and building work up to $25,000; the national board reviews capital projects that exceed that. The system is simple enough that it survives volunteer turnover and geographic dispersion. Importantly, they've published their thresholds openly, so new volunteers know the rules before joining.

**The Land (an intentional community in Ohio)** maintains a decision-making system where resident stewards approve operational expenses for their domains (food, energy, housing maintenance) but all capital purchases—whether a new roof, solar panels, or shared equipment—go through a consensus process involving the whole community. This slows major decisions but has kept the community's asset base aligned with its values and actual carrying capacity for 50+ years.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and networked intelligence change the texture of this pattern without destroying it. Real-time spending dashboards can now flag anomalies instantly—a category that's running hot, a threshold breach, a pattern that suggests miscalassification. Machine learning can surface which capital purchases actually generated promised returns, giving stewards better data for future decisions. You can model "if we approve this, what's our carrying capacity in year three?"

But the deeper gift is cognitive distribution. AI can hold the entire rule system—every threshold, every classification principle, every approved purchase—and make it instantly accessible to any steward. No more wondering "do I need permission for this?" The system becomes a conversation partner rather than a gatekeeping wall.

The risk: outsourcing judgment to the algorithm. A spending decision that matters—choosing between two equipment paths, each with different implications for labor and community—can't be reduced to thresholds and past patterns. The pattern still requires *human circles convening to deliberate*. Technology makes that easier, makes it faster, but it doesn't replace it.

The opportunity: stewards can spend less time in approval meetings and more time asking the generative question: *What should we be investing in next year that we're not investing in now?* AI handles the compliance layer; humans handle the possibility layer.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Stewards move with ease. They can name their thresholds and explain why. The living artifact (dashboard, board, ledger) is current; people refer to it naturally. When a steward brings a purchase request, the discussion is rarely about whether it's approved and often about whether it's *wise*—does this align with our values? Does this strain our carrying capacity? Could we combine it with something else?

Capital spending trends are knowable. You can see where renewal is happening, where deferred maintenance is creeping in, where the organization is investing in its future. New stewards are brought in and trained explicitly. There's narrative around past purchases: why that choice was right, why that one was a lesson.

**Signs of decay:** The artifact goes stale. Purchases happen but they're not recorded, or they're recorded weeks late. Stewards ask permission grudgingly or stop asking altogether—they work around the system. Approval meetings become rubber stamps; nobody actually deliberates. The organization is shocked by how much has been committed. Stewards leave, muttering about bureaucracy.

Capital spending is invisible. You don't know whether the organization is living off its accumulated assets or truly renewing them. Thresholds never change, even though the organization's actual carrying capacity has shifted. New people arrive confused about how decisions are made.

**Diagnostic question:** If you asked a random steward in your organization right now, *"Tell me the three thresholds for spending in your domain,"* could they answer without looking it up? If yes: the pattern is alive. If no: it's either disappeared or calcified into meaningless ritual. Either way, it's time to gather the circle, review what actually happened last year, and rebuild the distinction together.
