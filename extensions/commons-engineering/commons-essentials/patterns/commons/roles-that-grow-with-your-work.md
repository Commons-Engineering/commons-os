### Section 1: Context

Every cooperative system—whether business, nonprofit, or collective—requires clarity about who stewards what. In traditional hierarchies, this clarity is imposed downward through static job descriptions, creating brittle knowledge silos and role drift. In flat or cooperative structures, ambiguity about stewardship leads to duplicated effort, unowned work, and burnout. The tension is real: without boundary clarity, accountability dissolves; without adaptive revision, roles calcify and cease to reflect the actual work being done. The Orbit 1 principle of Subsidiarity demands that decisions about what a role demands happen at the lowest competent level—not in HR departments. Adaptive Management insists that all role definitions are hypotheses to be tested, not decrees to be obeyed.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Static role definition (clarity but stagnation) vs. fluid adaptation (responsiveness but ambiguity).**

Organizations face two recurring failures. First: roles are defined once, then slowly diverge from reality as work evolves, leaving people confused about authority, responsibility, and expectations. Role descriptions become legal artifacts rather than living guides. Second: in the rush to avoid bureaucratic overhead, some organizations abandon role clarity entirely, creating ambiguity that breeds conflict, duplication, and unowned critical work. Boundary roles (customer-facing, cross-functional, holding institutional knowledge) are particularly vulnerable—when continuity depends on a single person, turnover becomes catastrophic. Meanwhile, role titles become disconnected from actual authority, creating false status hierarchies that prevent people from stepping into stewardship where needed. The deeper problem: roles are treated as properties of individuals rather than stewardship commitments that the system itself must sustain.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Roles as Living Stewardship embeds six practices into governance cycles:

**1. Role Specification as Adaptive Document.** Every role has a documented specification (not a job description) that answers: What value does this stewardship create? What decisions are mine alone? What decisions require consultation? What work is non-negotiable; what is contextual? This spec is revised on a regular cadence (quarterly or semi-annually), not when crisis forces it. The revision happens in conversation between the role-holder, their collaborators, and their steward (supervisor/peer accountability partner), not in a remote HR database.

**2. Boundary Clarity Without Rigidity.** Use a Responsibility Matrix (RACI or similar) that documents interdependencies—where roles touch, where authority overlaps, where decisions flow. But the matrix is a conversation tool, not a command structure. It clarifies stewardship boundaries; it does not enforce hierarchy.

**3. Distributed Continuity Through Backfill Specification.** Every critical role (finance, operations, technical depth, institutional knowledge) explicitly defines: Who could step into this work if I leave or become unavailable? What knowledge must be made visible? Backfill is not a HR exercise—it is a design practice. Smaller organizations rotate people through critical roles deliberately. Larger ones maintain documented handoff protocols.

**4. Title Decoupling from Authority.** Separate role authority from job title. A person can hold deep technical stewardship without a manager title. A manager can hold limited decision authority in domains outside their expertise. This prevents false career ladders and allows people to serve where they have capacity and expertise.

**5. Competency Anchoring.** Role specifications link to the capabilities required—not as abstract competency models, but as concrete signals: What can someone in this role do that signals they are ready? This anchors hiring, development, and rotation decisions to actual work.

**6. Visibility and Reciprocal Accountability.** All role specifications are visible to collaborators and the broader community. Not as surveillance, but as stewardship covenant—"here is what I am accountable for; here is how I will know if I am succeeding; here is where I need you." This transforms role clarity from command-and-control into reciprocal commitment.

The pattern assumes that roles, like any living system component, require regular tending. It embeds role clarity into governance cycles, not HR workflows. It distributes the work of maintaining clarity to the people closest to it. It builds continuity by design rather than accident, and it prevents roles from drifting into irrelevance while remaining responsive to how actual work evolves.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by auditing what's actually happening in your organization right now. Before you write anything down, spend two weeks shadowing and interviewing people about their real work. What gets done? Who decides? Where do decisions get stuck? This is archaeology, not theory.

Then draft role specifications using a template that captures living context:

**The stewardship framing.** Begin with the value this role creates for members or customers—not duties, but outcomes. "This role ensures our financial systems stay honest and accessible" beats "Manages accounting."

**Decision authority levels.** For each major domain (hiring, budget, strategy, day-to-day work), write three categories: *Solo* (I decide and act), *Consultation* (I decide after listening), *Consensus* (we decide together). Be specific. "I solo-decide which invoices we chase" is clearer than "Financial decisions."

**Core commitments.** List 4–6 non-negotiable things this role always holds: availability hours, decision timelines, quality standards. Everything else is negotiable.

**Growth edges.** Explicitly name 2–3 skills or capabilities this role will develop over the next 6–12 months. Make learning visible.

Now anchor this in rhythm. Every quarter—not annually—the person in the role reviews their spec with a peer or mentor. Did the actual work match the spec? What's shifted? What needs rewording? This takes 90 minutes. The changes go into a shared document so everyone sees how roles are adapting.

When someone new takes a role, they inherit the specification *and* the last three quarterly reflections. They're not starting from abstraction; they're stepping into a documented, evolving conversation.

Finally, protect against role creep. When new work emerges, don't dump it into an existing role. Instead, ask: Does this belong in the spec? Or does it signal a new role emerging? Sometimes roles split. Sometimes they merge. Let the pattern stay visible.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Trust deepens because people know they're accountable for something real, not a paper description. Decision-making accelerates because authority is clear. People develop deeper expertise because roles are specific enough to protect learning time. Transitions work better because the incoming person inherits a documented practice, not a blank slate. Most subtly: people stop performing a fixed identity and start inhabiting a live relationship with work.

**The risks:** If you update specifications without a real cadence, they become fiction again—more dangerous than static descriptions because they feel current. If consultation requirements get too heavy, everything slows down and people revert to secrecy to get work done. If you mistake "adaptive" for "undefined," people experience it as chaos and load-shedding, and the most conscientious burn out trying to fill gaps.

There's also a subtle cost: role specifications require naming what matters, which means some priorities become visible and others disappear. A new role specification might reveal that something everyone assumed was essential is actually nobody's stewardship—including something your organization unconsciously relies on (mentoring junior staff, maintaining institutional memory, attending to equity). You have to be willing to see those gaps and own them.

The deepest risk is drift in the opposite direction: roles become so specific and documented that they ossify in a new way. The solution is radical: treat specifications not as contracts but as living maps. The moment they stop being questioned, they're dead.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Stocksy United** (a cooperative photography platform) uses role specifications that include both decision authority and learning edges. Members know exactly which decisions their role controls—scheduling, pricing, member acceptance—and which require collective consent. Critically, specs are paired with clear mentorship timelines, so someone growing into a role has a map of what mastery looks like. They update specs when work changes, not annually.

**The Evergreen Cooperative (Cleveland, Ohio)** embedded role adaptation into their worker-owner governance model. Each cooperative—a laundry, a food hub, a solar installation company—maintains role specifications that live alongside their governance documents. When a worker's skills grow or the market shifts, they renegotiate their stewardship without having to leave the cooperative or wait for a formal promotion cycle. Roles evolve with people's capacity and the organization's needs, in parallel.

**Seleda** (an Ethiopian women's coffee cooperative) uses a different cultural form: elder advisors meet monthly with role-holders to review what's working and what's shifting in their stewardships. Rather than written specs, they use oral reflection and narrative—"Tell us what you're stewarding, what you've learned, what's changing"—captured in collective notes. The practice is less about documentation than about keeping stewardship as a shared, living conversation rather than letting it calcify into hierarchy.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI surfaces a new dimension to this pattern: machines can now execute many defined roles faster and more consistently than humans. This makes the distinction between *stewardship* and *task execution* urgent.

A role specification written in the old way—as a list of activities to complete—becomes immediately vulnerable to automation. A spec written as stewardship—as a relationship to certain decisions and certain values—is harder to automate and actually becomes more essential.

The pattern shifts from "define roles precisely so people know what to do" to "define roles as stewardship so humans and machines can collaborate without humans being reduced to task supervisors."

This means role specs now need a fourth element: **What judgment calls are mine?** Which decisions require human presence, contextual understanding, or accountability that a system can't hold? A bookkeeper's spec becomes less about data entry and more about ensuring financial integrity, interpreting anomalies, and knowing when a number signals a problem that needs human attention.

Distributed intelligence—both human and artificial—also means roles can be more fluid. A person can stewarding multiple domains simultaneously without contradiction, because they're not blocked by sequential task execution. Specifications become less about protecting someone's turf and more about clarifying whose judgment matters in each domain when.

The risk: without clear stewardship specs, people become coordinators of AI systems rather than decision-makers. They experience their role as managed-by-algorithm rather than as authentic responsibility. The pattern, tended well, protects against that erosion.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People can articulate what they steward and why, without reading a document. Decisions move at a sustainable pace—not rushed, not glacial. New members are productive in weeks, not months, because they inherit a live map. When disagreement arises about who should decide something, you can reference the spec, update it if it's outdated, and move on—the process is visible and relatively frictionless. Quarterly reviews feel generative rather than evaluative: "What's changing? What are we learning?"

**Signs of decay:** Specs exist but nobody references them. Role titles have drifted so far from actual work that describing your job to outsiders requires a 10-minute explanation. Decisions don't happen, or they happen in whispered conversations outside formal channels, because authority is unclear. New people spend months confused about what they're actually responsible for. Burnout appears in people trying to fill undefined gaps, often the most conscientious ones.

**A diagnostic question:** If you removed someone from their role right now, could the next person step in and know what to do and what not to do within a week, using only what's documented? If the answer is "no," your specifications are either too vague, too outdated, or not being used as living documents. That's your signal to revive the practice.
