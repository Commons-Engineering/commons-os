> Recognize problems you've solved before, document them clearly, and share them so others can learn and adapt them. A pat

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every practitioner recognizes patterns—recurring structures in relationships, decisions, systems, problems. But pattern recognition is often private, unexamined, and mechanically applied. When a pattern surfaces in a new context, we either reinvent the wheel or borrow structure without testing fit. This cluster emerges from the lived experience of neurodivergent practitioners who see patterns others miss, of teams wrestling with identical dysfunctions across roles and contexts, of knowledge workers replicating work structures that could be templated. The commons engineering tradition inherits from Christopher Alexander: patterns are not rules but distilled wisdom—the intent, context, and resolution path encoded together. Pattern Stewardship treats this inheritance as a practice, not a tool—a way of tending the structural knowledge that holds systems together.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Treating each problem as novel (cognitive exhaustion, reinvention, isolation) vs. recognizing recurring structures (risk of false analogy, over-application, mechanistic reuse).**

The tension is acute: Pattern recognition without stewardship leads to either exhaustion (each problem treated fresh) or rigidity (patterns copied without validation). Shared patterns are vulnerable: a template that solved one team's meeting dysfunction becomes cargo cult in another context. Private patterns compound privately—insight locked in one practitioner's mind dies with them or transfers only through osmosis. Patterns are also dangerous: a relational pattern from childhood that worked then (appeasement, overfunction, withdrawal) replicates silently across work relationships, decade after decade. The inverse risk is equally real: treating all pattern-spotting as premature abstraction, losing the wisdom that could transfer. The deepest problem is invisible: without a stewarding practice, patterns remain ambient and unexamined—operating as scripts rather than structural choices that can be consciously interrupted.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Pattern Stewardship is a four-stage discipline:

**1. Recognition & Naming**: Actively identify recurring structures—in your own behaviour, team dynamics, decision-making, systems. Name them precisely. Use journaling to surface the pattern, distinguish signal from narrative, test it across instances. Build a living catalogue: what is the context where this pattern emerges? What tensions does it resolve? What costs does it carry?

**2. Contextual Validation**: Before applying a recognized pattern to a new domain, interrogate its fit. A meeting template that works in synchronous teams may break in distributed async contexts. A relational pattern from family may not transfer to work. This is disciplined analogical humility—testing assumptions before commitment. Document what changes; what holds; what breaks.

**3. Cultivation & Interruption**: Patterns are alive, not fixed. Refine them through use. Make them explicit so others can test and adapt them. When a pattern no longer serves—when it becomes mechanical, when context shifts, when it enables harm—interrupt it consciously. Build new patterns in place of old ones. This is the evolutionary pressure that prevents pattern catalogues from calcifying into dogma.

**4. Visibility & Commons Contribution**: Share patterns across the practitioner community. Use pattern language: name the problem it resolves, the context where it applies, the forces at play, the solution structure, the consequences. Make your pattern catalogue visible (not as finished doctrine but as work-in-progress). Invite testing, critique, adaptation. This transforms private insight into stewarded knowledge that serves the commons—alive because others are using and adapting it, shared because the catalogue is accessible, just because patterns are available to all cognitive styles and domains, built to last because each application extends their life and refines their understanding.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start small. Choose one domain where you already notice repetition—how you handle conflict, how decisions get made in your team, how a particular kind of project tends to unfold. Spend two weeks observing without documenting. Notice the sequence, the trigger, the resolution. What actually happens versus what you tell yourself happens.

Then write it down. Not as theory. As a story. "When tension rises between individual autonomy and collective need, we've noticed three moves that hold: explicit naming of the tension itself (not solving it, just naming it), creating a bounded space where both needs are present without immediate resolution, then inviting a third perspective." Concrete. Testable. Anchored in lived example.

Next, find someone who's solved something similar in a different sector. A teacher who handles group dynamics, a facilitator of difficult conversations, a surgeon managing competing priorities in an operating room. Sit with them. Does your pattern land? Does it shift in their retelling? Document that shift.

Create a simple container for the pattern: name, the tension it holds, the context where it emerged, the moves involved, what you've noticed about when it works and when it doesn't, and what questions remain. Leave room for unknowing. A pattern library that claims completeness is already rigid.

Share it. Not as doctrine. As invitation. "This is what we've noticed. We're curious whether it holds in your context. What changes?" Invite others to adapt it, break it, add to it. When someone uses it and finds it breaks, that's not failure—that's the library growing more truthful.

Review it annually. Not to perfect it, but to notice what's changed. What did we miss? Where did we oversimplify? What new tensions emerged? A pattern is alive when it evolves with the communities that steward it.

### Section 5: Consequences

When a library of proven solutions grows, several things flourish. New practitioners can stand on the shoulders of experienced ones rather than reinventing from confusion. Institutional memory survives personnel changes. Teams spend less cognitive energy on recurring problems and more on what's genuinely novel. There's also a quieter flourishing: the relief of recognizing you're not alone in what you face, the generosity of sharing what took you years to learn, the sense that your struggle might benefit someone not yet born.

But unattended, several risks emerge. A pattern can become cargo cult—the form copied without the underlying understanding. Teams apply a solution because it worked elsewhere, not because they've diagnosed their own tension. The pattern library can calcify into orthodoxy, especially in hierarchical contexts where "this is how we do it" silences experimentation. There's also the risk of false analogy: two situations look similar on the surface but differ in crucial ways. The practitioner sees only surface and applies the wrong solution confidently.

Another subtle risk: pattern libraries can become another form of individualism, another way to believe that problems are solvable if you just have the right template. They can obscure the reality that some tensions can't be solved, only held. And they can concentrate power—whoever names and guards the patterns holds interpretive authority.

The antidote isn't to abandon the practice. It's to keep the library unsettled, to invite dissent and adaptation, to stay curious about failure, and to remember that a pattern is never more important than the particular human beings trying to live well together right now.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Brazilian healthcare worker networks (Parteiras tradicionais):** Traditional midwives in rural Brazil have stewarded patterns across generations—not written down, but embodied and tested. Patterns for recognizing complications, for calming fear, for working with limited resources. When NGOs tried documenting these as standardized protocols, they initially failed because they missed the relational dimension. The most effective adaptation came when health workers sat with experienced midwives, documented the pattern-in-context, and built in space for local adaptation. Now networks of health workers across four states use a hybrid library: documented knowledge that stays alive because it's regularly retested in practice.

**Japanese manufacturing (Toyota Production System):** The most sophisticated pattern library in industry. Not just documented, but embedded in training, in the physical workspace, in the expectation that workers will identify patterns and test variations. A production team notices that a particular sequence of moves reduces waste. They document it, teach it, then encourage others to break it and report back. The pattern library strengthens not because it's protected but because it's relentlessly examined and evolved.

**Open-source software communities:** GitHub repositories aren't just code; they're libraries of solutions to recurring problems. What makes them vital: they're forked, adapted, combined with other patterns, tested by thousands of contexts. When a pattern proves brittle, the community sees it immediately and either strengthens it or retires it. The patterns that persist are those that remain useful precisely because they're tested against radical diversity of use.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world of distributed intelligence—where AI systems can recognize patterns at scale, where practitioners can collaborate across continents, where knowledge can be versioned and traced—pattern stewardship changes shape but deepens in importance.

AI can accelerate recognition: scan your communications, your decisions, your interactions and flag recurring structures you might miss. This is genuinely useful. But it also concentrates a risk: patterns identified by machine learning are optimized for prediction, not for truth. An AI might surface a pattern in your team meetings that *predicts* who will speak up, without surfacing the *why*—the power dynamics, the silencing, the fear. The pattern becomes a tool of control rather than liberation.

This makes human stewardship more crucial, not less. The role shifts from solo recognition toward *collaborative interpretation*. A practitioner notices a pattern, an AI system surfaces similar patterns elsewhere, a community tests whether the analogy holds, distributed teams adapt and report back. The pattern library becomes genuinely collective—not owned by one organization but stewarded by a network.

There's also an inversion: as AI handles routine pattern-application, human practitioners can focus on the edges—the contexts where patterns break, where the pattern itself needs to evolve, where multiple patterns conflict and require wisdom rather than logic. The library becomes more useful precisely because it's less complete, because it leaves room for the irreducibly human work of judgment.

### Section 8: Vitality

A library of solutions is alive when patterns are regularly tested, adapted, and sometimes retired. When someone returns to add: "We tried this and it broke because..." When a pattern created in one sector gets translated to another and comes back transformed. When new practitioners can find what they need quickly, but also feel invited to question it.

Signs of decay: patterns hardening into rules. Patterns guarded rather than shared. A sense that the library is complete, authoritative, not to be questioned. Practitioners who use patterns without understanding the tensions they hold. Documentation that's so polished it loses the texture of real struggle. Success measured by adoption rather than by whether problems actually dissolved.

A diagnostic question: **When someone tried to use one of your patterns and it failed, did they feel permission to report that failure? And when they did, was the pattern revised or were they blamed for misapplication?**

If the answer is "they felt permission and the pattern evolved," the library is alive. If it's "they felt like they'd done it wrong," the library is already becoming museum.
