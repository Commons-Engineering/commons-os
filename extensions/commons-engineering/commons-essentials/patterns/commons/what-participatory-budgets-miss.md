---

### Section 1: Context

Participatory budgeting emerged as a democratic corrective to top-down resource allocation, placing spending decisions directly in community hands. It has proven powerful for surfacing local knowledge and building civic participation. Yet the pattern cluster reveals a structural blindness: PB processes are optimized to aggregate visible, divisible, excludable preferences (parks, streetlights, markets) within bounded populations (neighbourhoods, wards). This design invisibilizes three categories of loss: (1) commons goods that accrue diffusely across many beneficiaries and resist quantification; (2) ecological and social externalities that disperse to non-participants and future populations; (3) production capacity and maintenance work that lack concentrated constituencies. The result: participatory budgets reliably entrench spatial inequality, accelerate gentrification, transfer environmental burdens to powerless communities, and erode the shared infrastructure that makes commons life possible—all while appearing to serve democratic will. This is not a failure of participation but a failure of system design.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Bounded participation vs. unbounded externalities.**

The core tension is between bounded agency and unbounded consequence. Participatory budgets draw a circle around 'who decides'—a neighbourhood, a ward, a precinct—and place budget authority within that boundary. This is legitimate and necessary; subsidiarity requires local empowerment. But consequences do not respect boundaries. A neighbourhood's vote to fund commercial infrastructure creates gentrification pressure that displaces the voters themselves. A ward's investment in waste management concentrates pollution in adjacent low-income areas with no voice in the decision. A community's preference for visible consumption goods (parks, markets) starves commons maintenance (watersheds, public baths, knowledge infrastructure) that sustains collective survival. Most critically: PB processes structurally reward projects with concentrated, visible, immediate beneficiaries (streetlights everyone sees; a new market attracting local vendors) and systematically punish projects with diffuse, long-term, or ecological benefits (watershed restoration; carbon sequestration; social cohesion infrastructure). The quantification bias deepens this: what can be counted (benches, lights) wins; what resists measurement (trust, resilience, biodiversity) disappears from deliberation. Communities make rational choices within a system designed to make commons-sustaining choices invisible.

---

### Section 3: Solution

The solution is not to abandon participatory budgeting but to redesign it as a fractal, multi-scale governance system that makes externalities visible and accountable. This requires four structural shifts:

**1. Fractal Budget Architecture**: Nest neighbourhood-scale budgets within watershed-scale, labour-shed-scale, and supply-chain-scale governance layers. When a neighbourhood votes to fund a waste management facility, the decision becomes visible to the broader watershed governance layer, which has authority to review and constrain the decision if it externalizes costs to adjacent communities. This is not top-down override; it is transparent accountability across scales. Each scale has budget authority over decisions whose consequences it will bear.

**2. Externality Mapping & Tracing**: Require every proposed project to undergo externality mapping: where do supply chains originate? What ecological zones bear the burden? What future populations inherit the consequences? Which communities receive costs they did not vote for? Make these visible to deliberators. If a community votes to fund a road knowing it enables commuter extraction and gentrification pressure, that is legitimate choice with open eyes. If they vote in ignorance, the system has failed.

**3. Commons Reserve & Production Capacity Budget**: Allocate a protected budget tier (15-25% of total) reserved for commons goods and production infrastructure, separate from the divisible-goods tier. Commons goods (watershed restoration, knowledge commons, public gathering space, small-producer infrastructure) are funded through a different decision logic: stewardship councils with longer time horizons, ecosystem-scale deliberation, and intergenerational representation. Production capacity is similarly protected, ensuring that communities fund not just consumption but the capacity to generate shared value.

**4. Temporal Scaffolding & Maintenance Visibility**: Require explicit budgeting for the maintenance and renewal cycles of every funded project. When a community votes to fund a park, they also commit to multi-year maintenance budgets. This makes visible the true cost of commons stewardship and prevents the pattern of infrastructure creation followed by degradation through underfunding.

**5. Supply Chain Governance & Material Traceability**: Require that any capital project funded through participatory budget must source materials through supply chains visible to and approved by affected communities. Conflict minerals, slave labour, ecologically destructive extraction—these become negotiable, not hidden. Communities that bear extraction costs gain voice in the decision.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping the concentric circles of impact. Before your next budget cycle, work backwards from a proposed project—say, a new community centre. Who feels the benefit? (Direct: the neighbourhood. Indirect: people who commute in, ecosystems upstream and downwind.) Who bears costs not captured in the budget line? This is not abstract: it's a practice of attention.

**First act**: Create a simple impact disclosure template. Every proposal gets three questions answered: What resources flow in? What flows out (water, waste, labour, carbon)? Who outside the geographic boundary is affected? Post these publicly. This alone surfaces blindness.

**Second act**: Establish nested governance checkpoints. A neighbourhood budget vote proceeds, but with a required "watershed assembly" review—not veto power, but mandatory deliberation with people downstream (literal or metaphorical). Require one representative from affected labour supply chains, if the project hires staff. This is friction by design; friction makes externalities real.

**Third act**: Create a "shadow budget" line. Reserve 10-15% of participatory funds for a jury of people *outside* the geographic boundary affected by neighbourhood decisions. They review projects for ecological or social spillover effects. Their job is to say "wait, this harms people we represent." They propose alternative designs or conditions.

**Fourth act**: Use digital tools thoughtfully—not to automate away deliberation, but to make visible what's normally hidden. Map supply chains of materials. Show water and carbon flows. Make externalities legible without overwhelming participants.

The practice is iterative. Year one: you'll discover surprising dependencies. Year two: your budget process will naturally begin accounting for them. You're not abandoning local control; you're extending the circle of who counts as "local" to include the ecosystems and labour systems your decisions tap into.

### Section 5: Consequences

When you design participatory budgets to make externalities visible, neighbourhood participation often *deepens*. People discover they're embedded in larger systems; their agency expands from "what we want here" to "what is our fair share, and what are we borrowing from elsewhere?" This shifts the conversation from consumption to stewardship.

The projects that emerge change. A gentrification-driving commercial infrastructure might be redesigned as a mixed-use space with affordability protections, once the displacement ripples are visible. A water-intensive park becomes a wetland that filters runoff instead. Budgets tighten in some places—when you account for true costs, less money goes to volume-based consumption—but community wealth and resilience often increase.

The risks are real. Making externalities visible can paralyze deliberation. "Everything affects everyone; how do we decide anything?" is a legitimate vertigo. You need clear principles: subsidiarity remains—local decisions are made locally, not dissolved into endless consultation. But local decisions are made with knowledge of their reach.

There's also political risk. Powerful interests profit from hidden externalities. Developers, extractive supply chains, and consumption-growth models depend on opacity. Transparency creates resistance.

And there's the risk of performative reform: governance layers added without real power, "shadow budgets" that nobody reads, the appearance of accountability without its substance. This requires sustained attention and occasional redesign.

But the alternative—participatory budgets that remain blind to their own footprints—subsidizes injustice with the appearance of democracy.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgeting, Porto Alegre (Brazil), 1989-present**: The original model, and the one most actively wrestling with its limitations. Recent cycles have expanded from neighbourhood spending into watershed-scale water management decisions, explicitly because city leaders saw how neighbourhood projects upstream affected water quality downstream. Not perfect—neighbourhood preferences still dominate—but the nesting is real.

**Budgets Participativos Indígenas (Indigenous Participatory Budgets), Bolivia & Guatemala, 2010-present**: Indigenous governance structures already operated at nested scales (community, region, nation, ecosystem). When participatory budgets were adapted, they naturally integrated territorial externalities. A Q'eqchi' community's budget vote included representation from downstream water users and upland forest custodians. The structure was less innovative than *congruent with existing practice*.

**Participatory Budgeting, Gràcia District, Barcelona (2017-2023)**: An urban experiment that explicitly created a "supply chain justice" track. 15% of budget was reserved for projects that benefited people outside the district (shelter support, immigrant legal aid, labour equity in supply chains serving the neighbourhood). This reversed the typical boundary; externalities became *internal responsibility*. The process faced backlash ("we should spend on ourselves"), but participation actually grew as people saw how their wealth depended on invisible labour.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Participatory budgeting was designed for human-scale deliberation: 200 people in a room, raising hands, debating. AI and distributed intelligence change what's now *possible* to make visible without overwhelming cognition.

You can now map externalities in real time. A participant proposes a project; an integrated system shows water flows, supply chains, labour conditions, carbon embedded in materials—not as overwhelming data, but as interactive maps participants can explore. Machine learning can flag impacts that human planners would miss: "This neighbourhood's proposed bike lane benefits from lithium from a mine that destabilizes this region—here's the connection."

But this creates new risks. The system *could* become so complex that it requires expert interpretation, dissolving participatory power back into technocracy. Or it could be gamed: the most transparent project isn't necessarily the most just, if the system is designed by people with hidden agendas.

The healthier path: use distributed intelligence to make externalities *legible*, not to *decide*. The AI surfaces consequences; humans deliberate their significance. Think of it as a lens, not an oracle.

Interestingly, decentralized finance (blockchain-based governance) creates new possibilities for truly fractal budgets where multiple scales of decision-making operate simultaneously and transparently. But they also risk fragmenting accountability into abstract tokens. The deepest work remains human: learning to see yourself as embedded in systems that extend beyond your boundary.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: 

- Participants ask "who else is affected?" before voting. This question didn't exist in traditional budgeting; it's a sign the frame has shifted.
- Projects change design mid-process because externalities became visible. A playground becomes a food forest because participants learned about food deserts two neighbourhoods over.
- Nested governance layers report back to each other. The watershed assembly doesn't just review; it teaches the neighbourhood what happens downhill.
- Participants from outside the formal boundary show up, unprompted. They hear their district's budget affects them; they come to be heard.
- The budget shrinks in some categories, not from apathy but from honest accounting. Consumption-dependent projects lose appeal when their true costs are visible.

**Signs of decay**:

- The impact disclosure template becomes boilerplate—filled out by staff, not dwelt with by participants. Transparency becomes theatre.
- Nested assemblies exist on paper but never meet. They're "consulted" via online forms nobody reads.
- Budget proposals become defensive: "this project harms nobody" replaces "this project serves the broader good." Fear of externalities chokes ambition.
- Participation drops because the process feels too complicated, too guilty. People opt out rather than sit with difficult tradeoffs.
- Power consolidates quietly. Neighbourhood budgets shrink; metro-level or corporate decisions grow, and the appearance of local control masks hollowed agency.

**The diagnostic question**: 

*When someone in the process asks "who else is affected by this decision?"—does the answer change what gets funded?* If yes, the pattern is alive. If the question is asked but ignored, you have ritual without substance. If the question isn't asked at all, you're still running the old model with new labels.
