### Section 1: Context

Every living commons faces a recurring paradox: it must distribute decision-making to survive (because local actors have better information and faster feedback), yet coordination requires some clarity about who chooses what. In young commons, this clarity often emerges informally—through reputation, trust, or the founder's gravitational pull. But informal authority is invisible authority. It breeds resentment when expectations misalign, creates bottlenecks when the authority-holder is absent, and invites ambiguity exploitation (people operating in the shadows of unclear rules rather than openly renegotiating them). Formal hierarchies solve this visibility problem but often crush the adaptive responsiveness that makes commons valuable. The tension is not between formal and informal—it is between visible and invisible authority. This pattern draws from Subsidiarity (decisions at the lowest competent level), Polycentric Organisation (multiple decision-making centers operating simultaneously), and Conflict Resolution (the reality that unclear authority is latent conflict).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Distributed autonomy vs. coordinated coherence—how to preserve individual and local agency while ensuring decisions don't fragment the commons or duplicate effort..**

Authority ambiguity creates three cascading harms. First, decision paralysis: without clarity, people defer upward or seek consensus on matters that could be delegated, clogging the system with unnecessary escalation. Second, duplicated or contradictory decisions: when authority boundaries are unclear, multiple actors may decide the same thing, or their choices may collide. Third, resentment through mismatched expectations: one person believes they are consulting; another believes they are being informed; a third believes they have veto power. These mismatches feel like betrayal and erode the reciprocity that holds commons together. The problem deepens because authority is never purely formal—it also flows through relationships, expertise, sponsorship, and historical practice. Ignoring the informal layer produces a map that does not describe reality. But leaving it entirely informal means new participants, scaled work, and succession all become crises. The commons needs authority that is simultaneously explicit enough to guide action and flexible enough to adapt as context shifts.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Authority Stewardship Mapping is a systematic practice of making visible, challenging, and evolving the decision rights that hold a commons together. It operates across four parallel layers:

**Layer 1: Decision Domain Mapping.** Identify the categories of choice your commons makes: financial, strategic (direction and scope), cultural (values and norms), operational (daily work), and structural (governance itself). For each domain, distinguish which decisions are:
- Unilateral (one person or role chooses without consultation)
- Delegated (a person or role chooses within specified bounds; escalation required if bounds are exceeded)
- Consultative (one person decides but seeks input from named others)
- Consensus or consent-based (choice requires agreement or absence of blocking objections)
- Voted (choice follows democratic procedure)

**Layer 2: Subsidiarity Mapping.** For each decision type, determine the appropriate level: which decisions belong at the edge (individual or team), which at the local commons center, which at a larger coordination layer? Document escalation paths: under what conditions does a decision move upward?

**Layer 3: Consent Architecture.** For consensus or consent-based decisions, make explicit: Who has voice (who is consulted)? Who has veto (who can block)? What counts as resolved objection? What is the timeline? How do you handle persistent disagreement? This prevents consent from becoming hidden veto power held by the loudest or most senior voice.

**Layer 4: Periodic Stewardship Review.** Authority mapping is not a static document. Every 6-12 months (or when major conflict surfaces), the commons gathers to revisit the map: Which decisions took longer than expected? Where did authority become invisible again? Where did authority collapse (decisions unmade because no one claimed responsibility)? What has the commons learned about its own scale and competence? Revise the map deliberately, with full participation.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by convening a small stewardship circle—5 to 12 people who represent different roles and perspectives in your commons. Their task is not to *design* authority from scratch, but to *surface and name* the authority that already exists, often invisibly.

**First act: Domain inventory.** List every category of decision your commons actually makes. Don't start abstract. Ask: What got decided this week? Who decided it? Was anyone surprised? Cluster these decisions into domains—budget, membership, land use, conflict, strategy, cultural norms. You'll find some domains have clear gatekeepers; others are foggy.

**Second act: Consent mapping.** For each domain, ask: Who *can* decide? Who *must* consent*? Who deserves *input*? Who gets *information*? This is not majority rule or consensus—it's clarity about who holds what kind of voice. A land steward might decide site-specific practices unilaterally. A finance keeper might decide spending under $500, but anything over $5,000 needs collective consent. A cultural question might require input from elders or affected communities before any choice is locked in.

**Third act: Authority boundaries.** Mark where decision authority *stops*. This prevents mission creep. The operations team decides *how* to run a program; they don't decide whether the program exists (that's strategic). A working group decides their own meeting times; they don't decide everyone else's calendar.

**Fourth act: Escalation paths.** When authority isn't clear, or when a decision affects multiple domains, how does it climb the system? Define triggers—financial thresholds, community impact, precedent-setting choices—that automatically bump a decision upward for collective reflection.

Write this down. Not as a rigid org chart, but as a living document: "In our commons, budget decisions under $1,000 rest with the treasurer. Between $1,000 and $10,000, they decide with operations input. Over $10,000, the full circle consents." Keep it scannable. Revisit it quarterly.

### Section 5: Consequences

When authority is mapped with care, several things flourish. Decisions accelerate because people move without endless checking-in. Resentment softens because people understand the *logic* of who chose, not just the outcome. New members find a stable ground to stand on. And paradoxically, distributed authority strengthens because it's not secretly wielded—it's transparent, which makes it easier to challenge and evolve.

The pattern also creates room for *leadership without heroism*. A person can hold real authority—deciding things that matter—without being expected to know everything or carry the whole system. That's sustainable.

But unmapped authority breeds its shadow consequences. If authority remains vague, informal gatekeepers (often the loudest or most connected people) will fill the void, and their power will feel illegitimate because no one named it. Decision-makers burn out from unclear scope. Communities downstream from a decision discover they should have been consulted *after* the choice was made. Trustworthy people lose trust because the system is opaque, even if their choices were sound.

There's also a risk of *ossification*: mapping authority can calcify it, making it harder to adapt. The cure is cyclical review—authority structures should be re-examined when membership shifts, when the commons faces new scale or complexity, or when someone raises a friction. Not constant change, but deliberate evolution.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Mondragon Cooperatives (Spain).** Decision authority is radically explicit. The General Assembly holds ultimate authority on strategy and major resource allocation. Elected councils handle operations. Working groups decide their own workflow. Within this structure, any worker can propose changes through formal channels. Authority is nested and clear, which is why Mondragon has sustained democratic governance across 80+ businesses for 70 years.

**Ubuntu Ubuntu Land Trust (South Africa).** In post-apartheid land redistribution, they mapped authority across three layers: *land stewards* decide daily practices on their plots; a *land committee* handles boundaries, conflicts, and newcomers; the *broader collective* consents on who joins and strategic direction. This prevented the common pattern where absent external authority floods in—because internal authority was visible and legitimate.

**The Wikimedia Foundation Governance.** They explicitly separated *community decision-making* (volunteers decide content policy) from *operational decision-making* (staff decide hiring and budget process). The separation isn't perfect, but naming it reduced the invisible power struggles that plague volunteer-staff collaborations. They also created escalation: community members dissatisfied with staff decisions have a clear appeal path.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI and distributed intelligence reshape how commons work, Authority Stewardship becomes both easier and trickier.

Easier: You can now *visualize* authority structures in real time. A smart system can flag when two people are deciding the same thing, or when a decision needs escalation based on pre-set rules. You can simulate authority scenarios before implementing them. Decision logs become automatically searchable, so people can find *why* something was decided, not just *what*.

Trickier: AI systems themselves now make recommendations or semi-autonomous choices within a commons. Who decides whether to *trust* an algorithm's output? A human chain of command can be mapped. But when a machine learning model surfaces options, or automates a routine choice, the authority becomes diffused. You need new categories: *Who decides what gets automated?* *Who audits algorithmic recommendations?* *Who can override an automated choice, and how?*

The pattern's strength here is clarity. If you've already mapped human authority well, you can deliberately insert AI as a *tool within a domain*, not as a replacement for authority. The land steward still decides; the AI just surfaces relevant data faster.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of vitality:** People make decisions at the right level—fast, without unnecessary escalation. New members can quickly understand how to move things forward. When conflict arises, people disagree on *substance*, not on who had the right to decide. Authority is used, not hidden. When someone says "that's not my call," others accept it without resentment because the boundary is known.

**Signs of decay:** Decisions cluster at the top, even for small matters. People start working around authority ("I'll just talk to Sarah instead of following the process"). Authority is wielded but never named—insiders know the real power structure, outsiders guess. When challenged, authority-holders say "I have the experience" or "that's how we've always done it" instead of pointing to agreed-on logic. New decisions contradict old ones because no one remembers who decided or why.

**Diagnostic question:** If you asked five members of your commons "Who decides whether we accept a new member?", would they give the same answer? If yes, authority is visible. If you get five different answers, it's vague. If people pause and say "I'm not sure," it's invisible. The depth of that uncertainty tells you where to start mapping.
