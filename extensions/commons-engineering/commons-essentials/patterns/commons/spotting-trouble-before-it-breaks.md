> Plan your response to crises now, while things are stable. Identify early warning signs, prepare what you'll do at each 

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every living system — human nervous systems, relationships, communities, organisations — experiences cycles of stability and dysregulation. The classical error is to treat crisis as random catastrophe requiring emergency heroics. In reality, most crises follow recognisable escalation patterns: subtle warning signs (mood shift, isolation, sleep change, thought pattern drift), early-stage acceleration (increased symptoms, narrowing coping options), acute crisis (overwhelm, shutdown, loss of agency), and recovery or deterioration. Systems that endure design their response *during* stability, not during activation. This pattern draws from Information Shapes Systems (regulation depends on early detection) and Carrying Capacity (crises emerge when load exceeds adaptive capacity, and intervention at early thresholds preserves remaining capacity for actual recovery).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Reactive crisis response (exhausting, dangerous, late) vs. Proactive staged intervention (designed during calm, precise, preserves capacity).**

The tension is acute: crisis feels sudden and demands immediate, intense response. But by the time crisis feels urgent, multiple intervention opportunities have passed. Early warning signs are subtle — a small change in sleep, a shift in social withdrawal, a single anxious thought — and easy to dismiss. Response during acute crisis is chaotic: decision-making fails under stress, support systems become reactive and fractured, shame spirals compound the original dysregulation, and recovery is prolonged and traumatic. Worse, each crisis without a protocol teaches the system (and those supporting it) that dysregulation leads to chaos, making future crises more terrifying and harder to navigate. The trapped choice is between premature over-response (treating every warning sign as an emergency) and dangerous under-response (waiting for certainty before acting).

---

### Section 3: Solution

Design a staged crisis protocol during periods of stability, collaboratively with all participants (individual, helpers, support network, medical providers if relevant). The protocol has three parts: (1) **Staging Framework**: Map the specific escalation sequence *as it manifests for this person/system*. Not generic stages, but lived ones. Early stage: specific warning signs (which thoughts, behaviours, sensations appear first?), how long this stage typically lasts, what capacity remains. Mid-stage: acceleration markers (isolation deepens, coping narrower, insight begins to cloud), what interventions remain feasible. Acute stage: crisis definition (what constitutes a genuine emergency requiring immediate support), what safety steps are non-negotiable. (2) **Tiered Interventions**: For each stage, pre-design specific, concrete responses triggered *automatically* when warning signs appear — not requiring willpower or decision-making under stress. Early stage: micro-interventions (grounding technique, trusted call, journaling prompt, environment change). Mid-stage: medium-intensity responses (temporarily reduced demands, increased support contact, possibly professional consultation). Acute stage: pre-agreed safety protocols (emergency contact, medical support, crisis line, who decides what). Include communication scripts so supporters know exactly what to say and do. (3) **Recovery Sequence**: Most important: design what happens *after* crisis stabilises. A relapse often comes not from the crisis itself but from premature re-escalation of demands, shame-driven isolation, or loss of the support that was activated. The protocol includes graduated return-to-normal (what does day 1, week 1, month 1 look like?), non-shame reengagement (how to re-enter without catastrophising the setback), and protocol refinement (what did we learn? update the staging, update the interventions). Review and update this protocol every 6-12 months or after each real activation, treating each crisis as data that improves the next response. The protocol is alive: it adapts as the system learns its own patterns.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

The cultivation begins in calm. Gather the people who matter—the person at risk, trusted supporters, relevant professionals—and map together. Not in a clinical setting necessarily. A kitchen table works. A video call works. The goal is collaborative mapping, not diagnosis.

**Step One: Escalation Mapping.** Walk backward from the worst moment the person has experienced. What came just before? A week before? A month? Don't sanitise it. One person might say, "I stop answering texts." Another: "I start researching escape routes." Another: "I get this brittle cheerfulness." Write these down. These are *your* early warnings, not someone else's textbook symptoms. Add the middle stage: "I'm isolating more, sleeping less, thoughts are speeding up." And the acute stage: "I can't sit still, I'm convinced everything's falling apart, I've stopped eating."

**Step Two: Response Design.** For each stage, what actually helps *this* person? Not what helps people generally. Some people need gentle company. Others need solitude. Some need structure imposed from outside. Others need permission to slow down. Write specific, concrete actions: "At early stage: friend calls weekly, no pressure to talk, just company." "At middle stage: I see my therapist twice weekly, I text my safety person each evening, I avoid alcohol." "At acute stage: I go to the hospital, or we activate the crisis team, or I stay with [person's name]." Make it specific enough that someone could actually do it at 2 a.m.

**Step Three: Responsibility Clarity.** Who notices? Who responds at each stage? Write names, not roles. "Sarah notices my mood shift. Tom helps me structure my days. Dr. Chen sees me in crisis. My sister coordinates." This prevents the paralysis of wondering "whose job is this?"

**Step Four: Practice and Revision.** The protocol isn't a prophecy. It's a living document. When a smaller crisis comes—and it will—use it. Notice what worked. Revise. Small tests build confidence and realism.

**Step Five: Access and Remind.** Keep it where people can actually find it. Not filed away. Visible. Share it with everyone named in it. Remind people it exists every few months. A protocol nobody remembers is just paper.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, several things flourish. The person at risk moves from shame and secrecy into agency—they're collaborating on their own stability, not being managed. Support networks become competent rather than reactive; people know what to do instead of freezing in panic. The system develops *granularity*: small interventions at early stages prevent escalation to devastating ones. A weekly call at stage one might prevent a hospitalisation at stage three.

Crises still come. But they're shorter, less severe, less likely to destroy relationships or leave lasting damage. The person recovers faster because people aren't scrambling; they're executing a plan. Confidence builds—both for the person and for their supporters.

The risks are real if this pattern isn't tended. A protocol designed once and never revised becomes useless; people change, circumstances shift, what worked last year might not work now. Without explicit responsibility names, the protocol becomes abstract—everyone assumes someone else is watching. The person might feel infantilised if the protocol becomes too controlling, if stages are imposed rather than co-created. And there's a subtle risk: if early intervention always works, there's temptation to intervene at smaller and smaller moments, turning life into constant management rather than flourishing with vulnerability intact.

The deepest risk is false confidence. A good protocol reduces crisis severity; it doesn't eliminate it. Pretending it will can lead to betrayed trust when crisis still comes, when someone still struggles despite the plan.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Individual mental health.** The WRAP plan (Wellness Recovery Action Plan), developed by Mary Ellen Copeland in the US, is exactly this: a staged crisis protocol that people with mental illness design with supporters. It maps triggers, early warning signs, middle-stage coping strategies, and crisis response. Thousands of people use it to prevent hospitalisation and manage their own recovery.

**Indigenous fire management.** Australian Aboriginal fire practice, refined over millennia, follows this pattern precisely. Rather than waiting for catastrophic wildfires, communities set small, controlled burns during specific seasons when conditions allow precise intervention. They've mapped the escalation sequence of fire behaviour and intervene at early stages. This prevents the climate-driven megafires now devastating lands where this practice was suppressed. The "protocol" is held in story, law, and practice, refined continuously.

**Family and relationship crisis.** The Gottman Method, developed by John and Julie Gottman, identifies early warning signs of relationship breakdown (contempt, criticism, defensiveness, stonewalling) and teaches couples to intervene at those stages. Couples design together what "turning toward" their partner means for them specifically, what repair attempts look like, how to de-escalate their particular pattern. The staging is recognisable; the response is custom.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence change what's possible here, but not the core insight: human systems still escalate in recognisable patterns that respond to early intervention.

What shifts: *Pattern recognition becomes distributed.* A person might wear a sensor that tracks physiological escalation (heart rate variability, sleep, movement patterns). A language model might analyse their writing or message frequency for subtle shifts in thought patterns. Their support network might get gentle alerts—not alarms, alerts—suggesting an early-stage intervention might help now. This isn't surveillance; it's an external nervous system, an extension of self-awareness.

The risk is real: outsourcing crisis detection to algorithms erodes embodied knowledge. If a person stops learning to recognise their own warning signs because an app does it, they lose agency. If an algorithm flags "risk" based on patterns it can't explain, it overrides human judgment.

The opportunity: scaled collaboration. A person can share their escalation pattern anonymously with others in their community who have similar conditions. Collectively, they refine understanding of early interventions. A therapist gets a richer picture of what actually helps because they're integrating thousands of refined protocols, not working from textbook stages.

The pattern remains: calm mapping, specific staging, clear response, continuous revision. Technology amplifies sensing and coordination. It doesn't replace the human work of designing what helps.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

A living protocol shows these signs: people actually reference it. In crisis, someone says, "I'm in stage two, I need X." Support people say, "That matches what we planned." The protocol changes every few months because conditions have taught something new. People talk about it not as burden but as relief—"I know what happens and I know we have a plan."

A protocol in decline shows different signs: it hasn't been mentioned in months, even though smaller crises have come. It's become a document rather than a living conversation. When real crisis comes, people say, "I forgot we had a plan," or worse, "The plan didn't work," without bothering to revise it. Roles blur again—nobody's quite sure who's responsible. The person at risk starts keeping their struggles secret again, reasoning, "The protocol just means people will worry."

The diagnostic question cuts to the core: *When a small warning sign appears, do people notice it and do something? Or does everyone pretend it's not there until crisis is undeniable?*

If the first is happening, your protocol is alive. If the second, the pattern has become mere decoration. Return to calm. Map again. Rebuild trust in small interventions. The protocol works only if people believe early action matters—and they believe it by seeing it work.
