> Power shapes every commons, and hidden power corrodes trust. Learn to name it, analyze it together, and redistribute it 

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every commons is a power system. Formal authority, expertise, income, identity, access to networks—these are the real currents that move resources and shape whose voice counts. In living systems, power is not a problem to eliminate; it's a flow to steward. The Orbit 1 principles name this clearly: Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation require that communities understand how power shapes exchange; Collective Action demands visibility into who actually decides; Visibility as Commons Act insists that power patterns be made discussible; and Polycentric Organisation assumes power is distributed, not concentrated. Yet most commons practitioners inherit habits from hierarchical systems where power was either wielded covertly (as authority) or denied (as egalitarian myth). The result: power dynamics silently corrupt relationships, resources flow to the already-connected, and marginalized participants withdraw. This pattern teaches stewards to make power visible, name it honestly, and redistribute it deliberately—not once, but continuously.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Invisible power (silent accumulation, structural dominance, unexamined privilege) vs. Stewarded power (named, audited, redistributed for justice).**

Power dynamics are structural, relational, and often invisible. A facilitator's expertise silences quieter voices. A funder's income shapes whose ideas get implemented. A member's network access becomes gatekeeping. Historical privilege makes some identities automatically credible. Unexamined power creates three failures: (1) Silent accumulation—those with initial advantage compound it without accountability; (2) Structural harm—marginalized participants absorb costs (emotional labor, exclusion, unheard voice) while privileged participants extract value; (3) Fragility—when power imbalances are not named, they generate resentment that corrodes trust. Communities that deny power differences produce false belonging: some members are centered, others remain peripheral, and everyone pretends it's natural. The deeper problem: naming power feels risky. It requires acknowledging complicity, privilege, and inequality. Ignoring power feels safer. But silence makes power toxic.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Power Literacy as Stewardship is a continuous cycle of visibility, naming, and redistribution. It unfolds in three nested layers:

**LAYER 1: MAKE POWER VISIBLE**
Regularly map power flows in your commons. Not as abstraction but as concrete channels: Who speaks in meetings and whose voice shapes decisions? Whose labor is invisible (emotional, organizational, relational)? Who controls resources, time, access to networks? Who is believed? Whose interests are served by current structures? This is not blame-naming; it is systems diagnosis. Use frameworks: Power Mapping (who holds leverage over key decisions?), Power Audit (where does power concentrate?), Power Asymmetry Legibility (make visible without judgment). Document patterns. Notice who is centered and who is peripheral. Track whose ideas are implemented. Measure voice share, decision influence, resource access, opportunity cost.

**LAYER 2: NAME POWER DELIBERATELY**
Power becomes discussible only when named aloud. Deliberately invent or reclaim language that makes power patterns visible and changeable. Examples: "This decision was made by people with financial security; people precarious in income face different constraints." "My expertise in this domain gives me formal authority, but your lived experience holds knowledge I lack." "This group includes three people with access to major funders; that access shapes what gets proposed." Name formal authority (roles, titles), informal influence (trust, network centrality, expertise credibility), status (identity privilege, education, professional credentials), and coercive dynamics (financial control, time extraction, emotional labor expectation). Do this in meetings, in planning, in conflict. Make power a discussible design variable, not a hidden current.

**LAYER 3: REDISTRIBUTE POWER STRUCTURALLY**
Naming alone changes nothing without structural redistribution. Concretely: Rotate facilitation. Redistribute decision-making authority (bring marginalized participants into governance roles, not just consultation). Create time and space for quiet voices (structured rounds, written input, async participation). Audit labor distribution—who does invisible work?—and redistribute. Create financial transparency so income disparities don't become invisible leverage. Establish accountability mechanisms: Who has power? To whom are they accountable? How is that enforced? Design decision-making processes that account for power imbalance (e.g., decisions affecting precarious workers require their consent, not majority vote). Create conflict resolution processes that surface power dynamics before they become abuse. Make redistribution ongoing, not one-time: "Every quarter, we audit who influences decisions and correct imbalances we find."

**THE CYCLE**
These layers repeat: visibility reveals imbalances, naming makes them discussible, redistribution changes structures, which then reveals new imbalances. This is not failure; this is adaptation. A commons that practices power literacy recognizes that justice is not a destination but a practice—continuous, collective, built into how the community stewards itself.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming power in plain language, together. Create a "power audit" as a shared practice, not a judgment. Gather your core group—ideally monthly or quarterly—and ask: Who decided this? Whose absence shapes us? Whose work goes unremunerated? Whose discomfort are we ignoring? Document answers in a shared document. This sounds simple because it is; the cultivation lies in repetition and honesty.

Make rotation non-negotiable. If one person facilitates every meeting, expertise pools and influence calcifies. Build a facilitation schedule three months ahead. Pair experienced facilitators with newer members. Create written facilitation guides so no person becomes indispensable. Rotate decision-making roles—budget keeper, conflict resolver, agenda-setter—every 6-12 months. Friction appears. That's the signal that power is moving; tend it rather than smooth it away.

Redistribute labor visibility. Create a commons labor ledger that names all work—not for surveillance, but for recognition. Who organized the event? Who listened to conflict? Who did the emotional processing? Who maintained relationships? Pay attention to gendered, racialized, and age-based patterns of invisible labor. Some commons use "labor circles" where members name tasks they've carried and explicitly choose who supports them next. This moves work from invisible to chosen.

Build transparency into structure, not rhetoric. If your commons controls a budget, publish it. If decisions are made by a subcommittee, rotate membership or broadcast the deliberation. If expertise matters (technical, relational, domain-specific), name it and create pathways for others to develop it. When someone needs special authority—a licensed facilitator, a person with security clearance—say so plainly and set an expiration date on their power.

Create grievance channels that are genuinely safe. Power analysis without accountability breeds cynicism. Build confidential ways for members to raise concerns: a trusted elder, a rotating ombudsperson, an anonymous form reviewed by a small group. Make remedies possible—apologies, practice changes, restitution, or separation if necessary. And follow through.

### Section 5: Consequences

When power is stewarded with visibility, several things flourish. Trust grows because people believe their concerns will be heard and addressed. New voices emerge—not from coercion but from genuine safety. The work feels lighter because invisible labor is named and its burden is shared. Decisions stick longer because more minds helped shape them. Accountability becomes a feature, not a threat; people stay because they know they can be seen and addressed.

But power analysis without care breeds other shadows. Hypervigilance can emerge—members exhausted by constant examination of who's getting what. Some people retreat into silence rather than risk being named as "problematic." Transparency can become performance; a commons that audits power loudly while still gatekeeping decisions behind closed doors breeds deeper cynicism than quiet opacity. The work of power literacy is relentless; if you stop tending it, old patterns resurface within months.

There's also the risk of false equivalence. Not all power is equivalent; an elder's wisdom-power differs from a funder's resource-power, which differs from a facilitator's role-power. Flattening these into identical categories can erase legitimate forms of authority and make the commons less effective. Stewardship means knowing which forms of power can rotate freely, which need boundaries, and which deserve deeper trust precisely because they've earned it.

The deepest risk: power analysis without love. Communities that name power coldly, as a technique of control, fragment. This work flourishes only when underneath the naming is genuine care for each person's dignity and a commitment that redistribution serves the whole, not just ideological purity.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Emergent Strategy Facilitation (Movement for Black Lives, US):** Adrienne Maree Brown and others developed facilitation practices that name power explicitly in activist spaces. Rotated facilitator roles, intentional attention to who's not speaking, and "co-conspirator" language that acknowledges interdependence rather than hiding unequal access to resources or safety. Power audits happen in debrief circles after actions.

**Amartya Sen's Capabilities Approach & Village Deliberation (West Bengal, India):** Sen's framework treats power as the capacity to deliberate and choose. In West Bengal, participatory budgeting processes explicitly map whose voices shaped budget allocation and rotate decision-making membership across castes and genders. Power literacy here means understanding how historical exclusion requires active redistribution, not just inviting everyone.

**Diggers' Alliance (Tanzania & Kenya, East Africa):** Agricultural commons networks use monthly "power-sharing councils" where decisions about land access, water rights, and labor are made by rotated groups of farmers, elders, and younger members. They explicitly audit whose voice shapes grazing rights and whose labor (especially women's) is recognized in distribution. They also maintain elder authority on certain traditional matters while rotating civic decision-making.

All three practice power literacy not as a one-time audit but as seasonal cultivation. All three experience friction when power redistributes; they stay with it rather than resolve it too quickly.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI enters commons spaces—algorithms allocating resources, bots facilitating decisions, data platforms capturing member behavior—power literacy becomes more urgent and more slippery.

An algorithm for "fair" resource distribution seems neutral but encodes the assumptions of whoever trained it. A bot that summarizes member input shapes which voices get amplified. Distributed ledger systems for decision-making promise transparency but often obscure who controls the technical layer. Power analysis must now include algorithmic literacy: Who designed this system? What data does it capture? Whose interests does it serve? Can we audit it together?

The inverse also emerges: AI can surface power flows invisible to human observation. Sentiment analysis of meeting transcripts reveals whose voice dominates. Network graphs show communication gatekeepers. Data on uncompensated labor becomes undeniable. This is opportunity—if used for redistribution, not surveillance.

The deeper shift: as coordination spreads across platforms, the commons needs stewards who hold *relational* power—people who understand the human texture of the group and can name when technology is eroding trust. This actually increases the importance of human power literacy and makes it safer to be transparent about which decisions require full-group deliberation and which can be distributed.

### Section 8: Vitality

A commons with living power literacy shows recognizable signs. Conflict surfaces quickly and people stay to work through it. Newer members speak up within weeks, not years. Labor gets named and people volunteer for it. When someone accumulates influence, others name it without shame—"You've been deciding too much; let's redistribute." There's laughter, friction, repair. The emotional temperature is honest.

Signs of decay are quieter. Fewer people speak in meetings, and the same voices dominate. Decisions happen before the meeting in side conversations. People leave without saying why. Work that should be visible—emotional labor, relationship maintenance, conflict processing—stays invisible. An elder or funder is deferred to silently. New members watch for months before speaking. The commons looks harmonious but feels lifeless.

**Diagnostic question:** If you suddenly removed the person with the most informal influence in your commons (the person whose opinion shifts outcomes, whose absence changes the room), could the group still function? Could decisions still get made with the same legitimacy? If the answer is no, power has pooled. If the answer is yes, power is stewarded. Ask this question together once a year. The conversation itself is the cultivation.
