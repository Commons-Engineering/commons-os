### Section 1: Context

Every municipality, institution, and large employer wields purchasing power—often 20-50% of annual budget. That power is inert capital unless it is designed. Historically, procurement has been treated as a cost centre: minimize spend, accelerate delivery, outsource management to algorithms and RFP processes. This treats suppliers as interchangeable units competing downward on price. The result: local contractors are displaced by distant firms; workers absorb wage suppression and unsafe conditions; community enterprises cannot compete; algorithmic systems systematize incumbent bias while appearing neutral. The lineage is clear: this pattern addresses Collective Action (how shared purchasing becomes shared power), Economic Constraint (revealing opportunity cost—what we don't build when we extract from suppliers), Reciprocity (procurement as two-way obligation, not extraction), and Subsidiarity (decisions about local spending should rest with those who live with the consequences).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Procurement as extraction (lowest cost, vendor lock-in, displacement) vs. procurement as cultivation (justice, local agency, living systems).**

The core conflict: procurement systems optimize for a single dimension (cost, speed, risk reduction) while externalizing costs onto suppliers, workers, and communities. Three tensions animate this: (1) Algorithmic capture—opaque scoring systems embed incumbent preferences and historical bias, appearing neutral while systematically excluding cooperative, minority-owned, and local enterprises. (2) Labour suppression—lowest-bid procurement triggers races-to-the-bottom in wages and conditions, as contractors externalize costs to workers to win contracts. (3) Democratic exclusion—procurement standards are set by procurement officers and vendors, not by communities who live with the consequences. Communities want local hiring and living wages but lack governance voice. Small enterprises and cooperatives lack access to capital, certification, and transparent criteria. Workers absorb risk and cost. Municipalities remain captured by incumbents despite stated equity goals.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Procurement as Democratic Stewardship operates through five interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Community-Anchored Standards (Participatory Design)**
Communities and workers co-design procurement criteria—not as input to professional procurement officers, but as governance authority. Criteria are transparent, weighted, and publicly visible (scorecard methodology). This reveals what businesses must do to win work: hire locally, pay living wages, recognize worker councils, demonstrate community ownership. Standards are no longer imposed by distant procurement systems; they are authored by those who live with consequences.

**2. Multi-Criteria Preference System (Justice-Weighted Scoring)**
Procurement weights criteria beyond cost: local ownership, cooperative structure, living wages verified through community audit, local hiring rates, workplace democracy, environmental stewardship, anti-displacement impact. Preference is quantified (5-15% scoring advantage or reserved contract allocation). This internalizes the true cost of extraction: a contractor that wins through wage suppression carries that cost visibly into the scoring. Communities can choose to pay slightly more for suppliers aligned with justice—and transparency shows this is a choice, not a failure.

**3. Supplier Ecosystem Cultivation (Pre-Contract Development)**
Municipalities and institutions don't just select from existing suppliers; they develop local capacity before procurement. This includes cooperative formation programs, financing, business planning, certification support for informal economy workers. Communities co-invest in suppliers who will serve them. When procurement contracts are issued, local enterprises and cooperatives are ready to compete—not as underdogs, but as intentionally developed partners. This converts suppliers from interchangeable vendors into community members with skin in collective success.

**4. Polycentric Coordination (Metropolitan Reciprocity)**
Municipalities harmonize procurement standards and accept each other's certifications rather than duplicating verification. This creates economies of scale in sustainable supply chains and enables inter-municipal purchasing—a supplier meeting one city's living-wage standard is recognized across the region. Coordination is voluntary (subsidiarity), but standards are binding once adopted. Dispute resolution mechanisms preserve trust. This prevents the fragmentation where every jurisdiction creates new procurement requirements, overwhelming small suppliers.

**5. Governance Integration (Democratic Accountability)**
Worker-cooperative networks, small-business associations, and community groups hold seats on procurement boards. They review scoring methodology, challenge hidden bias, audit contract compliance. Transparency protocols expose procurement decisions—vendor selection, contract awards, performance outcomes—to public scrutiny. This shifts power: procurement officers implement community standards rather than set them unilaterally. Algorithmic systems (if used) are open-source, community-auditable, and required to explain scoring logic. Lock-in is prevented through interoperability requirements and source code escrow.

**Outcome Structure**: The pattern resolves the core tension by making extraction visible and justice enforceable. Suppliers that pay living wages, hire locally, and practise workplace democracy win contracts reliably—creating stable demand for enterprise models that regenerate rather than extract. Communities retain purchasing power as democratic authority, not delegating it to procurement algorithms. Local ecosystems strengthen because purchasing is routed through them intentionally. Reciprocity becomes institutional: municipalities commit to preferential procurement; suppliers commit to standards; workers commit to local hiring and wages; communities commit to transparent scorecards and governance participation. The pattern is self-reinforcing: as procurement standards reward justice, suppliers organize to meet them; as local enterprises grow, they create employment that reduces displacement pressure; as governance becomes participatory, communities develop the sophistication to steward complex procurement; as standards harmonize across municipalities, smaller suppliers can scale without duplicating certification.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by auditing. Map your organization's spending: vendors, contract values, renewal dates, worker conditions at each supplier. This is not a compliance exercise—it's an inventory of your power. Share it publicly. Transparency is the first act of cultivation.

Next, convene. Bring together workers (including frontline staff and suppliers' employees), community members, local business owners, and procurement staff. Not as a one-time advisory board, but as an ongoing design body. Their job: rewrite your procurement criteria. What matters? Living wages, yes—but also: Does this vendor hire from the neighborhood? Do they invest in apprenticeships? Do workers have a voice in scheduling and conditions? Are they cooperative-owned or majority-minority-owned? Frame these not as nice-to-haves but as core performance metrics, weighted equally with cost and delivery speed.

Pilot with a category. Don't overhaul everything at once. Choose something concrete—janitorial services, food procurement, IT support—where you can experiment and learn. Set a timeline: 90 days to redesign criteria, 6 months to transition existing contracts, and quarterly review with your governance body.

Create a preferential procurement pathway. Reserve a percentage of spending (10-20% is a common start) for businesses that meet your justice criteria. This creates a market signal: businesses that want your contract will reshape themselves to compete on these terms. It also protects small vendors from being undercut on price alone while they're building capacity.

Track and publish results quarterly. How many local jobs created? What wages, benefits, worker voice measures? What percentage of spend went to cooperatives or minority-owned enterprises? This feedback loop shows workers and community what their collective purchasing power has accomplished—and where gaps remain.

Finally, connect procurement to other systems: workforce development (fund training pathways for jobs created), community investment (require vendors to source locally), and democratic voice (let workers at supplier firms participate in your governance body).

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, three things typically flourish: **Wealth circulation.** Money spent locally circulates through the community 2-3 additional times before leaving—increasing local income, tax revenue, and economic resilience. Workers see stable employment with predictable hours and voice. **Visibility and accountability.** When procurement criteria are public and participatory, hidden exploitation becomes harder. Workers and community members can monitor conditions. Procurement becomes legible, not a black box.

**Network effects.** As anchor institutions (cities, hospitals, universities) shift procurement, they reshape entire supply chains. A local food vendor who supplies your school district has incentive to pay farmers fairly, source regionally, and ensure worker voice. The market reorients.

But risks emerge if the pattern isn't tended carefully. **Tokenism without power.** Community input on procurement criteria can become theatrical—gathered, acknowledged, then ignored by procurement staff. Governance authority must shift, not just input channels. **Vendor capture and cronyism.** Preferential procurement can become a tool for political favoritism rather than justice. Criteria must be transparent, applied consistently, and subject to appeal. **Displacement through gentrification.** If local business growth drives property values up, landlords may displace the very communities the pattern aims to strengthen. Link procurement to land access and anti-displacement strategies. **Complexity and cost.** Evaluating suppliers on 10 criteria instead of one takes time and expertise. Build procurement capacity deliberately; don't treat it as a cost savings.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Jackson, Mississippi's People's Assemblies (2015–present).** The city shifted municipal purchasing toward Black-owned cooperatives and local enterprises through participatory procurement design. Workers and community members set criteria; procurement staff implemented them. Over seven years, the model anchored 300+ local jobs and demonstrated that cities could use procurement as reparations infrastructure without additional budget.

**Evergreen Cooperatives, Cleveland (2008–present).** A network of worker-owned businesses (laundry, food, energy auditing) grew from a deliberate procurement strategy by anchor institutions: Cleveland Clinic, Case Western Reserve University, and the City. These institutions committed to preferential purchasing from Evergreen businesses. In return, Evergreen committed to hire from low-income neighborhoods and maintain democratic worker ownership. The model has created 250+ good jobs and demonstrated that procurement can fund cooperative infrastructure.

**Bogotá's Procurement for Social Inclusion (2014–present).** The city redesigned purchasing criteria to favor cooperatives and women-led enterprises. Criteria included: gender representation in leadership, worker voice mechanisms, local sourcing, and wage standards. A participatory budget process involved community members in annual priority-setting. Over ten years, the city directed 15% of procurement to cooperatives and women-owned businesses—not through subsidy, but through transparent, democratic criteria. The model is now spreading to other Latin American cities.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems have accelerated procurement's tendency toward extraction. Automated vendor scoring and RFP evaluation systems encode historical patterns—if past contracts favored large, established vendors, algorithms will continue that bias at scale and at speed. The pattern becomes urgent here: **Democratic design of procurement AI is foundational**.

Participatory governance bodies can now audit and redesign algorithmic criteria in real time. Rather than a fixed, "neutral" scoring system, criteria become transparent and contestable: *Why does the algorithm weight delivery speed higher than worker voice? Who decided?* Community members can request algorithmic audits, challenge weightings, and demand human review of edge cases.

Distributed ledgers can also amplify this pattern's power. Supply chain transparency—tracking where money flows, who is employed, wage and benefit data—becomes verifiable and immutable. Workers can authenticate their own labor history; communities can see procurement's cascading effects. This makes hidden extraction harder to hide.

Conversely, AI can help. Matching algorithms can surface local, minority-owned, and cooperative vendors that traditional procurement officers might miss. Benefit-impact modeling can forecast economic outcomes of procurement choices: *If we shift 20% of purchasing to local cooperatives, what jobs, wage growth, and community wealth accrue?* Predictive analytics can identify when vendors are drifting from agreed standards—wage stagnation, worker turnover spikes—and trigger renegotiation before contracts renew.

The cognitive shift: procurement becomes less about risk reduction and more about *collective agency in shaping market conditions*. AI becomes a tool for making visible what distributed intelligence already knows: how procurement shapes lives.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Workers report predictable schedules and wage increases. Community members can name the local businesses their institution supports. Procurement governance meetings are attended by frontline workers and vendors, not just administrators. Spending data is published monthly and accessible without technical knowledge. When a vendor drifts from agreed standards—cutting wages, reducing worker voice—community members and procurement staff flag it together, and the contract is renegotiated (not simply terminated). New local businesses emerge because they know they can compete for institutional contracts. Procurement criteria evolve quarterly based on feedback from workers and community.

**Signs of decay:** Procurement meetings are attended only by staff; community members stop coming. Standards are set but not monitored; vendors are not held accountable. Spending remains opaque or published in forms only analysts can parse. When standards are violated, enforcement disappears. Contracts are awarded to vendors with the "right" political connections, not the strongest alignment with community values. Procurement returns to a single metric: lowest cost. Workers report hours are cut or wages stall because vendors can't meet both institutional pricing demands and justice criteria. The gap between what procurement says it values and what it actually funds widens.

**The diagnostic question:** *If you audited your institution's spending today, would the community it serves be able to point to themselves in the results—jobs created in their neighborhood, businesses they recognize, wages and conditions they know are fair?* If not, procurement is extractive, however well-intentioned. If yes, and if community members have genuine authority to reshape criteria and hold vendors accountable, the pattern is alive.
