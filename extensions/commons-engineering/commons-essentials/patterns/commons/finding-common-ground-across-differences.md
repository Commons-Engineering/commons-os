### Section 1: Context

Whenever multiple actors share a resource, pursue a joint objective, or face a common challenge, they confront a threshold: will they engage as adversaries defending fixed positions, or as co-gardeners cultivating shared understanding? Contemporary systems default to the first—stakeholder battles, zero-sum negotiations, winner-take-all voting—which exhaust energy on positional defence rather than problem-solving. Yet groups repeatedly discover that the problem itself was the real barrier. Once adversaries jointly name what they're actually trying to solve, incentives shift. Suddenly free-riders have less cover, commons-tragedy framing dissolves, and parties see how their interests align. This pattern draws from reciprocity (contributions matched over time), conflict resolution (all cooperative systems generate conflict; the question is how to evolve rules), and collective action (how to channel self-interest toward joint outcomes rather than deny it).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Winner-take-all position-staking vs. joint problem-ownership; Fixed problem definition vs. collectively-shaped framing.**

Groups face a critical fork early in any collaboration: Do we lock in problem definition quickly and move to solutions, or do we spend time together naming what we're actually solving? The pressure to act fast pushes toward the first choice. But locked-in problem definitions often embed the hidden assumptions of whoever spoke first or held most power. The result: solutions that don't fit the real situation, stakeholders who feel unheard, and consensus built on compliance rather than genuine alignment. Meanwhile, the second path feels inefficient until the moment it isn't—when a group discovers they've been solving the wrong problem entirely, or when a reframing suddenly makes everyone's interests visible as compatible rather than opposed. The tension is real: time spent on dialogue costs momentum; time saved on definition costs legitimacy and adaptation later.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Collective Sense-Making inverts the sequence: invest in shared problem definition *before* solutions. The pattern unfolds in three phases, each designed to shift from positional thinking to joint problem-ownership.

**Phase 1: Values & Constraints Mapping.** Bring stakeholders together not to debate solutions but to surface what matters. What are your core values in this domain? What constraints (ecological, economic, relational) are non-negotiable? What outcomes would feel like success? This phase makes the otherwise-invisible incentive landscape visible. It's not asking people to sacrifice self-interest; it's asking them to be explicit about it so the group can design around it rather than pretend it doesn't exist. A commons respects self-interest as a design parameter, not a character flaw.

**Phase 2: Collaborative Problem Framing.** With values and constraints on the table, jointly name the actual problem. This is where reframing happens. A stakeholder defending "my right to extract" and a steward defending "ecosystem health" may both be protecting the same underlying concern: security and predictability over time. Collective framing asks: What if we named this as *How do we create stable livelihoods without degrading the resource base?* Suddenly both stakeholders have skin in the same game. The process requires structured dialogue: one voice at a time, genuine listening without counter-argument, questions that deepen rather than defend. The goal is not consensus on values (those may genuinely differ) but alignment on the problem shape.

**Phase 3: Iterative Solution Testing & Evolution.** Once the problem is jointly held, solutions emerge faster because they're being designed to fit the actual constraints, not imagined ones. The group brainstorms without judgment (variation), tests small interventions (selection), observes what works, and evolves. Rules are treated as live documents, not etched commandments. Fairness is enforced through transparency: everyone can see how decisions map to the agreed-upon values and constraints. If a rule isn't working, the group revisits the problem frame—did we misunderstand something? Did conditions change?—rather than blame individuals for non-compliance.

The pattern works across value-distance because it doesn't require agreement on values, only clarity about them. A group with genuinely different worldviews can still find workable agreements when the problem is jointly framed and constraints are explicit. It builds reciprocity because every voice shapes the definition; later, when sacrifices are needed, people consent because they recognise their own concerns in the rule structure. It creates surplus because the group gains both the practical solution *and* the relational capacity to solve future problems together.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by creating a *holding space*—a place (physical or virtual) where people show up not as representatives defending positions but as humans who care. This matters. Set norms early: curiosity before critique, listening before countering, questions before answers.

**Phase 1: Values & Constraints Mapping** unfolds over 2-3 sessions. Invite each stakeholder group to map their non-negotiables separately first—the things they genuinely cannot compromise on, and why. Not as debate ammunition, but as self-knowledge. What do they actually need? What do they fear losing? Have them articulate constraints too: legal requirements, resource limits, time pressures. Then bring groups together to *see* each other's maps. Often there's surprising overlap in what matters, even when solutions differ wildly.

**Phase 2: Shared Problem Naming** asks everyone to name the problem *together*, not defend pre-existing definitions. Use structured reflection: What do we observe happening? What patterns repeat? What outcomes are we trying to shift? Write these down collaboratively. Let disagreement surface—it often points to different pieces of a larger truth. A water scarcity issue might be simultaneously about agricultural yield (one group), ecosystem health (another), and urban equity (a third). The real problem is often the *tension between these*, not any single one.

**Phase 3: Iterative Solution Building** emerges from shared problem naming. Now design options that honor multiple values at once. Not compromise (where everyone loses something), but integration (where solutions address multiple concerns). Test these designs with stakeholders: Does this still honor your core constraint? What did we miss?

Throughout, rotate facilitation. Don't let one person or group control the narrative. Use visual methods—maps, timelines, system diagrams—so understanding lives in the room, not in whoever speaks best.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, collaboration becomes *generative*. Solutions are more robust because they've been pressure-tested against real concerns. Trust accumulates—people see that their values were genuinely heard, even if not fully mirrored in the final agreement. Energy that went into defensive positioning now flows toward joint problem-solving.

Groups also develop *adaptive capacity*. When conditions change, they know how to return to shared problem-naming rather than retreating to old positions. The process becomes an ongoing practice, not a one-time event.

But unattended, this pattern decays. **Risk:** Groups become exhausted by endless dialogue and retreat to faster, positional modes. If no clear agreements emerge from sense-making, people lose faith in the process. **Risk:** Power imbalances hide beneath "open" conversation. The loudest voice, the person with institutional access, the group with resources still shapes what gets named as "the problem." Facilitation must actively interrupt this.

**Risk:** Consensus-building can calcify into a new orthodoxy, where dissent becomes framed as unwillingness to collaborate. The goal isn't agreement on everything—it's clarity about where genuine tensions remain and how to live with them.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Dayak peoples of Borneo** (Indonesia/Malaysia) practice *musyawarah*—extended deliberative gathering where forest-use conflicts between indigenous communities, logging interests, and conservation groups are worked through by surfacing each actor's relationship to the forest, constraints they face, and what thriving looks like to them. Solutions emerge that protect sacred groves, allow sustainable harvesting, and acknowledge market pressures—not because everyone agrees on values, but because everyone understands what's actually at stake for others.

**The Water Dialogue in the Jordan River Basin** (Palestine, Israel, Jordan) has convened technical experts and community representatives across decades to name shared water challenges—not as "us vs. them" but as "How do we sustain this resource for all our peoples?" Agreements remain fragile, but the process itself, when it functions, prevents position-taking from calcifying into irreversible conflict.

**The Participatory Budgeting movements** (Porto Alegre, Brazil; now in cities worldwide) invert how municipal resources get allocated. Rather than elected officials defining problems and solutions, residents engage in collective sense-making: What matters in our neighborhood? What constraints do we face? What tradeoffs are we willing to make? It shifts budgeting from a zero-sum battle to joint stewardship.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence reshape this pattern's pace and reach. **Acceleration:** Tools can map stakeholder values, constraints, and problem framings at scale—surveying thousands, clustering patterns, surfacing tensions that would take months in traditional dialogue. This creates possibility for larger groups to engage genuinely.

**Risk of false consensus:** AI-generated "syntheses" of stakeholder input can appear neutral while embedding the assumptions of whoever trained the system. The pattern requires that humans *see* real tension, not have it smoothed away by algorithmic averaging.

**New capability:** Distributed problem-naming becomes possible. Stakeholders don't need to gather in one room. They can contribute asynchronously, seeing others' inputs, iterating collectively—but the *human work* of understanding why someone holds a position differently remains essential. AI can surface contradictions; humans must sit with them.

**Deepened challenge:** In a cognitive era where algorithmic systems themselves become stakeholders (optimizing for engagement, profit, data extraction), collective sense-making must expand to name *their* constraints and values too—making visible what systems are actually optimizing for, so humans can decide if they want that.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People arrive at meetings having thought about what others said. Someone shifts a stated position after genuinely hearing a constraint they hadn't considered. Stakeholders use language like "I didn't see it that way, but now I understand" rather than "You're wrong." New options emerge that no single group proposed alone. Silence in the room feels generative—people thinking—not hostile.

**Signs of decay:** Meetings become performative; people repeat scripted positions unchanged. Powerful actors dominate the naming while others stay silent or withdraw. Solutions emerge that one group clearly imposed, generating resentment. Facilitators become exhausted, trying to force dialogue that doesn't want to happen. Groups splinter back into camps.

**Diagnostic question:** *Can stakeholders articulate what matters to someone across the table, in language that person recognizes as true—even if they disagree?*

If yes, sense-making is alive. If no—if each group still sees others only as obstacles—the pattern hasn't yet taken hold. This question itself becomes an invitation: What would it take to get there?
