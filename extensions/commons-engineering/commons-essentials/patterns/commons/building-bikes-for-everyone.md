### Section 1: Context

Cycling infrastructure worldwide is designed for a phantom rider: young, able-bodied, unencumbered, brave, unbothered by weather or thermal burden, without dependents or unpaid care responsibilities. This imagined cyclist exists in Copenhagen or Amsterdam, but even there, most people who cycle are ordinary—children, elders, people with disabilities, people managing care work, people in service jobs with dress codes and sweat, people on hills they cannot climb, people in seasons when paths are iced, people living in places where cycling is coded as poverty. The 28 Orbit 1 principles clarify why generic cycling infrastructure fails: it violates Reciprocity by not accounting for the full texture of lived experience; it violates Subsidiarity by designing at city scale without embedded feedback from actual users; it violates Polycentric Organization by centralizing decisions about what barriers matter; it ignores Carrying Capacity by assuming infinite scalability without asking what the network can sustain. The cluster of 74 patterns reveals a commons that is suffocating under its own invisibility—invisible care work, invisible disability, invisible menstrual cycles, invisible thermal burden, invisible winter maintenance costs, invisible theft as property-security tension, invisible cultural adoption barriers in places where cycling means poverty.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Universal design vs. situated need | Infrastructure for the imagined cyclist vs. infrastructure for actual humans | Mobility as individual freedom vs. mobility as care practice.**

The core conflict is between infrastructure as fixed asset and cycling as lived practice embedded in care, embodiment, seasonality, and social reproduction. A protected cycle lane from home to workplace is liberation—unless you have two children and school pickup is not on that route. Unless you menstruate and there is no bathroom. Unless winter comes and the path is iced while car lanes are cleared. Unless you are blind and there are no auditory signals. Unless you are in a place where cycling is coded as low-status and no amount of infrastructure changes the cultural meaning. Unless you are poor and an e-bike costs three months' rent. Unless you have thermal burden from your body, your clothes, your medication, or your pregnancy. The forces at play are not technical but systemic: the invisibility of care work as transport demand; the ableist assumption that one standard body designs for all; the seasonal blindness of temperate-zone planners; the property-security tension where bike-share systems collapse under theft because community stewardship is absent; the cultural adoption barrier where infrastructure is imposed without legitimacy; the economic lock-in where cycling affordability is treated as a luxury, not a justice issue. These tensions cannot be resolved by adding more lanes. They require redesign of the entire governance, feedback, and stewardship architecture.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Cycling Commons Stewardship is a pattern for cultivating cycling networks as living commons—systematically alive, shared, just, built to last. It operates across five integrated dimensions:

**1. CARE-INTEGRATED TOPOLOGY:** Design cycling networks with explicit accommodation for unpaid care work as a design constraint, not an afterthought. This means cargo infrastructure (wider lanes, loading zones, secure parking for cargo bikes), temporal design (routes through schools and care facilities, timing flexibility for childcare hand-offs), and facilities (shower/changing spaces, nursing-friendly rest zones, accessible bathrooms with hygiene supplies). The cargo bike becomes a commons tool—subsidized fleets, maintenance cooperatives, cultural legitimacy as livelihood and care practice.

**2. EMBODIED ACCESSIBILITY MANDATE:** Every infrastructure standard must pass an intersectional accessibility audit: Can a blind cyclist navigate it (multi-sensory wayfinding, auditory signals)? Can a deaf cyclist (visual warnings, sightlines)? Can a wheelchair user (smooth surfaces, zero forced dismounts, rest nodes)? Can a person with pain (accessible parking, rest infrastructure)? Can a menstruating person (accessible bathrooms, privacy, rest zones, pain-aware scheduling)? Can a person with thermal burden (shower facilities, flexible dress codes, shaded rest spaces)? Accessibility is not retrofit; it is design from origin.

**3. SEASONAL STEWARDSHIP COVENANT:** A binding covenant guarantees year-round maintenance to equal or superior standard as car infrastructure—snow clearance, surface repair, surface drainage—with dedicated funding and annual equity audit. Seasonal maintenance is not amenity; it is equity. When winter cycling is reliably safe, it becomes transport for people without car access, care workers, low-income communities who cannot afford alternatives.

**4. POLYCENTRIC GOVERNANCE WITH EMBEDDED FEEDBACK:** Network design is stewarded through nested governance: youth co-design councils (children as experts in their own mobility); community safety audits (identifying barriers through lived experience, not traffic modelling); maintenance cooperatives (bike repair commons, theft prevention through community embedding); seasonal review cycles (observing what works, what breaks, what new barriers emerge). Feedback is not annual—it is seasonal and continuous. Decision-making is subsidiized: local decisions about local routes, with upward escalation only when conflicts arise.

**5. CULTURAL LEGITIMACY & ECONOMIC JUSTICE:** Infrastructure alone does not shift culture. This dimension includes: e-bike subsidy programs (addressing hills, thermal burden, economic access); integration of cycling into livelihood (cargo-bike delivery cooperatives, economic autonomy pathways); destigmatization through cultural work (cycling as normal childhood development, cycling cultures in Global South cities formalized rather than criminalized, normative design for ordinary people in everyday clothes); and community stewardship that prevents theft through embedding bike-share systems in neighbourhood identity rather than treating them as abstract objects.

The pattern succeeds when: cycling becomes autonomous childhood mobility (children navigating independently); care work is integrated into transport (cargo bikes carrying children, laundry, goods, livelihood); winter cycling is equal to summer; disabled people cycle at the same rate as abled people; thermal burden is accommodated; menstrual equity is embedded; theft is prevented through community stewardship; cultural adoption happens because the infrastructure reflects the actual bodies and lives in that place, not imported phantoms.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by listening—not to planners or cyclists, but to the people who don't cycle yet, and to those already cycling while managing care. Run seasonal listening tours in winter, spring, early school years, caring-for-aging seasons. Ask: What would need to change for you? What weather stops you? What route do you actually need, not the one we think you should want?

From that listening, map the *actual* topology of care and movement. Where do children need to go? Where do elders wait for buses? Where do people with mobility aids need rest points? Where do service workers move between scattered sites? Layer these maps. The cycling infrastructure you build serves these actual routes first.

Design for the body you have today. This means: gentle slopes, not exclusionary grades. Wide lanes that absorb a load carrier, a wobbly learner, a person whose balance shifts with seasons. Rest stations every 400-500 meters with weatherproofing, seating for waiting (not just moving). Visible, accessible repair stations. Public bathrooms. Winter maintenance that treats ice on cycle paths as urgently as on car roads.

Build skill and belonging together. Community repair workshops are not auxiliary—they're infrastructure. Intergenerational riding groups normalize cycling as a social practice, not a fitness performance. Winter skills clinics, carrying clinics for parents, accessible cycling groups for people rebuilding confidence after injury. These are acts of cultivation that make the physical infrastructure live.

Establish care-based governance: include parents, elders, people with disabilities, service workers, and seasonal residents in deciding what gets built and maintained. Not as consultants, but as decision-makers whose knowledge is foundational. When a winter freeze hits, they should be the first to know the path is passable—and involved in the response if it's not.

Tend continuously. Cycling infrastructure dies from deferred maintenance as surely as from bad design. Establish seasonal stewardship cycles: spring pothole response, autumn leaf clearing, winter gritting, summer seating repair. Fund this as permanently as road upkeep. Assign maintenance to local crews who live there—they see what's breaking.

### Section 5: Consequences

When you design for care, cycle ridership expands dramatically—not because you marketed it, but because it became possible for people managing real lives. Families move together. Elders stay mobile longer. Care workers can reach scattered sites without transferring between systems. The health gains compound: more movement, less isolation, less car dependency for the tasks that don't require one.

The infrastructure itself becomes more durable. A wide, well-drained lane used by cargo bikes and children survives better than a narrow, neglected one. Shared governance surfaces problems early—a broken light, a flooding corner, a missing bathroom—before they accumulate into abandonment. Communities that steward together maintain better.

But the risks are real. If maintenance is promised and then starved, care-integrated infrastructure becomes a trap: you've reorganized your life around a path that now ices over and isn't cleared. That's not just inconvenience—it's broken trust. And if governance is performative (listening without power-sharing), care workers and elders learn once more that their needs don't actually shape decisions. The pattern can become another form of extraction.

There's also a risk of scaling the personal: "build community repair workshops" can become an unpaid burden on already-overburdened people, usually women. The pattern works only if skilled maintenance is properly funded and paid, and if community participation is genuinely voluntary, not a condition of belonging.

Another edge: care-integrated design sometimes conflicts with speed and directness. A route that includes schools and rest stops isn't the fastest line. You're designing for plural time, not optimization. That's the point—but it means this pattern is not for every corridor.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Bogotá, Colombia—Ciclovía:** Sunday closure of major streets to cars, opening them to bicycles, pedestrians, skateboarders, and families. Emerged from grassroots organizing in the 1970s, now a weekly ritual involving 2+ million people. The pattern works because it's explicitly social and temporal: families ride together, elders and children participate equally, care work (pushing children, walking with mobility aids) is normalized, not hidden. The infrastructure is reclaimed by design, once a week, in a rhythm that communities govern.

**Utrecht, Netherlands—Intergenerational cycling practice:** The city systematized what was already happening: elders teaching younger people to cycle, cargo bike culture made visible and supported, cycling skill-building as embedded pedagogy rather than formal instruction. Governance includes parent-led cycling schools and elder-led safety mentoring. Maintenance is professionalized and funded year-round, with winter service treated as essential infrastructure.

**Accra, Ghana—Tricycle (tro-tro) and pedestrian integration:** While not purely cycling-focused, informal transportation networks here are explicitly organized around care patterns: school runs, market transport, care work. Recent advocacy for protected routes for hand-tricycles and bicycles is emerging from informal traders and caregivers, centering embodied knowledge of what actually moves. Seasonal flooding and road degradation are design constraints openly discussed; maintenance is unpredictable, so design must be resilient to seasonal variation. Governance is still contested, but the pattern is growing from use, not policy.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI changes the optimization problem. Algorithmic routing now offers dynamic information about path conditions, seasonal maintenance schedules, accessibility features, and real-time crowding. This is useful—but only if it's transparent and controlled by communities, not by platforms extracting data on movement patterns.

The deeper shift is distributed intelligence for maintenance and observation. Sensor networks on paths can flag ice, flooding, damage—and if those signals flow to maintenance crews and to a shared map visible to riders, the infrastructure becomes genuinely responsive. But this only works if data is public and governed locally. If it's captured by a company optimizing for retention or monetization, it becomes another form of surveillance wrapped in convenience.

AI also enables new forms of participatory design: simulations that show how a proposed lane change will affect school pickup flows, elder walking routes, or seasonal flooding patterns. Communities can see consequences before building. This is powerful if the tool is open-source and locally controlled; dangerous if it's proprietary and used to manufacture consent.

The pattern also needs to resist algorithmic pressure toward efficiency. Cycling for care is not efficient by optimization metrics. A route that includes schools, rest stations, and repair shops is longer and slower than a direct line. An AI trained on speed metrics will recommend against it. Keeping the human values—care, belonging, seasonal rhythm—visible and protected becomes a deliberate act of governance against the pull toward pure optimization.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Parents are cycling with children in weather that previously kept them home. Elders are visible on paths, often in groups. Maintenance crews know riders by name and respond quickly to problems. In winter, cycle paths are cleared as urgently as roads. Repair workshops have waiting lists. Local governance is actually contested—people disagree about priorities and solutions, which means they care and power is distributed. New riders are arriving, learning skills, becoming confident. The infrastructure is used all year, in all weather, by bodies of every kind.

**Signs of decay:** The paths are pristine but empty. Maintenance is deferred; potholes accumulate, snow stays, ice forms. Governance meetings happen but recommendations are ignored. Skill-building programs are cut to save money. The infrastructure becomes a monument to good intentions rather than a living practice. Surveying users reveals that cycling is still only accessible to young, fearless, unencumbered people. Care work is still invisible. Winter arrives and the paths are abandoned.

**Diagnostic question:** If you close your eyes and imagine the busiest moment on the cycle path in your city—a school morning, a seasonal transition, bad weather—who do you see? If the faces that appear are diverse in age, ability, embodiment, and care responsibility, the pattern is alive. If you see only the phantom rider, it's time to listen harder and redesign.
