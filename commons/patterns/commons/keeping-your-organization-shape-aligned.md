### Section 1: Context

Every organization is a structured system of nested authorities, information flows, and accountability relationships. As strategy shifts, teams grow, people move, and business structures evolve, the form that worked yesterday becomes misaligned with the purpose of today. In traditional practice, org design is treated as a periodic event—a restructuring project completed, documented, then left static until the next crisis forces redesign. Meanwhile, the actual structure drifts: promotions don't propagate; reporting lines contradict systems; role boundaries blur; new hires inherit outdated charts. This gap between formal design and lived reality becomes a source of friction, duplication, and silent failure. The Orbit 1 principle of Power at Every Scale teaches that living systems organize at multiple scales with semi-autonomous centres. But semi-autonomy requires clarity about boundaries and authority. Visibility as Commons Act and Information Shapes Systems demand that this clarity be maintained as a continuous practice, not a one-time event.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Organizational structure as fixed blueprint vs. organizational structure as living adaptive system.**

Organizations face a persistent structural coherence crisis: the gap between the organizational form that exists on paper and the form that actually coordinates work. This manifests as multiple nested tensions: (1) Design Authority—who decides what the structure is?—remains ambiguous, causing paralysis or unauthorized drift. (2) Visibility Decay—org charts, role definitions, and reporting lines become obsolete within weeks as people move, but no systematic refresh occurs. (3) Misaligned Systems—HR systems, payroll, benefits, equity vesting, org charts, and manager knowledge exist in disconnected versions, creating cascading errors. (4) Uncoordinated Transition—when restructuring occurs, old and new structures overlap without controlled handover, leaving accountability unclear. (5) Silent Failure of Alignment—strategy shifts but structure remains unchanged, or structure changes but strategy communication never cascades, leaving frontline teams executing against misaligned mental models. Without a governing system that keeps structure visible, versioned, and continuously reconciled, organizations accumulate structural debt—increasing friction, duplication, and coordination cost.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Structural Coherence Stewardship is a closed-loop governance system with five integrated components:

**1. Design Authority & Constraint Mapping**
Establish explicit, written clarity about who has authority to make what structural decisions (reporting line changes, span of control, role creation, boundary decisions). Document the constraints that govern design: strategy imperatives, capability thresholds, regulatory requirements, coordination patterns. This prevents drift into unauthorized or incoherent changes. Authority is distributed by domain (CEO for top-level structure, functional leaders for their teams, HR for cross-functional role definitions), not concentrated.

**2. Living Version Control**
Treat the organizational structure as a versioned artifact, like code. Establish a "source of truth" structure (single system, typically HRIS + design tool). Define a versioning cadence—weekly or bi-weekly snapshots capture what is. Each change receives a reason, effective date, and cascading impact notification. Old versions remain retrievable; this creates institutional memory and prevents the amnesia of constant drift.

**3. Synchronization Cycle**
Establish a regular cadence (monthly or quarterly, depending on change rate) where design authority meets to review current state vs. intended state. What has drifted? What assumptions are broken? What needs proactive redesign? This is not a redesign meeting but a coherence audit. Outputs: any needed corrections, communication to stakeholders, plan for next iteration.

**4. Cascading Communication & Manager Alignment**
Structure changes only stick if the people who coordinate work understand them and can interpret them for their teams. Establish a phased communication cascade: Design authority → Senior managers (understanding intent and constraint) → Functional leaders (translating to team impact) → Individual contributors (understanding how their role, peers, and authority relationships shift). Each level has time to ask, adapt local practice, and teach the level below. This prevents the "org change announcement" that leaves people confused.

**5. System Reconciliation**
Organizational structure is recorded in multiple systems (HRIS, payroll, benefits, equity, org chart tools, manager attestation). Regular reconciliation cycles (quarterly or semi-annual) ensure these systems agree. When they diverge, it creates silent failures—promotions that don't propagate, roles that exist in one system but not another. Assign a steward (often in Organizational Development or HR Operations) to maintain reconciliation; treat it as essential infrastructure, not optional.

**Integration Pattern:**
These five components form a cycle: Authority sets constraints → Version control captures current state → Sync cycle identifies drift → Manager cascade communicates needed change → System reconciliation ensures all records agree → Next cycle begins. The cycle is continuous, not event-driven. It creates conditions for the organization to adapt its form as strategy and context shift, while maintaining the clarity necessary for coordination.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming your Design Authority explicitly. This isn't about centralizing power—it's about clarity. Meet with your leadership team and write down: Who decides when a role should exist? Who approves reporting line changes? Who owns the boundary between teams? Document these decisions in a simple, visible place. This becomes your design contract.

Next, create a quarterly Structural Coherence Review—a specific time when you look at what's actually happening versus what your org chart claims. Invite people from different levels and functions. Ask: Where are we acting as though the structure is different from what's written? Where are communication patterns broken? Where is decision-making unclear? This isn't a complaint session; it's diagnostic.

Build a Role Clarity Practice. For each significant role, write or refresh three things: (1) What decisions does this role own? (2) What decisions require input from others? (3) What are the three key outcomes we measure this role by? Make these visible—post them, share them, refer to them in conversations.

Establish a lightweight feedback loop. Create a simple way for people to signal structural misalignment—a quarterly pulse, a standing "shape check" conversation, or a channel where tensions surface. The goal isn't to eliminate friction but to notice when structure is preventing good work.

When change happens—a team grows, a strategy shifts, someone leaves—treat the structural response as a deliberate act, not an emergency patch. Announce what you're changing, why, and how it serves the work. This slows you down slightly but keeps everyone tracking the same reality.

Finally, measure coherence itself. Track: How long do decisions take? How many escalations happen? How aligned are people on who owns what? These numbers tell you when your structure is working with you versus against you.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern is tended well, several things flourish. Decision velocity increases because people know who decides what. Trust deepens because the gap between formal structure and actual work narrows—people aren't constantly confused about authority. New people onboard faster because the structure is visible and explicit. Adaptation becomes possible because you notice misalignment before it hardens into culture.

There's also a secondary flourishing: psychological safety around structural questions. When people know that role boundaries and reporting lines are *meant* to be coherent and reviewed, they feel permission to name when something isn't working. Structure stops being a sacred artifact and becomes a practical tool.

The risks, if this pattern decays, are equally real. Structural drift accelerates silently—people create informal workarounds that become permanent. Decision authority becomes murky; the same decision gets made differently depending on who asks whom. New hires encounter contradictory answers about "how we actually work." Talented people leave because they can't tell if they're succeeding or failing within an invisible structure. Worst case: the organization develops a shadow structure that works, while the official one becomes decorative.

There's also a subtle risk of over-designing. If Design Authority becomes too centralized or the quarterly reviews become bureaucratic theater, you've created the rigidity you were trying to escape. The pattern works only if it stays in service to the work itself, not to the elegance of the design.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Crisp, UK worker cooperative network**: Crisp operates without formal hierarchy but has explicit Design Authority. They defined: team members own their team's shape; the network council owns how teams connect. Quarterly reviews surface structural questions. By keeping authority clear while the actual structure stays fluid, they've managed to scale from 12 to 60+ members without recreating traditional hierarchy—and without the chaos of pure consensus.

**Natura & Co, Brazil**: This major cosmetics and retail group went through a multi-year structural evolution as it integrated acquisitions. Rather than impose a design, they created cross-functional "shape stewards" who, each quarter, walked the entire organization asking the diagnostic questions. They mapped what was actually coordinating work versus what the org chart showed. This revealed that their real decision-making structure was 30% different from the formal one. Instead of forcing conformity, they made the actual structure official—and it worked better because it already reflected how people moved.

**Mukurtu CMS, Australia (Kimberly Aboriginal community-led project)**: This open-source platform evolved without a traditional org chart. Instead, they established explicit authority about *what kinds of decisions* belonged to the broader community, what belonged to core developers, and what belonged to specific cultural groups using the platform. By making the authority boundaries clear rather than the hierarchy, they kept the structure adaptive while everyone stayed aligned on who decided what.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world where AI tools can model organizational dynamics and distributed teams span continents, the old org chart becomes even less useful. But paradoxically, clarity about Design Authority becomes *more* critical.

AI will make it tempting to optimize structure algorithmically—mapping communication patterns, suggesting span of control, predicting where bottlenecks will form. Some of this is useful. But the moment you treat the algorithm as the authority, you've lost the human coherence that keeps people aligned on *why* the structure exists.

The pattern strengthens in this era by becoming more transparent and more participatory. Instead of an org chart drawn by HR, imagine a living model where people can see the design decisions, the constraints, and the trade-offs. A quarterly review that includes AI-generated insights about information flow but is ultimately authored by humans who live the structure. This keeps technology in service to coherence rather than replacing human judgment about authority and purpose.

Distributed teams also mean you can no longer rely on physical proximity or informal hallway conversations to maintain structural clarity. The written, explicit clarity this pattern demands becomes essential infrastructure, not bureaucracy.

### Section 8: Vitality

A structurally coherent organization has distinct signs of life. People can quickly answer: "Who decides this?" and get the same answer regardless of who you ask. New people find their role boundaries clarifying within weeks, not months. Decisions happen at the right level—some bubbling up, some staying local—and people trust the decisions because they trust who made them. You hear less "I didn't know who to ask" and more "I knew exactly who owned that."

Structurally incoherent organizations have different markers. You'll hear the same question answered differently depending on whom you ask. New hires are confused about who owns what for months. Decisions get re-made at multiple levels. Talented people leave because they can't tell if they're succeeding. The gap between the org chart and actual work becomes common knowledge and a source of cynicism.

Here's the diagnostic question: **If I asked ten people in your organization "Who decides whether we hire for this role?" would I get the same answer?** If yes, you have structural clarity. If no, you have structural drift. Not because your people are confused, but because your Design Authority isn't actually clear. That's the place to tend.
