> Regularly examine the invisible assumptions that shape how you see the world. Surface blind spots, refresh outdated beli

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every person operates from a constellation of mental models—implicit frameworks about how people behave, how change happens, what is possible, who belongs, what fairness looks like. These models are learned, not innate; they are shaped by experience, culture, and proximity. They work until they don't. In complex, changing systems, the models that once predicted outcomes reliably become liabilities. A commons depends on members being able to perceive the same reality and coordinate around shared understanding. When mental models remain hidden—defended unconsciously, inherited without examination, invisible even to their holders—they become sources of mutual misunderstanding, invisible exclusion, and collectively irrational decisions. The steward's task is to make cognitive frameworks explicit, test them, and refresh them deliberately, both individually and in dialogue with others.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Implicit mental models (invisible, defended, calcified) vs. Explicit, tested, evolving frameworks (alive, transparent, adaptive).**

Three interlocking tensions. First: models that are implicit are invisible and therefore difficult to challenge or update. We defend what we don't name. Second: when mental models differ silently across a group, apparent disagreement often reflects unexamined differences in how members perceive reality itself—not just different preferences. Third: power hides in unexamined assumptions. The frameworks that seem most 'neutral' or 'natural' are often the ones that reflect the experience and privilege of those who set the terms—marginalizing other ways of knowing and being. A commons cannot be just if its cognitive foundations remain opaque and unexamined. Members cannot adapt if they cannot name what they believe or why.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement a three-phase cycle of excavation, reconciliation, and collective refresh—embedded in the regular rhythms of the commons' life.

**PHASE 1: EXCAVATION (Make Models Explicit).** Individually and in small groups, deliberately surface the mental models shaping major decisions. Use structured reflection: What causal story am I telling about how this works? What do I assume to be true about people, change, agency, fairness, possibility? Where did this framework come from? What evidence would shake it? Use diverse inquiry methods—solo journaling, AI-assisted worldview stress-testing, dialogue with those who hold different frameworks. Document frameworks without judgment; the goal is visibility, not righteousness.

**PHASE 2: RECONCILIATION (Test Against Reality & Difference).** Bring excavated models into dialogue. Where do frameworks conflict? Not to eliminate conflict but to understand its sources. Whose perspectives are centered in our shared models? Whose ways of knowing are absent or marginalized? What patterns do the models predict? How well? Test assumptions against new evidence, emerging data, and the lived experience of those most affected by decisions made from these frameworks. Reverse mentoring accelerates this—systematic learning from those with different expertise, generation, or proximity to reality.

**PHASE 3: DELIBERATE REFRESH.** Based on what testing reveals, explicitly update the models. What frameworks are no longer predictive? Which need modification? Which were hiding unexamined exclusion? Codify updated models so they become teachable, transmissible, testable—shared infrastructure rather than private assumption. Make the refresh visible, celebrated even; it signals that the commons learns, that outdated frameworks don't linger as dead weight.

This cycle repeats on a regular cadence—quarterly or semi-annually—timed to seasons of reflection in the commons' life. The practice belongs to everyone, not a cognitive specialist: it is how the commons cultivates collective intelligence and ensures that its stewards remain cognitively alive.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with **structured excavation sessions** held quarterly or before major decisions. Frame these not as critique but as collective archaeology—you're digging up the foundations your thinking rests on, together.

**Phase 1: Individual Excavation.** Give people a focused prompt tied to a real decision facing your commons: *What story am I telling about why people resist this change? What am I assuming about fairness here? What would have to be true for my proposed solution to work?* Ask them to write for 15 minutes without filtering. The rough texture matters—half-formed thoughts surface faster than polished ones.

**Phase 2: Small-Group Reconciliation.** In groups of 3–5, people read their excavations aloud without defending them. Others listen for the logic underneath—not to agree or disagree, but to map the model. A facilitator names it back: *I hear you assuming that people change when they feel respected. Is that right?* This is collaborative, not cross-examination. The goal is mutual recognition: *Oh, I see why you're reading this differently than I am.*

**Phase 3: Collective Refresh.** Bring findings to the wider group. Don't try to consensus on truth. Instead, make visible the **range of operating models** in the room. Document them. Ask: *Which models do we want to test? Which are we willing to let go?* Then commit to small experiments that will reveal whether assumptions hold: *We assumed people would self-organize if we removed the sign-up sheet. Let's try it for a month and notice what happens.*

**Tending the practice:** Rotate facilitators so the skill spreads. Keep excavation notes accessible—they become institutional memory, showing how thinking has evolved. When conflicts arise, return to excavation: *We're disagreeing about the solution. Let's first map where our models diverge.*

The cultivation happens not in reaching agreement, but in the collective act of making thinking visible, vulnerable, and revisable.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Groups that check their thinking develop genuine adaptive capacity. They can pivot when the world shifts because they're not rigidly defending invisible assumptions. Conflict becomes clarifying rather than destructive—disagreement surfaces faster and can be addressed at the root. Trust deepens because people feel truly seen; you're not just heard, but understood in how you actually think. Decisions become more robust because they've survived scrutiny of their unstated premises. New members integrate faster because the mental models are documented and discussable, not hidden in tribal lore.

**What risks emerge if neglected:** Without regular excavation, groups harden around default models—usually the ones held by whoever speaks first or holds most power. Newer members, those from different backgrounds, or those who think in different patterns grow silent, sensing they're seeing the world "wrong." Conflict calcifies into identity: *I'm a realist, you're an idealist*, instead of *We're making different causal assumptions*. The commons becomes brittle, unable to learn. Decisions made on unexamined assumptions fail predictably, and the group blames circumstances rather than their own thinking. Over time, the commons becomes a closed system defending what it already believes, unable to integrate new information or perspectives.

---

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory budgeting in Seoul, South Korea** uses structured model-surfacing before allocating public resources. Citizens explicitly name their assumptions about what "benefit to the neighborhood" means—safety, cultural preservation, economic opportunity—before voting. This has reduced zero-sum conflict because people understand they're not actually disagreeing about values; they're weighting different goods differently. The process takes longer but reduces implementation friction.

**The Mondragon Cooperatives (Spain)** embed quarterly *reflexión* sessions where worker-owners examine the mental models driving major decisions. A decision to invest in automation wasn't reversed, but preceded by collective excavation of assumptions about technology, skill, and human dignity. This prevented the resentment that might otherwise have calcified.

**Ubuntu circles in South African truth-and-reconciliation contexts** use a practice of explicit model-checking rooted in the philosophy *"I am because you are."* Facilitators help people surface the assumptions each party holds about harm, responsibility, and what repair means. This isn't conflict resolution in the Western sense; it's a shared re-examination of the frameworks through which each side has been seeing the other.

---

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence amplify both the need and the difficulty of this pattern. Large language models can now surface hidden assumptions at scale—feeding decision logs into models trained to detect unstated premises. This is powerful. But it also creates new risk: outsourcing the excavation itself to machines, treating model-surfacing as a technical problem rather than a collective practice.

The real shift: as systems become more complex and networked, **mental models that worked locally stop working globally**. A commons that once coordinated within a single cultural frame now works across distance and difference. The invisible assumptions that united a group are precisely what fragments it at scale. Checking your thinking becomes less optional.

Distributed systems also make thinking more visible by accident—actions generate data trails that reveal unstated assumptions. A commons can now see *in aggregate* what mental models are driving behavior. This data can fuel excavation: *Look—we say we value participation equally, but these decision patterns show we're deferring to the same three voices. What story are we telling about expertise?*

The cognitive work shifts from individual reflection to collective pattern-recognition across a network. The tools change. The need intensifies.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** The commons regularly surfaces disagreement *before* it becomes conflict. People describe change of mind without shame—*I used to think this was about fairness, but excavating my assumptions, I see it's also about power, and that changes what we should do.* Newer members are welcomed with genuine curiosity about what different perspectives they bring, not just absorbed into existing frameworks. When experiments reveal assumptions are wrong, there's relief, not defensiveness. Decisions take slightly longer but stick.

**Signs of decay:** Disagreement gets personal instead of structural—*you're wrong* instead of *we're operating from different models.* Long-term members stop inviting newer ones to participate in decisions; the models are "already set." Excavation sessions become performative: people nod along but nothing changes. Conflict resolution defaults to deferring to seniority or expertise rather than reconciling different ways of seeing. The commons loses its capacity to surprise itself.

**The diagnostic question:** *When someone in your commons sees things differently than you do, can you ask—with genuine openness—what mental model they're operating from? And can they ask you the same?* 

If yes: your thinking is still alive. If the question itself feels unsafe or unfamiliar: it's time to excavate.
