> Design how profits flow back to people and planet. Every allocation decision shapes who benefits and what gets built nex

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every living system generates surplus—the difference between what is created and what is consumed. In healthy ecosystems and cooperative human systems, this surplus circulates: it nourishes growth, builds resilience reserves, repairs damage, and sustains contributors. In extractive systems, surplus concentrates upward, breeding stagnation, resentment, and eventual collapse. Commons-based organizations face an explicit choice that markets obscure: once value is created, who holds it, who decides its fate, and where does it flow next? This is not a sideline question. It is governance. The question lives in households redesigning their finances, cooperatives allocating yearly profits, nonprofit boards deciding whether to hoard reserves or distribute returns, and individuals negotiating their own relationship to accumulation. Every choice embodies a theory of fairness and regeneration.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Hoarding vs. Circulation — whether surplus accumulates in few hands or breathes through the whole system.**

Surplus allocation reveals every system's actual values, not its stated ones. Yet most organizations drift into default patterns: corporations funnel profits to distant shareholders; nonprofits hoard reserves out of fear; households concentrate financial control in one person; cooperatives distribute equally by formula, ignoring different needs. Meanwhile, contributors—workers, suppliers, communities—remain uncertain whether their contribution is recognized, whether the surplus they helped create will circle back, or whether they are merely extracting what they can before the system fails them. The deeper tension: without explicit design, reciprocity dissolves. Self-interest becomes hoarding. The system stops breathing. Concentration breeds resentment and power imbalance. Opaque distribution breeds cynicism. The practitioner must design how surplus flows, not because fairness is nice, but because circulation is structural—the difference between a living system and a dying one.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Build a Stewardship Surplus Flow—a deliberate governance structure that makes surplus distribution an observable, repeatable, values-aligned act. This has five interlocking moves:

**1. Name the Surplus and Its Origin.** Before allocating, make visible what surplus exists (retained earnings, profit margins, unrestricted gifts, value created beyond costs) and trace who contributed to it—workers, suppliers, environment, community capital, risk-bearers. This dissolves the illusion that surplus is mysterious or owner-rightfully-owned.

**2. Design Distribution Domains.** Establish explicit categories for allocation: reinvestment in mission capacity, worker wealth-building (wages, bonuses, equity, skill investment), community benefit (local hiring, gifts, restoration), resilience reserves (20-40% buffer), and stakeholder returns if applicable. The domains should reflect who contributed and what the system needs to stay alive.

**3. Create a Distribution Deliberation Rhythm.** Set a regular cadence—quarterly, annual, or per-project—where decision-makers explicitly revisit the allocation formula. Use time-sliced iteration: allocate based on hypothesis, observe outcomes (Do workers feel recognized? Is the reserve growing? Is the mission thriving?), and adjust. Treat it as adaptive management, not dogma.

**4. Establish Concentration Circuit Breakers.** Design thresholds or mechanisms that prevent wealth from pooling indefinitely in any one place or person: progressive distribution scales (if surplus is high, larger percentage goes to workers/community), wealth limits (individuals commit to maximum accumulation, then redirect), rotating decision authority (who chooses allocation shifts), or generational resets (wealth resets each cohort). These are not punitive—they are governance structures that keep circulation flowing.

**5. Make the Flow Visible and Participatory.** Publish the distribution decision, the rationale, and the outcomes. Invite stakeholders to contest or refine the model. Surplus flows through known channels, not hidden ones. This transparency reinforces reciprocity: contributors see that their work is recognized, that surplus circles back, that the system remembers them.

The pattern works because it treats surplus distribution as a calibration knob, not a moral crusade. It acknowledges self-interest (people want to know their contribution matters) and designs systems that satisfy it. It prevents concentration not through guilt or redistribution mandates but through structural flows that keep capital alive and circulating. It generates feedback: when workers feel recognized, retention improves. When communities see benefit, trust grows. When reserves are visible and adequate, the system can weather shocks. The pattern embodies reciprocity as a design fact, not a hope.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making surplus *visible*. Sit with your core team—whoever holds financial data—and build a simple surplus map. List every source of incoming value (revenue, donations, volunteer hours translated to money, ecosystem benefits you've received). Subtract direct costs. What remains? Name it. Quantify it. Post it somewhere everyone can see.

Next, draft your allocation philosophy as a living document. Don't aim for perfect fairness; aim for transparency about trade-offs. Ask: What does our surplus need to do to stay alive as a system? Common buckets emerge—reinvestment (tools, training, infrastructure), reserves (weathering shocks), contributor care (wages, bonuses, sabbaticals), community repair (giving back where you've drawn), and resilience (experimenting with what's next). Assign percentages, but frame them as questions, not commandments: "We're testing 40% reinvestment. What are we learning?"

Cultivate consent-based decision-making for allocation. Gather quarterly. Present the surplus map. Listen to what people closest to the work need. A worker might say wages are tight; a supplier might need faster payment; the community might need soil restoration funding. Don't average these into numbness—genuinely trade them off together. Sometimes the answer is "we can't do that yet, but here's what we can."

Create visible feedback loops. If you allocate surplus to worker bonuses, track whether it actually improved stability and morale. If you fund local repair work, return six months later to see what took root. Adjust. Let people see their allocation choices ripple outward.

Finally, ritualize the act. Some organizations do a Surplus Blessing—gather, acknowledge the abundance, jointly decide how to steward it. Others hold a Surplus Council quarterly. The ritual matters because it transforms allocation from an invisible accounting function into a communal practice.

### Section 5: Consequences

When surplus flows intelligently, you feel it: wages stabilize, people breathe easier, investing in training becomes possible, community trust deepens. Infrastructure gets maintained before it breaks. Contributors develop longer time horizons because they know the system will hold them. Creativity flourishes when people aren't in survival mode. The system becomes antifragile—shocks don't cascade as catastrophes.

But unmanaged consequences emerge. Explicit allocation invites conflict. Someone will feel their need was overlooked. Transparency about money can trigger old wounds—scarcity narratives, resentment toward anyone who "gets more," anxiety about whether there's ever enough. Some people will game the system, inflating their contribution claims or need statements. You'll discover misalignments: perhaps your allocation philosophy says "we value care work equally" but your actual distribution privileges seniority or visibility.

If you don't tend this pattern, drift happens fast. The allocation committee becomes an inner circle that decides for everyone. Reserves grow untouched while contributors struggle. Surplus gets spent on symbols of status—fancy offices—rather than actual flourishing. Founder capture emerges, where one person's preferences become law. Communities begin to sense they're being extracted from again, just more softly.

The deepest risk: false transparency. Publishing a surplus allocation without genuine consent-building creates theater. People see decisions already made and feel the performance of inclusion. This breaks trust more deeply than honest opacity would.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Corporation (Basque Region, Spain).** The 80,000-worker cooperative network has operated surplus allocation through their "Second Social Transformation" since the 1990s. Surplus is distributed through a formula: a portion to individual worker accounts (scaled by role and tenure), a portion to cooperative development funds, a portion to community projects in the Basque region, a portion to cooperative education. This allows both stability and collective reinvestment. Workers see their labor directly seed regional schools and new ventures.

**Grameen Bank (Bangladesh).** Muhammad Yunus designed surplus distribution to keep interest rates low while funding village centers and borrower education. A percentage of profit returns to borrowers as dividends based on borrowing history and participation. The rest funds community health clinics and schools in lending zones. This created a flywheel where borrowers saw surplus healing the communities they came from.

**Zebras Unite (Global Network).** This contemporary community of values-aligned founders and operators explicitly rejects the VC playbook of hoarding capital for exponential exits. Instead, each member organization publicly commits to surplus-sharing agreements: investing in other zebra ventures, funding public goods, and returning value to customers and communities. Their surplus flows sideways and backward rather than upward only.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed systems change what "surplus" even means. When algorithms generate insights, do they own part of the value? When communities contribute training data, does that data surplus belong to them? Distributed networks enable new allocation structures—smart contracts that automatically route surplus according to pre-agreed rules, transparent from inception. But this creates new risks: automation can hide allocation logic until it's too late to contest.

The real shift: with distributed intelligence, surplus becomes *collaborative and visible in real-time*. You can see daily who contributed what and allocate daily if you choose. This enables micro-allocation, reward velocity, and faster feedback. But it also tempts constant optimization—chasing the "perfect" algorithm and losing the ritual, the deliberation, the human sense-making that makes allocation meaningful.

The gift: AI can handle the complexity of multi-stakeholder allocation. You can genuinely weigh worker needs, community repair, reinvestment, and reserves simultaneously, modeling trade-offs. But humans must keep the *choice* about values. Algorithm literacy becomes a survival skill.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of healthy flow:**
- People can articulate why surplus goes where it goes (not just "the board decided")
- Allocation decisions shift visibly when conditions change (crop failure, sudden growth, community crisis)
- Contributions feel seen and held, even if not rewarded identically
- Communities improve measurably where you allocate repair funds
- Contributors stay longer; burnout drops
- Someone new asks "how does money flow here?" and gets a clear, honest answer

**Signs of decay:**
- Surplus allocation is a closed-door finance meeting; people don't know what it is
- "We distribute equally" is the stated policy, but actual distributions follow invisible status hierarchies
- Reserves balloon while contributors struggle
- Decisions are made by founders or inner circles, then announced
- People say "I don't know where my work's value goes"
- Communities you draw from see no return; extraction is prettier, but still extraction
- Allocation happens once a year, disconnected from real need

**The diagnostic question:** *If you asked any contributor where the surplus from their work goes, could they point to something that actually exists and thrives because of it?*

If they hesitate, point to a person, or have to guess—the surplus isn't flowing. It's pooling somewhere, waiting to be named.
