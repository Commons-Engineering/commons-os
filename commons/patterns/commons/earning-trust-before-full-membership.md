---

### Section 1: Context

Every living commons faces the same threshold problem: How do new participants join without destabilizing what already works? Traditional hierarchies solve this through probation as control—surveillance, conditional employment, asymmetric risk. But in commons-oriented systems, the probation interval serves a different function: it is a mutual learning period where both the newcomer and the existing community prove reciprocal commitment. Lineage runs through Adaptive Management (the interval is itself an experiment), Reciprocity (both sides contribute before full membership), Subsidiarity (local assessors hold authority), and Time-Sliced Planning (short, reviewable cycles rather than monolithic bets).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Rapid integration (risk of misalignment) vs. cautious gatekeeping (risk of exclusion and slow adaptation).**

Entry into a commons creates immediate tension. Admit everyone instantly: misaligned values, weak commitment, or poor fit cascades through the whole. Close the door too tightly: the commons stagnates, excludes talent, calcifies into gatekeeping. Existing probation patterns in formal sector organizations frame this as one-way evaluation—the organization tests the hire. In a commons, the question is bidirectional: Does the newcomer reciprocate commitment? Do the existing members prove trustworthy stewardship? Further, traditional probation is opaque—criteria hidden, feedback sporadic, authority withheld arbitrarily. This breeds resentment and extracts unfair labor. The core conflict: How to create safe, graduated entry that serves both integration speed and genuine alignment without recreating hierarchical control.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Threshold Passage is a time-sliced interval (typically 60–120 days, customized to the commons's actual decision-making rhythm, not arbitrary calendar months) structured in three concurrent flows:

**Graduated Authority Vesting**: Rather than full authority withheld until the end, the newcomer assumes responsibility in layers. Week 1–2: observation and orientation; explicit mentorship from existing stewards. Week 3–6: limited autonomous decisions within a defined scope (e.g., 'can initiate projects <£5k,' 'can participate in decisions affecting their own work'). Week 7+: authority expands toward full stewardship role, contingent on demonstrated alignment. Vesting is not automatic—it is earned and reversible. At any point, if misalignment becomes clear, the interval can be extended or the participant can step back without full-employment severance. This distributes risk fairly: the newcomer does not bet their livelihood on unknowns; the commons does not bet its coherence on unvetted commitment.

**Transparent Reciprocal Assessment**: The interval includes structured feedback loops at fixed checkpoints (day 14, day 30, day 60). These are not top-down evaluations but reciprocal conversations: Newcomer assesses the community (Do they practice what they preach? Is the work what I expected? Am I treated fairly?). Community assesses the newcomer (Does their work align with shared intent? Do they reciprocate effort? Can we trust them with deeper responsibility?). Criteria are explicit and visible to all—not hidden in manager discretion. Disagreements are surfaced early and negotiated transparently.

**Subsidiarity in Gate Authority**: The decision to vest full membership belongs to the smallest competent group—usually the team or circle the newcomer joins—not a distant HR function. This preserves local knowledge and keeps assessors accountable to the people they assess. Escalation happens only if the local group cannot reach consensus (true disagreement, not bureaucratic delay).

**Completion Threshold**: At the interval's end, there are three possible outcomes: (1) Full vesting—the newcomer becomes a full member with commensurate authority and security. (2) Extension—there is genuine fit but incomplete readiness; the interval extends with clear milestones. (3) Parting—fit is not mutual; both parties can step back with dignity and no rancor. This is not punishment; it is honest stewardship.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making the trial period a *real* collaboration, not a hidden test. On day one, the newcomer receives a written map: what decisions they can make now, what requires mentorship, what remains off-limits. This isn't a secret rubric—it's shared openly. Update it weekly as their confidence and track record grow.

Assign a single guide—someone who has been in the commons long enough to embody its actual values, not its stated ones. This person is not a judge but a translator. They show the newcomer where the written rules and the living practice diverge, and why. This relationship often becomes the deepest trust signal.

Create three to five concrete micro-commitments that matter to your commons. If you're a product team, it might be: attend standup (consistency), submit one code review (quality judgment), contribute to sprint planning (alignment). If you're a cooperative, it might be: attend a full board cycle, lead one initiative from pitch to close, shadow the treasurer. Make these *visible*—other members should see the newcomer doing these things.

Build in deliberate handoff moments. After 30 days, explicit conversation: "What have you learned? What surprised you? Do you still want in?" After 60, a peer review—not just from the guide, but from 2–3 people who've watched them contribute. This feedback is not confidential; the newcomer hears it directly and can respond.

Create a lightweight graduation ritual. It doesn't need to be elaborate, but it should mark the crossing: a vote, a shared meal, a public acknowledgment. This signals to both the newcomer and the existing members that something has genuinely shifted. They now belong here.

Document your decisions during the trial transparently. If someone doesn't graduate, the why matters as much as the outcome. Sometimes it's clarity: "This commons needs X skill you don't have—let's find a better fit." Sometimes it's values misalignment. Sometimes it's simply timing. Own it cleanly, and leave the door open for future application if the situation changes.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern thrives, newcomers arrive with clear eyes. They self-select better because they see what they're joining before committing fully. Existing members relax because they have time to sense whether someone shares the commons's actual DNA, not just its mission statement. Trust grows on a firmer foundation than a single hiring decision or a handshake.

The commons also gains agility. As the newcomer contributes in small ways first, the group learns where their strengths lie and route them accordingly. You're not guessing at placement; you're discovering it.

But there are genuine risks. A trial period can become a hazing ritual if the guide is indifferent or the standards invisible. If the existing members are fractured in their values, the newcomer gets whipsawed—one mentor says "we're collaborative," another shows them harsh gatekeeping. The trial becomes a test of conformity rather than fit.

There's also a subtler trap: **extended probation as de facto exclusion**. If trial periods stretch beyond the stated interval, or if the graduation bar keeps rising, the pattern inverts. You've created a permanent underclass—people doing the work but never gaining voice. This happens often in organizations that claim to value equity while practicing slow-motion gatekeeping.

Another shadow: the guide relationship can become a bottleneck. If one person holds all the context about the newcomer, their departure destabilizes everything. The knowledge stays locked in relationship rather than flowing into the commons's collective memory.

Watch too for cultural homogeneity. Trial periods, if they reward adaptation to existing norms, tend to produce newcomers who look and think like incumbents. Diverse trial standards—explicit pathways for different kinds of contribution—help counter this.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Musician Collectives in West Africa**: The Burkina Faso tradition of griot families doesn't admit new apprentices into performance or storytelling authority immediately. A young person joins, learns repertoire and technique for 2–3 years under a senior griot's direct mentorship, and gradually takes on public narration at ceremonies. The trial is embedded in practice, not abstracted into bureaucracy. The newcomer proves commitment through consistent presence and learns the commons's actual values—which griots are truthtellers, which are paid flatterers, which carry dangerous stories—long before they're trusted with the full repertoire.

**Apache Software Foundation**: ASP uses a structured path: contributor → committer → PMC member. Individuals start by fixing bugs and contributing patches. Recognized contributors are invited to become committers (can merge code) after demonstrating alignment with the project's values and technical judgment. PMC membership—governance voice—comes only after 6+ months of committer-level work and peer sponsorship. The criteria are explicit. The pathway is transparent. Thousands of projects have adopted this model because it works: it builds trust without unnecessary gatekeeping, and it surfaces genuine fit.

**Mondragon Cooperatives (Spain)**: New worker-members enter with a trial employment period (typically 6 months, renewable). During this time, they attend required training on cooperative principles, work alongside existing members, and are evaluated on both technical competence and willingness to participate in collective decision-making. Only after this interval can they vote on business decisions or participate in profit distribution. The pattern is deliberately dual: the commons tests the newcomer, but the newcomer also tests the commons. Many people leave during the trial—not because they failed, but because cooperative governance turned out to be different than they expected.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world of remote work and distributed teams, the *relational core* of this pattern becomes even more critical—and harder to achieve accidentally.

When most interaction is asynchronous, the trial period gains a new function: it's the interval where you establish enough synchronous rapport that later async communication lands clearly. A guide who has met the newcomer—even once, over video—transmits far more nuance than a Slack bot sending task lists.

But distributed work also enables a *richer trial*. You can now offer graduated authority vesting across multiple projects simultaneously. A remote contributor might observe in one repo, contribute code in a second, lead documentation in a third—all in parallel, with AI-assisted tools making feedback and mentorship less synchronous. The trial becomes less linear, more constellation-like.

AI tools introduce a new risk: the temptation to automate the guide role. You can build LLM-based systems that read pull requests, measure contribution patterns, flag anomalies. But the guide's real function—to sense whether someone shares the commons's *values*, not just competence—remains irreducibly human. The pattern requires that humans make the final judgment calls. Lean on systems for data and consistency. Don't let them replace the relational spine.

The broader shift: in a cognitive era with AI-augmented decision-making, trial periods become *diagnostic instruments for the commons itself*. Each newcomer reveals where your written values diverge from your living culture. Watch that gap closely. It's where growth or decay is happening.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life**: Newcomers graduate and, weeks later, are visibly making independent decisions that align with the commons's direction without having been explicitly told how. The guide relationship often persists as friendship, not just task management. Existing members voluntarily sponsor new applicants because they've seen the trial work—they trust the process. The commons occasionally graduates people earlier than the nominal timeline because the signals are so clear. Occasionally it extends a trial because everyone agrees it's productive, not because anyone's hiding a gatekeeping impulse. You hear newcomers saying they *knew* they belonged after 3–4 weeks, even though the formal trial ran longer.

**Signs of Decay**: Trial periods stretch without clear logic. Newcomers graduate but invisible gatekeeping persists—they have the title but not the trust. The guide role becomes resentful labor, assigned to whoever is least busy, not whoever is most trusted. No one can articulate what the graduation standards actually are; it's just a feeling. The commons admits people who soon discover the culture is hostile or fractured, and they leave. You hear "we're very selective" uttered with pride while watching the organization slowly shrink. Existing members complain about newcomers without ever having invested in their onboarding.

**Diagnostic Question**: *If we asked a recent graduate what surprised them most about how we actually work—versus what we say we work like—would we recognize ourselves in their answer, or would it show us a blind spot?*
