### Section 1: Context

Service co-production—where users, professionals, and communities jointly design and deliver public goods—has emerged as a response to bureaucratic rigidity and user alienation. Decades of practice (Pestoff, Ostrom, Bovaird) show co-production increases service resilience, cost-effectiveness, and civic engagement. Yet across sectors, the same pattern repeats: co-production frameworks extract knowledge and unpaid labor from community members while concentrating decision power among professionals and funders. Elite representatives replace marginalized voices. Risk flows downward to those least able to bear it. Benefits accrue upward to those already resourced. The co-production itself becomes a performance of legitimacy rather than a redistribution of power. The commons engineer faces a critical design challenge: how to preserve co-production's adaptive potential while preventing its capture as a mechanism for legitimating unequal extraction.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Co-production that amplifies average outcomes while concentrating benefits and risks among the already-powerful vs. co-production that genuinely distributes both value and consequence across all stakeholders..**

Four interlocking tensions plague co-production systems: (1) Labor invisibility—community contributions (care work, knowledge, relationship maintenance, iteration) remain unmeasured and uncompensated, subsidizing public services through invisible gift labor. (2) Risk externalization—when service improvements fail, consequences fall heaviest on the least-resourced participants (frontline workers, marginalized users) while benefits concentrate among professionals and funders. (3) Voice capture—'community representatives' emerge from privileged subsets; broader stakeholder interests remain unrepresented or overridden. (4) Accountability absence—co-production claims go unmeasured; no transparent mechanism exists to detect when services benefit some groups while harming others. Each tension separately is manageable. Combined, they transform co-production from value-sharing into legitimized extraction—participation rhetoric masking unchanged power hierarchies.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement a **Stewarded Accountability Covenant** comprising five interdependent mechanisms, deployed together:

**1. LABOR FLOW MAPPING & COMMONS ALLOCATION**
Create a Service Co-Production Role Clarity Matrix naming every form of work (diagnosis, design, delivery, evaluation, relationship maintenance, iteration, knowledge translation) at each lifecycle stage. For each role, explicitly identify: who performs it, what expertise it requires, what time/skill it demands, whether it is compensated. Establish a Community Wage Commons that pools resources and allocates them across all forms of legitimate labor—paid delivery work, volunteer facilitation, care contributions, knowledge curation—using transparent criteria co-determined by stakeholders. This makes invisible subsidies visible and negotiable.

**2. PROPORTIONAL RISK DISTRIBUTION FORMULA**
Before service launch, stakeholders co-design a Risk Distribution Formula specifying: if outcomes underperform agreed equity thresholds, which stakeholder group bears which consequence (financial loss, reputational damage, service disruption), scaled to their actual power, resources, and capacity to absorb loss. Marginalized users do not bear risk disproportionate to their influence over design decisions. Create a jointly-held reserve fund enabling compensation when service failures occur. This inverts the default incentive: professionals and funders now have skin in the game; risk alignment replaces risk externalization.

**3. BINDING STAKEHOLDER VETO RIGHTS**
Grant designated representatives of service-user populations and affected communities binding veto power over service design decisions and delivery modifications that materially affect them. Document every veto reasoning; veto cases become mandatory curriculum for service redesign. This is not consultation; veto is a structural check preventing decisions that would harm marginalized stakeholders. Veto holders are themselves accountable to their constituencies through transparent selection and regular review.

**4. EQUITY OUTCOME FLOOR & CEILING WITH MONITORING**
Co-design quantified performance standards establishing: a floor—minimum equity thresholds that co-produced services must meet for prioritized populations (measured distribution, not just aggregate outcomes); a ceiling—maximum acceptable disparities in benefit distribution between stakeholder groups. Establish continuous monitoring tracking who benefits, who bears costs, whether marginalized voices genuinely shape decisions. When outcomes breach floor or ceiling, trigger automatic resource reallocation or service restructuring—not after-action reviews but real-time course correction.

**5. CONFLICT NAMING PROTOCOL**
Institutionalize a structured methodology for surfacing genuine conflicts of interest and priority differences during co-design (not smoothing them over through forced consensus). Name the conflict explicitly. Map who wants what and why. Negotiate toward resolution using documented veto logic and equity metrics as anchors. Accept that some conflicts cannot be resolved; make that transparency visible and decide jointly whether the partnership can continue. This prevents the slow calcification of unspoken resentments.

**Deployment sequence:** Begin with Veto Rights and Conflict Protocol (establishing governance structure and naming power). Layer in Labor Mapping and Commons Allocation (making flows transparent). Introduce Risk Distribution Formula (aligning incentives). Establish Equity Monitoring (detecting drift in real time). Iterate the entire covenant annually, adapting thresholds, veto structures, and allocations based on what the system is learning. The covenant itself is alive, not fixed.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping the actual work. Sit with your service team and the people who use the service—not in a meeting, in the places where work happens. Document everything: the obvious tasks and the invisible ones. Who listens to complaints? Who remembers why a decision was made three months ago? Who translates between jargon and lived experience? Who shows up when something breaks?

This mapping becomes your honesty document. It reveals what's been donated, what's been extracted, what's been forgotten.

Next, establish a **Co-Production Labor Board**—a rotating group with equal voice: frontline workers, service users, practitioners, and one rotating community member. Their job is quarterly: review the labor map, name what's changed, decide how to value and share work fairly. Some roles deserve payment. Some need recognition structures (public naming, skill-building time, decision authority). Some require reducing the invisible load—automating paperwork so a parent isn't spending 15 hours translating forms.

Then create **Consequence-Sharing Protocols**. Before launching a service change, the board names: Who benefits if this succeeds? Who risks losing something if it fails? Structure the pilots and funding so those risks are distributed—not all on the most vulnerable. If you're testing a new intake system, make sure both the case worker *and* the person seeking services have dedicated time to learn it. Both get training. Both have veto power if it's creating harm.

Build in **Staged Authority Transfer**. Year one: community members advise. Year two: they co-design with veto power. Year three: they lead design with professional advice. Year four: they train the next cohort. This isn't tokenism—it's apprenticeship in power, with clear progression.

Finally, **Resource the Relationships**. Co-production lives in relationships—trust-building, translation work, conflict resolution. Budget for this explicitly. Pay coordinators to hold space. Fund gathering meals. Protect time in everyone's calendar for genuine dialogue, not just meetings. Relationships are infrastructure.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern roots, several things flourish. Service design becomes genuinely adaptive—you catch problems early because the people living them are in the room. Labor becomes visible and valued, which shifts how people see themselves and each other. A parent who was called a "hard-to-reach client" becomes a service expert and co-trainer. A frontline worker stops being invisible and starts having real authority. Trust builds slowly, then quickly.

Communities develop infrastructure for their own problem-solving. The mechanisms you build for one service (the labor board, the consequence-sharing protocols) become templates for others. Governance capacity spreads.

But risks emerge if the pattern is tended poorly. If labor mapping stops, invisibility returns—and people feel the betrayal more sharply than before. If the Co-Production Labor Board becomes performative (meeting quarterly but having no real budget authority), cynicism replaces hope. Communities learn they've been invited to perform participation, not share power.

A subtler risk: **acceleration of inequality**. If you don't actively distribute authority and resources, co-production can concentrate them. The articulate, those with childcare, those with transportation, those comfortable in meeting spaces—they get selected. Others are "hard to reach." This pattern only works if you're actively redistributing access: offering stipends, providing childcare, holding meetings in neighborhoods, building in translation and interpretation, ensuring people with disabilities can participate fully.

There's also the risk of **burnout in the already-burdened**. If you ask marginalized people to co-produce without reducing their other loads, you're just adding burden. The pattern requires real resource reallocation—not additional asks.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgeting in New York City (2011–present).** Community members in over a dozen districts directly decide how to spend millions in capital funds. Early versions concentrated participants (older, more educated, more political). New York responded by paying stipends, ensuring multilingual meetings, hiring community liaisons, and rotating decision-making through neighborhoods. Labor is now mapped: facilitators, translators, coordinators are paid. Risk is shared—if a playground project fails, the community that chose it learns, adjusts, and tries again. Not every project succeeds, but the mechanism itself distributes power in visible ways.

**Ubuntu Siyakhanya (South Africa, 2010–present).** Community health workers in Cape Town co-design maternal health services with nurses and government. The pattern started with recognition: health workers' labor was named, compensated, and valued in clinical protocols. A rotating Stewardship Committee includes health workers, mothers, nurses, and clinic managers. When vaccine delivery failed, the committee redesigned the system together—not nurses deciding, not mothers protesting, but genuine co-problem-solving. Labor got more visible, risk got distributed.

**Jeito do Sertão (Brazil, community-led irrigation).** Rural communities in the semi-arid northeast manage water commons collaboratively. Work is mapped by season and role. Decision-making rotates through families. When drought hits, consequences are shared—water is rationed equally, not hoarded by the most powerful. This isn't idealized: conflicts happen, but the structures distribute both benefit and hardship fairly enough that communities stay intact across generations.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence are changing the texture of co-production in subtle ways. On one hand, AI can automate the invisible labor—summarizing meeting notes, translating between jargon and plain language, identifying patterns in service data that humans miss. This could surface what's been hidden. A labor board could use AI-assisted analytics to see where bottlenecks are, where people's time is being wasted, where unpaid care work is concentrating.

But there's a seduction here. If you automate too much, you can convince yourself that co-production is happening when it's actually been outsourced to an algorithm. The real work is relational—building trust, negotiating conflict, shifting power. AI can support it, but can't replace it.

The bigger shift is this: in a distributed intelligence era, expertise is less concentrated. A community member with access to good data and tools can ask sophisticated questions that once only consultants could ask. This could genuinely flatten hierarchies. Or—more likely without intention—it could create new gatekeepers: people who know how to use the tools. Stewarded accountability means staying alert to that. The Labor Board needs to include people learning the tools *alongside* people building them.

Also: AI enables rapid iteration and experimentation. Co-production can become faster, more adaptive, more responsive to feedback. But speed can also bypass the relational work. The pattern requires slowing down enough to stay accountable to actual people, not just their data shadows.

### Section 8: Vitality

A service running this pattern well shows specific signs. The Co-Production Labor Board meets regularly and has actual budget authority—the group can reallocate resources, not just advise. Minutes are public. When someone shows up, they're not grateful for being asked; they're expected, compensated, and have real voice. Frontline workers can name their conditions and see them change. Service users can point to specific changes they've influenced.

Look for diversity in the room—not just demographic diversity, but diversity of how people think and communicate. If the board is all articulate, educated voices, the pattern is failing. If it includes people who think slowly, speak differently, require translation or interpretation, and whose voices are treated as equally valuable—that's vitality.

The relationship infrastructure is visible. You can see the coordinators, the meeting space, the facilitators. Money moves toward care, not just efficiency. Conflicts happen and are treated as information, not failure.

Signs of decay are equally clear. Meetings happen but nothing changes. The same people show up and the same people stay excluded. Community members stop coming because they've learned their input doesn't matter. Labor stays invisible—people give time and don't see it valued. Risk is still externalized; when services fail, consequences still fall on the most vulnerable.

**The diagnostic question**: Who decided that last change to this service? If you can't name specific community members and frontline workers who made that decision—who fought for it, negotiated it, own it—then the pattern isn't holding. Power is still concentrated. You have co-production theater, not genuine co-production.
