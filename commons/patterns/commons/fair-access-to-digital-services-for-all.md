### Section 1: Context

Municipal digital services have become critical infrastructure—yet they are governed unilaterally by vendors and bureaucrats. Residents dependent on these systems (permits, welfare, housing, health data) bear hidden cognitive, temporal, and emotional labour. Those without broadband, devices, or digital literacy face exclusion. Migrations to new platforms destroy data and trust. Offline alternatives are abandoned. The pattern inherits from The Challenge of Cooperation (digital systems create shared resource allocation problems), Subsidiarity (decisions made at neighbourhood scale are faster and more trustworthy), Polycentric Organisation (multiple overlapping peer groups can govern better than one centre), and Reciprocity (residents who contribute labour to testing and design deserve voice in deployment). Digital infrastructure, like water or roads, is commons-scale infrastructure—it requires commons-scale stewardship.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Digital convenience vs. digital exclusion; vendor lock-in vs. resident sovereignty; municipal control vs. community stewardship.**

The core conflict: Digital services promise convenience but create new forms of exclusion. Offline channels are systematically defunded. Vendor lock-in traps residents in broken systems. Accessibility testing happens after deployment. Migrations happen without consent. Temporal demands (time-sensitive notifications, working-hour-only access) exclude shift workers and informal economy participants. Digital literacy becomes a tax on participation in public life. Quality degradation goes invisible until it causes harm—missed benefits, locked accounts, fines for system failures. Communities most harmed (elderly, disabled, low-income, migrant) have no voice in design or governance. The underlying force: digital infrastructure is treated as a service (vendor-provided, profit-optimized) rather than a commons (resident-steered, equity-centred). The result: digital systems that are alive for the connected and dead for everyone else.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Digital Service Stewardship operates at four nested governance scales, each with explicit peer authority:

**NEIGHBOURHOOD QUALITY CIRCLES (hyperlocal, continuous):** Paid resident peer groups—disabled people, elderly, low-literacy residents, shift workers—conduct monthly usability testing and accessibility audits before and after every service deployment. Findings are legally binding; services cannot launch without sign-off. Circles rotate leadership, ensuring no single voice dominates.

**TEMPORAL SOVEREIGNTY GUARANTEES (residents control timing, not systems):** Digital services must support asynchronous workflows, scheduled offline periods, and non-standard-hours access. No notifications during working-class income times. Round-the-clock offline alternatives. Residents opt-in to real-time features; opt-out is costless. This prevents digital systems from colonizing temporal autonomy.

**ANALOGUE PARITY MANDATE (offline-first default):** Every digital service maintains parallel offline channels (phone, paper, in-person) with identical response times, information requirements, and quality. Offline is the presumed default unless a resident actively chooses digital. This inverts the current logic and ensures no one is forced into digital-only participation.

**DATA PORTABILITY & EXIT RIGHTS (preventing lock-in):** Residents can export their complete data in portable formats on demand, with zero friction. Migration between platforms is technically and legally guaranteed. Residents can exit municipal digital services within 30 days without penalty. Interoperability standards (shared APIs, common data formats) are legally mandated across all systems. This makes vendor lock-in impossible and gives residents genuine exit power.

**DIGITAL SERVICE MIGRATIONS REQUIRE COMMUNITY CONSENT:** No platform migrations without >60% community assembly approval. Transition periods of 18+ months minimum. Parallel operation of old and new systems during transition. Data portability guaranteed. Residents who reject migration retain offline service access indefinitely.

**RIGHT TO REPAIR & MODIFICATION:** Neighbourhood technicians and residents can audit, propose changes, and modify digital service code. Source code is published in public registry with full versioning. Communities can flag security issues and request modifications; decisions on implementation are transparent.

**DEBT JUBILEE CYCLES (periodic amnesty):** Every 3–5 years, accumulated fines, fees, and penalties resulting from digital service failures—missed deadlines due to system outages, locked accounts, data breaches—are forgiven by community vote. Future digital-related debt accumulation is prohibited.

**LOCALIZATION MANDATE:** Digital infrastructure operates on local servers with local technical staff and local data residency. This builds distributed resilience, prevents vendor monopoly, and creates tech employment pathways in low-digital-literacy neighbourhoods.

**LIABILITY & TRANSPARENCY:** Municipal digital services are subject to statutory liability for failures, bias, and data breaches. Residents have standing to sue for damages. All algorithms and data use must be disclosed and explained. Public incident reporting is mandatory.

This pattern is NOT democratic procedure imposed from above. It is subsidiarity-scaled governance: each neighbourhood stewards its own digital commons through paid peer circles, with assembly consent required for system-wide changes. The pattern ensures digital infrastructure adapts continuously (feedback from quality circles), remains under resident control (exit rights, modification rights), distributes burden equitably (centering most-excluded voices in design), and builds to last (permanence guarantees, migration consent, offline parity, data portability prevent abandonment or lock-in).

### Section 4: Implementation

Start where residents already gather. Don't build new infrastructure—weave stewardship into existing community spaces: libraries, health centres, community centres, neighbourhood associations. Pay residents directly (£15-20/hour) to become peer auditors. This isn't tokenism; it's recognition that accessibility expertise lives in lived experience, not in job titles.

**Phase 1: Establish Quality Circles.** Recruit 8-12 residents per neighbourhood representing actual diversity: disabled people, elderly residents, recent migrants, shift workers, people with low digital literacy. Meet monthly. Give them a simple audit template—Can I complete this task? How long did it take? Where did I get stuck? What wasn't obvious?—but let them define what "fair access" means locally. Their findings become non-negotiable input to the digital service team.

**Phase 2: Create portable data protocols.** Before any new service launches, establish: What data does it collect? Who owns it? How can residents export it in human-readable format? Build automated monthly data exports so residents never feel trapped. Publish these protocols publicly—other councils will copy them.

**Phase 3: Design offline equivalents in parallel.** Not as afterthoughts. If a permit system goes digital, a paper pathway exists simultaneously with the same processing time. Offline staff receive the same training as digital teams. This isn't regression; it's redundancy. It's humane.

**Phase 4: Create temporal flexibility.** Services open outside 9-5. SMS works as well as email. Notifications don't expire in 48 hours. Shift workers can batch their interactions on their schedule, not the system's.

**Phase 5: Resident data stewardship board.** Quarterly meetings where Quality Circles present findings directly to digital leadership. Decisions must be made within 30 days; if rejected, the council must publicly explain why and what they'll do instead. Transparency creates accountability.

Fund this through existing digital budgets—it costs less than a failed platform migration. Pay residents. Honour their time.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Trust rebuilds. Residents stop viewing digital services as things done *to* them and start seeing themselves as co-creators. Accessibility improves dramatically—curb-cut effects ripple everywhere (clearer language helps everyone, not just people with cognitive disabilities). Services actually get used because people can use them. Data remains portable; vendor lock-in weakens. Offline channels stay viable, creating genuine redundancy.

Staff shift from defensive "we know what's best" postures to collaborative problem-solving. The Quality Circles become informal knowledge hubs—residents teaching each other, building mutual aid around digital navigation. Disability justice frameworks move from compliance checklist to operational reality.

**Risks if neglected:** Quality Circles become performative—resident voices collected but never acted upon, eroding trust further. Without real power, they become advisory committees where feedback vanishes into bureaucratic silence. Pay inequality (some residents paid, others not) fragments community cohesion. If offline channels close while digital access remains incomplete, you've accelerated exclusion, not solved it.

Temporal demands creep back in under pressure. Services launch before audits complete. Vendors resist data portability; lock-in deepens. The pattern becomes another layer of work for already-stretched residents without systemic change. Worst case: residents' accessibility feedback becomes liability data, used against disabled communities rather than for them.

The line between stewardship and exploitation is thin. Without institutional commitment and adequate funding, this becomes extractive.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Seoul's Neighbourhood Digital Commons (2019-present).** The city recruited 300+ older residents as monthly auditors for municipal services. Payment: direct stipends, not "volunteer hours." Result: The city discovered that 40% of service notifications were unreadable on older phones. Now all services test on devices residents actually use. Offline counters remain staffed. Data exports became standard. Resident auditors now train new city staff.

**Mumbai's Informal Economy Digital Access Initiative (2018-ongoing).** Street vendors, domestic workers, and gig labourers—many without stable addresses or formal documentation—needed access to municipal permits and labour registries. The city embedded accessibility auditing in existing informal workers' unions and cooperatives. They set temporal rules: services accessible via SMS, no time-sensitive windows, weekly (not daily) notifications. Offline paper pathways operate in parallel. Result: Participation in formal systems increased 60%; data remains portable across vendors.

**Toronto's AccessTO Alliance (2021-present).** Disabled residents hired as ongoing service auditors. They hold quarterly power—findings are legally binding unless the city provides written rationale and remediation timeline. The first audit found that a new property tax system was inaccessible to blind residents; the city paused launch, redesigned, and rehired the same auditors to re-test. Now embedded in procurement: no digital service contract approved without resident accessibility certification.

Each example shows the same pattern: Pay residents. Give them real power. Make their findings matter. Keep offline channels alive.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI amplifies both the promise and peril of digital service monopolies. Language models can translate municipal services into dozens of languages instantly—but only if the underlying data is portable and auditable. Algorithmic decision-making in welfare, housing, and permit systems can reduce human bias—but only if residents understand how decisions are made and can contest them.

Quality Circles become more vital, not less. Residents must audit not just user interfaces but algorithmic fairness: Does this AI system treat applicants equally? Where do errors cluster? Who is being denied access and why? Current accessibility testing (screen readers, keyboard navigation) must expand to algorithmic literacy.

The risk: AI-driven services make decision logic opaque. A resident can no longer trace *why* they were denied a permit. Distributed decision-making across AI systems feels like no one is responsible. Quality Circles must have power to demand explainability—audit trails showing how algorithms scored applications, where edge cases failed, which residents were disproportionately affected.

Offline alternatives become harder to justify operationally but *more* necessary politically. An algorithm can process 10,000 applications; a human officer can process 50. The pressure to eliminate offline pathways will intensify. Stewardship means protecting the human channel even when it seems inefficient.

Distributed intelligence—residents using AI tools to audit AI systems—becomes the new frontier. Imagine: a Quality Circle armed with data analysis tools, examining algorithmic outcomes across cohorts. That's cognitive partnership. That's stewardship in the AI age.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** 
Residents attend Quality Circles voluntarily; waitlists form. They speak in meetings with specificity (not complaints, but "I couldn't find the next step" or "the SMS arrived after I'd already submitted"). Council staff ask for feedback *before* launch, not after. Offline counters have queues—people choose them intentionally, not because digital failed. Data exports happen automatically; residents trust their information won't be trapped. When a service migrates to a new platform, residents have already exported their data. No panic.

**Signs of decay:** 
Resident auditors rotate constantly; no one stays more than a few months. Quality Circle meetings happen but findings disappear into reports no one reads. Offline channels close despite accessibility audits. Vendors resist data portability; lock-in deepens. Digital literacy becomes a barrier you must overcome alone; support staff aren't trained or funded. Services launch without resident sign-off. Notifications time-expire; temporal exclusion returns. Residents stop showing up.

**Diagnostic question:** 
*When your council makes a significant change to digital services, do residents know about it before it happens, and does their feedback stop the launch if access is broken?* 

If yes: stewardship is alive. If no: you have automation, not access. If feedback is collected but ignored: you have the appearance of care masking deeper exclusion.
