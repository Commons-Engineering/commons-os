### Section 1: Context

Every human community operates according to values—some explicit, most invisible. In the early life of a commons, shared purpose feels obvious; everyone 'just knows' what matters. But as the community grows, as conflict surfaces, as external pressures shift priorities, those unexamined assumptions collide. A person joins believing the group prioritizes transparency; another has joined believing the group protects privacy. One member allocates time generously; another carefully rations it. One views hierarchy as temporary scaffolding; another as necessary structure. These collisions fracture trust precisely because they feel like betrayal—'you changed,' when in fact you were never aligned. The commons cannot be alive if built on phantom agreement. It cannot be just if some members' core values are systematically deprioritized while others are privileged. This pattern inherits from principles of Collective Action (what are our actual shared objectives?), Truth-Seeking (what do we genuinely believe, not what do we claim?), Cultural Meaning (what binds us beyond transaction?), and Reciprocity (whose burden falls where?).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Implicit shared assumptions vs. explicit negotiated agreements; espoused values vs. revealed allocation of time, resources, and care.**

Three interlocking tensions create the need: First, implicit vs. explicit — communities default to assuming alignment because stating values explicitly feels unnecessary, vulnerable, or divisive. But silence permits misalignment to grow in the dark. Second, espoused vs. enacted — members articulate values they don't actually resource or embody. A group claims to prioritize equity but allocates decisions to those with formal authority. A team says trust is foundational but makes decisions behind closed doors. This gap between speech and action erodes legitimacy faster than honest disagreement would. Third, power-blind conflict — when values collide, the response often treats all parties symmetrically, ignoring that power holders can afford to lose and powerless members cannot. A boss and report disagree on work-life boundaries; symmetric solutions ask both to compromise equally, but the boss has authority to impose, while the report has only the option to accept or leave. Without explicit power-aware protocols, 'conflict resolution' reproduces injustice.

---

### Section 3: Solution

This pattern unfolds in three interlocked movements: Surface, Map, and Tend. SURFACE begins with deliberate values articulation — periodic structured conversations (quarterly minimum for active communities) where members name what they actually hold sacred. Not vision statements but honest inventory: What would feel like betrayal? What non-negotiable must any agreement honor? Where are you willing to bend? Where do you need others to meet you? This surfaces the real map, not the fantasy one. Invite conflict here; if no tensions appear, you have not dug deep enough. Use protocols like Values Alignment and Divergence Mapping — name core values (care, autonomy, transparency, stability, growth, justice) and explicitly plot where alignment is required, where difference is acceptable, and where compromise is impossible. MAP involves translating articulated values into visible agreements — written working compacts that translate shared values into actual decision rules, resource allocation, and conflict protocols. Make the implicit explicit. If transparency is sacred, describe what transparency looks like in decisions, finances, interpersonal repair. If autonomy matters, describe the boundary between individual choice and collective constraint. If care is central, specify how members support each other through difficulty. The map becomes the governance document — not ornamental but operational. Run regular congruence tests: Does our actual allocation of time, money, and attention match our stated values? Where are we living a lie? This reveals drift before fracture. TEND means continuous reconciliation through power-aware repair. When conflict erupts (and it will — this is healthy), use conflict as a signal that values have shifted or misalignment has emerged. Ask: What values are in collision here? What were we not explicit about? Power-aware repair means the party with more formal authority takes proportionally more responsibility for safety, humility, and repair — not because they are morally superior, but because their power to harm is larger. A manager apologizing to a report requires different structure (public acknowledgment, visible behavior change, restitution for harm) than the reverse. Outside View practice helps: When status quo (current alignment, current power structure, current assumed values) feels inevitable, deliberately ask: If we were starting this community fresh tomorrow, knowing what we know now, what values would we choose? This breaks the sacredness of inherited arrangements and makes evolution possible. The pattern treats conflict not as failure of alignment but as opportunity for Second-Order Change — the chance to examine and redesign the underlying patterns that created the conflict, not just smooth over disagreement. A values conflict about decision-making reveals that the community never explicitly chose how power flows; the conflict becomes the catalyst for governance design.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by creating a **values surfacing ritual**. Quarterly, gather your community in a space that feels safe for honest speech—not a rushed meeting, not online if you can help it. Ask three questions, written down, answered individually first:

*What would feel like a betrayal of why you're here?*
*What do you need this community to protect, no matter what pressures come?*
*Where are you willing to compromise, and where aren't you?*

Collect these without attribution. Read them aloud together. The silence after is important—let people hear what they didn't know others held dear.

Then **map the tensions visually**. Draw a simple two-axis graph: one axis for each collision you sense. (Transparency vs. psychological safety. Growth vs. sustainability. Inclusion vs. cohesion.) Invite people to place themselves on the graph, marking where their values sit. Don't aim for consensus. Aim for *seeing each other*.

**Name the real trade-offs.** This is where the work deepens. When values genuinely conflict, you cannot have both. A community that prioritizes both "radical transparency" and "psychological safety for vulnerable members" will eventually choose. The choice itself isn't the failure—the failure is pretending you can have both and then silently resenting the person who pays the cost.

**Create decision protocols that honor what you've learned.** If your community values both autonomy and accountability, build that into how you choose leaders or allocate resources. If you value both speed and deliberation, establish which decisions move fast and which pause. Make the values visible in your structures.

**Tend continuously.** When conflict emerges—and it will—treat it as data. A clash between two members often reveals a values collision the community hasn't named. Return to the surfacing ritual. Ask: What assumption did we make that isn't holding? What value are we actually prioritizing in how we responded?

This is cultivation, not resolution. You're not solving disagreement. You're making disagreement legible, so it can strengthen rather than corrode the whole.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Trust deepens when people know they've been genuinely seen. Members stop performing alignment and start showing up as themselves. Decision-making becomes faster because the underlying negotiations are explicit—you're no longer arguing in code. New members can be onboarded into actual values, not mythology. Conflict becomes information instead of betrayal.

Communities that do this work develop **resilience under pressure**. When external forces demand compromise, the group can negotiate from a grounded place: *We value X and Y; we'll accept a cost to Z, but not to X.* Without this clarity, communities splinter in crisis—suddenly discovering they were never aligned.

**Risks that emerge if neglected:** Unexamined values collisions become chronic low-grade resentment. A member who values transparency but the group prioritizes swift decision-making will feel unheard and will eventually leave—or stay and undermine. When values remain implicit, the most powerful people in the room get to define them unilaterally. Marginalized voices especially suffer; they're told "that's not how we do things" without knowing the rule existed.

Communities without this practice often fracture during their second or third transition. The founding energy wears off. Growth happens. External pressure increases. And suddenly people realize they never actually agreed on what they were building.

There's also a risk of **performative values surfacing**—people articulate values they think they should hold rather than ones they actually live. Counteract this by checking against allocation: Where does time and money actually go? That's your revealed values. Espoused values without enacted ones breed cynicism faster than no values at all.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Zapatista communities in Chiapas, Mexico** practice rotational leadership and rotate people out of power specifically to prevent value drift. They surface their commitment to horizontalism and collective decision-making through ***asambleas*—assemblies where values collisions are debated explicitly. When a decision violates the community's commitment to inclusion or consent, it's challenged publicly, and the conversation re-centers shared purpose. This has held their movement together across decades of external pressure.

**Mondragon Cooperative Corporation** (Basque region) embedded values surfacing into their founding: all members participate in an annual general assembly where decisions about profit distribution, wages, and governance are made collectively. When tensions emerge between paying higher wages and investing in expansion, the conversation is explicit and rooted in their articulated values of solidarity and democratic control. New cooperatives joining the network receive induction into these values precisely because they're not assumed.

**Ubuntu Ubuntu** (a South African restorative justice initiative) uses **values circles** in communities healing from violence. They surface what each person holds sacred—dignity, honesty, connection—and then work through collisions explicitly. When victim and perpetrator's values clash (justice vs. mercy, for instance), the circle doesn't smooth over the tension. It holds both values in conversation, allowing genuine transformation rather than suppressed resentment. The practice is rooted in traditional African consensus-building, adapted for modern contexts.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed intelligence—whether AI systems, remote teams, or hybrid human-algorithm governance—makes values surfacing **urgent and more difficult simultaneously.**

More difficult because: Asynchronous communication obscures tone and intention. Large-scale communities can operate at such speed that the slow, relational work of values articulation feels like a luxury you can't afford. AI systems can optimize for measurable metrics while erasing unmeasurable values—like belonging, or sacred tradition—that humans hold dear but can't easily quantify.

More urgent because: When decisions are made by algorithms informed by ambiguous objectives, collisions surface faster and with less room for repair. A content moderation AI trained on a community's stated values but in tension with its enacted ones will make choices that feel like betrayal. Distributed teams spanning continents and time zones need explicit values *precisely* because there's no ambient culture to hold people in alignment.

**The adaptive shift:** Communities in the cognitive era need to surface values *before* encoding them into systems. Ask your values questions before you automate decisions. And build feedback loops: when an AI system makes a choice that feels wrong, treat that as a values collision signal. The friction isn't a failure of the system; it's the system revealing a value you hadn't made explicit.

Some communities are experimenting with **"values registries"**—public declarations of priorities and trade-offs that AI systems reference. Others create human-AI governance councils where values collisions get reviewed by people who can deliberate, not just optimize.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

Conflict surfaces quickly and is treated as information, not scandal. New members ask *What do we actually care about?* and get real answers. When someone advocates for a decision, others ask *Which of our values does this serve?* and mean it genuinely. There's visible discomfort—people tensing when their held values are questioned—but also visible repair: people returning, acknowledging the tension, and working through it.

You hear people say *I disagree with this choice, but I understand why we made it.* You see resource allocation that matches espoused values. When external pressure demands compromise, the community negotiates from clarity, not panic.

**Signs of decay:**

People leave quietly, saying "it's just not my community anymore" without explaining why. Decisions feel arbitrary, driven by whoever spoke loudest. New members get told *That's just how we are* when they ask why something is done a certain way. Conflict gets suppressed—people stop raising tensions because they've learned it won't be heard. Values are invoked selectively: this person's concern is dismissed as not aligned with "our values," but a different person's similar concern is honored.

You notice sarcasm about stated values. *Oh sure, we're about equity,* said with a bitter laugh. Time and money allocation contradicts what leaders claim to prioritize.

**The diagnostic question:**

*Can a new member, after one month, articulate three genuine tensions in what your community values—not from a handbook, but from observing how you actually choose?*

If yes: your values are alive, being tested, held with integrity.

If no: you're operating on mythology. The surfacing work is waiting.
