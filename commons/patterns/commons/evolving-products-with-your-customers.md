> Build products that grow and change with what people actually need, treating your whole lineup as a living system instea

### Section 1: Context

Organizations bring products to market within living systems—not just technological or commercial, but social and ecological. The classical approach treats product development as a predetermined sequence (stage-gate, phase-gate, PDCA) with innovation contained in the front end and execution in the back. But this assumes prediction is possible in complex markets. Modern practice recognizes a deeper reality: customers don't buy products; they hire solutions for lived needs (JTBD). Markets are not stable containers but shifting ecosystems with real carrying capacity limits—point at which offering proliferation destroys rather than creates value. Stewardship means managing this portfolio as a dynamic whole: parallel exploration to generate variation, rapid feedback loops to sense what truly serves, and periodic rationalization to release offerings that consume resources without reciprocal value. This lineage traces through Information Shapes Systems, Evolutionary Adaptation, and Carrying Capacity.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Predetermined product roadmaps vs. adaptive responsiveness to actual user needs and system limits.**

Organizations face three interlocking tensions: (1) *Prediction vs. Learning*—detailed roadmaps commit resources based on forecasts that prove wrong, while reactive development loses strategic coherence. (2) *Growth vs. Carrying Capacity*—portfolios expand because each offering has a champion, but proliferation confuses customers, strains operations, and dilutes focus below the threshold of excellence. (3) *Speed vs. Alignment*—concurrent teams can accelerate execution, but without shared understanding of the actual job being done, speed produces misdirection. The result: organizations ship faster but learn slower, portfolios grow without intention, and the relationship between offering and user need becomes obscured. The tension is not merely logistical but systemic: a portfolio that ignores its own capacity for stewardship degrades.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement **Product Stewardship as Adaptive Cycles** — a governance structure that treats portfolio evolution as a continuous, multi-scale feedback loop:

**1. DISCOVERY GATE (Sensing Job & Need)**
Before designing, listen. Use Jobs-to-be-Done inquiry to understand the actual work users are trying to accomplish. Map this against current offerings: which jobs are well-served? Which are orphaned? Which are being served by competitors because your portfolio has no answer? This is not market research—it is stewardship intelligence. The output is a portfolio hypothesis: "Our offerings should serve these three core jobs, and we should intentionally abandon these three because they distract from our carrying capacity." Discovery cycles every 6-12 months (not every roadmap, but every portfolio decision).

**2. PARALLEL EXPLORATION (Variation & Selection)**
When pursuing a new offering or re-imagining an existing one, run 2-3 parallel development streams using concurrent engineering principles. Each stream tests a different hypothesis about how to serve the job. Cross-functional teams work in parallel, not sequence, sharing learning in weekly synthesis sessions. The goal is not to optimize one path early but to generate intelligent variation and learn which approach aligns best with both user need and organizational capacity. After 4-6 weeks, converge: select the strongest direction, retire the others, and move forward with disciplined execution.

**3. PHASE-GATED DELIVERY (Rapid Feedback Cycles)**
Once a direction is chosen, move through short phases (2-4 weeks each), with explicit gates between them. At each gate: Does this still serve the core job? Have we learned anything that changes our hypothesis? Does this still fit our portfolio carrying capacity, or have we discovered that it conflicts with another offering? Gates are not approval checkpoints but adaptive decision points. This embeds PDCA cycles at product level and allows real-time rationalization—the ability to pause, pivot, or sunset an offering based on emerging evidence, not sunk cost.

**4. PORTFOLIO RATIONALIZATION CADENCE (Systemic Carrying Capacity)**
Quarterly, the portfolio stewardship team convenes to assess the whole ecosystem. Which offerings are at capacity (mature, profitable, defended)? Which are still ramping (learning curves in place, tracking toward productivity)? Which are consuming resources without reciprocal value and should be retired? Use explicit carrying capacity metrics: customer cognition (how many distinct offerings can our market segment hold in mind?), operational complexity (does this offering share infrastructure or create isolated burden?), strategic coherence (does this offering reinforce or distract from our core job thesis?). Rationalization is not cost-cutting—it is stewardship. Retiring a mediocre offering frees energy for excellence elsewhere.

**5. LIFECYCLE PROGRESSION (Stages of Stewardship)**
Each offering moves through recognizable stages with different stewardship needs: *Emergence* (heavy learning, high resource intensity, high uncertainty—suited to parallel experiments); *Growth* (hypothesis confirmed, optimization begins, productivity ramp tracked); *Maturity* (stable, well-defended, generates surplus that funds exploration); *Decline* (market shifting, carrying cost rising relative to value—prepare for graceful retirement); *Renewal or Release* (either reinvent to serve an evolving job, or retire to restore carrying capacity). Clear handoffs between stages prevent offerings from persisting in the wrong stewardship mode.

**6. ALIGNMENT THROUGH SHARED MEANING**
Beyond structure, this requires that every team member can articulate which core job their work serves. This is not mission-speak but precise understanding: "We serve the job of X; our offering is one of three ways we serve it; here is why we chose this approach; here is what we are learning about whether it works." This coherence prevents the portfolio from fragmenting into isolated projects. It also creates the conditions for graceful letting-go: when an offering is retired, it is not seen as failure but as stewardship—we tried, we learned, we released resources to serve what matters more.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by establishing a **Portfolio Sensing Council**—a rotating cross-functional team (product, support, sales, customers) that meets monthly to listen for weak signals. Don't wait for quarterly reviews. Assign someone to actually read support tickets, attend customer calls unfiltered, and track which products are being used in unexpected ways. This is cultivation work: you're learning the ecosystem's actual health, not what dashboards claim.

Next, create **three portfolio zones** with explicit care protocols:

*Growth Zone* (15-25% of effort): New offerings solving orphaned jobs. These are experiments. Fund them modestly, set a clear decision point (6-12 months), and be willing to retire them without shame. A failed experiment teaches more than a bloated launch.

*Maintenance Zone* (50-60% of effort): Products serving core jobs well. Here, optimize ruthlessly—not for growth, but for reliability, simplicity, and ecosystem fit. This is where you build the trust that funds the rest.

*Sunset Zone* (5-10% of effort): Products past their useful life. Rather than abandoning them, migrate users intentionally to what serves their job better. Document why each product is ending; that knowledge belongs to your org.

Within each zone, **run quarterly discovery sprints**: five days of intensive customer immersion per product line. Bring developers, not just researchers. Let engineers hear firsthand what breaks, what confuses, what delights.

Finally, establish a **Portfolio Coherence Review** (twice yearly) where you ask: Does this lineup *together* serve our core customers' evolving needs? Or are we maintaining products because we built them, not because they belong? Use this moment to make hard retirement and investment calls, grounded in real listening rather than politics.

The discipline isn't planning better. It's *staying close to actual use* and letting that teach you what to build next.

### Section 5: Consequences

What flourishes: **Customer loyalty deepens** when people sense you're genuinely responsive to their changing needs, not defending old product bets. Support costs drop because you're solving real problems, not designing features nobody asked for. Teams develop **true ownership mentality**—they're stewards of customer outcomes, not feature factories. The portfolio becomes coherent: products work *together* to solve job clusters, not compete cannibally. You attract people who care about craft over velocity metrics.

What risks emerge if neglected: Without disciplined listening, you drift into **responsive chaos**—every loud customer shapes the roadmap, fragmenting focus. Products accumulate cruft because retiring them feels like failure. The portfolio bloats, becoming a liability: more to support, more to integrate, more cognitive load on customers choosing which tool fits their need. Engineers burn out maintaining zombie products. Worse, **misaligned products actually harm each other**—a half-baked new offering undermines trust in your core. If you skip the Sunset Zone work, customers feel abandoned: you launch new solutions while ignoring their legacy problems, signaling you've moved on from them.

The deeper risk: **Losing voice in the market.** When your lineup is incoherent, customers can't narrate what you *do*. They see a toolbox, not a worldview. Competitors with clearer purpose pull your best customers away—not because they're technically superior, but because they stand for something.

The antidote is discipline: ruthless portfolio hygiene, transparent retirement processes, and the humility to admit when an offering no longer belongs.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Basecamp** (productivity software) exemplifies this pattern sharply. They've deliberately *shrunk* their product line, retiring features and even services that didn't align with their core philosophy of simplicity and asynchronous work. When they killed Basecamp's task management in favor of a cleaner approach, it felt like subtraction—but it was stewardship. They listen constantly to how teams actually use the product, and they're willing to say "that job isn't ours to solve anymore."

**Patagonia** operates across a sprawling product ecosystem (apparel, gear, advocacy) but treats portfolio decisions as ecological questions: Does this product serve our environmental mission? Does it strain manufacturing capacity beyond regenerative limits? Can we make it better than last season, or should it rest? Their decision to use only traceable materials forced portfolio simplification—some products were retired because the supply chain couldn't be made transparent. This was stewardship, not failure.

**Grameen Bank** (microfinance, Bangladesh) evolved its service portfolio by staying radically close to borrower needs in rural communities. Rather than imposing a fixed product, they adapted loan sizes, terms, and repayment schedules as they learned what actually enabled poverty reduction. This adaptive approach meant some early offerings were abandoned because they didn't match real economic rhythms. The discipline of continuous discovery—listening to what jobs villagers were actually trying to solve—kept the portfolio alive and locally coherent.

In each case, the pattern isn't about launching more. It's about *listening more*, retiring more honestly, and treating the whole lineup as a breathing organism that must earn its place.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI transforms this pattern in three ways:

First, **discovery scales without adding researchers.** Sentiment analysis across support tickets, call transcripts, and usage logs can surface weak signals in real time—which job clusters are emerging, which are being abandoned. But AI alone is a false compass. You still need humans *immersed* in actual use to sense what the data is missing. The pattern intensifies: AI handles the volume; humans provide the judgment.

Second, **portfolio coherence becomes computable.** You can map which products serve overlapping jobs, visualize where gaps exist, and simulate the impact of retiring one offering on customer workflows. This lets you make portfolio decisions faster and with less politics—the data speaks. But beware: optimizing a portfolio map isn't the same as serving real customers. AI surfaces what's possible; stewardship decides what's *right*.

Third, **distributed intelligence changes how you listen.** Customers increasingly co-develop in public (open-source communities, Discord servers, GitHub discussions). The boundary between "user" and "builder" blurs. Your portfolio sensing work becomes less about extracting insights from customers and more about *participating in evolving ecosystems*. You're a steward among stewards, not an oracle of need.

The risk: AI can trick you into thinking you understand customers better when you're actually just processing signals faster. Predicting what people need remains hard. The antidote is the same—stay close, stay humble, let listening drive design.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Support teams surface new customer needs *before* they become complaints; you act on them, and customers feel heard.
- Your quarterly portfolio review includes genuine retirement decisions, not just additions. Teams can articulate *why* a product was sunsetted—and why that decision honored the customers it served.
- New offerings take 6-9 months from discovery to launch, not 2-3 years. You're learning fast, not planning endlessly.
- Customers can tell a coherent story about your lineup: "You help teams coordinate asynchronously" (Basecamp) or "You make durable gear for wild places" (Patagonia). The portfolio reinforces one another.
- Engineers volunteer for the "boring" work of maintaining core products because they see the impact there, not just in launches.

**Signs of decay:**
- Your roadmap is locked in, negotiated a year ago. Customer feedback doesn't substantially change it.
- Products linger in the portfolio despite low usage and high maintenance burden. Retiring them feels like admitting failure.
- The portfolio is confusing: customers ask "do you do X or Y?" and the answer is "kind of both, it's complicated."
- Support teams are cynical about whether feedback reaches builders. They document problems but expect nothing to change.
- Decision-making about product investments is political, not evidence-based. The loudest stakeholder wins, regardless of customer signal.

**Diagnostic question:**
*When a customer job changes, how many months before your product lineup reflects that?* If the answer is "months of discussion" or "never," the pattern has atrophied. If it's weeks, with clear listening and stewardship visible, you're alive.
