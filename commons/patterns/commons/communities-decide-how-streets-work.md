### Section 1: Context

Urban mobility has been designed as a technical problem solved by planners and engineers—optimal routes, traffic flow, parking efficiency. But mobility is not primarily a technical problem; it is a question of power and belonging: Who decides how public space is used? Whose movement is prioritized? Who pays and who benefits? In cities where these decisions remain technocratic, they systematically reflect the interests of car owners (politically vocal, concentrated) over transit-dependent residents (dispersed, often marginalized). The O1 principles of Subsidiarity, Collective Action, and Reciprocity suggest a different foundation: those whose lives are shaped by mobility systems should design them. Neighborhood assemblies—monthly or quarterly gatherings where residents deliberate mobility choices together—offer a structure for distributed decision-making at the scale where impacts are felt and knowledge is lived.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Technocratic mobility allocation (speed, routes, space, budgets decided by planners and engineers) vs. Democratic stewardship (residents governing their own movement and public space through participatory assembly).**

Three tensions drive this pattern. First: technocratic allocation vs. democratic stewardship. Engineers optimize for system efficiency (vehicle throughput, cost per rider). Residents optimize for belonging (safe streets, walkable neighborhoods, equitable access). These are not the same. Second: concentrated interests vs. dispersed needs. Car owners lobby for parking and wider lanes; transit-dependent residents (elderly, disabled, low-income) have weaker political voice despite greater dependence. Participatory budgeting without structural bias amplifies concentrated car-owner interests, locking in automobile dependence. Third: static planning vs. adaptive rhythm. Mobility needs shift seasonally (school calendars, agricultural patterns), demographically (migration, aging), and unpredictably (new employment centers, community events). Rigid 5-year plans cannot respond; residents living in neighborhoods can. The pattern must distribute decision-making power, weight it toward equity, and embed it in short feedback cycles.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish permanent neighborhood mobility assemblies (20–50 persons per neighborhood, reflecting resident demographics) meeting quarterly with binding authority over local mobility commons. The assembly governs four domains:

**1. Street Space Allocation.** Quarterly assemblies decide how to distribute street cross-section—width and priority for walking, cycling, transit, cars, loading, vendors, seating, gathering. Decisions are data-informed (resident mobility surveys, equity mapping) but community-sovereign. This displaces technocratic engineering with democratic stewardship. Weighted voting ensures mobility-poor, disabled, and car-free residents have amplified voice (e.g., 3x weight). Allocation is binding; city adjusts infrastructure accordingly.

**2. Microtransit Routing & Scheduling.** Assemblies design hyperlocal demand-responsive service (flexible-route vans, microtransit, adaptive shuttle schedules) informed by real-time resident requests. Routes and schedules adjust seasonally, respond to demographic shifts, and adapt to community events. Residents propose changes; assembly deliberates; transit operator implements. This makes service responsive rather than forecasted.

**3. Participatory Mobility Budget.** Annual participatory budgeting cycle allocates fixed portion (15–25%) of neighborhood transit/mobility budget through assembly vote. Residents directly choose whether to fund new routes, frequency increases, station upgrades, cycling infrastructure, or loading zone expansion. Equity weighting ensures choices serve mobility-poor residents. This aligns incentives: residents who benefit from decisions help make them.

**4. Speed & Signal Governance.** Assembly collectively sets safe speeds for corridors (replacing citywide defaults), signal timing, and transit signal priority—balancing transit speed with pedestrian safety and neighborhood character. Binds on traffic authority.

**Structural elements:** Assemblies meet quarterly on fixed schedule; decisions are public and transparent; implementation is tracked and reported back to assembly; unequal voice is addressed through weighted voting (mobility-poor residents, disabled residents, youth, elderly have 2–3x vote weight); conflict resolution is deliberative (disagreement triggers deeper assembly dialogue, not bureaucratic override); assembly membership rotates to prevent capture; transit workers and operators participate as deliberators, not just implementers; outcomes are monitored for equity gaps and fed back into next cycle.

**Why this holds as commons:** Governance is distributed (not concentrated in city hall), adaptive (quarterly cycles allow fast feedback), just (weighted voice ensures equity), alive (learns from outcomes and adjusts), and built to last (residents hold ongoing authority, not temporary consultation).

### Section 4: Implementation

Start small and local. A neighborhood mobility assembly begins not with citywide authority but with one street, one neighborhood, one season. Here's how to cultivate it:

**Convene deliberately.** Recruit 20–50 people who represent the neighborhood's actual composition—renters and owners, elderly and young families, people with disabilities, immigrants, essential workers, shop owners. Don't wait for perfect diversity; start with whoever shows up, then deliberately invite the missing voices. Meet in a familiar place: a community center, a church basement, someone's front porch. Quarterly is often enough to matter without burning people out.

**Make the authority real.** This is not a suggestions box. The assembly needs genuine power over a real budget (even if modest—$50,000–$200,000 annually for a neighborhood of 5,000 people). They decide: Where do we paint a bus lane? How much parking do we keep, and where? Do we expand the sidewalk here and narrow the street there? Can vendors use the curb? Which intersection needs traffic calming? The city staff executes what the assembly decides, not the reverse.

**Use structured conversation, not debate.** Design each meeting so people think together rather than argue past each other. Start with concrete observation: walk the street together, photograph problem spots, listen to how different people use the same space. Then move to preference: What do you want for your kids playing here? For your elderly neighbor getting to the bus? For delivery drivers, shop owners, people with strollers? Finally, allocate trade-offs together: We can have wider sidewalks *or* more parking, not both. Which matters more? Watch how consensus often emerges when people feel heard.

**Document and iterate.** Record what the assembly decides and why. Paint the new lane. Then observe what actually happens over three months. Bring the results back to the assembly. Did the bus lane work? Did people still park illegally? Did walking feel safer? Let evidence and real experience guide the next iteration.

**Connect assemblies.** Once three or four neighborhoods are running assemblies, create a federation where they meet together on issues that span boundaries—a major transit corridor, a district-wide parking strategy. This prevents one neighborhood from externalizing its problems onto another.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Streets become places again, not just movement systems. Neighbors notice each other because they're making decisions together. A street that felt hostile becomes legible—people understand *why* the design is as it is because they chose it. Walking and cycling increase not because the city mandates it but because the street actually feels safe and pleasant. Essential workers and renters gain a voice they've never had; their movement patterns (shift work, multiple jobs, children to school) finally enter planning. Local businesses benefit because vendors and shopkeepers help shape the space they depend on.

**What risks emerge if not tended:** Assemblies can be captured by the loudest people—car owners who show up angry, wealthy residents who treat mobility as a zero-sum game. Without deliberate inclusion, they replicate neighborhood privilege rather than challenge it. Some assemblies may make decisions that seem locally optimal but create problems downstream (a neighborhood bans through-traffic and sends it pouring into the next neighborhood). If the city doesn't genuinely surrender decision-making power, if staff override the assembly because of pressure from citywide interests, the whole thing becomes theater and trust evaporates. Budget constraints can create resentment—people learn that the real power remains with the city budget cycle, not the assembly. And if one assembly makes radically different choices than neighbors, it can feel unfair.

The antidote: real power, genuine inclusion, transparent trade-offs, and federation to prevent local optimization from becoming local burden-shifting.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Barcelona's Superblocks program** (Spain): Since 2016, Barcelona neighborhoods have organized assemblies to convert intersecting streets into car-restricted zones prioritizing children, elderly people, and lingering. Residents decided which streets to close to through-traffic and how to reallocate the space. The result: 70% reduction in traffic, measurable increases in outdoor play, stronger neighborhood social ties. The city provided the framework; residents made the choices street by street.

**Participatory budgeting in Medellín** (Colombia): For over a decade, Medellín's most marginalized neighborhoods have held assemblies where residents directly allocate municipal budgets—including for street redesign, transit stops, and pedestrian infrastructure. Residents of informal settlements made decisions that planners had never considered: footbridges instead of street widening, narrow stairways lit and beautified, sloped ramps for handcarts. The pattern shifted power away from technocrats toward the people most vulnerable to bad mobility design.

**Kowloon's street vending assemblies** (Hong Kong): Though operating in a more constrained context, neighborhood merchant and resident associations in Kowloon have won the right to collectively decide which streets accommodate vendors, at what hours, and under what conditions. These assemblies balance mobile vendors' livelihoods, resident commerce preferences, and pedestrian flow through regular negotiation rather than top-down regulation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As distributed intelligence enters city planning—real-time traffic data, routing algorithms, autonomous vehicles—the need for democratic mobility assemblies *intensifies* rather than diminishes.

Algorithms optimize for patterns, not purposes. They route trucks through quiet neighborhoods because the math says it's efficient. They price parking dynamically, which optimizes revenue but can displace the people who need the most affordable parking. They predict demand and shape supply in ways that feel inevitable but are actually value-laden choices. An assembly of residents can't out-engineer an algorithm, but it can refuse it—or demand that algorithms serve goals the assembly defines.

The pattern changes in three ways. First, assemblies gain better diagnostic tools: real-time data about how their street is actually used, simulation tools showing what happens if we change the design. But second, they also face more sophisticated capture. Ride-share companies and autonomous vehicle makers will lobby these assemblies hard; staff will need support to resist. Third, assemblies become spaces where humans reclaim choice in a world of optimization. The assembly is where we ask: *Should we* optimize this, or do we want something else—safety, beauty, chance encounters, justice? An algorithm can't answer that. Only people, together, can.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Residents show up not just once but multiple times, bringing new people and stories.
- The assembly decides something the city staff initially said was "impossible," and then the city finds a way to do it.
- A resident who uses the street differently than the majority—a delivery driver, a person with a cane, a child—is genuinely heard and their need changes the decision.
- The street noticeably changes. New paint. Removed parking. Different rhythm. Neighbors who weren't in the assembly see it and understand what happened.
- Conflict surfaces and resolves. Two groups want different things; the assembly sits with the tension, trades off carefully, and comes to a decision people can live with even if it wasn't their first choice.

**Signs of decay:**
- Attendance drops. The same 8 people show up quarter after quarter, and they look tired.
- The city accepts the assembly's decision but doesn't fund it or implements something different.
- One group dominates—the wealthy owner of a corner building, a vocal car advocate—and everyone else goes quiet.
- Nothing actually changes on the street. The assembly feels performative.
- Conflict becomes personal or stays unresolved. People stop coming because they dread the meeting.

**Diagnostic question:** *If the city stopped requiring the assembly to exist tomorrow, would neighbors choose to keep meeting?* If no, something vital is missing. If yes, you've built something real.
