> Make clear who has the power to decide what, and let those boundaries shift when context or crisis demands—without losin

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Business domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every organization that distributes authority — whether corporate federation, cooperative network, or public agency — faces a gravitational pull in two directions: downward, toward autonomous action and local knowledge; upward, toward coherence and shared constraint. This tension is not a problem to solve but a permanent condition to steward. The 28 O1 principles show us that Polycentric Organisation, Subsidiarity, and Conflict Resolution are the gravitational centers. In living systems, decision authority is not hoarded at a center but cultivated at multiple nested scales simultaneously. Yet without explicit boundaries, autonomy becomes fragmentation. Without adaptation mechanisms, boundaries become brittle. The commons engineer must design decision authority as a living structure — one that is mapped, maintained, and refreshed as the system evolves.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Autonomy at scale (distributed decision-making that doesn't fragment the commons) vs. Coherence under pressure (shared purpose that doesn't collapse into paralysis or arbitrary hierarchy).**

Organizations distribute decision authority for speed, local knowledge, and empowerment. But without systematic clarity, authority becomes ambiguous, informal, or contested. The costs are severe: (1) Decisions stall in escalation bottlenecks or deadlock when authority overlaps or is unclear. (2) Informal leadership (actual power) diverges from formal roles (stated authority), creating hidden hierarchies and eroded trust. (3) Crises expose the absence of rules — authority collapses into either paralysis or autocracy. (4) Compliance and segregation-of-duties rules become theater if authority boundaries are not documented and maintained. (5) As roles, volumes, or structures change, old authority rules persist until they cause visible failure. The problem is not whether to delegate authority but how to steward it as a living, transparent, contested, and continuously refreshed commons — rather than allowing it to calcify into policy or fragment into shadow power.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Design and steward decision authority through five nested, reinforcing practices:

**1. Authority Boundary Mapping (Nested Levels).** Make explicit, in writing, which decisions belong to which roles, teams, and organizational layers. Use subsidiarity as the rule: a decision stays at the lowest competent level unless it violates commons coherence. Create nested matrices: individual authority (who can approve a single transaction up to what amount?), role authority (what is the span of authority for this job?), team authority (which decisions belong to the team vs. escalated?), and organizational layer authority (which decisions require board, executive, unit, or functional approval?). Include context variance: the same person may have different authority in normal operations than in crisis. Document segregation-of-duties constraints: which two authorities can never co-exist in one role? Maintain this as a living artifact, not static policy.

**2. Conflict Resolution & Revision Cycle.** Authority boundaries will be contested or will fail. Treat this as signal, not noise. Maintain an explicit log of decisions that created conflict, were escalated unexpectedly, or stalled. Every 12 months (or after major structural change), review these logs and revise authority boundaries. Who escalated more than expected? Where did informal leaders emerge? Which boundaries created delays? Adjust the map to match both the work and the trust. This is adaptive management applied to governance itself.

**3. Crisis Authority Variance.** In stable conditions, authority flows through subsidiarity. In crisis (environmental incident, labor dispute, reputational threat, supply disruption), different rules may activate. Define in advance: which decisions centralize in a crisis? Which remain distributed? Who has emergency authority to act beyond normal limits, and for how long? Explicitly tie crisis authority to purpose and stakeholder obligation, not just speed. Once crisis passes, revert to normal authority and review what changed and why.

**4. Delegation & Acting Authority Protocol.** When a decision-maker is absent (leave, travel, interim) or departed (successor not yet seated), authority does not vanish — it is explicitly delegated. The delegating authority names the acting delegate, specifies the scope and duration, and makes this visible to everyone who might interact with that authority. Require sign-off from both delegator and delegate. For permanent departures, establish a clear transition: who holds authority in the interim? When must the successor be seated? This prevents informal power-seizure and maintains commons coherence during transition.

**5. Transparency & Visibility as Commons Act.** Publish the authority boundaries where those who are affected can see them. This is not transparency for compliance — it is visibility as a commons stewardship act. Every contributor should be able to answer: "Who decides this, and under what conditions?" When a decision requires escalation, the person escalating should be able to point to the boundary that makes that necessary. Visibility creates shared accountability, reveals where informal authority has diverged from formal role, and enables collective learning. When the map and the reality diverge, that divergence becomes visible and can be addressed.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping authority *as it actually exists*, not as the org chart pretends. Gather the people who make or influence decisions in your domain—product, hiring, budget, strategy—and ask: *Who decided last time? Who should have? Who will next?* Write down the answers. Contradictions are gifts; they show you where trust has already cracked.

Then design three nested layers of authority:

**Tier 1: Autonomous Decisions.** Document what each role or team can decide alone—spending up to $X, hiring for their team, choosing tools, adjusting process. The key is specificity and permission: "You own this" is weaker than "You own hiring for frontend roles, budget through $50K, and can veto tools that break our stack standards."

**Tier 2: Consent-Based Decisions.** These affect others but don't need approval. A team changing its sprint structure, a department shifting focus—others have a right to voice concerns or block if it violates shared commitments, but the burden isn't on them to say yes. Make consent *cheap*: a 48-hour async window, a shared document, clear what "blocking" means.

**Tier 3: Collective Decisions.** These touch core commons: mission, values, major resource allocation, rules that bind everyone. These require active agreement, not just absence of objection. Use ranked choice, consensus, or other explicit methods. Keep this layer small; decisions here move slowly because they should.

**The Emergency Override.** Write it down. If crisis hits—security breach, financial cliff, key person departs—who has the authority to act unilaterally, and for how long? When must they hand back authority to the normal structure? Knowing this reduces panic and prevents the power grab that happens in silence.

Post these maps where people actually work. Review them quarterly. Authority shifts when context shifts; make that visible. When someone asks "Can I decide this?" the answer should live in a document, not in someone's head.

### Section 5: Consequences

When authority boundaries are clear and trusted, decisions accelerate at every level. Teams move fast because they know what's theirs. Escalations shrink because people understand what they can't unilaterally change. Morale lifts because autonomy is real, not performative.

What flourishes: Distributed problem-solving. Local adaptation. Speed without recklessness. Trust that the system won't shift ground arbitrarily.

What can decay: **Siloed thinking**, where autonomous teams optimize locally and drift from shared purpose. Guard this by keeping Tier 3 (collective) decisions alive and visible—they're the glue. **Hidden hierarchy**, where informal influence becomes more real than formal authority. This happens when people distrust the official boundaries. The cure is not more documentation but transparency: who's actually deciding? Why? **Fragmentation under stress**, where the emergency override gets used too often or too loosely, corroding the trust that keeps distributed authority stable. Use it, but use it sparingly, and always return power explicitly.

The deepest risk: **Clarity without relationship**. A beautifully mapped authority structure means nothing if people don't trust the judgment of those who hold authority. Authority boundaries must be paired with transparency about *how* decisions get made, *who* influences them, and *why* trade-offs were chosen. Without that, even clear boundaries feel arbitrary.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Buurtzorg (Netherlands, healthcare).** This network of small nursing teams distributed hiring, scheduling, and client decisions to the team level while reserving major strategic shifts and shared standards to the network council. Each team of 10–12 nurses had near-total autonomy over their work; the network held boundaries on patient care protocols and shared pricing. This structure let them scale to thousands of nurses while keeping decision-making close to the patient.

**Ostrom's Irrigation Communities (Nepal and elsewhere).** Elinor Ostrom's research documented farmer collectives managing shared water systems across nested levels: individual field decisions, seasonal water allocation (collective), and long-term infrastructure investment (network governance). Clear rules about who decided what—and at what scale—let these communities sustain commons for centuries without external authority.

**Haier (China, manufacturing and appliances).** Rather than layers of approval, Haier restructured into thousands of microenterprises, each owning their P&L and hiring. Core decisions (brand, supply chain standards, major capital) stayed central. Frontline teams decided almost everything else. Decision authority shifted visibly as the company scaled, but the principle—clarity about what's yours to decide—remained consistent.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence change the *texture* of decision authority but not the fundamental tension. With real-time data and algorithmic recommendation, decisions that once required human judgment—which candidate to hire, which customer to prioritize—now arrive pre-digested. The temptation is to automate decision authority: let the algorithm decide.

This breaks trust. People accept decisions they don't fully understand if they know a *person* is accountable for them. Algorithms are not persons. So the new skill is *algorithmic stewardship*: clarity about which decisions humans will retain, which they'll delegate to systems, and how they'll oversee the delegation. "The algorithm recommends; the hiring manager decides" is a clear authority boundary in the AI age.

Distributed teams working across time zones with asynchronous tools need even clearer authority maps because real-time escalation is harder. The written boundary becomes the substitute for the quick conversation. And with AI assistants in the loop, the question "Who decided?" gets harder to answer. Audit trails and decision logs—not just outcomes—become part of stewardship.

Finally, the speed of AI-assisted change means authority boundaries need to refresh more often. Quarterly review becomes monthly or rolling. The pattern stays; the pace accelerates.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life:** People can answer "Can I decide this?" without asking their manager. Decisions move at the right speed for their layer—autonomous decisions happen in hours, collective ones take weeks. When authority boundaries shift (a crisis, a new market, a merger), the organization discusses it openly; change doesn't sneak in as informal workaround. Exit interviews don't cite "not knowing who was really in charge" as a reason people left.

**Signs of Decay:** Escalation creep (more decisions climbing to leadership). Long email threads asking "Is this my call?" Authority diverging from responsibility—people held accountable for decisions they didn't make. Informal leaders making real decisions while formal roles go through theater. New people confused about how things get decided.

**A Diagnostic Question:** Pick a recent non-trivial decision in your organization—a hire, a product pivot, a budget reallocation. Now ask three people: *Who decided that, and should they have?* If their answers differ, your authority boundaries are unclear. If they're the same, ask one more: *Could they explain why it was their decision to make?* If the answer is "it's just how things work," your clarity is more ritual than trust.
