### Section 1: Context

Every enterprise—whether a three-person cooperative, a scaling platform, or a multi-stakeholder hybrid—now depends on diverse contributors: employees, suppliers, community members, investors, knowledge-holders, and risk-bearers. For centuries, businesses concentrated surplus into shareholder hands, externalizing costs onto workers and the Earth. Today, labour markets, regulatory pressure, and moral clarity demand a different architecture. Skilled workers reject precarity. Communities withhold consent from extractive ventures. Investors increasingly recognize that durable value requires stakeholder alignment. This cluster of 29 patterns reveals a common root: the need to design profit distribution as a transparent, rule-governed system—not as charity, discretion, or an afterthought—that visibly connects contribution to reciprocal return. This grounds two foundational O1 principles: Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (fair rules build lasting cooperation) and Transparency as Commons Act (visible allocation corrodes the distrust that kills commons).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Concentrated extraction vs. equitable stewardship — the fundamental choice between profit flowing to a single centre of power or circulating through the whole system that created it..**

Three tensions collide: First, opacity corrodes cooperation. When stakeholders cannot see how profit flows from value creation through allocation and reinvestment, they default to zero-trust accounting—assuming extraction before distribution. This fear paralyzes voluntary participation. Second, concentration creates fragile systems. When surplus concentrates into a single stakeholder's hands, all others become mercenaries optimizing for exit rather than durability. Third, fairness is invisible without rules. Without transparent allocation principles, 'fair distribution' becomes subjective politics. Disputes fracture coalitions. Legal complexity—tax codes, jurisdictions, equity structures—creates loopholes that allow extractive intent to hide behind technical compliance. The result: businesses claiming to be commons generate the same stakeholder alienation as pure extraction, because the difference lives only in intention, not in visible operating system.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Design profit distribution as a transparent, rule-governed system embedded in the organization's operating architecture. This pattern has five interlocking moves:

**1. Make allocation rules explicit and visible.** Before profit is calculated, define which stakeholder groups share in distribution and by what formula. This might be: 40% to labour (distributed by hours, role, tenure), 25% to reinvestment (expansion, resilience, commons contribution), 20% to investor returns (tiered by capital tier), 15% to community stewardship. The formula is less important than explicitness. Publish it. Defend it. Evolve it through governance, not discretion.

**2. Separate calculation from distribution.** Build transparent accounting that traces value creation → gross surplus → distribution waterfall → stakeholder returns. This is not standard accounting; it is genealogical. Show where profit comes from. Show each allocation gate. This visibility corrodes the assumption that money vanishes into opacity.

**3. Govern through subsidiarity, not fiat.** Allocation rules should be set through deliberation among stakeholder groups, not imposed by founders or executives. A quota in a fishery, a rebate in a cooperative, a profit-share in a platform—each is a governance artefact that works only if those affected helped design it. This transforms distribution from extraction into reciprocal stewardship.

**4. Adapt the mechanism to context, not the principle.** A worker cooperative uses patronage rebates; a platform uses revenue-share tiers; a hybrid uses multi-stakeholder equity; a fishery uses catch quotas. The underlying pattern is identical: transparent rule-governed return correlated to contribution. Encode principle; vary mechanism.

**5. Close the feedback loop.** Annual or quarterly, report back to stakeholders: total value created, surplus generated, allocation by category, individual returns received, reinvestment deployed. This completes the cycle: contribution → value creation → transparent distribution → returned stewardship. It signals that the organization treats profit as a commons flow, not a private extraction.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping your value chain. Who contributes what to the creation of surplus—not just employees, but suppliers, knowledge-holders, communities, risk-bearers? Name them honestly. This map becomes your governance reference.

Next, design your allocation formula collaboratively. Bring stakeholders into the room (or async process) to propose how profit should flow. This isn't comfortable—people will disagree about their own worth—but that friction is the work. Document the formula in plain language. Avoid opaque percentages; show the logic. For example: "Labour receives 40% because wages are deferred; reinvestment receives 30% because scaling serves all stakeholders; community benefit receives 15% because we extract from shared resources; shareholders receive 15% because capital took risk upfront."

Implement transparency systems next. This means publishing (internally, then externally) your profit and loss, stakeholder payouts, and formula application. Some organizations use open-book management: monthly financials shared with all employees. Others publish annual sustainability reports with detailed allocation breakdowns. Choose a cadence and medium that fits your scale.

Build feedback loops into your system. Review allocation rules annually or every two years. Did the formula serve fairness? Did it produce unintended consequences? Which stakeholders feel undercompensated? Did any group's power grow unchecked? Adjust the rules based on what you learn.

Create decision-making structures that distribute authority over allocation. Don't let founders or executives alone determine how profit flows—establish profit councils, stakeholder committees, or democratic voting on major allocation changes. This anchors fairness in process, not goodwill.

Finally, tie compensation to the formula. When profit arrives, distribute it automatically according to the agreed rules. Use tools like blockchain transparency, profit-sharing accounts, or cooperative dividend payouts to make the flow visible and irrevocable. Remove discretion from the moment of distribution; the only discretion should be in the annual review of the formula itself.

### Section 5: Consequences

When allocation rules become transparent and shared, trust begins to accumulate. People see fairness in real time. Turnover decreases. Voluntary effort increases—people work harder when they know surplus returns to them and their community. Collaboration deepens because no one is hoarding information about who really benefits.

Reinvestment becomes strategic rather than extractive. When a portion of profit is reserved for community, infrastructure, or long-term resilience, the organization develops roots. It stops treating the system that sustains it as an externality.

However, transparent allocation creates visibility around inequality that some stakeholders may have preferred to ignore. A founder who previously extracted 60% of profit, now receiving 15%, may experience this as loss—and may leave. Investors accustomed to venture-scale returns may not accept cooperative dividends. This is not a bug; it's the pattern working as intended. It selects for stakeholders aligned with stewardship, not extraction.

The risk of formulaic thinking is real. If your allocation rule becomes rigid dogma, it may freeze unfairness in place. A formula designed for a 20-person team may break at 200 people. Labour's 40% may exclude unpaid care work that sustained the operation. Review and adapt, or the system calcifies.

There is also the danger of shallow transparency—publishing numbers while decision-making remains opaque. If stakeholders see the allocation formula but have no voice in changing it, transparency becomes theatre. The participation must be real.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Cooperative Corporation** (Basque region, Spain): This federation of over 80 worker cooperatives has allocated profits through democratic voting and transparent formulas for 70 years. Wages are compressed (the ratio between highest and lowest paid workers is typically 3:1 to 5:1, compared to 100:1+ in mainstream corporations). Surpluses fund community education, healthcare, and cooperative expansion. Members regularly vote on allocation changes. The pattern has survived economic crises and global competition.

**Patagonia (B Corporation transition)**: The outdoor apparel company has long used transparent profit allocation: significant percentages flow to environmental nonprofits (1% of sales, later increased), employee benefits above industry standard, and community reinvestment. In 2022, founder Yvon Chouinard restructured the company to lock this in permanently, creating a governance structure where profit allocation rules cannot be reversed by future shareholders. The formula is published in their sustainability reports annually.

**Dignity USA Agricultural Cooperative** (Zimbabwe): A farmer-owned collective has designed allocation rules that distribute proceeds from crop sales by land contribution (25%), labour participation (40%), cooperative investment (20%), and emergency reserves (15%). The formula was designed by member assemblies and is reviewed every harvest season. This transparency has increased smallholder farmers' income stability and reduced dependence on exploitative middlemen. The pattern adapts to seasonal variation and drought through democratic adjustment.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world of AI-driven analytics and real-time financial systems, profit allocation becomes auditable in ways that were impossible in previous eras. Every transaction can be traced. Every stakeholder's contribution can be measured and visualized. This creates opportunity and peril.

The opportunity: algorithmic transparency. Machine learning can model allocation formulas against outcomes—fairness, retention, innovation, resilience—and suggest adjustments. Blockchain systems can execute allocation automatically, eliminating human discretion (and corruption) from the moment of payout. Distributed stakeholders—suppliers in different countries, gig workers, open-source contributors—can be woven into allocation systems that previous eras lacked the infrastructure to coordinate.

The peril: metric capture. When AI quantifies contribution, what gets measured becomes what matters. Open-source contributors invisible to Github metrics get excluded. Care work, community stewardship, and knowledge-sharing—the glue that holds complex systems together—vanish from algorithmic sight. AI may also accelerate pressure toward efficiency-maximizing allocation rules that sacrifice resilience and equity for profit optimization.

The pattern's vital move: keep humans in the loop of allocation *decisions*, even as AI handles the execution and analysis. Use AI to reveal hidden contribution (mining data from Slack, email, informal networks). But let assemblies, councils, and democratic processes decide *why* certain contributions matter and how they should be valued. The formula itself should be a human choice, not an optimized output.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: Stakeholders can explain the allocation formula without consulting documents. People reference it in conversations, not defensively but matter-of-factly—"That's how we built it." Turnover in lower-paid roles stabilizes. Reinvested capital begins to show effects: community infrastructure improves, employees gain new skills, suppliers stabilize. When you review the formula annually, multiple stakeholder groups propose adjustments, not just leadership. There is productive tension about fairness, but it surfaces openly rather than festering as resentment.

**Signs of decay**: The formula exists on paper but nobody references it. Finance remains opaque despite official policies of transparency. When people ask "how does allocation work?" they get different answers depending on who you ask. Turnover remains high in roles that receive lower payout percentages. During profit surges, the formula is quietly ignored and surplus concentrates at the top. When the time comes to review the formula, only leadership shows up to the conversation. External stakeholders (suppliers, community members) have no seat at the table, even though they contributed to the value.

**Diagnostic question**: If you removed all incentive mechanisms—salary, bonuses, equity—and people still showed up and contributed their best effort, what does that tell you about your allocation system? 

If the answer is "they'd leave"—your system may be extractive, relying on desperation rather than fairness. If the answer is "they might stay, but with less energy"—you have baseline fairness but room to deepen it. If the answer is "many would stay, many would shift roles, the work would continue"—your allocation system is beginning to cultivate genuine stewardship.
