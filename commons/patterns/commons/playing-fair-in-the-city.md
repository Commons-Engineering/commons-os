---

### Section 1: Context

Sport infrastructure—courts, fields, equipment, coaching—is increasingly treated as a commodity: enclosed in private clubs, outsourced to for-profit operators, or designed to serve elite performance while excluding those with disability, low income, or precarious status. This mirrors broader enclosure patterns. Yet across Global South cities, informal settlements, neighbourhoods, and indigenous territories, sport functions as regenerative commons: gathering spaces, resilience infrastructure, cultural sovereignty expression, informal livelihood systems, and mechanisms for cross-group cohesion. In wealthy cities, community sport clubs, grassroots leagues, and neighbourhood equipment libraries demonstrate how sport can remain shared and locally stewarded. The tension is not resource scarcity—it is governance choice. Will sport commons be enclosed, extracted, and financialised, or will they be stewarded as alive, shared, just, and lasting value creation? This pattern draws from the commons definition itself and integrates principles of adaptive governance, subsidiarity, reciprocity, cultural meaning, and polycentric organisation to guide the design of sport as commons rather than commodity.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Sport as commodity (extracted, enclosed, elite-serving) vs. sport as commons (regenerative, stewarded, community-rooted).**

Sport systems face competing forces. Commercial sponsors capture governance, shifting priorities from community access to branding and profit. Formalisation displaces informal coaches, vendors, and self-provisioned infrastructure that sustain livelihoods and resilience in low-income communities. Elite pathways exclude low-income families, reproducing class inequality. Accessibility remains rhetorical rather than designed. School and public facilities lock their doors to communities. Infrastructure decays from deferred maintenance. Displacement through Olympic megaprojects and gentrification erases informal sport spaces and vendor economies. Intergenerational transmission fails—elders are invisible, youth are extracted, cultural practices are erased. At the same time, commodification creates dependency: communities lose capacity to self-provision, governance authority, and the ability to adapt rules to local context. Informal sport economies—coaching, tournaments, equipment rental, food vending—remain precarious and unrecognised as legitimate livelihood. The core problem: sport infrastructure is designed, governed, and maintained for external actors (sponsors, elite athletes, profit operators) rather than by and for the communities that use and depend on it. This breaks the commons cycle: communities cannot adapt, learn, or maintain what they do not govern.

---

### Section 3: Solution

To steward sport as living commons, design at three integrated scales: infrastructure, governance, and cultural practice.

**INFRASTRUCTURE STEWARDSHIP**: Sport facilities are nodes in community life, not isolated consumption sites. Co-locate with food systems (gardens, kitchens, local procurement), public transport, and neighbourhood gathering spaces. Design for multi-use (basketball, futsal, badminton, informal play) rather than single-sport specification. Establish community-led maintenance through rotating work parties, tool libraries, and skill-sharing—transforming maintenance from deferred decay into collective stewardship practice. Create equipment commons: neighbourhood sport kit libraries and peer-managed lending systems reducing purchase barriers while building social connection through shared care. Recognize informal sport grounds (vacant lots, parks, street corners) as legitimate commons, not problems to be formalised away.

**GOVERNANCE DISTRIBUTION**: Establish polycentric governance: facility steering committees with mandatory seats for disabled athletes, migrants, informal vendors, coaches, elders, youth, and marginalized communities. Use subsidiarity—decisions about rules, scheduling, maintenance, and resource allocation made at neighbourhood level, not by distant bureaucracies. Shift from municipal provision (top-down, one-size-fits-all) to community stewardship with municipal support (infrastructure investment, liability frameworks, capacity-building). Create neighbourhood sport league cooperative ownership where players hold membership stakes, governance is democratic, and revenues are reinvested in community sport. Embed conflict resolution: use restorative justice circles and peer arbitration for disputes, maintaining commons access while addressing harm.

**CULTURAL PRACTICE & RECIPROCITY**: Recognize and protect cultural transmission: indigenous sport as sovereignty expression, martial arts as cultural commons, informal coaching as legitimate expertise. Establish mentorship protocols pairing elders with youth, making aging athletes visible as active contributors rather than passive users. Support informal sport enterprises through accessible microfinance and cooperative structures—formalizing livelihoods without erasing informality. Protect vendor ecosystems: when sport sites undergo development, create revenue-sharing agreements and governance seats for vendors, treating displacement as commons loss requiring restitution. Design sport integration programmes for refugees and migrants as accessible entry points to community, rooted in peer connection rather than credential barriers.

**RECURSIVE PRINCIPLE**: Each scale—facility, league, neighbourhood, city—operates as a semi-autonomous commons with decision-making at the lowest competent level. Feedback flows upward: facility stewards inform league rules; league practitioners inform city sport policy. This creates adaptive cycles: communities monitor outcomes, learn what works, evolve rules through democratic participation. The commons persists because it belongs to those who use it, are governed by it, and maintain it—not because external actors fund or control it.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start where people already gather. Walk your neighbourhood. Notice the empty lot where kids play, the street corner where elders watch, the school yard locked after hours. These are your entry points.

**Phase 1: Inventory & Listen** (Months 1-3)
Map existing informal sports spaces and practices. Talk to street vendors who sell drinks at games, informal coaches, young people, elders, people with disabilities. Learn what's already working. Document the economics: who earns, who benefits, what's at risk. This isn't consultation theatre—it's recognizing existing stewards.

**Phase 2: Co-Design Infrastructure** (Months 3-9)
Work with community members to design or retrofit spaces. Include mixed courts (basketball/futsal/netball), seating for watchers and vendors, weather protection, accessible pathways and facilities. Design for repair and maintenance by community members, not just professionals. Choose materials locals can tend: concrete that's porous, nets that community volunteers can replace, lighting that's simple to fix.

Crucially: build vendor stalls into the design. Formalize the informal economy. Rent is paid to a community trust, not a distant corporation.

**Phase 3: Governance Structures** (Months 6-12, ongoing)
Establish a community sports trust or cooperative that holds the lease or deed. Mix governance: grassroots athletes, informal coaches, vendors, neighbours, municipal representatives (in minority positions). Meet monthly. Make decisions visible. Share finances transparently.

Create bylaws that protect access: free or low-cost hours for youth, elders, people with disabilities. Require programming in multiple languages and accessibility formats. Establish a "right to coach informally"—vendors and street coaches retain space without credentialism barriers.

**Phase 4: Cultural Stewardship** (Ongoing)
Host multi-generational tournaments, not just elite competitions. Celebrate local sports forms (street futsal, wheelchair basketball, hand games) alongside global ones. Create mentoring between informal and formal coaches. Document and share stories of athletes, coaches, vendors—their livelihoods and dreams.

Monitor continuously: Are low-income families still coming? Are informal vendors still earning? Are people with disabilities participating? Adjust quickly if access narrows.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What Flourishes:**
Sport becomes regenerative—physically, economically, socially. Young people find mentorship outside school. Informal coaches gain legitimacy and sustainable income. Vendors build businesses. Neighbourhood trust deepens; people look out for one another. Disabled athletes gain genuine access, not afterthought accommodation. The space becomes a gathering place where strangers become neighbours. Health outcomes improve. Most importantly: the community itself learns it can steward shared resources, which ripples into food systems, safety, childcare, and other commons.

**Risks if Left Untended:**
Without vigilance, creep happens. A corporate sponsor funds "improvements" and gradually expects naming rights. Formal coaches pressure governance to exclude informal ones. Rising property values attract developers. Gentrification follows; long-term residents are priced out. The vendor economy formalizes into licensed, taxed positions—which sounds good until you realize informal vendors can't afford licensing fees and lose their livelihoods. The space becomes increasingly "professional," designed for spectators and elite athletes. Accessibility language appears in policy but disappears in practice.

If governance is captured by municipal officials or well-meaning outsiders, community members stop attending meetings. Decision-making becomes invisible. Trust erodes.

The deepest risk: sport remains enclosed. The commons is stewarded well, but it's an island in a sea of commodification. The pattern must scale, or it becomes a beautiful exception that proves the rule.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Calcinha Futebol, São Paulo, Brazil**
Informal futsal leagues in low-income neighbourhoods operate as true commons. Players, informal coaches, and local bar owners co-manage spaces. No formal governance structure—but clear unwritten rules about access, respect, and benefit-sharing. Women's teams, youth, and disabled players participate. Coaches earn through door collections and small sponsorships from neighbourhood businesses. The model has replicated across dozens of favelas. It remains vulnerable to police harassment and land seizure, but its informality has also protected it from corporate capture.

**Vauxhall City Farm & Sports Hub, London, UK**
A community interest company integrates urban farming, sports courts, and community kitchens. Governance includes residents, former athletes, and food vendors. Free or low-cost access for young people and elders. Informal coaches run youth programs alongside qualified staff. The farm provides fresh food for concessions, creating a local supply chain. It's professionalized enough to access grants and maintain infrastructure, but democratic enough that residents shape decisions. Black British and Asian communities who were historically excluded from sport access now lead programming.

**Marae-Based Sports Commons, Aotearoa New Zealand**
Māori communities steward sport through marae (communal lands) as cultural commons. Governance is whakapapa-based (relational, genealogical). Sport—rugby, touch football, cultural games—is inseparable from whānau (family) connection, land responsibility, and cultural transmission. Informal coaching is valued. Accessibility includes physical disability and also spiritual/emotional accessibility. The model is explicitly anti-capitalist: sport is for hauora (wellbeing), not profit. It's under constant pressure from state and commercial interests but remains a living example of sport as regenerative commons.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As artificial intelligence and data systems proliferate, the risk is real: sport becomes datafied. Athlete performance gets tracked, predicted, optimized. Algorithms sort talent early, narrowing pathways. Informal coaches face pressure to use apps for certification. Governance becomes "streamlined" through platforms that actually concentrate power with technologists.

But this pattern can evolve differently.

Community sports trusts can use open-source tools for basic scheduling and finance, retaining control. Informal coaches can document knowledge in ways they choose—video, storytelling, writing—without algorithmic gatekeeping. Data about community health, participation, and economics can stay local, owned by the trust, shared only as communities choose.

More radically: distributed intelligence in communities becomes an asset. Informal coaches carry embodied knowledge about how specific bodies, ages, and disabilities learn. Elders carry memory of what sports meant across generations. Vendors understand local economics. An AI-era commons approach doesn't extract this knowledge into models; it amplifies it. Community members might use simple tools to share coaching techniques with each other, learn together, notice patterns—but remain the owners and interpreters of knowledge.

The key shift: AI serves community stewardship, not extraction. Governance structures that are already democratic become more resilient because information flows stay transparent. Trust strengthens when people know their data stays in community hands.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life:**
- Informal coaches are visible, respected, earning sustainable income
- Vendors operate openly; their economic data is tracked by the community trust
- Facilities are used across all hours: early morning elders, midday youth, evening families
- Disabled athletes participate in programming, not symbolically but substantively
- Governance meetings are well-attended and contentious (disagreement is healthy)
- Children and young people know the space as theirs; they repair and defend it
- Stories circulate: of athletes, mentors, vendors, neighbours. The space has narrative life
- Property values in the neighbourhood remain stable or rise slowly (not speculative jump)
- Multiple languages, accessibility formats, and cultural practices are visible

**Signs of Decay:**
- Informal coaches are pressured to "professionalize" or disappear
- Vendors are excluded or charged prohibitive fees
- Governance meetings are sparse; decisions are made by a small group
- Facilities are increasingly monitored, policed, or "improved" in ways that exclude
- The space becomes physically pristine but emotionally cold
- Access hours shrink (locked at night, "programming" replaces free play)
- Corporate branding increases; community naming decreases
- Long-term residents stop coming; faces are unfamiliar
- Stories disappear; the space becomes a service, not a commons

**The Diagnostic Question:**
*If your community trust dissolved tomorrow, would the space persist? Would people continue gathering, coaching, playing, caring for it? Or does it exist only because of formal structure?*

A vital commons is held by relationships first, structure second. If the answer is "it would persist," stewardship is real. If structure is doing all the holding, the commons is still fragile—and at risk of capture.
