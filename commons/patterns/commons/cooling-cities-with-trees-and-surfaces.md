### Section 1: Context

Urban heat islands—zones 2–7°C warmer than surroundings—concentrate in neighborhoods with minimal canopy coverage, often overlapping with low-income communities and communities of color. This reflects both historical disinvestment (redlining, industrial zoning) and present-day allocation disparities. Simultaneously, cities face carrying capacity thresholds: ambient temperatures trigger cascading infrastructure failures (power demand spikes, asphalt failure), heat-related mortality, and crop failures in peri-urban agriculture. Canopy and cool surfaces (vegetation, reflective materials, water features) modulate local radiation balance through albedo adjustment and evapotranspiration—mechanisms that operate at neighborhood scale. These interventions are nested: individual tree crowns create microrefugia; corridors of canopy connect cool zones; citywide canopy distribution patterns reflect deeper governance choices. This pattern draws from the Commons principles of polycentric governance, adaptive feedback, and subsidiarity: thermal stewardship belongs at neighborhood level, with city-wide coordination and equity oversight.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Thermal equity vs. thermal exposure: some neighborhoods experience 5°C+ temperature differentials while lacking canopy access; systematic cooling requires both measurement and justice-centered allocation..**

Urban thermal regulation is currently fragmented: heat mapping identifies hot zones but allocation of tree planting and cool surface retrofits remains disconnected from lived experience and community capacity. Neighborhoods with greatest heat burden often lack both canopy and decision-making power over greening priorities. Existing canopy management prioritizes aesthetics or property values rather than equitable cooling access or ecological regeneration. Measurement protocols (satellite thermal imaging, canopy closure surveys) exist but are rarely fed back to neighborhood stewards who maintain and adapt interventions. Monoculture street trees fail to support nesting birds or pollinators. Compost and soil preparation are decoupled from canopy establishment, limiting vegetation survival. Critically: thermal regulation is treated as a technical problem (install x trees, reduce y°C) rather than as commons stewardship requiring ongoing collective learning, maintenance, and adaptation. The result: canopy remains unequally distributed, cooling benefits accrue to already-privileged areas, and maintenance burden falls on under-resourced communities.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish a Thermal Commons Stewardship cycle operating at three nested scales: block, neighborhood, and city. At each scale, measurement, governance, and cultivation are woven together.

**MEASUREMENT & VISIBILITY**: Use thermal satellite imagery (Landsat, ECOSTRESS), street-level thermal surveys, and lived experience mapping to document temperature gradients and canopy disparities. Publish these findings as commons knowledge—open data, visual maps accessible to all neighborhoods. This satisfies the principle of Truth-Seeking Sustains Systems and creates the feedback signal that drives adaptation.

**EQUITY-CENTERED PRIORITIZATION**: Identify neighborhoods experiencing highest heat exposure with lowest canopy access. These become primary sites for intervention. Use thermal vulnerability mapping (heat + population age + cooling access) to guide allocation. Decisions on planting species, placement, and maintenance occur at neighborhood level with city providing resources and technical support—subsidiarity in action.

**CULTIVATED CANOPY NETWORKS**: Plant diverse native tree species (selected for high evapotranspiration, wildlife value, and local adaptation) across streets, parks, and peri-urban agriculture. Emphasize crown architecture that supports cavity-nesting birds and insect communities. Design plantings to form interconnected corridors (cooling bridges) linking vulnerable neighborhoods to cool refuges. Design in cool surface retrofits (reflective roofs/pavements, permeable surfaces, water features) that amplify canopy effects.

**SOIL & ESTABLISHMENT**: Invest in compost-amended soil preparation to ensure tree and vegetation establishment. Soil carbon and albedo contribute to local radiation balance; healthy soil supports plant survival and reduces maintenance burden.

**ADAPTIVE STEWARDSHIP CYCLES**: Organize neighborhood-based tree stewards (trained in pruning, pest management, watering protocols) who maintain canopy and provide seasonal feedback on performance. Use block-level monitoring (crown closure measurements, thermal spot-checks, wildlife observation) to guide species refinement and placement adjustments. Feed observations into city-level adaptive planning, updating species selection and corridor design based on 2–3 year cycles.

**JUSTICE-CENTERED GOVERNANCE**: Establish neighborhood commons councils with decision-making authority over local thermal interventions. City provides funding, technical expertise, and equity oversight; neighborhoods steward implementation and adaptation. Distribute economic benefits (tree care jobs, soil preparation contracts, agricultural product) to local residents.

This pattern resolves the primary tension—thermal equity vs. thermal exposure—by making canopy a shared, visible commons: cultivated collectively, monitored transparently, adapted through nested feedback, and governed by those experiencing heat burden.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin with *thermal justice mapping*—a collaborative exercise where residents use simple handheld thermal cameras, phone apps, or even tactile observation ("where do you feel safe at 3 p.m.?") alongside satellite data. This grounds measurement in bodies and experience, not just data. Invite neighborhood groups to co-design the map; the act of mapping becomes an assertion of thermal commons stewardship.

From that map, cultivate a *tiered planting strategy*. Prioritize streets and blocks with highest heat burden and lowest existing canopy—often parking lots, industrial corridors, or neighborhoods where disinvestment stripped away mature trees. Plant native species suited to local climate, soil, and water availability; avoid monoculture. Anchor trees in community care circles: block stewards, school groups, elders who know soil and seasons. Trees planted collaboratively have higher survival rates and deeper social roots.

Simultaneously, retrofit *light-reflective surfaces*—cool pavements, light-colored roofing, permeable surfaces that reduce runoff—on public rights-of-way first, then catalyze residential and commercial uptake. These work fastest where canopy can't yet establish. Frame this not as aesthetic upgrade but as infrastructure repair.

Establish a *Thermal Stewardship Circle* at neighborhood scale: residents, municipal foresters, local ecologists, and heat-vulnerable populations meeting monthly to tend measurement, adjust planting, troubleshoot cooling failures. This circle becomes a proto-governing body for the thermal commons. Give it modest budget authority and accountability to residents.

Create *intergenerational apprenticeships*: pair elders (who remember pre-heat-island neighborhoods) with young people in tree care, soil preparation, and thermal monitoring. This transfers ecological knowledge while honoring those most harmed by heat.

Finally, establish a *cooling equity covenant*—a neighborhood agreement that cooling benefits (shade, evaporative cooling, property-value increases from greening) are protected for current residents, resisting displacement that often follows green infrastructure investment.

### Section 5: Consequences

When tended well, these practices flourish into *tangible thermal equity*: neighborhoods see 2–4°C temperature drops within 5–7 years as canopy matures. Heat-related mortality declines. Children play outdoors during hot months. Community members report autonomy and dignity—they are not passive recipients of green infrastructure but its architects.

Soil health improves with tree planting and permeability; groundwater recharge increases. Stormwater flooding often decreases. Trees sequester carbon and produce oxygen; they become living infrastructure whose benefits compound. The work builds social cohesion and trust in institutions—rare gifts in fractured neighborhoods.

Yet risks emerge if the pattern is applied without justice centering. *Greenwashing*—planting a few trees while ignoring the deepest heat burden—allows inequity to persist. *Green gentrification*—cooling and beautifying neighborhoods, then displacing residents through rising rents—turns anti-racist infrastructure into a displacement weapon. This risk is real and documented.

Tree failure is common if planting outpaces care: young trees need water, mulch, and protection during establishment. Poorly chosen species die or become pests. Without maintenance culture embedded in community, a greening program becomes a cemetery of saplings.

*Thermal justice unmeasured is thermal justice unaccountable*. If measurement stops after initial mapping, the cooling work drifts. Adjustment becomes impossible. The commons slips back into fragmentation.

Finally, cooling without addressing heat sources—highways, industrial zones, power plants—offers partial relief, a balm without cure.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Los Angeles Urban Forest Initiative & Justice 40**: Following severe heat domes (2020–2022) that killed unhoused and low-income residents, LA partnered with neighborhood groups in South LA and East LA—historically redlined zones with 3–5°C heat differentials—to plant trees prioritized by community thermal mapping. The project embedded residents as paid tree stewards, not volunteers. By 2024, over 100,000 trees were planted in high-heat zones; thermal relief was measurable and coupled with anti-displacement covenants protecting longtime residents.

**Durban, South Africa—Urban Heat Action Plan**: Durban, facing both apartheid-era spatial inequity and intensifying heat waves, mapped thermal disparities in townships like Khayelitsha. Rather than top-down greening, the city supported community-led tree planting organized by women's cooperatives and youth groups. Trees were selected for dual benefit: shade and food production (fruit trees, nitrogen-fixing species). This wove thermal commons stewardship into food security and economic resilience for communities historically excluded from environmental decision-making.

**Mexico City—Green Corridors in Iztapalapa**: Iztapalapa, the city's poorest and hottest borough (5°C+ hotter than wealthy western zones), initiated a participatory mapping project where residents identified "thermal deserts"—paved plazas, industrial areas, rooftops. Community organizations then led cool surface retrofits and rooftop gardens. The work was slow, underfunded, but residents reported not just thermal relief but a restoration of voice: they were no longer zones to be managed from above but commons to be steered from within.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and thermal sensing accelerate *measurement democratization* but risk deepening extraction if not paired with justice practice. Satellite thermal imagery, now accessible via open APIs, allows anyone to map heat gradients in seconds. This is powerful: residents can instantly visualize what they feel, validating lived experience with data.

Yet AI-driven optimization algorithms—deployed by real estate firms or speculative developers—can identify cooling infrastructure as a signal of future property appreciation, triggering precisely the gentrification we seek to prevent. The commons becomes a targeting system.

Distributed intelligence tools shift the pattern toward *decentralized stewardship*: networks of neighborhood sensors (soil moisture, canopy density, surface temperature) maintained by residents and fed to community dashboards create real-time feedback loops. A block can adjust watering, monitor tree health, and share data with neighboring blocks—collective learning without centralized control.

*Generative simulation* offers possibility: residents can model planting scenarios, cool surface placement, and thermal outcomes before implementation, iterating toward locally optimized solutions. This deepens agency.

The cognitive shift is this: *measurement and governance become inseparable*. In the AI era, data is power. Cooling Cities With Trees must place measurement authority in community hands—not as advisory input to expert systems, but as the commons' own sensory apparatus. Otherwise, thermal commons stewardship becomes thermal surveillance and control.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: Residents speak with authority about their neighborhood's thermal conditions, unprompted. Tree survival rates exceed 80% after three years—a sign of embedded care culture. Thermal mapping is updated by community members, not external consultants. Temperature differentials with adjacent neighborhoods shrink measurably. Young people choose to stay in the neighborhood; displacement pressure eases. Schools report fewer heat-related absences. Elders say the place feels like it did before industrial sprawl.

**Signs of decay**: Trees planted but not watered; saplings die by season two. Measurement happens once, then stops; thermal data becomes historical artifact, not living feedback. Greening is aestheticized but benefits flow to new arrivals; longtime residents are priced out. Local governance circle meets sporadically or is captured by municipal bureaucrats; residents stop attending. Cooling infrastructure becomes a symbol of the neighborhood's "improvement," attracting developer interest and speculation. Heat mapping reveals disparities, but allocation of resources stays unchanged. The commons fractures into individual properties (private shade, private cool roofs) rather than collective thermal stewardship.

**Diagnostic question**: *Who decides which blocks get cooled first, and who tends the trees after they're planted?*

If the answer is "external experts allocate; city crews maintain," the pattern is a program, not a commons. If the answer is "residents map, residents decide, residents steward—with municipal support for supplies and apprenticeship," vitality is present. Listen for residents' language: do they say "they planted trees here" or "we're growing shade"? The difference is everything.
