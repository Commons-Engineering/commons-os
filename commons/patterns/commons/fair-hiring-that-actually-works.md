### Section 1: Context

Hiring is the act by which a commons admits new members and stewards its culture forward. In large organizations, hiring has devolved into a fragmented process: different managers use different criteria, recruiters lack training, candidates experience silence or inconsistency, and decisions rest on opaque judgment. This fragments trust and excludes capable people whose backgrounds don't fit unstated patterns. The lineage here runs through The Challenge of Cooperation (hiring is a commons decision, not a manager's private choice), Truth-Seeking Sustains Systems (bias hides in silence), Reciprocity Sustains Cooperation (candidates deserve to know the rules they're judged by), and Conflict Resolution (standardization is how we prevent hiring disputes from becoming culture wars). The 77 sub-patterns all point toward one core redesign: making hiring evaluation a *shared, visible, learnable practice*.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Consistency vs. Humanity — standardized evaluation can become dehumanizing; unstructured judgment preserves dignity but breeds opacity and inequity..**

Hiring managers evaluate candidates differently. Recruiters don't know what criteria matter. Interviews are shaped by interviewer mood, not role requirements. Candidates are ghosted or told nothing. Rejected candidates don't know why. Diversity drops at each stage, but no one sees the pattern until it's entrenched in culture. Decisions are defended post-hoc with rationalized narratives, not documented reasoning. Feedback loops don't exist—managers keep their hiring failures private. The system optimizes for speed and manager preference, not fairness or accuracy. The tension: standardizing evaluation can feel reductive and robotic, stripping the human relationship from hiring. But without standards, hiring reproduces existing power and privilege invisibly. The commons is trapped between dehumanizing rigor and comfortable opacity.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Build evaluation as a *disciplined shared practice*, held together by four interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Transparent Criteria & Training**
Before any hiring, the commons defines what success looks like in role-specific terms (not personality fit). Criteria are published—candidates see them; managers see them; recruiters see them. All hiring participants (managers, recruiters, panelists) train on these criteria and practice applying them to calibrated cases. Training is recurring, not one-time. The practice is: *we judge together on shared measures, not alone on hidden intuition*.

**2. Structured Assessment & Documentation**
Every evaluation moment—screening, interview, final decision—uses a standardized form that captures: evidence observed (specific example), criterion assessed (which requirement was this testing?), and rating (how well met?). No narrative-only feedback; no "seemed smart." This surfaces bias (generalizations are visible and challengeable) and creates an auditable record. Failed candidates receive written feedback tied to criteria. This is transparency made concrete.

**3. Calibration & Feedback Cycles**
After hiring rounds, the team reconvenes to calibrate: *Do our ratings match our criteria? Did our predictions hold? What feedback do new hires give about the process?* Calibration sessions are where the commons learns and evolves its standards. Hiring managers report back on whether hired candidates performed as expected—this feeds into validation of assessment tools. The pattern is: *we improve our standards by observing their impact*.

**4. Candidate Experience as Reciprocity**
Candidates receive timely communication at every stage, know why they were screened out, and experience the same standards applied to all. Ghosting is prevented by SLAs. Rejection is a learning moment, not a silence. This is reciprocity: we treat candidates as we would wish to be treated in their position. A rejected candidate who experiences fairness becomes a brand ambassador and may reapply.

**Governance structure:** Hiring authority remains distributed (managers choose; recruiters source; panels evaluate), but all authority is exercised within shared rules. A rotating review team audits hiring decisions for bias and pattern drift. Criteria and tools are revisited annually, with input from recent hires and rejected candidates. The system is alive: it adapts as the commons learns what assessment methods actually predict performance.

## Section 4: Implementation

Start by naming the role's actual work. What does this person do on day 30? Day 90? Sit with practitioners—not just managers—and extract the skills, knowledge, and ways of working that matter. Document them plainly. "Communicates clearly" is too vague; "writes documentation that non-specialists understand on first read" is testable.

Build a calibration session before you hire. Gather your interview panel (3–5 people) and score sample candidates together using your criteria. You'll discover where you disagree—and that's the point. A manager might weight "self-direction" heavily; a peer might value "asks good questions." Talk it through. You don't need unanimity, but you do need clarity about what different people are noticing and why.

Create a structured interview guide. Not a script—a menu of questions tied to each criterion, with what strong/weak answers sound like. This isn't about gotchas; it's about fairness. Every candidate answers roughly the same questions. You take notes on *what they said*, not your feeling about them. Write it down during the interview, not after.

Use a scoring sheet. After each interview, panelists rate candidates independently on each criterion (say, 1–4 scale) and write a sentence about what they observed. Then you discuss. The sheet makes invisible reasoning visible. You spot if one person is consistently harsh, or if someone's "gut feeling" isn't grounded in the role's actual needs.

Close the loop with candidates. If you reject someone, tell them why—in a way that's honest but human. "Your Python skills are strong, but this role needs someone deeper in systems design, and that's not where your experience sits." Rejected candidates often become advocates, colleagues, or future hires. Ghosting poisons reputation and culture.

Document decisions. Keep records of who was interviewed, scores, and the reasoning. This isn't bureaucracy—it's accountability. When you can see patterns (Are women scoring lower on "leadership"? Are people from certain schools always moving forward?), you can ask hard questions and adjust.

## Section 5: Consequences

When hiring becomes a shared, documented practice, trust hardens. Candidates know what matters. Rejected candidates understand the decision—even if they disagree. Inside the organization, new hires feel chosen fairly, not just lucky. People who didn't get the role still respect the process. That matters for retention and culture.

Diversity and capability flourish together. Removing unconscious bias—the halo effect, affinity bias, the pattern-matching to "people like us"—opens doors to talented people who were invisible under old opaque systems. Women, people of color, neurodiverse folks, career-switchers: they're less likely to be dismissed because they don't "seem like" the role. You hire strength you couldn't see before.

The risks are real if the pattern calcifies. Criteria can become proxy discrimination—if "executive presence" really means "tall, male, confident in a particular way," you've just encoded bias in a new form. Scoresheets can become theater: people mark boxes and then hire their gut choice anyway, and you've wasted time without changing behavior. Documentation can feel punitive if used to prosecute people rather than to learn.

Training must be ongoing. Interviewers drift. New hires join and don't understand the practice. Criteria need refreshing as the role evolves. If you abandon the discipline after a few hires, you'll slip back to the old fragmented way—and faster than you'd expect.

## Section 6: Known Uses

**Patagonia's Hiring Council** (outdoor apparel & activism): Before expanding leadership hiring, Patagonia created role-specific criteria that included "commitment to environmental stewardship"—not as personality but as demonstrated action. Interviews are scored on technical skill, judgment, and values alignment. Rejected candidates receive written feedback. The practice has helped them hire leaders who actually live the company's mission, not just espouse it.

**Wikimedia Foundation** (global volunteer coordination): Builds hiring scoresheets collaboratively with the teams doing the work. Criteria include "ability to work across cultures and languages" (testable: how have you navigated disagreement in a multilingual setting?). Documentation is public—candidates know exactly how they'll be evaluated. This transparency attracts mission-driven people and filters out those seeking extraction.

**A cooperative in Mondragon, Spain** (worker-owned manufacturing): Uses peer-based hiring panels where current workers help evaluate new members. Criteria explicitly include "capacity to work in democratic governance," not just technical skill. Interviews are conducted in the candidate's language or with translation. New worker-owners are trained in the cooperative's decision-making process before they start. The practice embeds culture from day one.

## Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI tools will amplify both the promise and peril of structured hiring. Resume screening tools can surface qualified candidates algorithm-screening would have buried—but only if the criteria are honest, not proxies for old biases. Large language models can help write job descriptions in plain language and generate interview questions tied to role requirements. They can flag inconsistencies: "This criterion wasn't tested in the interviews for three of five candidates."

The danger is outsourcing judgment to systems you don't understand. If an algorithm scores candidates and you can't explain why, you've traded human bias for algorithmic opacity. Worse: you've abdicated responsibility.

The pattern shifts in a cognitive era toward *distributed calibration*. Instead of gathering five people in a room, you might score asynchronously, with the system flagging divergence and asking why. Peer feedback from current employees could flow into candidate evaluation in real time—not as gossip, but as structured input about whether this person has worked well with teams before.

The discipline stays the same: make criteria explicit, test them against actual role requirements, document reasoning, and stay humble about what you don't know.

## Section 8: Vitality

**Signs the practice is alive:**

Candidates who don't get the job feel respected. They tell others the process was fair. You hear: "I understood what they were looking for, and it wasn't me—and that's okay."

New hires report feeling genuinely chosen, not randomly selected. They stay longer. They refer friends. They feel permission to be themselves, not a persona.

Your hiring team discusses criteria thoughtfully. When someone says "I just didn't get a good feeling," others ask what specifically they noticed. The practice becomes a language for thinking together.

Over time, you spot patterns: Which criteria predict success? Which turn out to be nice-to-haves? You adjust. The system learns.

**Signs it's decaying:**

Scoresheets exist but no one looks at them. Hiring reverts to "just talk and decide." Documentation piles up unread.

One person's opinion carries all the weight. The panel goes along. Criteria become theater.

Rejected candidates still get ghosted. You skip the "close the loop" conversation because it feels hard.

You notice patterns you don't act on: women score lower on criterion X; people from certain backgrounds never advance. You feel uncomfortable and stop looking.

**Diagnostic question:**

If you hired someone six months ago, could you explain—to that person and to a skeptic—exactly why they got the role, using the criteria that mattered? If the answer is vague or you're reconstructing a narrative, the practice has weakened.
