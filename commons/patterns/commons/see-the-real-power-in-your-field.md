> Map who actually holds power—both officially and behind the scenes—so you can work more effectively without accidentally

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Life domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every living system has power—formal authority, informal influence, resource control, information gatekeeping, veto rights, and decision-making momentum. In healthy commons, this power is visible and distributed across nested centres of authority. In degraded systems, power is hidden: concentrated in informal networks, wielded through unspoken rules, and defended by those who benefit from opacity. The Commons Engineer operates in real institutions—organizations, movements, communities—where both formal hierarchies and shadow structures shape what gets done. Without visibility into these power flows, you cannot navigate ethically or effectively. You either stumble blindly, or you exploit unconsciously. Truth-seeking requires making power visible. Reciprocity requires understanding who holds what authority and deferring appropriately. Subsidiarity requires knowing where decisions are actually made so authority can settle at the right scale.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Invisible power structures vs. transparent governance | Individual agency vs. systemic constraint.**

The core conflict: You must exercise agency and influence in complex systems, but power structures are systematically hidden. Formal org charts lie—they show titles, not actual decision-making. Informal networks are invisible—you discover them through repeated collisions. Budget authority, veto points, gatekeepers, decision criteria, unspoken rules, alliance patterns—these shape outcomes more than official channels, yet no one documents them clearly. Without this map, you waste energy on wrong pressure points, build alliances with the powerless, misread whose blessing you need, and fail to notice where you actually hold leverage. Worse: you may exercise unconscious power—privilege you don't see, influence you don't acknowledge—and become complicit in the very hierarchies you claim to oppose. The tension is between the need to act (agency) and the impossibility of acting wisely without seeing the actual system (truth). You cannot defer appropriately to legitimate authority if you cannot distinguish it from performative hierarchy. You cannot build just coalitions if you cannot see who actually decides.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Power Mapping is a systematic, repeatable practice of making invisible authority visible. It operates at three nested scales: personal, relational, and structural. Begin with yourself: inventory the formal authority you hold (title, budget, hiring, veto rights), the informal influence you carry (expertise, networks, proximity, platform, credibility), and the resources you control or access. Document this honestly—not to celebrate it, but to understand your responsibility. This is stewardship consciousness: knowing what you can do so you choose carefully what you should do. Next, map the decision ecosystem around a specific question or system. For each significant decision point, identify: (1) Who formally holds authority? (2) Who actually influences the decision? (3) What information do they need? (4) What veto points exist? (5) What criteria do they use? (6) How do they relate to each other? Use visual methods—boxes, arrows, nodes—to make relationships explicit. Distinguish between formal hierarchy (org chart) and actual power (shadow structure). Identify gatekeepers, connectors, veto holders, decision-makers, and constituencies. Map both allies and resisters, but also the undecided—where can persuasion happen? The map should show resource flows, information flows, and decision flows as separate layers. Crucially: map the permission structure. Where do you have genuine discretion? Where is permission softer than it appears? Where are boundaries real vs. assumed? This reveals where you can act without permission and where you must build coalition. Finally, use the map to navigate ethically. Use visibility to locate leverage points—not to extract, but to understand where pressure is most just. Build alliances with those holding actual power, not just formal titles. Frame proposals in the value system of decision-makers, not your own. Defer to legitimate authority; resist illegitimate concentration. Most importantly: share the map. Make power visible collectively, not for your private advantage. In commons work, transparency about power is itself a commons act—it enables others to navigate justly and to contest hidden hierarchies. Commit to regular remapping: power shifts, relationships evolve, new gatekeepers emerge. Keep the map alive.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping yourself. Write down your formal authority (what your title officially permits), your informal influence (who listens to you, who seeks your counsel, where you have credibility), and your resource access (budget, time, networks, information, tools). Be honest about gaps between the two. Most people discover they have more informal power than they realized, and less formal authority than they assumed.

Next, map your immediate network—the 10-15 people whose decisions shape your work. For each, answer: What formal authority do they hold? What do they actually decide? Who listens to them when the stakes are high? What resources do they control? What unspoken rules seem to guide their choices? Don't guess. Ask directly when you can. Listen for who people defer to, who they avoid, who they consult before deciding.

Then expand outward. Identify veto points—the moments where one person's refusal stops progress. Find gatekeepers—people who control information flow or access. Locate the decision criteria that aren't written down: Why does your organization fund some initiatives but not others? What makes someone trustworthy in your field? Whose endorsement matters most? Track these patterns across 5-10 decisions you've witnessed.

Create a simple visual: nodes for people, lines for influence (thick for strong, thin for weak, broken for contested). Mark formal authority differently from informal influence. Note resources. Highlight gatekeepers and veto points.

Update this map quarterly. Power shifts. New people arrive. Alliances form and break. The act of mapping itself changes your perception—you begin noticing power moves in real time instead of after the fact.

The deepest level: identify the structural conditions that concentrate power. Is information hoarded? Are decisions made behind closed doors? Do certain people always sit at the table while others must ask permission? These structural patterns are what your mapping is really meant to reveal—so you can address them, not just navigate them.

### Section 5: Consequences

When power is visible, several things flourish. People can make informed choices about where to invest their effort and how to build coalitions. You stop wasting energy trying to persuade someone without veto authority while ignoring the person who actually decides. Accountability becomes possible—you know who chose what and why. Newcomers onboard faster because the real rules are visible. Trust increases because informal influence is less mysterious, less dependent on being in the right room.

When power mapping becomes a shared practice, institutional learning accelerates. Your organization begins asking harder questions: Why do certain people always decide? Are there structural reasons some voices are systematically marginalized? Are veto points protecting important safeguards, or just entrenching privilege? The pattern creates conditions for distributed authority—many centres of power, each visible and accountable.

But there are genuine risks if this pattern operates without care. Naming power can feel like accusation. People who benefit from hidden power structures may experience transparency as threat and retaliate. Some gatekeepers exist for reasons—they prevent chaos, protect sensitive information, or slow decisions that would harm vulnerable people. Exposing them without understanding their function can destabilize necessary boundaries.

There's also a risk of reductionism: treating power as simple hierarchy when it's actually multidimensional. Someone might have formal authority but no credibility. Another person might lack title but wield enormous influence through networks. A third might control resources but defer to expertise. Map too simplistically and you miss the texture that makes systems actually work.

The deepest risk: using power maps tactically to manipulate rather than to democratize. The pattern is only healthy when it serves transparency and distributed agency—not when it becomes a tool for ambitious people to identify shortcuts to influence.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory budgeting initiatives in Brazilian municipalities** systematized power mapping to make hidden fiscal authority visible. They identified that budget decisions were concentrated in finance committees that rarely consulted residents or service workers. By mapping both formal authority and the expertise of frontline staff, they redistributed decision-making across neighbourhood assemblies. Porto Alegre's model has spread to over 1,500 cities globally. The pattern revealed that power had been invisible not through malice, but through habit—no one had asked "who actually decides how this money flows?"

**The Avaaz campaign strategy** in global advocacy work uses power mapping as foundational practice. Before launching a campaign, they map decision-makers, gatekeepers, veto points, and informal influencers within governments, corporations, and media systems. They identify which stakeholders actually move when pressure is applied, versus which are symbolic targets. This practice emerged from early campaigns that built massive public pressure on people with no actual authority to change policy—wasted effort that power mapping now prevents.

**Indigenous land governance in Australia and Canada** relies on deep mapping of authority that Western property law renders invisible. Aboriginal and First Nations commons management recognizes that land use decisions flow through kinship networks, seasonal councils, elder consultation, and often-unwritten protocols that regulate who can access which resources when. Western power mapping (official title, budget authority, org chart) would completely miss this structure. Indigenous power mapping explicitly names these invisible systems and protects them against colonial pressure to "formalize" authority in ways that concentrate power.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI systems gain prominence, power mapping becomes both more necessary and more difficult. AI introduces new gatekeepers: the engineers who train models, the organizations that own them, the data scientists who determine what questions get asked. These roles can feel technical and thereby invisible—power exercised through algorithms feels less like power and more like physics.

Distributed intelligence—many minds working together, augmented by AI—actually creates *more* power centers, not fewer. A single person with AI tools can influence outcomes that previously required institutional authority. Informal influence multiplies: who has access to which models? Who understands the model's biases? Whose data trained it? These become new sources of hidden power.

The pattern must evolve: power mapping now includes digital infrastructure. Who controls data? Who has API access? Which models run where? What happens in the training process? These aren't just technical questions—they're power questions. The commons requires that these decisions be visible and accountable in the same way we've learned to map human authority.

There's also an opportunity: AI can help make power structures visible. You can feed decision data into systems that identify patterns humans miss—veto points, gatekeepers, bottlenecks. The technology becomes a mirror. But only if those systems themselves are transparent about how they identify power. Otherwise you've just created invisible power at a higher level.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People can name who actually decides, not just who has the title. When conflict arises, people don't say "the organization decided"—they say "Sarah and Marcus decided, which surprised me because I didn't know they had authority there." Newcomers are welcomed with actual power maps, not just org charts. People question why certain decisions are made privately and propose alternatives. Veto points are debated explicitly: "Does this person need this power? Is there a reason, or is it just inherited?"

The healthiest sign: people actively work to distribute power once it's visible. They create structures where more people can decide. They establish clear criteria for authority. They rotate gatekeepers. They build redundancy so one person's absence doesn't freeze decision-making. They defend necessary veto points while questioning unnecessary ones.

**Signs of decay:** People know the informal power map but accept it as unchangeable. "That's just how it is. Sarah decides everything." Power mapping becomes gossip—people talk about who's really in charge but never propose restructuring. New people are warned about hidden power ("Don't go to the formal committee—it's all decided beforehand") and coached to navigate it privately rather than change it.

The deepest decay: power becomes explicitly hidden as a management strategy. "We don't talk about how decisions are made because it would be destructive." Gatekeepers become defensive. Veto points harden into privileges. People exhaust themselves navigating invisible structures.

**Diagnostic question:** If you asked three people in your organization "Who really decided this?" about the same recent decision, would they name the same person? If they would, power is relatively visible. If they'd name different people or seem confused—you have hidden power structures that need mapping.
