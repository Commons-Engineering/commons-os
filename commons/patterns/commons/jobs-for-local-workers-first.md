### Section 1: Context

Employment in contemporary commons operates at the intersection of three living systems: the ecosystem of shared assets (housing, infrastructure, land); the ecosystem of human capabilities (skills, care, attention); and the ecosystem of economic flows (income, surplus, reinvestment). Conventionally, these three are decoupled — employment becomes a mechanism for extracting labor-value to fund external capital accumulation, while local assets are stewarded by wage-workers with no ownership stake and no voice in how the work is structured. The lineage of this pattern runs through cooperative labour traditions (Mondragon, kibbutzim, worker collectives), community land trust practice, participatory budgeting, and municipal employment guarantee programs (India's MGNREGA, Korea's local hiring ordinances, South Africa's contractor development). It is grounded in the Orbit 1 recognition that incentives shape behaviour, that reciprocity builds trust, and that subsidiarity requires decisions to be made by those closest to the actual work.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Wage labor as commodity (exploitative, extractive, mobile) vs. Work as membership in place-based stewardship (rooted, dignified, regenerative).**

Place-based commons face a double bind. First: the resource stewardship problem. Community-owned housing, land, infrastructure require continuous maintenance and adaptive management. If this work is outsourced to transient contractors with no stake in place, stewardship becomes perfunctory — repairs are minimized, innovation is absent, institutional knowledge walks away. Second: the livelihood problem. Residents of commons-rooted communities (CLTs, social housing complexes, settlements, cooperative neighbourhoods) often lack stable income. Employment is transactional, precarious, geographically volatile — people commute long distances, move frequently, remain external to their own communities. Third: the governance problem. If employment is commodity labour, workers have no seat at the table where decisions about the assets they tend are made. The result: disconnection. Stewardship is hollow. Livelihoods are fragile. Residents are subjects of development rather than agents.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Embed employment as a form of membership. Rather than hiring workers, the commons inductsco-stewards through a three-phase pathway: (1) Entry through skills development and job matching. Use hyperlocal platforms and community coordinators to surface opportunities that account for actual constraints — childcare, transit, existing skills. Connect job seekers not to distant employers but to the maintenance, care, and infrastructure work that the place itself requires. (2) Transition to cooperative membership and democratic voice. As someone moves from temporary contract to ongoing role, they simultaneously transition into collective ownership and governance. They gain a seat — not in one year, but within a defined horizon (typically 2-3 years) — where they vote on maintenance priorities, asset reinvestment, and work schedules. Their contributions (labour, insight, presence) are weighted in surplus distribution. (3) Deepening through collective production. Workers coalesce into production cooperatives — not employed by external entities, but organised as federated workers' collectives delivering the maintenance, care, and infrastructure services the commons requires. This removes the middle profit layer and anchors decision-making within those doing the work. Across all three phases, income is guaranteed (not contingent on market demand) and set at living wage thresholds. This transforms the wage from a price signal into a covenant: "Your presence, your stewardship, your voice matter to us." The platform layer (job matching, skills tracking, governance participation) is itself governed cooperatively, not owned by a commercial intermediary. Finally, the pattern operates at multiple scales — individual employment contracts nest within neighbourhood hiring compacts, which nest within municipal guarantees, which nest within federated cooperative networks — allowing each scale to adapt while maintaining coherence.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping. Walk your neighborhood and document what needs tending: pothole cycles, stairwell graffiti timelines, vacant lot succession, seasonal infrastructure strain. This isn't analysis—it's witnessing. Write it down. Photograph it. Involve residents in the noticing.

Next, build the matching infrastructure. This is neither a job board nor a traditional hiring process. Create a lightweight coordination hub—digital or analog or both—where residents can signal availability and interests, and where the commons can signal needs. The crucial move: make this *bidirectional*. Not "we have jobs for you" but "what needs stewarding, and who can tend it?"

Begin with the most visible, most continuable work. Grounds maintenance. Stairwell care. Facade inspection. Small infrastructure repair. These aren't temporary make-work—they're the backbone of place-keeping. But frame them as apprenticeships, not just gigs. Pair newcomers with experienced stewards. Document the work. Create informal credentials that travel with people.

Establish a modest payment floor tied to local costs of living, not abstract minimum wage. Pay should be enough to matter, not just symbolic. Build in flexibility: part-time, seasonal, project-based, responsive to actual life conditions.

Create feedback loops. Monthly gatherings where stewards report on what they've noticed, what's emerging, what needs rethinking. These meetings are part of the work itself—they're where collective intelligence accumulates.

Crucially: resist the professionalization trap. The goal isn't to create a permanent municipal workforce. It's to weave employment so tightly into stewardship that the distinction dissolves. Someone who tends the community garden also watches for drainage issues. Someone who repairs stairs also mentors younger residents. Work becomes multivalent.

Anchor this in policy: local hire requirements in procurement. Community benefit agreements on larger projects. Reinvestment of surplus back into wages and training, not extraction.

### Section 5: Consequences

What flourishes: A place begins to know itself better. Stewards notice patterns others miss—the tree that's stressed, the corner where young people gather, the infrastructure that's failing before it fails catastrophically. This embedded knowledge becomes the commons' nervous system. Turnover slows. People stay because they're not just earning—they're recognized, needed, part of something. Economic multipliers activate: wages get spent locally, in neighborhood shops and services, creating secondary employment.

But also: trust builds differently. When someone tends your street for years, you relate to them as a neighbor, not a service provider. This changes how conflict gets resolved, how knowledge gets shared, how collective decisions get made.

The risks are real if this pattern isn't actively tended. *Patronage creep*: jobs allocated to friends and family rather than through genuine matching. *Wage stagnation*: the rhetoric of "stewardship" used to justify below-market pay. *Hidden precarity*: project-based work disguised as flexible when it's actually unstable. *Exclusion*: if entry requirements are opaque or credential-heavy, the most vulnerable get locked out.

There's also the risk of burnout—stewards carrying too much, with insufficient support or succession planning. And the risk of displacement: as neighborhoods become more stable and desirable through good stewardship, property values rise, and the original residents can't afford to stay.

These consequences aren't inevitable. They're visible if you're watching. The pattern requires active maintenance of its own justice dimensions.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Highlander Research and Education Center (Tennessee, USA)**: For six decades, Highlander has embedded local hiring into every program and renovation. Appalachian residents lead workshops, manage facilities, and guide strategy. The pattern: employment as political education. People learn the commons by maintaining it. Turnover is remarkably low; institutional memory is deep.

**Mercal cooperatives (Venezuela)**: State-owned but community-managed grocery networks prioritized hiring from surrounding neighborhoods, particularly women and formerly unemployed residents. At their height, these were places of economic dignity and community coordination. The pattern faltered when external supply chains destabilized and when hiring became political patronage rather than genuine matching. The lesson: local hire requires both stable supply and transparent process.

**Zapatista ejidos (Chiapas, Mexico)**: In autonomous zones, land stewardship and livelihood are inseparable. Community members rotate maintenance responsibilities and food production work. There's no separation between "job" and "membership." Work is uncompensated or minimally compensated but embedded in broader systems of mutual aid. The difference: this works because it's paired with land sovereignty and decision-making power, not just employment.

**Amsterdam's neighborhood maintenance cooperatives**: Small teams of residents, often recruited from long-term social housing tenants, tend public spaces, green infrastructure, and shared facilities. Deliberately low-tech, deliberately local. Employment is stable; wages are modest but living. The pattern sustains because it's backed by municipal commitment and because work is genuinely needed and valued.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence are already reshaping how this pattern operates. The matching function—connecting needs with capacities—becomes radically more sophisticated when algorithms can process occupant feedback, infrastructure sensors, skill inventories, and availability in real time. Hyperlocal labor matching becomes feasible at neighborhood scale rather than city scale.

But here's the tension: the more algorithmic the matching, the easier it is to optimize for efficiency rather than relationship. The pattern *requires* some friction. The coffee shop conversation where a steward learns that their neighbor needs work. The community meeting where needs surface in context rather than as data points.

The real possibility: use distributed intelligence to *amplify* local knowledge, not replace it. Sensors on infrastructure can surface early failure signals that human stewards then investigate and address. Skill-matching platforms can expand possibility horizons for people with non-traditional backgrounds. But keep the decision-making local and relational.

AI also enables new forms of peer coordination. Community members can collectively monitor and adapt the system itself—what's working, where is it failing, who's being left out—in real time. This creates feedback loops that were previously invisible.

The risk: if the pattern is mediated entirely through algorithms, it becomes legible to extraction. Gig platform companies are already packaging "community stewardship" as precarious contract work. The difference between this pattern and that extraction is the presence of collective agency and decision-making power, which AI can either strengthen or dissolve.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: People can name their stewards by first name. Work gets discussed in community meetings not as "a job program" but as essential. Turnover is measured in years, not months. When something breaks, neighbors know who to ask—and often, neighbors fix it themselves. Wages support actual living, not just supplementing. There's evidence of learning and growth: stewards develop new skills, take on more complex work, mentor others. Most importantly: people choose to stay because they belong, not because they're trapped.

**Signs of decay**: Jobs become political currency, allocated through invisible networks. Wages drift downward relative to cost of living. Communication about opportunities becomes opaque. You'll see it in turnover first—people cycle through quickly. You'll see it in work quality—repairs get done minimally, maintenance becomes reactive rather than adaptive. You'll see it in conversation: "that's a job program" rather than "those are our stewards." Trust in the matching process erodes.

**The diagnostic question**: If someone left today—moved away, found other work, got sick—would the community know they were gone? Would the work they carried stop? If the answer is yes, the pattern is alive. If the answer is no—if the work was always just a transaction anyway—then it's already hollow, regardless of how it looks on paper.
