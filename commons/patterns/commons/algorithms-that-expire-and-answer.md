### Section 1: Context

Urban governance increasingly delegates decisions to algorithms: permit processing, licensing, traffic management, resource allocation. These systems operate at temporal velocities that exceed democratic oversight—milliseconds vs. months. Once deployed, algorithms persist through administrative inertia, their discrimination becomes normalized, and the logic that justified them calcifies into unchallengeable technical fact. This creates a vacuum where algorithmic systems govern without ongoing consent. Meanwhile, the vendors and incumbents who designed the algorithms gain asymmetric influence over standard-setting, embedding market preferences into ostensibly neutral technical rules. The commons requires that no governance mechanism—algorithmic or otherwise—persist without periodic validation by those it affects.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Administrative persistence (algorithms persist by default, invisible harms accumulate) vs. Democratic legitimacy (communities must actively consent to algorithmic governance, with reversal built in).**

Three tensions collide: First, algorithmic velocity outpaces governance velocity. Systems that make decisions in milliseconds operate faster than deliberative bodies can oversee them, creating accountability gaps where harm happens before remedy. Second, algorithmic persistence prevents contestation. Once deployed, algorithms become infrastructure—invisible, unchallengeable, path-dependent. Communities cannot easily reverse decisions or demand redesign. Third, regulatory capture embeds incumbency. When vendors and established players influence algorithm design (consciously or through encoded assumptions about what data matters, who deserves priority, what constitutes risk), the algorithm becomes an instrument for protecting market power while appearing neutral. Small businesses, immigrants, informal operators, and marginalized communities experience discrimination not through explicit rules but through opaque algorithmic scoring that no one can see or challenge.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish three interlocking mechanisms: **Temporal Covenant**: Every algorithm deployed for urban governance automatically sunsets after a defined period (2–3 years). Redeployment is not automatic renewal—it requires complete redesign from scratch, fresh impact assessment, and active community reauthorization through participatory deliberation. The sunset date is published publicly and cannot be extended without new community approval. This prevents algorithmic sedimentation and forces periodic redesign based on documented harms. **Real-Time Governance Council**: Standing municipal body (resident majority, including those most affected by algorithms) meets weekly to review algorithmic systems operating at high velocity (traffic control, permit processing, emergency dispatch). The council has authority to request investigation, require slowdown (human-in-the-loop review), or suspend algorithms showing concerning patterns. This keeps governance pace with algorithmic velocity. **Reversibility Default & Community-Triggered Reassessment**: All algorithmic decisions are tagged with validity windows. If performance standards (set by the community, not the vendor) are unmet, decision reversal or intensive reassessment is automatically triggered without requiring new petition or majority consensus. Communities define what counts as "unmet"—not technicians or vendors. **Transparency & Purpose Limitation**: Each algorithm is permitted for a specific declared purpose and time period. Repurposing requires new impact assessment. Algorithmic logic, training data, decision boundaries, and documented biases are published in accessible form. This shifts from opacity ("the algorithm decided") to stewardship ("here is what the algorithm does and why; here is when it expires; here is how to challenge it"). The pattern embodies the commons principle that no system can govern the collective without continuous contestability and consent.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with inventory. Map every algorithm currently making decisions in your city—traffic signal timing, pothole prioritization, permit scoring, police dispatch weighting, zoning compliance checking, benefit eligibility assessment. Most cities discover they have no single list. Create one. This alone shifts power.

Next, establish your sunset schedule. Don't expire everything simultaneously; stagger sunsetting so you're continuously reauthorizing 20-30% of algorithmic systems per year. This creates rhythm without creating paralysis. Publish the calendar publicly and anchor it to a civic ritual—maybe late summer, when schools reopen and citizens expect institutional change.

For each algorithm approaching sunset, launch a *reauthorization process* that feels less like rubber-stamping and more like reckoning. Convene the people who experience the algorithm's consequences daily: residents denied permits, drivers stuck in traffic, workers whose schedules it controls. Bring together the technicians who built it and the civil servants who deployed it. Don't hide the code—translate it into questions citizens can actually ask: "Who does this help? Who does it harm? What counts as 'efficiency' here anyway?"

Document harms that emerged during the algorithm's tenure. What unexpected consequences materialized? What groups found themselves consistently disadvantaged? Create space for *refusal*—the option to not redeploy an algorithm at all, even if it's operationally convenient. This refusal must be frictionless; the burden of proof should rest with those wanting to continue.

If reauthorization happens, require redesign. Don't just tweak parameters; rebuild from principles the community agrees on this time. Different values might emerge. Maybe traffic efficiency matters less than pedestrian safety now. Maybe permit speed matters less than equitable access.

Make the timeline transparent but non-negotiable. If reauthorization hasn't happened 60 days before sunset, the algorithm *stops*. Cities discover they can operate without it. This threat of genuine discontinuation focuses minds on what algorithms truly serve versus what merely persists.

### Section 5: Consequences

What flourishes: *Democratic rediscovery*. When communities must actively choose algorithms every few years, they remember they have choice. Citizens learn to ask what their city optimizes for and discover they might want something different. Bias gets named and contested before it becomes tradition. Technologists and administrators become accountable to living deliberation, not to dead infrastructure.

Trust rebuilds slowly. Each successful reauthorization—where a community genuinely debates an algorithm and decides together—creates precedent that this city takes algorithmic governance seriously. Marginalized communities who've been harmed by opaque systems get a moment to say so and demand change. Sometimes they refuse redeployment. Sometimes they demand redesign. Either way, their voice enters the architecture.

Operationally, cities become more resilient. When an algorithm fails or biases emerge, sunsetting gives you a natural off-ramp. You're not wrestling with decommissioning legacy code; you're just letting the clock run out.

But risks deserve attention: *The window of harm*. Between discovery of bias and sunsetting, harm continues accumulating. A permit algorithm that discriminates still discriminates until its sunset date. Communities need early-exit mechanisms—the ability to emergency-pause an algorithm if serious harm emerges.

*Reauthorization theater*. Without genuine power to refuse, these processes become performative. Communities gather, comment, and the algorithm redeploys anyway because city operations depend on it. Prevent this by ensuring refusal is genuinely possible—by building fallback processes that function without algorithms, by treating discontinuation as an acceptable outcome.

*Deliberative burden*. Small cities may lack the civic capacity for deep algorithmic review. They need support—templates for reauthorization, peer learning networks, access to independent auditors. Otherwise, sunsetting becomes symbolic rather than substantive.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Barcelona's Citizen Algorithms**: The city's municipal government began requiring annual review of algorithms used in permit processing and public service allocation. Community assemblies received plain-language algorithm descriptions and impact reports. In 2021, the traffic management algorithm was *not* reauthorized after residents argued its optimization sacrificed neighborhood livability for downtown efficiency. The city operated for six months with modified timing and discovered the sky didn't fall.

**Seoul's Democratic Algorithm Audit**: Korean civic groups demanded transparency for the city's bus route optimization algorithm. Rather than defend it, Seoul established a participatory process where citizens and advocates reviewed the system every 18 months. In the 2022 cycle, disabled riders' groups successfully argued for redesign to prioritize accessibility over speed—the algorithm redeployed with different weightings reflecting their input.

**Participatory Budgeting (Global)**: While not purely algorithmic, cities using participatory budgeting create analog versions of reauthorization—communities actively choose resource allocation rather than delegating it to statistical formulas. Paris, Porto Alegre, and New York have shown that when citizens debate what gets prioritized, they often choose differently than optimization algorithms would.

**Ours Once More (Montreal)**: A network of Montreal residents, facing algorithmic zoning compliance checks they deemed discriminatory toward immigrant neighborhoods, demanded sunset clauses before accepting the system's deployment. The city agreed. Reauthorization happens publicly; refusal has been credible enough that actual redesign happened in the first cycle.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and automated intelligence will accelerate algorithmic proliferation. Cities will deploy systems faster, with less friction. This makes expiration clauses more critical, not less—they're your brake when velocity becomes reckless.

Large language models and recommendation systems will make algorithmic logic even more opaque. You won't be able to reverse-engineer decisions; you'll need mandatory explanation requirements built into reauthorization. Communities must understand what they're asking the AI to optimize for, even if they can't fully decode how.

Distributed governance becomes possible. Sunsetting doesn't require centralized reauthorization; blockchain or municipal web3 tools could enable continuous voting or weighted community input on algorithmic parameters. A neighborhood could collectively adjust its local traffic algorithm rather than waiting for a top-down review cycle. This distributes deliberation but requires infrastructure investment.

The pattern shifts from *periodic reauthorization* to *continuous contestation*. Instead of reviewing algorithms every two years, communities might challenge them constantly—appealing decisions, demanding explanation, requesting pilot alternatives. This feels chaotic but it's democratic. It also demands much better documentation and contestability built into algorithms from the start.

AI-assisted auditing becomes essential. As algorithms grow complex, human review alone won't catch emergent harms. You'll need AI systems that *audit* other AI systems, flagging bias and unintended consequences. Sunsetting timelines give communities moments to demand this auditing be improved before redeployment.

The cognitive shift: from treating algorithms as static technical objects to treating them as *living policies* requiring constant democratic tending. Sunsetting makes this explicit.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** A city where sunsetting creates genuine friction. When an algorithm approaches its sunset, stakeholders worry. Conversations begin early about whether to redesign or let it die. Administrators can't simply assume continuation. Communities name the algorithm's harms during reauthorization; sometimes they refuse redeployment. The city survives without it. Trust in algorithmic governance is conditional, actively renewed, never assumed.

**Signs of decay:** Sunsetting becomes theater. Algorithms are reauthorized automatically despite documented harms. Reauthorization processes lack genuine power to refuse. Communities are consulted but not heard. The deliberative process is tokenistic—a gesture toward democracy while technical decisions happen elsewhere. Or: sunsetting is abandoned quietly when it proves administratively inconvenient. The sunset date passes and the algorithm silently continues.

**Diagnostic question:**

*If your city's most consequential algorithm—the one making decisions about permit approval, traffic, resource allocation—arrived at its sunset date tomorrow, would discontinuing it require genuine deliberation and contestation, or would redeployment be assumed and only challenged if someone raised a fuss?*

If the second: your city hasn't truly installed this pattern. Sunsetting is symbolic. The algorithm persists by default.

If the first: you've shifted the burden of proof. Persistence now requires active choice. That's where democratic power lives.
