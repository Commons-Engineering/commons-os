### Section 1: Context

Urban commons—parks, plazas, commercial districts, maintenance systems—are increasingly managed by Business Improvement Districts and similar quasi-governmental bodies that concentrate decision-making power in property owners while excluding those who work, vend, dwell, and move through these spaces daily. This mirrors a deeper commons governance pathology documented by Ostrom and critiqued by urban theorists like Kayden: when abstract institutions govern tangible shared resources without direct participation from all constituencies, legitimacy erodes and power consolidates toward the wealthy. The Orbit 1 principles of Polycentric Organisation, Subsidiarity, and Reciprocity point to a different model: governance legitimacy emerges when communities directly steward resources and power is distributed across nested decision-making centres, not concentrated in unelected boards.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Concentrated property-owner power vs. distributed stewardship by all commons inhabitants.**

BIDs and similar structures extract commons value—labor, revenue, public space—while excluding those most affected from governance. Property owners set budgets; renters and workers are absent from boards. Services concentrate in commercially valuable zones while adjacent working-class neighborhoods receive minimal investment. Spending decisions remain opaque, enabling resource capture toward commercial tenants while informal economies and unaffiliated publics degrade. Workers are systematically underpaid—their maintenance labor subsidizes public realm quality for property owners rather than being fairly compensated. The result: a two-tier public realm where geography determines service intensity, legitimacy collapses because most inhabitants cannot influence decisions affecting them, and the commons itself becomes a mechanism for extraction rather than shared flourishing. The commons is alive only for those with capital; for everyone else, it is experienced as a system of control.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Replace concentrated property-owner governance with **Distributed Stewardship Commons**: a governance structure that makes power visible, seats all commons constituencies in decision-making, and ties legitimacy to tangible resource stewardship.

**Core mechanic:** Establish a Commons Stewardship Council with proportional representation: property owners (weighted by assessed value but capped at 40% voting power), tenant associations, worker representatives (cleaners, security, maintenance staff), street vendors and informal economy operators, unaffiliated public users (residents without property stakes), and a rotating citizen auditor drawn by lot. This council directly governs three tangible resource flows: (1) Annual budget allocation—with transparent line-item spending and mandatory equity auditing; (2) Service standards—defining maintenance, security, and amenity distribution with explicit targets to eliminate service disparities; (3) Stewardship roles—rotating paid positions for community members to manage specific commons zones, creating reciprocal labor value.

**Transparency layer:** Publish quarterly power maps showing decision concentration, voting patterns by constituency, and budget allocation against equity targets. Mandate independent community audits comparing BID spending to municipal spending in adjacent neighborhoods, with results visible at entry points to the commons. These audits are themselves commons acts—making structural inequality visible enables collective response.

**Labor reciprocity:** All maintenance, security, and service workers earn living wages determined through participatory budgeting, not contractor minimization. Worker representatives have veto power on service standards, ensuring stewardship labour is recognized as skilled commons work, not disposable cost.

**Subsidiarity & adaptation:** Smaller decision domains (e.g., which maintenance contractor, how public restrooms are scheduled) are delegated to neighborhood stewardship pods—semi-autonomous teams with their own representation. Larger resource allocation decisions escalate to the full Council. Governance rules are reviewable annually by lot-selected community members, ensuring the system evolves with changing composition.

**Legitimacy test:** The commons is alive when inhabitants across income, legal status, and stake in property can tangibly influence decisions affecting them. When a renter sees their voice change a maintenance schedule, when a cleaner's wage reflects their skill rather than their bargaining powerlessness, when a street vendor's allocation is decided through commons process rather than imposed by merchants—legitimacy emerges. This pattern persists because stewardship is distributed, power is visible, and reciprocity is built into resource flows.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by naming and mapping the actual commons you're stewarding. What streets, plazas, services, and systems do multiple constituencies rely on? Who benefits from them now, and who's excluded? Host listening circles—not surveys, circles—with street vendors, domestic workers, renters, shop owners, property managers, transit riders. Listen for whose voices the current system silences.

Next, draft a Commons Stewardship Charter. This isn't legal boilerplate; it's a public covenant. State clearly: who gets a seat on decisions? How are seats weighted and rotated? What spending is transparent, and what decisions require public deliberation? Make it readable by someone without a law degree. Post it everywhere.

Create the Stewardship Council with real teeth. Proportional representation means: property owners (capped at 40% of votes), commercial tenants (15%), residential renters (20%), workers/vendors (15%), transit/service users (10%). Rotate annually so no single group calculates permanence. Pay stipends—this is labor, not volunteerism.

Establish a **Budget Commons**: quarterly public forums where spending decisions are debated aloud. Not rubber-stamp approval; genuine contestation. When a BID wants to spend $200k on "place-making," the Stewardship Council asks: who gets to feel welcome here, and who's being priced out? Require impact assessment: who benefits, who's displaced, what's the three-year consequence?

Create visible accountability. Monthly spending reports posted in multiple languages on community boards, social media, and the council website. Track not just dollars but displacement risk: Are rents rising in newly "improved" zones? Are vendors being pushed out? Are service hours expanding or shrinking in low-income adjacent areas?

Build a Dispute Resolution layer. When constituencies disagree—and they will—establish a Commons Ombudsperson (elected by Stewardship Council, answerable to it) who mediates without power to impose. The goal: make conflicts visible and resolvable, not buried in closed meetings.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Trust, slowly. When renters see their voices actually shape spending, when vendors know their livelihoods are defended in public, when workers see services designed for their schedules—participation deepens. Decisions become more creative because diverse constituencies bring knowledge no property owner alone possesses. A plaza designed with input from night-shift nurses looks different from one designed only by real estate investors. Services stay, because they're rooted in actual need rather than speculative value.

**The risks if neglected:** Capture shifts rather than dissolves. Organized property owners learn to pack Council seats with allies. Commercial tenants form blocs. Renters lack time to attend meetings and fade from power. The Council becomes another talking shop while real decisions happen elsewhere. Worse: visible contestation creates backlash. Property owners, feeling their power diluted, withdraw from commons altogether—defund the plaza, hire private security, fragment the shared space.

Unequal capacity creates unequal voice. A real estate developer brings lawyers and data analysts. A street vendor brings expertise but no technical staff. Without deliberate capacity-building—funding for community organizers, training in budget analysis, childcare at meetings—formal representation masks actual power imbalance.

The deepest risk: commons governance without economic redistribution. A fairly-governed plaza serving a gentrified neighborhood is still exclusionary. If the Stewardship Council can't address rising rents, displacement accelerates despite better decisions. The commons remains commons only if people can afford to remain in it.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Toronto's Community Benefits Agreements (CBA Network):** Beginning in 2008, Toronto communities facing major development used Distributed Stewardship mechanics—proportional representation, transparent budgeting, binding arbitration—to negotiate with developers and the city. CBAs seat residents (renters weighted heavily), workers, and property interests equally. The Regent Park revitalization's CBA required affordable unit preservation, local hiring, and community control over public space programming. More transparent than a BID, though still partial: developers retain leverage.

**Buenos Aires Neighborhood Assemblies (Asambleas Barriales):** Following the 2001 economic collapse, neighborhoods established assemblies with rotating facilitation and proportional voice for residents, small business owners, and unemployed workers' collectives. They made real spending decisions—which streets got repaired, which community centers reopened. Not perfect (some neighborhoods more organized than others), but they shifted power from concentrated municipal bureaucracies to distributed stewardship. Participation has ebbed with economic stability, but the model persists in several barrios.

**Medellín's Participatory Budgeting (Presupuestos Participativos):** Since 2004, Medellín has allocated 5-10% of municipal budgets through citizen assemblies weighted toward underserved neighborhoods. Each district elects spending priorities; decisions are binding. Property owners and renters sit together. It hasn't eliminated inequality, but it's redirected resources toward commons that serve working people: schools, parks, water systems in historically neglected areas. The model shows Distributed Stewardship scaling beyond single neighborhoods to whole cities—though with constant pressure to reduce real power.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an age of algorithmic governance and data proliferation, Distributed Stewardship becomes both more necessary and more fragile. Algorithms now recommend where to invest, which neighborhoods are "improving," which commons to monetize. Without diverse, human-centered decision-making, automated systems will concentrate power faster than any BID.

But AI and real-time data can also serve stewardship. Imagine Commons Councils with live dashboards: spending by zone, displacement risk scores, service accessibility maps updated by residents themselves. Machine learning can surface patterns invisible to any single viewpoint—that evening plaza safety improved when vendors were welcomed, not excluded. Participatory budgeting scales when citizens use platforms to deliberate, not just vote.

The risk: digital tools become substitutes for power-sharing. An app that lets residents "vote" on pre-selected options feels democratic but concentrates choice. True Distributed Stewardship in the cognitive era requires: humans making binding decisions (not recommendations), transparency that's radical enough to be auditable by non-experts, and explicit refusal to outsource judgment to algorithms.

The opportunity: distributed intelligence. A Stewardship Council with diverse members can out-think any centralized authority because it harnesses genuine local knowledge—what patterns residents notice, what futures they can imagine together. That capacity grows in an age where attention is fractured and expertise is fragmented across communities.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Council meetings feel genuinely contentious—disagreement, not consensus. Decisions change based on testimony; someone's voice shifted the outcome. Budget items get redirected mid-year because commons stewards noticed what wasn't working. Vendors return because they trust the space is defended. Renters show up repeatedly, not once. Service quality shifts visibly toward constituencies long neglected. Property owners sometimes lose a vote and accept it because the process felt fair.

**Signs of decay:** Meetings grow smaller. Renters stop coming, citing work schedules no one accommodates. Decisions feel pre-made; Council discussions are performative. The same voices dominate; new people arrive once and vanish. Spending concentrates again in commercially valuable zones. Vendors report harassment increasing; the commons feels less safe. Property owners speak privately of withdrawal—"if we don't control it, we won't fund it." Disputes fester rather than resolve. The Ombudsperson role becomes decorative.

**The diagnostic question:** If a constituency lost a major vote tomorrow, would they stay engaged because the process was fair, or would they exit because their interests are undefended?

If renters would stay, if workers would stay, if vendors would stay—even in loss—the commons is alive. If anyone is already planning exit, stewardship is eroding.
