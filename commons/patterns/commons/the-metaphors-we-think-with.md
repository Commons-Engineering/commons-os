> The words and images we use to understand the world shape what we can imagine. Get conscious about your metaphors, test

### Section 1: Context

Every human system runs on metaphors. 'The organisation is a machine.' 'Growth is climbing a mountain.' 'Love is a journey.' These are not mere decorations of language—they are the cognitive structures through which we perceive reality, make choices, and imagine what is possible. Metaphors shape what we notice and what we ignore, what seems natural and what seems absurd. Most of the time, we inherit our metaphors without examining them, absorbing them from culture, profession, and habit. This inheritance is both gift and constraint: it gives us rapid sensemaking, but it also blinds us. A commons that endures must become conscious of its own metaphorical architecture—the root metaphors that structure its understanding of stewardship, reciprocity, time, and change. This pattern draws from cognitive science (metaphors structure thought), from teaching and learning (metaphors make the abstract concrete and transmittable), from embodied practices (metaphors live in the body, not just in language), and from cross-cultural wisdom traditions (different cultures encode different metaphors, revealing different possibilities).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Unconscious metaphor (shapes thought without awareness) vs. Conscious metaphor stewardship (deliberate examination, refinement, deployment).**

The core tension: unconscious metaphors limit what a commons can become. When a group operates within inherited metaphors—mechanistic views of organisations, competitive views of cooperation, extractive views of resources—those metaphors foreclose certain actions and possibilities while making others invisible. A team might describe conflict resolution as 'warfare' or 'winning arguments,' never noticing that the metaphor itself prevents genuine reciprocity. An institution might speak of 'growing' like a company must grow perpetually, never questioning whether that metaphor serves actual wellbeing. Across cultures, untranslated metaphors create false understanding—a concept rooted in 'rootedness' or 'flow' is misunderstood by a listener shaped by metaphors of 'climbing' and 'building.' Meanwhile, the commons cannot adapt if it cannot see its own assumptions. The problem deepens when metaphors are plural but unexamined: a group holds multiple contradictory metaphors simultaneously (organisation as machine AND living system), creating invisible tension and incoherence. Consciousness of metaphor—surfacing it, testing it, refining it, sharing it—is the prerequisite for a commons to evolve intentionally rather than drift according to inherited patterns.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Cultivate Conscious Metaphor Stewardship through four interlocking practices:

**1. Audit & Surface (Individual & Collective):** Make your root metaphors visible. What metaphors do you naturally use to describe your work, relationships, change, time? 'We're fighting against…' 'This community is growing like a garden.' 'Leadership is orchestration.' Write them down. In group settings, use dialogue (Mental Model Surfacing) to make explicit the often-invisible metaphors each person carries. The goal is not agreement but visibility—each metaphor reveals an assumption worth naming.

**2. Examine & Test (Analogical Discipline):** For each metaphor, ask: What does this metaphor illuminate? What does it hide? Which aspects transfer to our actual situation; which aspects mislead? Use Analogical Reasoning Audit to test the boundaries. A 'garden' metaphor for community may illuminate succession and feedback but obscure the role of conflict and choice. A 'machine' metaphor may clarify efficiency but hide emergence and adaptation. Hold the metaphor lightly, as a tool—useful for certain purposes, limiting for others.

**3. Refine Through Practice & Dialogue (Embodied Integration):** Metaphors live in the body and in relationship, not just in language. Use embodied practices—Visceral Metaphor Work with felt sense (heaviness, opening, flow), or Garden as Metaphor Laboratory to learn ecological principles directly through practice. Use Hammock Time Protocol for unstructured synthesis, allowing metaphors to migrate and recombine. Most importantly, use dialogue to refine metaphors in community: 'What metaphors help us think together?' Test candidate metaphors through storytelling, scenario work, and lived experience. A metaphor refined collectively becomes a commons asset.

**4. Deploy Intentionally (Teaching & Governance):** Once examined and refined, use your metaphors as teaching tools and governance signals. Metaphor and Analogy as Teaching Tool transmits complex ideas through familiar domains. Narrative and Framing in Governance Discourse shapes what becomes thinkable in policy and planning. When a commons consciously deploys metaphors—'we steward this resource like a forest' rather than 'we manage this asset'—it signals the values, principles, and possibilities it is committed to. This is Metaphor Architecture for Concept Adoption at the collective scale.

**Integration:** This pattern is fractal. Apply it at the scale of your own coherence (Metaphor Coherence Check), at the scale of team dialogue (Cultural Metaphor Translation, Mental Model Surfacing), and at the scale of institutional narrative (Narrative and Framing in Governance). The practice itself becomes alive when it adapts to context: a commons rooted in agriculture may refine garden metaphors; a commons rooted in networks may refine web and flow metaphors. The key is not which metaphors you choose, but that you choose them consciously, examine them rigorously, refine them in dialogue, and deploy them to expand rather than limit what becomes possible.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin with a metaphor audit—a simple act of attention. Spend a week noticing the metaphors you use naturally: in meetings, emails, conversations, planning documents. Write them down without judgment. 'We're drowning in work.' 'We need to scale.' 'That's a roadblock.' 'We're building something.' Don't try to change them yet. Just notice the landscape of your thinking.

Invite your commons to do this together. In a workshop or regular gathering, have people share the metaphors they hear themselves and others using. You'll often find conflict lives in competing metaphors—one person speaks of 'nurturing growth,' another of 'driving efficiency.' Neither is wrong, but they're pulling in different directions. This surface work alone shifts things. You stop being trapped by metaphors and start being *aware* of them.

Next, test them. For each central metaphor in your work, ask: *What does this metaphor make visible? What does it hide? Where does it lead us?* If you say 'the community is a body,' you see organic interconnection and holistic health—but you might miss individual agency and creative friction. If you say 'the commons is a garden,' you see cultivation and natural cycles—but you might underestimate conflict and the need for deliberate structure.

Then comes the generative part: consciously choose or craft new metaphors. Not to replace all old ones, but to expand the repertoire. If your group has been living inside mechanistic metaphors, introduce ecological ones. If you've been purely growth-oriented, introduce metaphors of *thriving in place*, *deepening roots*, *seasonal rhythms*. Test them in real decisions. Does thinking of conflict as 'weather moving through the system' open different responses than thinking of it as 'disease to cure'?

This isn't one-time work. Metaphors need tending. Quarterly, revisit the ones that are alive in your commons. Are they still serving you? Have they calcified into dogma? Are new ones emerging organically? Stewardship means staying awake.

### Section 5: Consequences

When a commons becomes conscious of its metaphors, remarkable things flourish. Conversations shift. People notice they've been operating in a trance of inherited language. Solutions that seemed impossible suddenly look obvious when the metaphor changes. A group stuck in 'scarcity' metaphors (not enough time, money, people) might discover abundance metaphors ('abundance of relationships,' 'richness of difference') and find their actual capacity expanding because they can perceive it.

Decision-making becomes more resilient. When multiple metaphors are alive and acknowledged, a group can hold complexity. You don't collapse all thinking into one dominant frame. You can say: 'Yes, this looks like a garden that needs tending *and* a living system that needs oxygen *and* a network that needs trust.' Each metaphor illuminates different angles.

But there are risks if this work isn't tended. Metaphors can become weapons. Someone will insist their metaphor is 'true' and others are 'false,' turning what should be a liberating practice into another form of control. You can also fall into navel-gazing—spending so much time examining metaphors that you never act. The point isn't philosophical purity; it's *expansive thinking that leads to wiser action*.

There's also the risk of appropriation: borrowing poetic metaphors from indigenous or ecological traditions without understanding their actual worldview, turning them into decorative language for business-as-usual. Real metaphor work requires humility—not just swapping words, but actually letting the metaphor change how you think and act.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Transition Towns (UK and global).** The Transition movement explicitly rejected the metaphor of 'fighting climate change' (which implied warfare, winning/losing) and adopted 'transition' itself—a metaphor of deliberate, community-led shift toward resilience. This single metaphorical reframing changed what became possible. Instead of fear-based mobilization, communities could imagine positive futures and work backwards, asking 'What does a thriving, low-carbon community look like?' The metaphor opened collaboration where a warfare metaphor would have created enemies.

**Ubuntu Philosophy (Southern Africa).** The metaphor embedded in ubuntu—'I am because we are'—inverts Western metaphors of isolated individuals competing in markets. It shapes how communities organize care, justice, and resource-sharing. This isn't new metaphor adoption; it's the recovery and conscious deployment of a root metaphor that had been obscured by colonial language. Many contemporary African commons initiatives are consciously retrieving ubuntu metaphors precisely because they open possibilities that imported mechanistic metaphors foreclose.

**The Regenerative Organic Alliance.** Rather than operating from the metaphor of 'sustainable agriculture' (which still implies extraction, just at a slower rate), they're cultivating the metaphor of 'regeneration'—land and communities becoming *more* alive, more fertile, more resilient through the practice of farming itself. This metaphorical shift changes everything: soil becomes a living partner, not a substrate; time becomes cyclical, not linear; success is measured in deepening relationships, not yield per acre.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a time of AI and algorithmic thinking, metaphor stewardship becomes more crucial, not less. Large language models operate through mathematical patterns, not metaphors—yet they are trained on human language saturated with metaphor. If we don't consciously tend our metaphors, AI systems will simply amplify the unconscious ones embedded in their training data. A system trained on 'growth is always good' and 'nature is resource' will optimize for those logics at scale.

This era also opens new possibilities. Distributed networks of humans thinking together *across* metaphors can generate novel combinations. A commons might use AI to help surface metaphors in their collective language, then deliberately generate alternative framings. You could feed your organization's documents into a system and ask: 'What are the dominant metaphors? What metaphors are absent?' This becomes a mirror that helps groups see their own blindness.

But there's a danger: as thinking becomes faster and more distributed, we might imagine metaphors become *less* important—just swap the language, move on. The opposite is true. Metaphors become *more* consequential because they're harder to notice, baked into systems. A commons that tends its metaphors consciously becomes a cognitive commons, capable of thinking in ways that pure AI cannot. You're cultivating the human capacity for imagination, reframing, and deliberate meaning-making.

### Section 8: Vitality

A commons alive in metaphor stewardship shows itself clearly. Conversations have a quality of *exploration*—people are discovering their own thinking as they speak. You hear phrases like 'Wait, that's an interesting way to say it' and 'If we think of it this way instead…' Conflict is generative because it's rooted in different metaphorical frames, not just different opinions. There's curiosity instead of defensiveness.

Decisions feel *resilient*. The group doesn't move in lockstep. People hold different metaphors and can explain why each one matters. There's philosophical playfulness alongside practical action. Art, poetry, and image-making show up naturally, because people are actively imagining.

Signs of decay are equally clear. Metaphors calcify. You hear the same phrases repeated like mantras: 'This is just how we do things.' 'That's not our culture.' Metaphors become invisible again—the group has lost the capacity to question them. Conflict becomes personal because people aren't noticing that they're actually operating from incompatible metaphors. The thinking flattens. Decisions feel imposed, not discovered.

**The diagnostic question:** *If a stranger arrived and asked, 'What metaphors are alive in this commons right now?' could people answer quickly and with specificity? Or would they look confused?*

If they look confused, the metaphors have gone unconscious again. Time to surface them.
