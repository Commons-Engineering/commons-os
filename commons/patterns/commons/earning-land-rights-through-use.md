> A system where people gain stronger rights to land by living on it, using it well, and being part of the community—letti

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Urban domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Across the Global South, tenure operates not as a binary (owner/non-owner) but as a lived layering of overlapping rights—customary claims, occupancy protections, use rights, inheritance expectations, and community stewardship all coexisting. Urban commons, refugee settlements, and customary land systems demonstrate that secure tenure does not require private ownership; it requires visible, predictable, negotiated recognition of what people actually do and contribute. Yet most tenure frameworks collapse this layering into single-title systems that either exclude newcomers entirely or create opportunities for speculative alienation that destroys collective stewardship. The Orbit 1 principles of Polycentric Organisation, Reciprocity, and Conflict Resolution point toward a different design: graduated recognition that deepens with demonstrated commitment, active use, and integration.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Tenure security (individual need for protection) vs. Collective stewardship (community's need to prevent alienation and preserve commons).**

Tenure insecurity afflicts those with no formal documentation and those with non-aligned documentation (customary rights in jurisdictions recognizing only statutory title; occupancy without ownership; inheritance rights without current occupancy). Yet complete security through private ownership creates a different pathology: once alienated, land leaves the commons permanently. Newcomers—migrants, refugees, strangers—face systematic exclusion from customary tenure systems designed to privilege lineage and long residence. Communities cannot easily distinguish between temporary absence (for work, education, family care) and abandonment, creating perverse incentives to either maintain continuous physical presence or lose all claims. Dispute resolution during tenure transitions (inheritance, migration, integration) often requires land abandonment while adjudication proceeds, degrading both productive use and social cohesion. No single tenure status fits all life circumstances; individuals need flexibility without community losing sovereignty.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Tenure Stewardship Vesting establishes a graduated pathway where land rights recognition deepens through demonstrable commitment, active use, and community integration—while a superior commons stewardship capacity is retained at the collective level. The pattern operates through six core mechanisms:

**1. VESTING STAGES WITH EXPLICIT TRANSITIONS**: Rights progress through defined, community-endorsed stages: Provisional Use (guest rights, seasonal occupancy, temporary shelter) → Conditional Occupancy (regular residence, demonstrable contribution, apprenticeship in local norms) → Heritable Usufruct (indefinite use and improvement, inheritance rights, but no alienation to outsiders) → Community Stewardship (full integration, voice in commons governance, but land remains held in collective memory and cannot be sold in perpetuity). Each transition requires community recognition (not state permission), visible markers of commitment (service, marriage, ceremony, fee), and explicit community endorsement. Advancement is not automatic; it reflects genuine integration.

**2. ACTIVE USE REQUIREMENT WITH DORMANCY PROTOCOLS**: Tenure requires demonstrable active use—occupancy, farming, building, community participation. BUT absence does not trigger forfeiture. Instead, use rights enter dormancy status with explicit conditions: temporary leave (migration for work, education, family care, pilgrimage) can last defined periods (typically 1-3 years) without loss of claim. Dormancy requires community acknowledgment (notification to stewards, caretaker arrangement if applicable, reactivation fee if land is reassigned). Upon return, the holder reactivates dormant rights through ceremony, fee payment, or steward approval. This preserves membership continuity across the life cycle while preventing speculative hoarding.

**3. LAYERED TENURE RECOGNITION**: Separate the right to occupy/use from the right to alienate. A resident family holds occupancy and heritable usufruct rights but cannot sell to outsiders; the collective retains alienation rights and stewardship authority. Non-resident heirs can maintain inheritance claims while resident caretakers hold productive use rights with revenue-sharing. Customary tenure, statutory occupancy, Islamic endowment claims, and indigenous stewardship can coexist within the same parcel without requiring conversion to a single title; parallel registries document each regime's recognition and conflict resolution protocols prevent hierarchical collision.

**4. GRADUATED EXCLUSION AND REINTEGRATION**: Violations of commons rules trigger graduated responses—warnings, usage caps, seasonal suspension, permanent exclusion—NOT arbitrary expulsion. Graduated Exclusion (temporary bans of weeks to seasons for rule violations) signals seriousness while preserving rehabilitation pathways. Explicit appeal mechanisms and transparent triggers replace capricious authority. This maintains legitimacy of exclusion while keeping doors open for reintegration.

**5. STRANGER INTEGRATION PATHWAYS**: Outsiders (migrants, refugees, strangers) enter at Provisional Use stage and progress through integration milestones: demonstrated commitment to community norms, reciprocal contribution (labor, ceremony, marriage, formal adoption), extended continuous residence (typically 2-7 years depending on context). Migrant Probationary Tenure Pathways accelerate advancement through service contributions or kinship adoption. Refugee Settlement Staged Tenure moves from provisional shelter allocation → conditional residence with service obligations → permanent customary tenure upon demonstrated integration. This prevents the systematic marginality that locks migrants into marginal land (slopes, waterlogged sites) while creating genuine pathways to full membership.

**6. INTERIM POSSESSION AND DISPUTE HOLDING**: During tenure disputes (inheritance conflicts, boundary disagreements, repatriation claims), land does not abandon; an Interim Possessor Holding Agreement allows the parcel to remain productively managed while acknowledging the superior claim of the litigant. The interim holder maintains usufruct rights and may share revenues while dispute resolution proceeds. This prevents land degradation during adjudication and maintains community cohesion.

**GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE**: Rights vesting is administered by LOCAL STEWARDSHIP BODIES (customary councils, neighbourhood assemblies, commons boards)—not state or market. These bodies are competent to recognize advancement, authorize transitions, manage dormancy, and resolve disputes. They maintain a Tenure Registry documenting each parcel's stewardship status, occupant rights, vesting stage, caretaker arrangements, and dispute history. The registry is transparent and accessible to all participants. Appeal mechanisms route disputes to higher nested councils (neighbourhood → district → regional customary authority) following subsidiary principles. Periodic community reviews (annually or at harvest/planting cycles) reaffirm active occupants and refresh dormancy statuses.

**TIME-BOUND REVIEW AND REVERSION**: Customary lease durations to outsiders face statutory ceilings (30-50 years) with mandatory community review at fixed intervals. Upon expiration, land reverts to community stewardship unless explicitly renewed. This prevents de facto permanent alienation while allowing temporary beneficial use.

The pattern thus resolves the core tension: newcomers and marginal actors gain security through graduated recognition of their actual commitment, while the collective retains sovereign capacity to steward land as commons—refusing permanent alienation while remaining genuinely inclusive. Rights are alive; they breathe with presence, disappear into dormancy, reawaken, and evolve through recognized stages. Membership is earned, not inherited; yet once earned, it persists across life's interruptions.

### Section 4: Implementation

Tenure Stewardship Vesting unfolds through deliberate cultivation rather than sudden transfer. Begin by mapping existing tenure layers in a place—who uses what, how long they've been there, what the community already recognizes as legitimate claim. This archaeology reveals the actual social contract, often invisible to outsiders.

**Establish recognition thresholds, not ownership gates.** Define what "use" means: occupancy, maintenance, production, care. These are community judgments, not bureaucratic checkboxes. A household living on land for two years while improving it, teaching neighbors, and participating in collective decisions has earned something different than one occupying the same plot for two years while neglecting it. Make these distinctions explicit and contestable.

**Create graduated documentation that protects without alienating.** Issue use certificates, stewardship acknowledgments, or occupancy records—not deeds. These should be recognized by the community, local authorities, and creditors (for loans) without creating saleable title. The documentation proves commitment and grants security for improvements, inheritance, and dispute resolution, but stops short of conferring the right to extract the land from collective oversight.

**Embed regular community review.** Every 3–5 years, gather neighbors and stewardship bodies to affirm continued vesting or flag concerns. Is the steward still using the land well? Are they contributing to collective care? Have circumstances changed? This prevents tenure from becoming invisible or inherited by absent beneficiaries. It also offers a natural moment for updating rights as the person's role in the community evolves.

**Protect improvements unconditionally.** Whatever vesting stage someone occupies, their labor—buildings, gardens, wells, soil amendment—belongs to them. If they leave or lose rights, they take those improvements or receive fair compensation. This safety rail encourages investment without risking total loss.

**Design commons stewardship capacity retention carefully.** Community bodies (land councils, neighborhood assemblies, customary authorities) retain the right to revoke rights for gross neglect, major harm, or violation of collective agreements—but only through transparent process with appeal. They also retain right of first refusal if someone wishes to leave, keeping land within commons rather than allowing sale to outsiders.

### Section 5: Consequences

When Tenure Stewardship Vesting works, several goods flourish. **Belonging deepens.** Newcomers and long-term residents both experience security tied to participation, not just documents. People improve what they live in because the improvements are theirs and because the community sees and honors that care. **Commons remain commons.** Land doesn't become a speculative asset pool; it stays embedded in social fabric. **Dispute resolution becomes faster and more legitimate** because the community adjudicates conflicts about tenure the same way it recognizes tenure—through shared judgment, not distant courts.

However, several risks emerge if the pattern is neglected or corrupted.

**Elite capture.** If stewardship councils become dominated by early residents or powerful families, vesting becomes a mechanism for entrenching privilege. Newcomers face invisible barriers; recognition is withheld from disfavored groups. Prevent this through rotation of leadership, transparent criteria, and appeal to wider community.

**Creeping formalization into commodification.** Over time, "use certificates" can drift toward de facto titles. Creditors may begin treating vesting documents as collateral for loans; outsiders may pressure communities to "complete" the market by allowing sales. Tenure inflation—where people claim more land or higher rights than the community supports—erodes the commons. Guard against this by explicitly prohibiting sales, refusing to register vesting in land market systems, and renegotiating rights when major economic shifts occur.

**Expulsion through process.** Community review can become a tool for removing people the majority dislikes—poor households, minorities, those who challenge norms. Build in strong appeal rights and require high evidence thresholds for revocation. Make the process public and open to intervention by wider networks.

**Abandonment of stewardship.** If the commons body atrophies (council members stop meeting, participation drops, decisions feel arbitrary), vesting rights lose their anchor. They become either worthless or subject to reinterpretation by whoever holds informal power. Regular, well-resourced stewardship work is not optional.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Chhattisgarh and Madhya Pradesh, India (Community Forest Rights).** Following the Forest Rights Act (2006), forest-dependent communities can claim collective rights to forest land through demonstrated use and stewardship. Rights deepen through documentation of sustainable harvest, restoration work, and participation in forest councils. Communities retain commons stewardship while individuals gain security for their use of specific trees, water sources, and gathering areas. Conflicts arise where state authorities and corporate interests resist community vesting; success requires robust legal backing and organized communities.

**Nairobi, Kenya (Mukuru Community Land Trust).** Informal settlements like Mukuru use a graduated tenure recognition model where residents earn stewardship acknowledgment through community participation, housing improvement standards, and contribution to collective infrastructure. A community land trust holds legal title while residents hold documented use rights. As tenure security increases, so does residents' willingness to invest in housing and negotiate collectively with city government. The model is spreading to other Nairobi slums and faces pressure from city displacement agendas.

**Mexico (Ejido System, reformed).** Ejidos—collective land grants to communities—evolved from revolutionary land reform into a system where families hold use rights to plots while the ejido assembly retains collective decision-making over common lands, renting, and subdivision. Families cannot sell their plots to outsiders; they can inherit them or transfer them within the community with assembly approval. This has protected peasant tenure for generations but also created rigidity. Recent reforms allow more flexibility in transfers while maintaining collective oversight. The system demonstrates how vesting can persist across generations, though it struggles when markets intensify pressure to commodify.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Tenure Stewardship Vesting becomes more powerful and more difficult with AI-mediated land systems. On one hand, **digital verification of use** becomes cheaper. Satellite imagery, IoT sensors, and community apps can document maintenance, occupancy, and improvements continuously, strengthening the evidence base for vesting decisions without requiring expensive surveying or constant bureaucratic oversight. Blockchain and distributed ledgers could create immutable records of graduated rights that no single authority can alter.

On the other hand, **algorithmic governance of tenure introduces new capture risks.** If an AI system scores stewardship, communities may outsource judgment to metrics that miss context—a household with temporary illness may appear neglectful; a family practicing seasonal migration may be flagged as non-occupant. The appearance of objectivity can silence dissent. Communities must remain the source of tenure legitimacy, with algorithms as tools for transparency and consistency, not replacement for collective deliberation.

**Distributed networks of land councils** become easier to coordinate at scale. A township network can share best practices for vesting standards, dispute resolution, and appeals without centralizing power. This allows the pattern to scale beyond intimate village contexts to cities and regions.

**Data sovereignty becomes critical.** Tenure documentation—who lives where, what they own, how they use it—is power. Communities must control these records, not state registries or corporate platforms. This requires investment in community digital infrastructure and explicit refusal of interoperability with market-facing land databases.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Residents improve their homes and surroundings visibly—new structures, repaired roofs, planted gardens. Disputes are resolved quickly by community consensus, not through courts or intimidation. People speak of "our land" and mean it collectively. Newcomers report feeling welcome after participating in community work; longtime residents mentor newcomers into stewardship norms. The stewardship body meets regularly with good attendance; decisions are debated openly and revised when needed. Young people stay or return because they can build secure lives. Investment—in housing, business, education—happens locally. The commons remains commons: land doesn't drain away to absentee owners or speculators.

**Signs of decay:** Improvements stop; homes deteriorate. Stewardship council meets rarely or feels like theater. Tenure recognition becomes informal and unpredictable—"the chief decides" or "whoever has money gets rights." Newcomers are excluded or face endless probation. Disputes linger because there's no trusted process. Young people leave; the community ages. Outward migration to seek formal property elsewhere. Pressure from outside buyers intensifies; locals begin talking of selling. The commons erodes into individual holdings or empties into speculation.

**Diagnostic question:** If a newcomer arrived today—a stranger with no kin in the community—what clear pathway would they follow to earn tenure security over five years? Can they articulate it? Does it feel fair to them? If the answer is vague, expensive, or seems to depend on the evaluator's whim, the pattern is weakening.
