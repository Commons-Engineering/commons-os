> A neighborhood-led approach to substance use that treats it as a health issue, not a crime. People with lived experience

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Urban domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Substance use exists in every neighbourhood. For decades, dominant responses—criminalisation, medical institutionalisation, and concealment—have fragmented communities, driven users underground, and paradoxically increased both harm and disorder. The Commons Engineer recognises substance use as a collective health responsibility requiring visible, decentralised stewardship. This lineage traces through Subsidiarity (decisions made by those closest to the problem), Collective Action (shared governance of a shared challenge), Visibility as Commons Act (transparency as safety infrastructure), and Carrying Capacity (understanding thresholds of harm that can be sustainably reduced). The shift is architectural: from punishment and professional monopoly to peer accompaniment and neighbourhood accountability.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Criminalisation & concealment vs. care & visibility.**

Three forces collide. First: substance use cannot be eliminated, yet hidden drug markets generate violence, overdose deaths, and community fracture. Second: criminalisation and medical gatekeeping concentrate power away from those navigating addiction, producing shame-driven concealment and dependence on distant professionals. Third: visible, peer-led harm reduction infrastructure is treated as abnormal, requiring constant justification and funding fragility, rather than embedded as legitimate neighbourhood infrastructure. The core tension is whether substance use support will be organised around punishment and professional control, or around visible care and collective stewardship. Communities cannot choose between harm and safety—they must choose who decides how harm is managed.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Establish a three-layer commons stewardship architecture for neighbourhood substance use health. Layer 1—Governance: Create a binding substance use health council with 60%+ seats held by people with lived experience of substance use and recovery, with authority over local harm reduction policy, treatment service design, housing support, and police protocols. This council is not advisory—it holds decision-making power. Layer 2—Visible Infrastructure: Embed harm reduction services as normalised neighbourhood commons infrastructure: supervised consumption nodes, naloxone distribution stations, needle exchanges, peer accompaniment services, and recovery housing cooperatives sited openly and integrated with primary care. This visibility transforms concealment dynamics; visible care infrastructure paradoxically reduces associated crime and violence by eliminating hidden markets and enabling rapid response to crises. Layer 3—Peer Stewardship Networks: Train and employ people with lived recovery experience as neighbourhood navigators and peer support companions. They provide non-coercive accompaniment, health screening, connection to services, and accountability rooted in relationship rather than punishment. They also feed lived experience into council governance. The pattern operates on reciprocity: each person supported becomes potential steward. Substance use support becomes distributed across the neighbourhood, not concentrated in distant institutions. Accountability is mutualized—the community holds itself responsible for reducing harm, not outsourcing to police or medical gatekeepers. Care is alive because it adapts to neighbourhood conditions through continuous council governance feedback; shared because stewardship is distributed; just because it centres those most affected; and built to last because it roots support in neighbourhood reciprocity and peer continuity, not professional dependency or funding fragility.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping the actual terrain. Walk your neighbourhood with people who use drugs. Document where they gather, what they need, where they get hurt. Listen without judgment. This isn't research—it's relationship-building. You're learning the ecology of your street.

Convene the governance council deliberately. Recruit people with lived experience first—those in recovery, current users willing to speak openly, families who've navigated loss. Pay them. This is skilled labour. Hire people from the neighbourhood itself; they carry credibility and accountability that outsiders cannot. Create real decision-making power, not token inclusion. Council members should control budgets, hire staff, and set policy direction.

Establish a low-barrier drop-in space—physically visible, not hidden. Staff it with peer counsellors who've walked this road themselves. Provide supplies (clean needles, naloxone, testing kits), toilets, water, sometimes meals. The space is sanctuary and listening post both. People come because they're welcome, not because they've been arrested.

Build treatment and housing pathways that don't require abstinence as an entry condition. Some people need medication-assisted treatment; others need housing first; others need jobs. One pathway doesn't fit all. The council decides what gets offered based on what people actually ask for.

Develop clear protocols for police interaction—written agreements about when and how officers engage, designed to reduce arrest and increase connection to care. Some neighbourhoods establish co-response teams pairing officers with peer counsellors.

Train community members in overdose response and naloxone administration. Make naloxone available everywhere—not locked in clinics, but in corner stores, community centres, and homes. Document lives saved; celebrate them.

Hire people who use drugs now for paid roles—outreach, peer support, governance. This isn't charity. It's recognition that they are the experts in their own survival and the architects of a different future.

### Section 5: Consequences

When care becomes visible and decentralised, overdose deaths often fall within months. People stop dying alone in bathrooms because they know a space exists where they can be found. Trust accumulates. People begin using treatment services because peer counsellors—people they know—are offering them, not because a stranger with a clipboard insisted.

Community cohesion strengthens. Neighbours who feared "drug users" begin to see their own relatives, friends, and schoolmates. The moral distance collapses. Violence associated with hidden drug markets often decreases because the economy of concealment shrinks. Drug dealing moves from corner urgency to negotiated, quieter arrangements. Police have fewer reasons to flood the neighbourhood.

Economically, money circulates locally. Peer counsellors spend wages in the neighbourhood. Drop-in spaces hire local cleaners, cooks, maintenance staff. Housing support generates rental income and neighbourhood stability.

But risks emerge if the pattern isn't tended carefully. Visibility can invite overpolicing if protocols aren't enforced—authorities may use the drop-in location to conduct sweeps. Funding may become unstable; politicians change, grants expire. Without sustained investment, the council becomes exhausted and co-opted. Lived-experience staff face burnout; they carry both their own recovery and others' crises. Turnover fractures institutional memory.

If harm reduction is offered without housing, employment, or treatment options, people remain trapped in cycles of use and crisis. If the council loses real authority and becomes advisory, legitimacy erodes. Community members feel manipulated, used.

The deepest risk: the pattern can be absorbed into state control, gutted of its radical intention, and remade as another professional service that extracts care labour from the community while maintaining the fundamental structures that produce addiction.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Insite, Vancouver, Canada** (established 2003): A supervised injection site operated by peers and nurses, embedded in the Downtown Eastside where drug use is concentrated and visible. Monitored overdoses drop to zero on-site; off-site deaths decline. Clients transition to housing and treatment at higher rates than in comparable cities. The facility is run with input from people who use drugs and has generated measurable trust.

**La Sala, Barcelona, Spain**: A peer-led day centre in a working-class neighbourhood where people with addiction can rest, shower, eat, and access social workers and healthcare providers. Designed and managed by recovered people and current users. The model emphasises dignity and low-barrier access; no one is refused. Over 15 years, it has become a stabilising anchor for an entire district.

**Harm Reduction International's work in Hanoi, Vietnam**: Community health workers—many with their own histories of substance use—deliver naloxone training and needle distribution in neighbourhoods where heroin use is high. The programme is led by Vietnamese organisations, not imported expertise. Overdose deaths fell significantly while community trust in health services grew. The model centres local knowledge and recognises that outside solutions often fail.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and data systems will reshape this pattern if we're not deliberate. Predictive policing algorithms can identify drop-in locations and increase enforcement around them—the opposite of what we're trying to build. Surveillance systems trained on past criminalisation patterns will flag people with lived experience as "high-risk," reproducing historical harm.

But distributed intelligence also offers gifts. Peer networks can use encrypted platforms to share real-time information about contaminated drugs, new dangers, and safe sources of care—knowledge that moves faster than official channels. Community data systems can be governed locally, with people with lived experience controlling what gets measured and how findings are used. Naloxone distribution networks can use simple coordination tools to optimise supply.

The crucial move: ensure the governance council controls what data is collected, how algorithms are trained, and whether AI systems are used at all. Don't let "efficiency" disguise surveillance. A neighbourhood-led system might deliberately choose slower, more relational methods because trust matters more than speed.

### Section 8: Vitality

A healthy pattern shows these signs: People with lived experience occupy the majority of leadership roles and are paid fairly. The drop-in space has regulars who treat it as theirs. Peer counsellors are recruited from the community and stay in role for years, not months. Overdose deaths are documented and declining. People in the neighbourhood talk openly about substance use without shame—kids know what's being offered, adults know where to get help. Police protocols are followed; sweeps don't happen. Housing is available. Treatment slots are filled by people who chose them.

Signs of decay: The council becomes mostly professional staff with token lived-experience members. Funding becomes grant-dependent and unpredictable; staff turnover is high. The drop-in space empties; people stop coming. Peer counsellors burn out and leave. Overdose deaths rise or are hidden. Conversations about substance use return to whispers. Police begin sweeping the neighbourhood again; the protocol is ignored. People stop believing change is real.

**Diagnostic question**: If you removed all the paid professionals tomorrow, would the neighbourhood still hold this care? If the answer is no, the commons hasn't truly been cultivated—it's still a service run by outsiders, just with a different name. The pattern is alive only when people with lived experience have real power and the community has learned to steward this work themselves.
