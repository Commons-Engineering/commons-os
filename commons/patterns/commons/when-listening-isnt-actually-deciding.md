### Section 1: Context

Urban participatory democracy has become institutionalized across dozens of governance contexts: citizen assemblies, participatory budgets, community consultations, beneficiary design councils. These procedures embody a commons aspiration — that those affected by decisions should help shape them, that power should distribute, that legitimacy should flow from participation rather than hierarchy. Yet across contexts, a consistent pathology emerges: formal procedures generate strong legitimacy among participants while actual authority remains elsewhere — in facilitators' framing choices, informal coalitions, administrative reinterpretation, or political override. The gap between feeling heard and being heeded becomes a commons-killing wound. This pattern is rooted in three O1 principles: Collective Action (governance must distribute power, not just appearance), Truth-Seeking (systems that hide where decisions are really made degrade faster), and Polycentric Organisation (legitimacy and authority must align across nested levels).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Procedural Legitimacy vs. Substantive Authority.**

Participatory procedures operate on a dangerous assumption: that deliberative process alone can distribute power. In reality, four separable decisions precede and structure any assembly's legitimacy: (1) Boundary — who is included/excluded; (2) Agenda — which issues are on the table, which are not; (3) Framing — how problems are named, what solutions are conceivable; (4) Authority — whose voice binds implementation. Formal assemblies typically distribute voice within a pre-bounded, pre-framed, pre-determined scope. Simultaneously, informal power structures persist: personality-based influence, affinity coalition pre-meetings, facilitator gatekeeping, off-the-record negotiations. Citizens experience these as two parallel worlds — the assembly where they deliberate, and the shadow space where decisions are already made. The living tension: participatory procedures generate legitimacy precisely because they appear open and consequential, but that appearance masks the foreclosed decisions upstream and the decoupled authority downstream. The more sophisticated the procedure, the more complete the legitimacy performance, and the sharper the betrayal when outputs are ignored.

---

### Section 3: Solution

To couple legitimacy and authority, the commons must make visible and distribute the four upstream decisions that currently remain invisible. This requires a structural redesign of participatory governance:

**1. BOUNDARY DELIBERATION (Subsidiarity & Inclusivity).** Before any assembly convenes, a deliberative process itself must address: Who should be in this assembly, and why? This is not an administrative choice but a governance decision that belongs in the commons. Different boundary choices produce different legitimacy landscapes — random selection distributes differently than self-selected volunteers, which distributes differently than representative delegation. The boundary decision must be made collectively and justified publicly, not hidden in procedural design.

**2. AGENDA POWER DISTRIBUTION (Non-Decision Making Visibility).** The assembly's scope — what is *in* the frame and what is *out* — must be set through a preliminary deliberative process, not by authority or facilitators. This prevents agenda-setting from becoming the hidden locus of power. Some decisions will necessarily remain out of scope (budget constraints, legal boundaries); these constraints themselves must be named transparently, not buried in problem framing.

**3. FACILITATION AS EXPLICIT GOVERNANCE ROLE (Orchestration Over Execution).** Facilitators' power to frame, sequence, and interpret must be acknowledged and distributed. This means: rotating facilitators, making framing choices explicit and deliberatable, appointing co-facilitators from participant populations, and requiring facilitators to surface their own stakes in outcomes. Facilitation becomes a governance function subject to the same scrutiny as any other power.

**4. AUTHORITY-LEGITIMACY CLOSURE (Implementation Accountability).** The binding authority for implementation must be identified before deliberation begins. If an assembly cannot implement its own decisions, it must name who can, and build an accountability loop: assembly → authority → implementation → feedback → assembly. If an external authority reserves override power, this must be explicit ('we can reverse your decision only if...'), bounded (time limits, public justification required), and subject to escalation procedures. Citizens need to know: what does this process actually decide?

**5. SHADOW STRUCTURE FORMALIZATION (Polycentricity & Legitimacy).** Informal coalitions will emerge; this is inevitable. Rather than denying this, the pattern requires formalizing it: affinity groups, neighborhood delegations, identity-based caucuses must be made visible, given recognized roles in the process, and held to the same transparency standards as formal procedure. Shadow governance becomes legitimate governance, tracked and accountable.

**6. ITERATIVE CLOSURE & FEEDBACK (Adaptive Cycles).** After decisions are made, implementation must be monitored and fed back to the assembly. If a decision is modified by implementers, this triggers a redeliberation cycle. If an authority overrides an assembly decision, this is logged and must be justified publicly. Over time, repeated override reveals whether the 'participatory' process is genuinely distributing power or merely legitimating predetermined outcomes.

This pattern does not eliminate power asymmetries or make governance risk-free. It makes them visible, deliberatable, and subject to course correction — the conditions for a living commons rather than a legitimacy machine.

### Section 4: Implementation

The cultivation of genuine participatory power begins with making invisible decisions visible—and then redistributing control over them.

**Start with Boundary Deliberation.** Before convening any assembly, establish a smaller working group (8-15 people, ideally including those typically excluded from such conversations) to ask: Who is affected by this decision? Who is missing from previous processes? How do we reach people who don't show up to meetings? This group doesn't decide membership—they design the *process* for deciding it. They might recommend sortition (random selection), targeted outreach to underrepresented neighborhoods, translated materials, childcare, or stipends. The working group's reasoning becomes public. This isn't decoration; it's where real power starts to shift.

**Make Agenda-Setting Deliberative.** Give the assembly time—real time, not an hour at the end—to reshape what's on the table. What issues were excluded? Why? Can residents add problems the government hadn't framed? A participatory budget that only allows residents to allocate 5% of spending while 95% remains untouchable isn't deliberation; it's managed dissent. Build in a "reframing round" where participants propose how the problem itself should be understood differently.

**Distribute Framing Authority.** Hire facilitators from the community, not external consultants. Train residents as co-designers of how options are presented. Insist that government staff present multiple framings of solutions, not a single "staff recommendation." Transparency means showing the trade-offs that were *chosen*, not hidden.

**Lock Authority Into Structure.** This is the hardest step. Before the assembly meets, establish in writing what decisions the assembly will actually make, and what happens if the government disagrees. Will the assembly's recommendation be binding? Require a supermajority to override? Trigger a public debate if ignored? Without structural teeth, listening remains theater.

The entire arc—boundary through authority—should take 6-12 months, not six weeks. Speed signals that legitimacy is cheaper than power.

### Section 5: Consequences

When upstream decisions become visible and distributed, real consequences emerge—both fertile and thorny.

**What Flourishes:** Residents develop what might be called *governance literacy*—they begin to understand *why* decisions happen, not just *what* gets decided. This shifts participation from complaint to systems thinking. Communities that practice boundary deliberation often discover they disagree with each other productively; the deliberation becomes a commons good in itself, not just instrumental. Authority-coupling creates accountability: if the government overrides an assembly, residents know exactly where power lies and can organize around it. Cities like Barcelona's participatory budgets and some Brazilian municipalities have found that when residents shape the frame, their proposals become more implementable because they've already grappled with constraints.

**What Risks Emerge:** Distributing upstream power is slower and messier. Boundary deliberation can surface deep disagreements about who belongs—these don't disappear when named; they intensify. If authority isn't genuinely coupled to legitimacy (if the government still ignores the assembly's reframed agenda), disillusionment becomes sharper because the process was more transparent. Residents can feel betrayed more acutely. There's also a risk of *elite capture at a higher level*—if only the most skilled deliberators shape framing and boundary decisions, you've just moved exclusion upstream rather than eliminated it. And some residents will refuse to participate even in redesigned processes; no structure solves political apathy.

The pattern works only if the city is willing to distribute real power, not just the appearance of it.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgets in Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989-present).** The original model began not with listening procedures but with explicit boundary deliberation: which neighborhoods would be included in which year? Who decides spending priorities? Over three decades, residents moved from voting on pre-selected projects to helping design the very categories of spending. The assembly's recommendations became quasi-binding; government override requires public justification. This shifted participation from 2% to 15-20% of eligible residents in some years.

**Citizens' Assembly on Climate (Ireland, 2016-2018).** Rather than asking residents "what do you think about climate?" the assembly's design included a deliberation phase where 99 randomly selected citizens, aided by facilitators, reshaped what issues were in scope. They moved beyond "renewables vs. fossil fuels" to questions about agriculture, land use, and justice—framings the government hadn't centered. Their recommendations, though not binding, carried weight because the boundary and framing decisions had been transparent. The government implemented many proposals because residents had already worked through feasibility.

**Kampung Laki-Laki Participatory Governance (Jakarta, Indonesia).** In this informal settlement, community leaders moved away from annual "consultation meetings" toward ongoing boundary deliberation about whose voices counted: migrants, renters (not just property owners), women organizing across households. They formalized authority through a rotating council where decisions made in assembly were binding unless overridden by a two-thirds super-majority. The shift from annual theater to distributed upstream power reduced corruption in local resource allocation.

Each example shows the same pattern: power distributed over upstream decisions, not just downstream voting.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems introduce both opportunity and peril to this pattern.

**The Opportunity:** AI can make upstream decisions *more visible*. Algorithmic tools can map whose voices have historically been excluded from participatory processes (by analyzing attendance data, demographic representation, voiced concerns). Simulation software can let assemblies test different framings: "If we define the problem this way, what solutions become possible?" Residents can prototype boundary decisions computationally before implementing them. Distributed networks can enable asynchronous deliberation—not everyone needs to attend a meeting at 7pm on Tuesday.

**The Peril:** AI can launder upstream decisions back into invisibility. An algorithm that "objectively determines" who should be invited to an assembly can hide value choices in mathematics. Recommendation systems can narrow what framings are presented ("here are the three most popular solutions") rather than expand them. Chatbots that "listen to residents" can collect input while making the illusion of power distribution feel real without any actual redistribution. And in the cognitive era, the framing decision—what gets fed into the AI, what questions the model is trained to answer—becomes *even more* consequential and *even more* hidden.

The pattern holds, but it requires explicit vigilance: upstream decisions about *how* to use AI must themselves become visible and distributed. The algorithm's training data, its optimization target, its boundary conditions—these become the new frontier of invisible power.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life:**
- Residents can articulate *why* they were included or excluded from a process, and that reasoning was collectively deliberated, not administratively decided.
- The agenda changed between planning and implementation. The assembly reshaped what problems were being solved.
- The government publicly justified overriding assembly recommendations (rather than quietly ignoring them), and residents organized response.
- Participation includes people who have never attended a civic meeting before, not just the usual suspects.
- The process itself became a commons good—people report learning about how power works, not just getting a specific outcome.

**Signs of Decay:**
- Participation stabilizes at 5% or less of eligible residents (signal that the process still feels inaccessible).
- The assembly's recommendations are implemented, but the upstream decisions (who was invited, what was on the agenda) were unchanged year-over-year (signal that power hasn't actually redistributed).
- Facilitators are hired consultants, not community members; residents don't design how the process works.
- The government announces decisions before the assembly deliberates, then asks for feedback (procedural legitimacy without substantive authority).
- Residents describe the process as "they asked us, then did what they wanted anyway."

**Diagnostic Question:**
*If the assembly recommended the opposite of what the government planned, would that recommendation change the decision—or only require the government to explain itself?*

If the answer is "only require explanation," you have listening theater, not power distribution. The pattern isn't working.
