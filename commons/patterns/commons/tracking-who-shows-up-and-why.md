### Section 1: Context

Every cooperative endeavour has a carrying capacity — the quantity and quality of participation it can sustain without degrading. In shift-dependent operations (healthcare, manufacturing, public service), retail networks, and distributed teams, absence ripples outward: gaps in coverage cascade into stress on remaining participants, which accelerates further absence, which degrades service quality, which breaks reciprocity with those served. Simultaneously, absence is data — a signal of stress, health change, misalignment, or capacity overload in the system itself. The O1 principles of Carrying Capacity, Information Shapes Systems, and Truth-Seeking demand that presence patterns be visible, interpreted systemically, and responded to with both rigour and care. This is not surveillance; it is stewardship of a shared resource.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Individual autonomy & consent vs. Operational continuity & collective capacity.**

Organizations face an impossible choice: ignore absence and risk operational collapse and unfair burden on present participants; or implement rigid tracking that treats absence as moral failure rather than system signal. Current practice oscillates between two extremes. Punitive models drive secrecy (people hide illness or stress, making the true health of the system invisible). Permissive models lose operational coherence (coverage gaps propagate, reciprocity breaks, the system becomes unreliable). Neither treats absence as what it is: feedback about carrying capacity, individual wellbeing, and systemic stress. The tension is real and living: the collective needs reliable presence to function justly; individuals need agency, rest, and support when stressed. Without a commons-native governance structure, this tension becomes pathological.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement Presence Stewardship as a polycentric feedback system operating across four nested scales: individual cycle, team threshold, systemic pattern, and collective adaptation.

**Individual Cycle (Subsidiarity):** Establish a lightweight notification protocol: participants communicate absence (planned or unplanned) to their immediate circle — team, shift lead, coverage pool — as early as possible, with no punitive framing. The act of notice is the contribution; the receiver's job is to absorb or redistribute the gap. Trust default: absence is legitimate until shown otherwise.

**Team Threshold (Local Governance):** Define for each work unit the minimum viable presence to sustain operations without cascading stress. This is not a rule imposed from above but a number negotiated by the team itself — grounded in actual work, not policy. When absence reduces presence below threshold, the team escalates (not the individual — the team). Escalation is a request for support, not an accusation.

**Systemic Pattern (Transparency & Adaptive Cycles):** Record absence data transparently — not hidden in HR systems but visible to participants and their stewards. Analyze monthly for patterns: clustering (signal of seasonal stress, occupational hazard, or event), trajectory (is absence rising or stable?), correlation (does absence cluster with specific roles, times, or cohorts?). Share findings openly. A rising absence pattern is not "employee problem" — it is a system signal. The response is investigation: What changed? Is the work load exceeding carrying capacity? Is there hidden stress, health change, or unsupported transition?

**Collective Adaptation (Reciprocity & Justice):** When pattern analysis reveals systemic stress, the response is collective redesign, not individual discipline. If absence clusters in night shifts, redesign shifts. If it correlates with training completion, examine whether training load is realistic. If it signals health concern, offer support (occupational health, flexibility, accommodation). If it reveals capacity overload, adjust staffing, workload, or threshold expectations. The cycle is: notice → pattern → diagnosis → collective response → monitor → adapt.

Data validation and reconciliation happen continuously (not as post-hoc audit) to ensure the signal is true. Thresholds are reviewed quarterly as conditions change. Escalation matrices exist but operate via subsidiarity — the lowest capable level decides; escalation moves up only when local decision-making lacks authority or information.

This pattern treats absence as a commons stewardship problem, not a personnel problem. It sustains operational capacity, protects individual wellbeing, makes system health visible, and distributes governance across scales.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin with **noticing without judgment**. Create a simple shared record—a spreadsheet, a messaging channel, a physical log—where absence is noted alongside its category: planned leave, illness, emergency, unexcused, pattern unclear. The act of recording itself becomes a mirror. Don't hide the data; make it visible to those it affects most directly (the team covering the gap), then gradually wider.

**Establish a rhythm of small conversations**, not investigations. When someone misses a shift, someone reaches out within 24 hours—not to enforce compliance, but to understand. Is there a childcare crisis? A health issue emerging? A sign of burnout? A conflict with scheduling? Each conversation is an opportunity to learn what the system is asking of people and whether it's sustainable.

Create **tiered response protocols** that scale with pattern:

- **One absence**: simple check-in, offer support if needed.
- **Two in a month**: a brief conversation about obstacles. Can the schedule flex? Is there a resource gap?
- **Recurring pattern**: a deeper dialogue. This person may be telling the organization something important about its rhythms, demands, or culture.

**Track not just presence, but *why* absences happen**. Over time, categories reveal system truth: if illness clusters in winter or after major pushes, that's a design problem. If certain shifts see chronic no-shows, that shift may be poorly constructed. If absences spike after policy changes, people are voting with their feet.

**Build in transparency**: Share aggregated patterns with the team quarterly. "We've noticed more illness in November. Here's what we changed." This shifts the frame from individual accountability to collective problem-solving. People who see their absence as data that improves systems rather than evidence of their failure begin to report more honestly.

Assign **one person stewardship responsibility**—someone who notices, remembers context, asks follow-up questions. This role isn't punitive; it's relational. They're the keeper of the pattern, not the enforcer of rules.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes**: Early warning systems. A manufacturing plant that tracks absence patterns catches the exhausted supervisor before she burns out. A healthcare clinic notices that Friday absences spike and discovers scheduling is genuinely unsustainable. A distributed team sees that certain time zones are chronically understaffed and adapts. Trust deepens because absence is treated as communication, not transgression.

People stop hiding struggles. They report illness earlier because there's no shame in the report. They ask for shift swaps before disappearing. Managers understand their teams' real constraints and can advocate upward for resources or changes. Operational continuity improves not through enforcement but through design responsiveness.

**Risks if neglected**: When absence goes untracked, crisis management becomes the norm. Someone burns out completely before anyone notices. Coverage gaps create cascading stress that drives more absences. Resentment festers among those always present. The system becomes opaque—leaders don't know whether a problem is individual or structural.

Conversely, if tracking becomes surveillance-like, people game the system in worse ways: they come in sick, spreading illness; they hide struggles until collapse; they leave the organization rather than be monitored. Trust erodes. The data becomes useless because it no longer reflects reality.

The deepest risk: **mistaking individual accountability for system design**. If absence is framed purely as personal failure, the organization never asks whether its demands are reasonable. It simply cycles through people, burning them out and replacing them, never improving.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Healthcare shift-work coordination (NHS, Canada Health Services)**: Hospitals use tiered absence tracking to identify both individual burnout and understaffing patterns. When one ward shows chronic absences, it often signals inadequate staffing ratios, not worker failure. This data drives hiring and workload rebalancing rather than discipline.

**Mondragon Cooperatives (Spain)**: These worker-owned factories track participation across assembly lines with explicit attention to fairness and sustainability. When absence patterns emerge, teams collectively redesign the work or rotate roles. The data belongs to the cooperative, not management, so it's used for collective problem-solving rather than control.

**Kibbutz rotation systems (Israel)**: Traditional kibbutzim tracked who participated in shared work rotations not to punish but to ensure equitable distribution and sustainability. When someone consistently couldn't manage certain roles, the community adapted assignments rather than assigning fault. The practice recognized that people have seasons and capacities; the system needed to flex around that reality.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI shifts what **noticing** can become. Machine learning can surface patterns humans miss: that absences cluster before product launches (indicating anxiety), that they correlate with specific team configurations (indicating interpersonal friction), that they predict turnover three months ahead. This is powerful—if used wisely.

The risk is automation of judgment. A system that flags absences and auto-escalates discipline removes the human conversation that makes this pattern work. Instead, **use AI as a noticing amplifier**: let algorithms surface patterns, then have humans engage in genuine dialogue about what the pattern means.

Distributed systems (remote teams, gig work) make absence **less visible but more significant**. When there's no physical office, someone's silence can mean anything. Lightweight asynchronous check-ins—"I won't be online tomorrow" posted in a shared channel—become essential. AI can help aggregate these signals across time zones and send timely, human reminders rather than punitive alerts.

The deeper shift: AI makes it possible to operate with **radical transparency about capacity**. Rather than hiding absence, people could declare their available hours honestly. Systems could match work to actual capacity in real time. Absence wouldn't be exceptional; variable presence would be designed-in.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- People report absences early and honestly, without shame or fear.
- When someone is missing, teammates check in as a reflex—not because they're required, but because they care and need to coordinate.
- Conversations about absences surface real obstacles (childcare, health, schedule mismatch) that get addressed at the system level.
- Patterns emerge clearly: leadership sees them and adapts scheduling, workload, or resources accordingly.
- The rate of absence stabilizes or improves because the system is actually getting less punishing.

**Signs of decay:**
- Absence tracking becomes a gotcha: people sense judgment and start hiding or gaming the system.
- Conversations stop happening; absence is recorded but never discussed.
- Patterns emerge but nothing changes—data accumulates without response, so people stop reporting honestly.
- A culture of resentment: "She's always gone" replaces "Our shift is understaffed."
- Turnover accelerates because people see absence as moral failure and leave rather than face judgment.

**The diagnostic question:**
*If you stopped tracking absence for a week, would the people doing the work notice? Or would everything stay the same?*

If they'd notice immediately—calling each other, coordinating, asking what's happening—the pattern is alive. If no one would care, then tracking has become theater. The real work is happening elsewhere, or the system has stopped mattering to people.
