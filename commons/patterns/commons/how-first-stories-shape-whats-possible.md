> The way a group first frames a problem tends to stick around—and can block better solutions from even being considered. 

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Urban domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

In deliberative assemblies—citizen juries, participatory budgeting, neighbourhood planning forums—the moment someone articulates a problem, it begins to harden. A housing shortage is framed; soon all solutions are supply-side. A budget is framed as fixed; soon all deliberation becomes zero-sum antagonism. Repetition locks the frame deeper; alternative framings become 'off-topic'; early framers acquire disproportionate power over the solution space. This is not a flaw in deliberation—it is how human cognition works. Frames are cognitive anchors; they reduce uncertainty; they enable coordination. But in complex, contested domains (urban development, resource allocation, policy design), premature frame-locking forecloses genuine deliberation and transfers power to early speakers. The assembly becomes a theatre for ratifying predetermined conclusions, not a space for collective thinking. This tension is rooted in Adaptive Management (acknowledge uncertainty, treat interventions as experiments) and Conflict Resolution (rules must evolve as understanding evolves).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Frame Stickiness vs. Deliberative Openness.**

Deliberative assemblies face an invisible contradiction: frames enable coordination but prevent evolution. Early framings—'housing shortage,' 'congestion problem,' 'budget constraint'—function as cognitive anchors. They reduce overwhelm. They allow small groups to deliberate. But they also become self-reinforcing. Participants internalize the frame; it shapes what counts as 'relevant'; alternative framings require exhausting consensus to voice. The mechanism is repetition: each time a frame is invoked, it strengthens. Each time an alternative framing is introduced, it competes against an already-established reference point. Over time, the initial frame becomes invisible—it stops feeling like a choice and starts feeling like reality. By mid-assembly, reframing is practically impossible; it feels like rejecting months of deliberation. The problem deepens when early framers (facilitators, agenda-setters, influential voices) have disproportionate power over which frames get articulated first. The assembly then ratifies their framing as consensus, not recognizing it as a choice made early under uncertainty.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Embed deliberate reframing cycles into the assembly's governance structure. Reframing is not a failure to 'reach consensus'—it is the assembly's way of learning. Treat frame-revision the way adaptive management treats policy revision: as a scheduled, structured practice, not an emergency intervention.

Three mechanisms:

**1. Scheduled Reframing Pauses (Temporal Subsidiarity).** Every 3–5 deliberative sessions, pause execution. Ask: What problem are we actually solving? Invite participants to articulate the frame they are working with, then explicitly introduce 2–3 alternative frames (generated by participants or guided by commons language: 'shared stewardship' vs 'scarcity competition'; 'root cause' vs 'symptom management'). Deliberate on frames themselves, not just solutions. This works because it makes the frame visible—no longer invisible architecture but explicit design choice. It happens on schedule, so it is not experienced as 'wasting time' but as governance rhythm.

**2. Frame Genealogy & Epistemic Transparency.** Early in assembly, document the moment a frame emerged: who articulated it, what context, what alternatives were considered and rejected. Make this genealogy visible throughout deliberation. When a participant feels constraint by the frame, they can trace its origin—recognizing it as a choice, not a fact. This also reveals whose framing got locked in, enabling the assembly to calibrate for voices that did not get heard early.

**3. Reframing Authority (Distributed Epistemic Power).** Designate rotating 'frame stewards'—participants who have explicit permission (and responsibility) to propose alternative framings without needing consensus. A frame steward says: 'I notice we are treating this as a scarcity problem. What if we framed it as a design problem?' This is not debate; it is permission to make frames visible. The assembly then decides whether to explore the alternative or return to the working frame. This distributes epistemic power: it is not only facilitators or early speakers who get to shape the problem definition.

These mechanisms keep deliberation alive—capable of evolution without dissolution. The assembly learns its own framing habits and can interrupt them before they calcify.

### Section 4: Implementation

Reframing isn't mystical—it's a craft that can be learned and embedded into how groups actually work together.

**Start with a Reframing Charter.** Before your assembly digs into solutions, spend a session naming the frames you're *already* operating under. Write them down. A neighbourhood planning group might surface: "This is a *scarcity* problem" or "This is a *character preservation* problem" or "This is a *fairness* problem." Make frames visible. Acknowledge that different frames lead to different solutions—that's not confusion, that's richness.

**Install scheduled pause points.** After every 4–6 deliberative sessions, pause. Bring in a skilled facilitator (sometimes an outsider, sometimes someone trained internally) to ask: *What would we see differently if we reframed this?* Use structured techniques: inversion ("What if we flipped the problem?"), sector-crossing ("How would a health worker frame this? A business owner?"), temporal shifting ("What if we're solving for 50 years from now, not next year?"). Write down 2–3 alternative frames. Vote on which to explore in depth over the next cycle.

**Rotate frame-holders.** Don't let one person or faction own the dominant frame. Deliberately invite participants who think differently—from other neighbourhoods, age groups, professions, migration histories—and give them explicit authority to propose reframings. Make this a role, not an accident.

**Document frame journeys.** Keep a living archive: *Here's how we first framed housing. Here's how that frame shifted. Here's what opened up.* This becomes institutional memory. New members see that reframing is normal, even expected.

**Train your core team.** A handful of facilitators who understand frame analysis—how language shapes what seems possible—can become the pattern's keepers. They notice when a frame is hardening. They ask gentle questions that loosen it.

The key: treat reframing as legitimate work, not as dysfunction or backsliding. Build it into timelines, budgets, and role descriptions.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** When reframing becomes routine, several things come alive. First, *latent possibilities emerge*. A housing discussion framed as "shortage" yields towers and density; reframed as "belonging and stability," it might yield cooperative housing, repair of existing stock, or rethinking who gets to stay. Second, *minority voices gain standing*. The person who says "wait, maybe this isn't a scarcity problem—maybe it's a distribution problem" stops being disruptive and starts being a necessary voice. Third, *solutions become more resilient*. Decisions made after multiple frames have been explored tend to hold better over time because they've been stress-tested against different logics.

**What risks emerge:** Reframing can become exhausting. Participants may experience "frame fatigue"—the sense that the group never settles, never commits, keeps relitigating basics. This is real; it requires skilled facilitation and clear boundaries (e.g., *we will explore 3 reframes, then commit to a direction for 18 months*). There's also a risk of *performative reframing*—the assembly goes through the motions, changes language, but the power dynamics that shaped the first frame remain unchanged. A reframing process can mask rather than heal structural inequality. Finally, *some frames are genuinely better than others*. Not all framings are equally true or just. The pattern doesn't mean "all frames are equal"—it means *surfaces the frames so they can be genuinely chosen*.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory budgeting in Paris (2015–present).** Paris's citizen budget process initially framed projects as a competition: "Which projects win funding?" This created winner-and-loser dynamics, particularly in lower-investment neighbourhoods. Around 2017, facilitators introduced reframing pauses. Projects began to be framed as *complementary investments in district wellbeing* rather than competitive bids. This opened space for cross-neighbourhood collaboration and shifted attention from headline projects to systemic gaps. Investment patterns shifted; so did participation from residents who'd felt their district was always "losing."

**Neighbourhood assemblies in Tlaxcala, Mexico.** Indigenous and mestizo communities used a seasonal reframing cycle (aligned with agricultural calendars) to revisit how they understood "development." Early frames centered on income and infrastructure. Reframing pauses—often led by elders and women's groups—gradually surfaced frames rooted in *community cohesion, language preservation, and autonomy*. This didn't erase infrastructure needs, but it recontextualized them: infrastructure as servant to community life, not its primary goal. Decisions became more durable across political cycles.

**Flood risk planning in Rotterdam (Netherlands, 2000s onward).** Initial frame: "How do we protect against water?" Standard answers: bigger dykes, more pumping. Reframing (influenced by climate science and indigenous water wisdom) shifted to: "How do we live *with* water?" This opened entirely new solution spaces—wetlands, permeable streets, controlled flooding zones, water plazas. The shift unlocked designs that were cheaper, more ecologically alive, and more adaptable to future change.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In a world of distributed intelligence and AI-assisted deliberation, reframing becomes both more necessary and more challenging.

**More necessary** because AI systems amplify frame stickiness. Large language models are trained on existing discourse; they reflect and reinforce dominant frames at scale. An AI summarizing citizen input may flatten alternative frames into the statistically dominant one. A chatbot answering planning questions within a particular frame may make that frame feel inevitable. Humans must actively work against this gravitational pull.

**More challenging** because reframing requires cognitive flexibility that's increasingly hard to sustain. Algorithmic feeds, polarized media, and information overload push people toward frame-locking—the sense that *this* is how reality is. Deliberative assemblies become refuges where that locking can be gently undone, but they require more active protection and skill.

**New tools emerge:** AI can be trained to *surface* hidden frames. A system might analyze a planning discussion and say: "I notice you're using scarcity language 60% of the time and rights-based language 10%—would you like to explore the rights frame more?" This is frame-visualization rather than frame-imposition. Humans decide what to do with it.

**The key shift:** In a cognitive era of continuous information flow, reframing becomes less about finding *the right frame* and more about building *frame literacy*—the ability to move fluidly among frames, to see their affordances and limits. Deliberative assemblies that teach this skill become countercultural spaces where people remember they can think differently.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Someone proposes a reframing mid-discussion and others *pause to genuinely consider it* rather than dismiss it as derailment.
- Participants from different backgrounds offer competing frames without it feeling like conflict—more like "oh, I hadn't thought of it that way."
- Archive of reframings exists and is consulted. "Remember when we thought this was about X? Now we see it as Y."
- Newcomers encounter frame-shifting as normal. They're not surprised; they expect it.
- Solutions that emerge feel genuinely novel, not minor variations on early ideas.

**Signs of decay:**
- Frame proposed early; all discussion orbits it thereafter. Reframing suggestions met with "we've already decided what the problem is."
- Reframing cycles exist on paper but are routinely skipped due to time pressure or facilitator fatigue.
- Different groups develop *incompatible* frames and stop talking across the divide. Reframing becomes factional rather than collective.
- Participants express resignation: "We'll talk about this for months, then end up doing what was decided at the start anyway."
- Archive of frames exists but isn't consulted; it's museum, not living memory.

**The diagnostic question:**

*If someone in this assembly says, "Wait—what if we're asking the wrong question?" does the group lean in or shut down?*

If they lean in, the pattern is alive. The assembly is learning. If they shut down, reframing has become decorative, and the first frame still runs the show.
