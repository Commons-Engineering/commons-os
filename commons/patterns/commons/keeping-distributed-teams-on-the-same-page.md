### Section 1: Context

Complex organizations—especially those with multiple business units, geographies, or functional domains—face a recurring tension: centralized strategy-setting preserves coherence but stifles adaptive response to local conditions; decentralized autonomy enables speed and fit but creates fragmentation, waste, and misaligned incentives. The solution is neither top-down mandate nor loose federation, but polycentric governance: nested centres of authority, each with explicit scope, mandate, and reciprocal accountability. This pattern builds on three Orbit 1 principles: Polycentric Organisation (which mandates distributed decision-making at multiple scales), Subsidiarity (decisions belong at the lowest competent level), and Reciprocity (cooperative systems require visible contribution and fair exchange). The pattern's lineage is also in Patterns Over Protocols—encoding the intent behind authority boundaries, not just the rules.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Autonomy vs. Coherence — distributed decision-making that serves the whole without collapsing into either fragmentation or recentralization.**

In decentralized organizations, four interlocking tensions emerge. First, undefined authority: which decisions belong to the unit, which escalate? Without clarity, units either over-escalate (slowing response) or over-commit (creating misalignment). Second, invisible alignment: units optimize locally without seeing how their choices interact with peers and strategy. Third, unfair allocation: shared services (finance, HR, IT) are allocated opaquely, creating perception of subsidy or tax. Fourth, low reciprocal accountability: units cannot easily see whether peers are honoring agreements or contributing fairly. Traditional responses—centralized approval, annual strategy cascades, formulaic scorecards—sacrifice adaptability and local knowledge. The deeper problem: governance is treated as static documentation rather than a living practice of nested conversations about authority, contribution, and mutual obligation.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Stewardship Through Nested Charters establishes a multi-layer governance structure that distributes authority while maintaining coherence through four interlocking practices.

First: Nested Charter Documents. Each organizational unit (business unit, function, shared service) maintains a living Charter that documents (1) its purpose and stewardship responsibility, (2) decision authority (what it decides alone, what requires consultation, what escalates), (3) key relationships and reciprocal dependencies, and (4) how it measures contribution. Charters are not imposed top-down but co-authored through deliberate negotiation between the unit and its stakeholders. They are updated quarterly, not annually, and versioning is transparent.

Second: Mandate Specification with Constraint Boundaries. Each unit operates under an explicit Mandate (what it is authorized to do, what resources it controls, what results it must deliver) and Constraints (what it may not do, what decisions require consent from other units, what thresholds trigger escalation). Constraints are not restrictions but reciprocal agreements—they exist because they protect the coherence that enables all units to operate effectively. Constraints are renegotiated annually as part of strategy alignment.

Third: Alignment Ceremonies, Not Cascades. Rather than a top-down cascade where corporate strategy is handed down and units "align" by implementing, the pattern calls for three reciprocal ceremonies: (a) Strategic Coherence Verification—quarterly cross-unit conversations where units surface initiatives and dependencies, identify conflicts early, and adjust without formal approval; (b) Cross-Functional Checkpoints—when major strategic decisions affect multiple units, a structured meeting where all affected units contribute their perspective before decision; (c) Performance Accountability Conversation—not a scorecard review but a relational conversation where units report on mandate delivery, acknowledge reciprocal support from peers, and surface emerging constraints.

Fourth: Shared Service Allocation Transparency. Any shared service (finance, HR, IT, legal) publishes its allocation methodology—how it prices services, how it prioritizes requests, how it handles conflicts—and submits to quarterly review by its client units. This transforms shared services from hidden tax into negotiated partnership.

The pattern persists because the charter structure (once established) requires minimal overhead—mainly updating and ceremony facilitation. Authority remains distributed; escalation is rare; adaptation is continuous. Coherence emerges not from mandate but from reciprocal visibility and repeated alignment conversation.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping your organization's natural decision domains—the choices that genuinely belong to each unit versus those with system-wide impact. This isn't a one-time org chart exercise; it's an act of honest conversation. Bring leaders together and ask: "What do we need to decide together? Where do you need real freedom?"

From that clarity, draft Unit Charters collaboratively. Each charter names three things: the unit's core stewardship (what it owns), its decision authority (what it can decide unilaterally), and its escalation triggers (what it must flag before deciding). Make these documents short and living—a page or two that teams actually reference, not a binder that sits on a shelf. Include concrete examples of decisions that *do* require escalation and those that *don't*.

Next, establish a rhythm of Alignment Rituals. Monthly or quarterly gatherings where unit leads present decisions, trade-offs, and emerging constraints. Not to seek permission, but to make invisible dependencies visible. A product team learning that supply-chain is pivoting vendors? That surfaces here. Finance restructuring that affects partner economics? Surfaced. The point is early signal, not surprise.

Create Mutual Accountability Checkpoints. Quarterly, each unit reports three things to peers: (1) decisions made within its authority, (2) any decisions that crossed into gray zones and how they navigated them, (3) where they see misalignment emerging with peer units. This isn't surveillance; it's collective stewardship. Peers respond not with judgment but with perspective: "We didn't know that. It affects us here. Can we talk?"

Finally, invest in Escalation Clarity. When gray zones appear—and they will—treat them as data. Document what happened, why current charters didn't address it, and what you learned. Update the charters. Over time, the organization learns where true interdependencies live and where apparent conflicts dissolve with better information.

The cultivation metaphor matters: you're tending the soil of trust and clarity, not imposing a structure. Units that feel genuinely trusted to lead their domain will self-regulate better than units buried under policy.

### Section 5: Consequences

When this pattern takes root, several things flourish. Decision velocity increases—units move faster because they know their zone of authority and don't wait for blessing on every choice. Local adaptation accelerates; teams respond to customer needs, market shifts, and operational realities without the lag of centralized review. Trust grows; leaders feel genuinely empowered, not micromanaged through policy. And paradoxically, coherence improves because misalignments surface early, in conversation, rather than accumulating as silent friction.

The organization also develops what we might call "intelligent redundancy"—multiple units exploring similar problems, sharing learnings, and building resilience. If one approach fails, others have learned from it.

But tensions persist if the pattern weakens. If charters become rigid or forgotten, units revert to either over-escalation (everything gets committee-reviewed, nothing moves) or hidden autonomy (units decide in isolation, discovering misalignment only in crisis). Decision authority that stays ambiguous breeds either empire-building (units claiming authority they shouldn't have) or paralysis (no one will move without consensus).

There's also a risk of false coherence—the appearance of alignment (leaders nod in meetings) without actual integration. Teams still operate in silos; they've just gotten better at hiding it.

The deeper risk: if accountability becomes blame-oriented rather than learning-oriented, units start gaming the escalation process—escalating early to diffuse responsibility, or hiding decisions to avoid scrutiny. The pattern collapses into politics.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Spotify's Squad Model** (2010s-present): Autonomous squads organized around features or services, each with decision authority over their domain. Governance happens through shared OKRs and quarterly "all-hands" syncs where squads surface decisions and interdependencies. Squads are empowered to move fast; the organization stays coherent through transparency and shared metrics, not permission-seeking.

**Haier's Platform Organization** (2005-present, China): The appliance manufacturer broke into thousands of microenterprises, each a small business unit with profit-and-loss responsibility and decision autonomy. They stay aligned through shared platform services, transparent accounting, and a culture of mutual accountability. Units compete and collaborate simultaneously; coherence comes from shared purpose (customer obsession) and clear boundaries, not central planning.

**DNV GL's Business Unit Governance** (maritime classification and certification, 2010s): Multiple business units across shipping, energy, and infrastructure maintain decision charters. A central "governance forum" meets monthly where units surface decisions with system-wide impact. The pattern works because it's paired with a strong shared mission (safety and integrity in high-stakes industries) and because escalation isn't punitive—it's about collective learning. When a unit's decision affects another's work, peers learn early and adapt.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an AI-distributed landscape, this pattern gains urgency and complexity. When teams are augmented with AI agents making semi-autonomous decisions—routing customer calls, prioritizing resources, adjusting pricing—the need for transparent decision charters becomes critical. An AI system in one unit making choices that cascade across the system creates new alignment challenges.

The pattern evolves: charters now must document not just human decision authority but AI decision rules and their constraints. What decisions has the AI learned to make? Under what conditions does it escalate to humans? What does it *not* decide?

Monitoring becomes easier and more granular. Shared dashboards can surface decisions in near-real-time, creating faster feedback loops. A unit's AI system proposing a price change? The system can flag it immediately to peers who might be affected, rather than waiting for a monthly meeting.

But the risk sharpens: if AI systems are trained in isolation, optimizing for their local unit, they can create misalignment faster than humans ever could. An AI system in sales pushing aggressive customer acquisition while the service unit's AI is optimizing for cost-to-serve creates hidden conflict.

The pattern becomes: **Transparent AI Decision Charters**—documenting not just what humans decide, but what the organization's AI systems are authorized to decide, with clear escalation triggers when actions cross into shared domains. The rhythm of Alignment Rituals accelerates; you might monitor key decisions continuously, not quarterly.

### Section 8: Vitality

A healthy pattern shows these signs of life: Units operate decisively within their domain; decisions happen on a natural rhythm without backlog. Escalations are rare and focused (fewer than 10% of decisions), and when they happen, peers engage seriously rather than rubber-stamping. Charter documents are actually read and debated; when someone says "that's not in my authority," people know where to look. Misalignments surface in conversation, not in crisis. Leaders report feeling trusted and responsible, not second-guessed.

Signs of decay appear as: Decision backlog grows (units waiting for approval). Escalation becomes a political game (allies get waivers, rivals get blocked). Charters become outdated or ignored; people operate by intuition and precedent, not principle. Silent conflicts emerge—units discovering mid-project that they've made incompatible choices. Meetings multiply but alignment doesn't improve. A unit announces a major decision and peers say, "We had no idea."

The diagnostic question: **When a unit makes a decision that affects other parts of the organization, do peers find out in time to adapt, or in time to be frustrated?**

If peer input happens early and shapes the decision, the pattern is alive. If units find out after the fact, or if early input is ignored, the pattern is dying—and you're drifting toward either recentralization (to prevent future surprises) or fragmentation (units protecting themselves by moving even faster into isolation).
