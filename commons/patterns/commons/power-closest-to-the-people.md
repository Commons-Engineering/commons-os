### Section 1: Context

Metropolitan commons—community land trusts, housing cooperatives, water stewardship networks, food systems—face a biological constraint: human governance works best at scales where reputation effects operate, roughly 50–500 people with repeated interaction. Yet systemic challenges (speculative land cycles, watershed protection, emergency mutual aid) demand coordination across thousands. The 28 Orbit 1 principles establish that living systems are polycentric (multiple centres of authority at nested scales), that subsidiarity (decisions at smallest competent scale) reduces information loss and motivation drain, and that reciprocity (matched contributions over time) sustains cooperation. The lineage runs through Elinor Ostrom's polycentric governance research, anarchist federalism, and Indigenous multi-nation confederacies—all of which solved the paradox by refusing to choose between local autonomy and system coherence.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Local autonomy and face-to-face accountability vs. coordination capacity and systemic resilience across metropolitan scales.**

Metropolitan-scale commons collapse when forced to choose between two failure modes: *Centralization* (pulling decision-making upward for coordination) kills local motivation, enables distant power concentration, and loses the knowledge embedded in neighbourhood conditions. *Fragmentation* (keeping each local commons isolated) prevents economy-of-scale purchasing, leaves small commons vulnerable to individual targeting, and loses access to shared resources (skilled mediators, emergency funds, governance capacity). Housing commons federations struggle because expansion requires professionalization that threatens resident participation. Inter-commons coordination becomes either bureaucratic (creating a new administrative layer) or toothless (generating recommendations no one follows). The fundamental tension: *How do you preserve the face-to-face accountability and contextual knowledge of local stewardship while enabling the cross-scale resource sharing and systemic resilience that urban commons need?*

---

### Section 3: Solution

Fractal Stewardship organizes commons through explicitly nested layers—household, block, neighbourhood, district, city, bioregion—where each layer is a coherent decision-making body with three critical properties:

**1. Subsidiarity with Escalation Clarity.** Establish a decision-scale map: which decisions are made where, with explicit pathways for escalation only when outcomes affect multiple scales. Household decisions (internal resource allocation) stay local. Block-level decisions (shared tools, dispute resolution) require block consensus. Neighbourhood decisions (park stewardship, local hiring) require neighbourhood assembly. City-wide decisions (watershed protection, anti-displacement covenants) require city-scale participation. This prevents both the paralysis of "every decision communally" and the tyranny of distant bureaucrats.

**2. Reciprocal Veto and Nested Accountability.** Each scale retains veto power over decisions at adjacent scales that directly affect its commons. A neighbourhood cannot unilaterally override block governance; a city-wide commons cannot starve neighbourhood commons of resources. Simultaneously, each scale answers to nested accountability circles: local stewards to their block, block stewards to the neighbourhood, neighbourhood delegates to the city federation. Rotation of representation and mandate recall prevent delegation from calcifying into oligarchy.

**3. Federation Through Resource Mutualization.** Larger-scale commons—city-wide housing trusts, metropolitan food systems, watershed councils—exist primarily to pool resources and capacity that smaller commons cannot maintain alone: auditors, mediators, backup emergency funds, bulk purchasing power, legal defence against enclosure. These resources flow *downward* as mutualization covenant (larger commons contribute proportional support to smaller ones). Simultaneously, smaller commons contribute *upward* by delegating representatives and participating in city-scale governance assemblies. This is not redistribution imposed from above but reciprocal obligation embedded in the federation structure itself.

**4. Scale Limits and Fission Protocols.** Name the carrying capacity of each layer (research suggests face-to-face coherence breaks around 500 people; digital-augmented governance can extend to 1000–2000). When a commons approaches its scale threshold, trigger fission: spawn sub-commons or exit federations for minority groups, licensed to operate within the broader federation while maintaining solidarity covenants. This prevents both tyranny of the majority (minorities trapped in oversized assemblies) and balkanization (factions spinning into isolation).

**5. Conflict Prevention Through Scaled Mandate Clarification.** Establish a regular protocol (annual or after major decisions) for documenting and reconciling mandates across nested levels. Who decides what? Where are mandate conflicts? This surfaces contradictions early, before they metastasize into structural schism. Implement a "Nested Governance Veto Doctrine" where any scale can call for mandate clarification and any scale can invoke veto over decisions that transgress agreed boundaries.

The outcome: metropolitan commons that remain alive and just because they preserve local coherence and face-to-face accountability while enabling cross-scale solidarity. They last because they replicate the fractal structure of living systems—each scale autonomous, each scale nested within a larger whole, resilience through redundancy and reciprocal obligation rather than centralized command.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping your existing governance reality. Identify natural clusters—a neighbourhood is typically a few blocks where residents recognize each other, where a local street fair feels intimate rather than anonymous. This is your first decision layer. Document what decisions already happen there (street cleaning rosters, informal dispute resolution, skill-sharing) and what's currently orphaned (no one decides about it).

Next, establish a **Decision Ledger** for each layer. At neighbourhood scale, this might be: block safety, local green space, dispute mediation, event coordination. At district scale: waste aggregation, bulk purchasing, infrastructure maintenance, dispute escalation. The ledger isn't permanent—it evolves—but it makes visible who decides what and why. Residents need to understand the logic, not just follow rules.

Create **Escalation Protocols**, not as bureaucratic chains but as genuine thresholds. A neighbourhood dispute stays local unless it affects water rights or public safety. A district budget proposal stays local unless it requires city-wide subsidy. Write these thresholds plainly. "If your decision costs the district money or changes shared infrastructure, here's who needs to know and when."

Implement **Rotating Representation** between layers. Don't create a permanent neighbourhood board separate from residents. Instead, have 2–3 neighbourhood members sit on the district assembly for 18-month terms. They don't "report back"—they're decision-makers with accountability in both directions. This embeds local knowledge upward and brings district-scale reasoning downward.

Build **Transparency Rituals**: monthly neighbourhood assemblies (open, recorded, visible), quarterly district councils where neighbourhood reps share what's moving, annual city-wide forums where tensions surface early. These aren't consultation exercises. They're where actual power circulates visibly.

Crucially: **Start small and visible.** Don't redesign all governance at once. Pick one neighbourhood, one domain (say, green space or dispute resolution), run it for a year, document what breaks, iterate. Others will adopt patterns that work; bad patterns will be obvious quickly.

### Section 5: Consequences

When power settles at the right scale, remarkable things flourish. **Local motivation resurges**—people tend their block because they live there and face each other daily. Decisions reflect neighbourhood texture: a street market works differently in a dense immigrant neighbourhood than in a suburban precinct. Trust deepens because reputation operates: you see the person who made the decision.

Coordination becomes **credible and efficient**. When district-scale actors include neighbourhood voices, they're not imposing solutions; they're synthesizing local knowledge. Purchasing becomes cheaper (bulk buying across neighbourhoods), infrastructure maintenance becomes faster (local teams know what's broken), crisis response improves (trust networks already exist).

But fractal structures are fragile. **Local capture is a real risk**: a vocal minority, a landlord, a charismatic person can dominate a small assembly. Homogeneous neighbourhoods can exclude newcomers or enforce conformity. The answer isn't centralization but **active rotation of roles, transparent conflict processes, and diversity requirements** in representation—deliberate, not magical.

**Coordination fatigue** can emerge if escalation protocols aren't clear. If every decision potentially bubbles upward, you've created gridlock. If protocols are too rigid, local needs get crushed. This requires continuous calibration: Are we stuck? Are we ignoring effects on other layers? Adjust.

The deepest risk is **recreating informal power hierarchies** within formal structures. If the same 12 people always speak in assemblies, if one neighbourhood dominates district decisions, if district actors patronize local governance, the pattern becomes theater. Vitality depends on genuine rotational power and real willingness to be overruled by lower layers.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgeting in Porto Alegre, Brazil** (since 1989): The city invites residents to vote directly on how municipal budgets are spent, organized through neighbourhood assemblies that feed district and city-wide votes. Power genuinely rests with assemblies; major infrastructure decisions follow their priorities. It's survived 30+ years and spread globally because outcomes are visible: residents see their votes become real streets, schools, health clinics.

**Swiss Canton System**: Switzerland's federated cantons and communes represent layered subsidiarity at state scale. Communes make local decisions (land use, local tax rates, school curriculum), cantons coordinate across communes (healthcare, major transport), the federal government handles borders and cross-cantonal issues. The famous "Landsgemeinde" assemblies in some cantons—open-air, direct democracy at small scale—show subsidiarity at its most literal. Citizens know which decisions are theirs.

**Community Land Trusts (CLTs) in Harlem, New York and Oakland, California**: CLTs hold land collectively while residents own houses. Decision-making happens at neighbourhood scale through CLT boards, but financial sustainability and discrimination prevention require coordination through city networks. East Bay Community Land Trust (Oakland) explicitly structures nested decision-making: individual CLTs decide housing terms, clusters coordinate across neighbourhoods, city networks prevent price collusion. Power stays close to residents because decisions about your home happen locally; stability comes from cluster-level insurance and knowledge-sharing.

**The Manyatta Governance Structure (Maasai pastoralist communities, East Africa)**: Traditional age-set councils organize decisions at grazing-land scale (roughly 200–400 pastoral families), with escalation to clan councils and regional gatherings only when disputes affect multiple manyattas or resource access. No written bylaws, but strict reputation effects and customary protocols maintain fractal authority. Modern pressures (state overlay, climate stress) strain this structure, but it shows how subsidiarity functioned for centuries without formal bureaucracy—through repeated interaction and escalation thresholds everyone understood.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Fractal Stewardship becomes more viable and more dangerous with AI and distributed intelligence. **More viable**: digital governance tools can now make decisions transparent and updatable in real time. A city's decision ledger can be visible and queryable. Neighbourhood assemblies can run async voting, real-time impact modeling, predictive consequence mapping. A resident can ask, "If we change block parking rules, what happens to district delivery logistics?" and get an answer in minutes. Coordination becomes cheaper, faster, less dependent on charismatic coordinators.

But **the danger is outsourcing deliberation**. If AI systems aggregate neighbourhood preferences and produce "optimal" district decisions automatically, the deliberative layer—where you actually *meet* people with different views—collapses. Subsidiarity requires friction: the uncomfortable conversation where your neighbourhood learns why the district needs something different. Without that, you have algorithm-mediated preferences, not shared governance.

**Distributed intelligence also enables new forms of local capture.** Coordinated bot networks could manipulate neighbourhood assemblies. Algorithmic systems could be tuned to advantage wealthy neighbourhoods. The answer isn't to avoid tools but to **treat governance platforms as commons infrastructure**: open-source, auditable, locally controllable, with human review thresholds for consequential decisions.

The deeper shift: AI makes it possible to **scale personal reputation effects**. Blockchain, smart contracts, and verified digital identity could extend face-to-face accountability to larger, more dispersed groups. A resident doesn't know 500 other people personally, but cryptographic proof of behaviour creates new forms of trust. Fractal Stewardship at metropolitan scale (10,000+ people) might become governable for the first time.

### Section 8: Vitality

A fractal stewardship structure is alive when:

**Rotation actually happens.** Neighbourhood representatives genuinely rotate off district councils. New people learn governance. Decisions don't concentrate around the same voices. Check: Can you name who represents your neighbourhood at district level, and do you know when they're cycling off?

**Escalation protocols are invoked but rare.** If nothing ever escalates, neighbourhoods are too isolated. If everything escalates, the structure is broken. Healthy systems have visible escalation (2–3 issues per year moving up a level) with genuine impact. Check: Can you point to a recent decision that escalated upward and changed because of district-level input?

**Local decisions reflect local knowledge.** A neighbourhood makes choices you wouldn't make if you didn't live there. Density feels different. Immigrant concentration creates different needs. This isn't fragmentation—it's signal that subsidiarity is working. Check: Would an outsider, reading your neighbourhood's decisions, recognize the specific texture of your place?

**Conflict surfaces early and gets resolved locally.** Tension between neighbourhoods or layers appears in assemblies, gets aired, gets addressed. If conflicts simmer silently or explode suddenly, the system is hiding friction. Check: Can you describe the last serious disagreement between your neighbourhood and another, and how it was resolved?

**Signs of decay:**

- The same people always speak. Assemblies feel performative.
- Escalation is either nonexistent (fragmentation) or constant (gridlock).
- Decisions benefit one demographic consistently. Newcomers don't participate.
- No one can explain why a decision happened at this scale, not another.
- Coordination requires external pressure. Without city mandate, layers stop talking.

**Diagnostic question:** If your neighbourhood made a decision that harmed the broader city, would anyone know? Would the district actually override it? If the answer is no—either because isolation is complete or because power isn't truly local—the pattern is architecture without life.
