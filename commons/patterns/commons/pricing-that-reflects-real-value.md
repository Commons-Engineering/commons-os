> Set prices to show what you're actually delivering, share gains fairly with everyone involved, and explain your logic so

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Business domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Pricing in any living economy sits at the threshold between value creation and value extraction. Every exchange involves a promise: I give you currency; you give me something worth at least that much. In mature ecosystems—digital platforms, global supply chains, fractured markets—this promise grows fragile. Businesses face unprecedented visibility into customer willingness-to-pay and algorithmic power to exploit it. Simultaneously, customers face information asymmetry, behavioural manipulation, and the knowledge that competitors employ predatory tactics. The commons principle demands that pricing mechanisms be systematic, alive, shared in their logic, just in their outcomes, and built to last through trust rather than opacity.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Revenue extraction (maximizing what we take) vs. Value stewardship (ensuring price reflects worth and serves all participants justly).**

Three forces collide: (1) Real heterogeneity—customers have genuinely different willingness-to-pay, cost structures vary by context, scarcity is uneven. Static pricing leaves value on the table or excludes those who cannot pay. (2) Algorithmic capability—systems can now compute dynamic pricing at scale, segment customers microscopically, and adjust offers in real-time. This power seduces extraction: anchor prices high, charge segments what they'll bear, hide true costs, manipulate psychology. (3) The fairness crisis—when pricing logic is opaque, customers experience it as arbitrary or exploitative. Trust erodes. Competitors race to the bottom. Margins collapse into unsustainability, or prices spiral upward until entire segments are excluded. The system becomes zero-sum: my gain is visibly your loss.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Stewardship Pricing operates on three interlocked mechanics:

**1. Cost-Transparent Value Grounding**
Price begins not with "what can we extract?" but "what does fair exchange require?" Map the true cost of value delivery: materials, labour, infrastructure, legitimate margin for continuity. This is not cost-plus pricing rigidly applied; it is cost as the *anchor of honesty*. When customers see the cost structure (or a honest summary of it), they understand why the price is what it is. Opacity breeds suspicion. Transparency breeds legitimacy.

**2. Segmentation as Stewardship, Not Exploitation**
Willingness-to-pay heterogeneity is real and valid. Tiered and dynamic pricing can serve justice: premium tiers for those who value convenience, speed, or features; base tiers for those with tighter constraints. The test: does each tier offer genuine value proportional to its price? If tiering is merely a method to extract maximum surplus from those who would pay more (first-degree discrimination disguised), it corrodes fairness. If tiering ensures that those with less purchasing power still access core value while those with more can access premium features, it distributes access justly.

**3. Reciprocal Surplus Distribution**
When cooperative surplus emerges—when the business and customers jointly create value that exceeds what either could alone—that surplus should not flow entirely to one side. If margins become unsustainable, raise prices honestly and explain why. If demand surges and costs fall, share the gain: lower prices, invest in quality, expand access. This is not charity; it is the reciprocity that keeps stakeholders invested across time. A business that hoards surplus in boom cycles finds customers defect in tight times. A business that shares surplus fairly builds resilience through loyalty.

**4. Governance and Visibility**
Pricing logic should be auditable and adjustable. As conditions change (costs shift, demand evolves, competitive landscape moves), repricing should happen with visibility, not stealth. Communicate *why* prices change. Build mechanisms—governance structures, regular review cycles, stakeholder input—that ensure pricing does not drift into predatory logic simply because the algorithm permits it. Make the elasticity tests, the segment analysis, the cost structures visible to stakeholders. This is not naive; it is the foundation of trust.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin with cost mapping—not as an accounting exercise, but as an act of clarity. Gather your team: operations, customer service, sourcing. Name what genuinely goes into delivering value. Labour hours. Material waste. Infrastructure overhead. Reasonable margin for reinvestment and resilience. This isn't about publishing every detail; it's about *you* knowing the honest floor beneath your pricing.

Then make a choice about transparency. Some businesses publish cost breakdowns directly (Everlane, Patagonia). Others explain their methodology in general terms—how margin is allocated, what sustainability costs are built in. The practice is less important than the discipline: you cannot steward what you refuse to see.

Next, segment fairly. Identify genuinely different customer contexts. A student, a freelancer, a corporation—their ability to pay differs. Their value from your offering differs. Design tiered pricing that serves multiple constituencies without becoming a trap. The tier structure should be comprehensible; if customers cannot explain why they chose their level, your segmentation is too clever.

Build reciprocity into margins. Who shares in the upside when your business flourishes? Direct employees should participate—through wages that reflect profitability, profit-sharing, or stock options. Suppliers should receive terms that allow their own stability. If you're selling to other businesses, consider whether your pricing leaves them room to pay their own teams fairly.

Create feedback loops. Regularly ask customers: Does this price feel fair for what you receive? Are there people who need this but cannot afford it? What would make the exchange feel more equitable? This isn't sentiment analysis; it's active listening for erosion of trust.

Finally, audit your margins against temptation. When you have algorithmic power to surge-price, to exploit information asymmetry, to extract maximum willingness-to-pay—that's when stewardship matters most. The question is not "Can we?" but "Should we, given what we've promised?"

### Section 5: Consequences

When pricing reflects real value and is transparently grounded, trust deepens. Customers become advocates because the exchange feels fair, not because they've been duped into thinking they got a bargain. Employee retention strengthens when workers see their labour fairly valued in the price customers pay. Suppliers stabilize when margins allow them dignity. The business becomes less vulnerable to reputation shocks because the logic of pricing can withstand scrutiny.

Efficiency often improves. When you're not optimizing for extraction, you optimize for delivering exactly what the price promises. Waste decreases. Customer service becomes simpler—fewer disputes over "Why does the same product cost different amounts?" Regulatory risk shrinks; fair pricing is harder to legislate against.

But stewardship pricing carries real risks if tended poorly. If you publish costs without explaining margin, you invite endless negotiation and resentment. If your tiered pricing becomes too visible, the lower tier may feel like second-class service. If employees see the business thriving while their wages stagnate, transparency about profit becomes a wound rather than a bond.

There is also the risk of competitor capture. If your competitors use algorithmic extraction while you practice stewardship, they may temporarily undercut you or poach price-sensitive customers. This is why stewardship pricing works best when customers care about fairness enough to pay for it—and when you can articulate why that matters.

The deepest risk: stewardship can become performative. Publishing feel-good narratives about fair pricing while quietly maximizing extraction through complexity, fine print, or hidden fees. This is worse than traditional extraction; it's exploitation wrapped in virtue signaling.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Patagonia** publishes cost transparency for its clothing lines, showing materials, labour, manufacturing, and margin. Customers see that higher prices reflect actual environmental and labour standards, not brand tax. This has become a recruiting tool (employees want to work there) and a moat against competitors who cannot claim the same integrity.

**The Cooperative Group** (UK) integrates pricing stewardship into its ownership model. Margins are shared with member-owners through dividends and reinvested in community. Pricing is deliberately set below extractive competitors on essentials (food, fuel) while maintaining profitability. Customers understand they're part-owners, not just consumers.

**M-Pesa** (Kenya/East Africa) priced mobile money services at a level that covered genuine operational costs while remaining accessible to unbanked populations with minimal income. Rather than surge-pricing during peak demand, they maintained flat rates, knowing that financial inclusion—not extraction—was their real value proposition. This made them trusted across income levels.

**Grameen Bank** (Bangladesh) pioneered lending at rates transparent enough that borrowers understood the actual cost of capital, staff, and risk. Prices reflected real expenses rather than maximum-extractable interest rates. This transparency enabled borrowers to see lending as a partnership, not predation, transforming how people relate to debt and economic participation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence change the *temptation surface* of pricing. Real-time data on competitor pricing, customer behaviour, willingness-to-pay signals, and demand elasticity now flow continuously into algorithmic systems. The power to personalize pricing—to charge each customer the maximum they'll bear—is no longer theoretical.

This makes stewardship pricing both more necessary and more difficult. Necessary, because the power to extract is now so granular that trust becomes the only real constraint. Difficult, because the cognitive overhead of resisting that power grows. Algorithms naturally optimize for revenue; human values don't compute easily.

But AI also enables new forms of stewardship. Transparent cost analysis at scale becomes possible. You can model fair pricing across thousands of contexts—geographic variance, seasonal scarcity, customer circumstances—with less manual labour. You can test whether pricing changes erode trust by monitoring language signals, retention, and advocacy metrics.

The real shift: pricing becomes a conversation with AI, not a monologue by humans. If stewardship pricing is programmed as a constraint—"This algorithm will not charge more than X% above cost, will not exploit surge moments, will explain its logic"—then AI becomes a tool for consistency rather than a mechanism for hidden extraction. The cognitive task moves from "How do we price fairly?" to "How do we keep our pricing system honest as it scales?"

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Customers can articulate why they pay your price without resort to emotional justification ("I like the brand"). Employees see themselves as stewards, not just workers, because they understand the connection between fair pricing and their own security. Suppliers contact you proactively for renewals rather than requiring repeated negotiation. Your margins are stable and adequate without requiring constant extraction tactics. When you publish pricing information, customers trust it rather than hunt for hidden costs. Criticism of your pricing tends to focus on accessibility, not fairness.

**Signs of decay:** Price disputes become common; customers feel nickeled-and-dimed. Employees express cynicism about published values ("They say they're fair, but..."). Suppliers churn; relationships feel transactional. You find yourself increasingly dependent on algorithmic surge-pricing or microtargeting to maintain margins. Customer service load increases around billing questions and price justification. Public scrutiny of your pricing triggers crisis rather than pride. You begin explaining pricing in ways you wouldn't want employees to hear.

**Diagnostic question:** If you removed all opacity from your pricing—showed every customer your cost model, your margin allocation, your reasoning—would they feel the exchange is fair, or would they feel betrayed? If the honest answer is betrayal, you have an extraction problem masquerading as a business model. If the answer is fair, you're tending something worth protecting.
