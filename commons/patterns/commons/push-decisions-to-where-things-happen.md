### Section 1: Context

Organisations often grow by centralizing decision authority — founder decides, then hires managers who decide for teams, creating predictable but brittle hierarchies. As complexity increases, this model fails: decisions that require local knowledge get made far away, talented people become passive, and burnout concentrates at the centre. Yet decentralisation without structure produces chaos — no alignment, competing initiatives, duplicated work. The Commons recognises this as a false choice. Living systems — forests, immune systems, healthy communities — make decisions at multiple scales simultaneously. Authority is distributed not by abandoning structure but by clarifying it: which decisions require collective wisdom, which need expert input, which can move fast at the edge. This pattern is rooted in subsidiarity (decide at the lowest competent level), polycentricity (nested centres of authority), and feedback loops (each decision strengthens local capacity).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Coherence vs. Autonomy — how to maintain strategic alignment and accountability while pushing decision authority to the edges where information is richest and speed is needed..**

Three tensions arise when centralizing authority: First, bottlenecks — every decision waits for the person with formal power, slowing adaptation and blocking others from acting on what they know. Second, capacity erosion — centralised decision-makers become the bottleneck AND the only ones developing decision-making skill; others remain passive and dependent. Third, legitimacy fragility — decisions made distant from impact lack the local credibility needed for genuine commitment; compliance follows rules, not shared conviction. Conversely, distributing authority without clarity creates chaos: people act without knowing if they have permission, duplicating efforts or stepping on others' work. Teams become fragmented, unable to coordinate. The real problem is neither centralisation nor distribution alone, but *invisible* authority — ambiguity about who decides what, when, and how. This generates two pathologies: either decision paralysis (no one is certain they have the right to decide) or silent power accumulation (authority concentrates despite rhetoric of distribution).

### Section 3: Solution

Distribute Decision Authority through four interlocking designs:

**1. Decision Type Mapping.** Classify each class of decision by its nature: Strategic alignment (requires collective wisdom — decide together), Implementation expertise (requires competent judgment — consult widely, decide with expert input), Local impact (affects those closest — decide with those affected), Escalation (threatens core values or creates dependencies — escalate to next governance level). Map each decision type to its appropriate authority structure. This is not bureaucracy; it's clarity.

**2. Authority Boundaries Protocol.** For each decision type, define: Who can decide (individual, team, full circle)? What information must they gather? What escalation triggers apply (threshold of impact, cost, reversibility, conflict)? How is reversibility handled — can this decision be unmade? Write these boundaries explicitly; make them visible and revisable. Individuals gain autonomy within clear edges; the system gains coherence.

**3. Competence Maturation Cycle.** Treat delegation as skill-building, not task-offloading. When authority is distributed, build capacity alongside it: mentoring, peer feedback, gradual complexity increase, reflection cycles. New decision-makers start with lower-stakes choices and expand into higher-risk domains as competence grows. This transforms delegation from "dump work lower" to "cultivate capability throughout the system."

**4. Asynchronous & Synchronous Decision Rhythms.** Most decisions don't need real-time debate. Use asynchronous processes (decision documents, comment windows, consent-based silence) to move fast without excluding voices. Reserve synchronous time for decisions requiring creative conflict, rapid response to crisis, or decisions where presence itself convenes authority (full circle gathering). This prevents both bottlenecks and invisibility.

**Feedback Loop.** After each decision class is distributed, track: Did it reach those closest to impact? Did it speed up iteration? Did new leaders emerge? Did people learn? Use this feedback to refine authority boundaries and adjust the next cycle. The system grows more intelligent with use.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping decisions together. Gather people across your organisation and ask: What decisions do we make regularly? What happens when we decide this way? A decision map isn't a document to file — it's a conversation that builds shared understanding of *why* certain decisions need different processes.

Then, write decision rules clearly enough that someone new can follow them. Not policy documents. Rules. "Budget under £5k: team lead decides with finance input. Budget £5k–£50k: funding committee consults affected teams. Budget over £50k: board approval required." The clarity lets people act without constantly asking permission.

Create feedback loops that matter. When someone at the edge makes a decision, ensure they learn what happened next — not as judgment, but as signal. Did the choice work? What surprised them? What would they decide differently? This is how local decision-makers develop judgment instead of just executing.

Protect the escalation path. Make it genuinely easy to escalate when something feels larger than the decision type suggests. If someone pushes a local decision upward, treat it as valuable caution, not failure. The opposite — making escalation feel risky or shameful — creates hidden risks.

Build in review cycles. Every six months, look at where decisions actually cluster. Are there bottlenecks people have learned to work around? Are certain types of decisions consistently being escalated when they don't need to be? Adjust the map together based on what you learn.

Start small. Pick one domain — hiring, project scoping, resource allocation — and push authority there first. Let people feel what autonomy actually means before redesigning everything.

### Section 5: Consequences

When decision authority moves to where work actually happens, several things flourish. Speed increases immediately — actions that would wait weeks now happen in days. Local knowledge gets used instead of ignored. People develop judgment through repeated small decisions, becoming capable leaders. Accountability clarifies: you decided, you own the outcome. And morale often deepens because people feel trusted.

But consequences cut both ways. Inconsistency emerges — different teams may decide the same type of problem differently, creating friction at boundaries. Some people weaponise local autonomy, using it to protect turf instead of serve the work. Weak decision-makers at the edge can damage things before anyone notices.

The pattern also reveals which leaders were genuinely leading and which were just controlling. People who led through formal authority sometimes struggle when they lose it. And in organisations with low trust, pushing decisions down creates anxiety — people worry they'll be blamed for mistakes they didn't cause.

There's also the risk of decision fatigue at the edges. If you push *all* decisions down without also removing unnecessary ones, you simply spread exhaustion instead of bottleneck.

The deepest risk: without shared principles about what you're deciding *for*, local decisions diverge until they work against each other. A team might decide fast in ways that undermine another team's work. Autonomy without alignment becomes fragmentation.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Buurtzorg (Netherlands, healthcare).** This home-care cooperative dissolved the traditional management hierarchy. Teams of 10–12 nurses manage their own rosters, client assignments, and daily decisions. Regional coaches provide support and input on strategic questions, but decisions about how to serve clients stay with the nurses who know them. The model spread across Europe because it produced better care outcomes and lower burnout than traditional agencies — nurses made decisions based on what clients actually needed, not what a distant scheduler thought was efficient.

**Mondragon Corporation (Spain, manufacturing cooperatives).** Each cooperative operates with significant autonomy over production, hiring, and local investment decisions. Strategic direction — market positioning, technology investment — stays collective. But decisions about how to implement that strategy move to the shop floor. This created responsiveness in manufacturing that competitors couldn't match, and deepened worker commitment because people shaped their own work conditions.

**Ubuntu Ubuntu (South Africa, community organising).** This grassroots movement pushes resource and campaign decisions to neighbourhood cells. Central coordination sets broad strategy and handles funding negotiations with larger institutions. But which campaign to run, how to resource it, who leads it — that stays local. The pattern created resilience: when state repression targeted central leadership, the work continued because decisions were distributed.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As distributed intelligence systems become normal — some decisions will be made by algorithms, some by humans, some collaboratively — this pattern becomes more essential and more complex.

AI can handle decision type mapping better than humans: it can track patterns in decisions, flag when a class of decision is consistently being escalated, suggest where authority might move. But this creates a new risk: letting systems tell us where to push decisions without the human conversation that builds legitimacy.

Distributed teams with AI assistance can make faster, more local decisions — but they'll need new norms for when to consult each other and when to trust their local knowledge. The pattern's emphasis on feedback loops becomes critical: how does a distributed team learn from its decisions when some decision-making happens in real time with AI support?

There's also an opportunity: as information becomes more accessible to more people (through AI systems that synthesise complex data), the bottleneck of expertise moves. Decisions that required a few experts to understand can now be understood by many. This allows genuine democratisation of certain decision types that were previously locked behind specialised knowledge.

But it also creates risk: if everyone has access to the same AI-processed information, the illusion of democratised decision-making might mask that all those people are actually following algorithmic suggestions. Real distribution of authority requires that people can still *disagree* about what the information means — and systems need to be designed to preserve that space.

### Section 8: Vitality

A healthy distributed decision system shows certain signs. Decisions get made close to where information is richest — you rarely hear "we're waiting to ask headquarters." People make mistakes, learn from them, and get better at deciding. New leaders emerge at the edges because people get practice deciding. The conversation stays lively: people respectfully disagree about what decision type something is, and that disagreement is seen as thoughtful rather than insubordinate.

When the pattern decays, you see different symptoms. Decisions cluster in whispered conversations before or after formal meetings — people have learned that formal channels don't actually have authority. People decide individually in ways that contradict each other, fragmenting the work. Or the opposite: decisions get pushed up anyway, slowly, because people have learned that formal authority doesn't trust their judgment. Authority and actual power diverge.

The most telling sign of decay: people stop escalating. When escalation feels risky, people either hide their uncertainty (decisions made in false confidence) or they stop trying to decide at all (passive waiting for someone else to choose).

**A diagnostic question:** When someone makes a decision at the edge that affects others, do people learn what happened? Can you trace the decision backwards to its maker and forwards to its consequences? If that connection is missing, the pattern is hollowing out.
