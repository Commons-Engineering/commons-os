### Section 1: Context

In functioning commons across Africa, South Asia, and the Global South—compound settlements, markets, territorial collectives—dispute resolution does not wait for state courts. Instead, conflicts are adjudicated through nested circles of trusted authority: household elders, lineage councils, community paladins, customary tribunals, ritual specialists. This is not informal *absence* of law; it is the presence of plural legal systems with their own coherence, precedent, and legitimacy. The Orbit 1 foundation here is Polycentric Organisation (multiple scales of authority), Subsidiarity (decisions made closest to the conflict), and Conflict Resolution (systems that treat conflict as generative, not pathological). The commons persists through these mechanisms precisely because they are alive—adapting to new circumstances while anchoring in inherited wisdom—and shared, distributing authority across multiple guardians rather than concentrating it in a single court or bureaucracy.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Accessibility & cultural legitimacy (local adjudication) vs. fairness & appeal (higher authority).**

When informal dispute resolution exists but is invisible to formal institutions, three pathologies emerge: First, dual-track injustice—powerful kin may override weaker members in household councils, while state courts remain inaccessible or untrustworthy, leaving no true recourse. Second, institutional fragility—if the elder adjudicator dies or loses authority, the whole system fractures; decisions are oral and scattered, not preserved as precedent. Third, jurisdictional collision—when customary law, state law, religious law, and informal rules all claim authority over the same dispute, disputants face forum-shopping, contradictory outcomes, and loss of legitimacy in all quarters. The core tension: local adjudication is culturally legitimate, fast, and relationally restorative—but without formal escalation, appeal, and memory-keeping, it becomes opaque and subject to domination. Conversely, state courts offer appeal and written record—but they are slow, alien, expensive, and inaccessible to the poor, draining legitimacy from local institutions.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Formalize and visualize the tiered stewardship system already operating, by doing three things:

**1. Codify the Escalation Hierarchy with Defined Handoff Criteria.** Establish explicit thresholds for when disputes move from one tier to the next: household negotiation → lineage council (if kinship unresolved within 7 days) → community palaver (if lineage deadlocked within 14 days) → customary tribunal (if community mediation fails within 21 days) → state court (if customary tribunal decision is rejected by one party). Each tier names the authority (which elders, which council, which ritual keeper), the evidence standard (witness testimony, boundary markers, precedent cases, oath-taking), the decision-making process (majority vote, consensus, deliberation circles), and the appeal mechanism (remand to lower tier with new mediator, or escalation to higher). This makes the system legible without formalizing it into rigid bureaucracy.

**2. Cultivate Living Precedent Memory.** Every dispute resolution, at every tier, is documented in a commons registry—not as abstract case law, but as a narrative of the conflict, the witnesses, the reasoning, and the outcome. Elders are designated as memory-keepers; the registry is oral and written, held by the community. When new disputes arise, mediators consult precedent cases not to apply rigid rules but to understand how similar tensions were handled before, what worked, what restored relationship. Precedent becomes the commons' collective learning system, adapting over time as new circumstances demand interpretation.

**3. Establish Multi-Tier Legitimacy and Pluralism.** Recognize that customary law, state law, religious law, and commons custom may all be operating simultaneously; rather than forcing hierarchy (which one is supreme?), create a negotiated-boundary system: define clearly which disputes are customary (land, inheritance, kinship), which are religious (marriage, dietary practice), which are statutory (commerce, registration), which are commons-specific (resource use, access). When disputes span multiple jurisdictions, create hybrid panels (elder + state mediator + religious authority) that render decisions respected across all four systems. Document this pluralism openly so disputants know which authority governs.

The result is a commons with distributed stewardship of justice: multiple centres of authority; escalation only when needed; decisions embedded in community memory and relationship; and appeal pathways that preserve legitimacy because they honour the hierarchy of trust that the commons already lives by.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping the existing informal system—not to replace it, but to make it visible and accountable. Work with community elders, matrons, and respected figures to document *how* they currently resolve disputes: what kinds of cases they handle, what criteria guide their decisions, how long resolution typically takes, and what happens when someone disagrees with an outcome.

Next, co-design the escalation thresholds with these stewards themselves. Don't impose timelines from outside; listen to what feels natural and sustainable within the community's rhythm. A lineage council might need two weeks to gather; a market palaver might resolve in three days. The goal is clarity, not speed.

Create simple, visible documentation—not dense legal code, but community-accessible records. This might be a wall chart in the chief's compound, a register kept by a trusted scribe, or a digital ledger managed by a community secretary. Each tier records: who heard the case, what the dispute concerned, how it was resolved, and whether either party escalated. This creates both transparency and institutional memory.

Establish clear handoff criteria. When does a household dispute move to the lineage council? Perhaps when negotiation hasn't resolved it within an agreed timeframe, or when it touches property or honor beyond one household's scope. When does a community palaver move to customary tribunal? When it involves multiple communities, or when the palaver itself deadlocked.

Build in *qualified appeal*—not endless reopening, but a structured way to escalate if someone believes the decision violated the community's own norms. This might mean the next tier reviews not whether they agree, but whether the lower tier followed its own stated process.

Finally, establish a clear bridge to formal courts: a named official, a simple written statement, a pathway that doesn't humiliate those who choose it. Some disputes—those involving state law violations, or where community processes have demonstrably failed—must be able to reach magistrate courts without starting over.

### Section 5: Consequences

When the system works, several things flourish: Disputes resolve faster, cheaper, and with parties still living in the same neighborhood afterward. People trust the process because they know the adjudicator, because the decision is explained in local terms, and because escalation is possible if they're wronged. The community's elders regain formal standing—their authority is acknowledged rather than erased by state absence. And informal knowledge—about family history, land use patterns, contractual custom—becomes usable evidence rather than invisible context.

Institutional memory improves. When decisions are recorded, new elders can learn from how past cases were handled. The system becomes more consistent and harder for one person's whim to distort.

But risks emerge if the system is not tended. First: *elite capture*. Without transparency and appeal mechanisms, powerful families can weaponize community tribunals to silence dissent or grab land. Women, youth, and minorities may find themselves systematically overruled by patriarchal councils. Second: *stagnation*. If the codified process becomes rigid and the recorded decisions are treated as precedent rather than context, the system loses its adaptive flexibility. Third: *abandonment*. If formal courts remain more accessible for those with resources, the community tier becomes a poor person's justice—fast and cheap precisely because it lacks due process safeguards.

The bridge to formal courts must be real. If people fear that appealing to a magistrate will anger their community and isolate them, the formal system becomes theoretically available but practically unusable.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Uganda's Local Council Courts**: Embedded within the Local Council system, these neighborhood tribunals handle property disputes, family matters, and minor contracts across rural and peri-urban areas. Cases are heard by elected councils with defined appeal to magistrate courts. Over two decades, they've resolved thousands of cases—often for under $5 in fees—while maintaining cultural legitimacy. The system weakens, however, when political pressure turns councils into tools of local power brokers rather than neutral arbiters.

**India's Gram Nyayalayas (Village Justice Courts)**: Formally recognized under the Legal Services Authorities Act, these courts operate in rural areas with judges trained in both state law and local custom. They handle civil disputes up to a certain monetary limit and report their decisions to district courts, creating accountability. They've succeeded where community trust in the adjudicator runs deep; they've failed where they're seen as state impositions.

**Somali Xeer Assemblies**: In the absence of functioning state courts for over three decades, Somali pastoral communities have maintained customary justice through neighborhood xeer (covenant) assemblies. Elders hear disputes about grazing, livestock, and honor, with layered appeal to regional councils. The system has proven surprisingly durable and inclusive—younger voices increasingly participate, and women's councils address matters of domestic violence. Yet without any formal bridge to documentation or appeal beyond the customary system, those seeking different adjudication have no recourse except violence or flight.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As neighborhoods become visible through mobile platforms and neighborhood data systems, Community-Led Dispute Resolution enters new territory. One possibility: *transparent escalation*. A simple app—used by the community secretary or elder—logs disputes, timelines, and outcomes. This same log becomes visible to formal courts and oversight bodies, creating accountability without bureaucracy. Community adjudicators gain data on their own patterns: Are certain groups overrepresented as complainants? Do certain types of cases systematically fail at the community level?

Another shift: *distributed expertise*. AI tools can help identify relevant precedent—not by imposing state law, but by surfacing similar cases resolved within the community's own custom. An elder adjudicating a land dispute might query: "What did we do when this family disputed boundaries ten years ago?" Institutional memory becomes searchable rather than trapped in one person's recollection.

The risk is seduction by formalization. Community dispute resolution thrives on relationship, narrative, and contextual judgment—precisely what algorithmic systems resist. If the process becomes too documented and standardized, it loses the flexibility that makes it culturally legitimate.

The most vital use may be *integration with community intelligence networks*. Disputes don't exist in isolation; they emerge from and affect neighborhood fabric. If dispute resolution connects to community safety systems, land registries, and livelihood networks, it becomes part of a larger ecosystem of care. The system that only adjudicates, without attending to root causes or prevention, will always be reactive.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Disputes resolve within stated timeframes; both parties accept outcomes even when disappointed
- New elders are trained in the process; it's not dependent on a single person's memory
- The escalation pathway is used—sometimes, but not constantly—showing it's available but not the default
- Community members consult the recorded decisions when facing new conflicts
- Women, youth, and minorities appeal when dissatisfied, and those appeals are heard fairly
- The formal court bridge is transparent; people know when and how to use it

**Signs of decay:**
- Decisions are seen as arbitrary, driven by relationship rather than principle
- People bypass the community system for expensive formal courts because they don't trust local adjudicators
- Appeal mechanisms exist on paper but are never used; people perceive appeal as fruitless
- The same family or faction consistently wins disputes; others disengage
- New conflicts aren't resolved; old decisions are forgotten, forcing re-litigation
- Women's and minority voices are systematically absent from adjudicating councils
- Formal courts and community systems actively undermine each other rather than collaborate

**A diagnostic question:** If a young person from a marginalized group lost a dispute in your community's tribunal, and they asked, "Can I go to the state court if I think this was unfair?"—would the answer be genuinely yes, or would saying yes cost them their place in the community?
