### Section 1: Context

Every cooperative system faces a recurring paradox: centralized authority can respond quickly but misses what frontline stewards know; distributed authority captures local knowledge but creates coordination gaps and decision drift. Traditional hierarchies collapse under complexity. Pure consensus consumes energy and suppresses urgency. This pattern draws from Ostrom's polycentric governance research, federal systems design, Quaker meeting practice, and the emerging experience of decentralized organisations. It recognises that decision authority is not a scarce resource to be hoarded but a living capacity that must be cultivated at multiple scales. The choice is not *whether* to distribute authority but *how*—with what clarity, at what scale, and with what rituals to hold it accountable.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Concentration of authority (creates bottlenecks, ignores local knowledge) vs. Diffusion of authority (creates chaos, prevents coordinated action).**

Organisations operating in stable environments could afford centralised decision-making; information moved slowly enough that hierarchy could gather it, process it, and broadcast response. That world has ended. Markets shift in days. Regulatory landscapes rewire overnight. Customer expectations evolve in weeks. Yet most organisations have not redesigned their nervous systems. Either they cling to centralised authority (creating bottlenecks where decisions pile up, waiting for distant approvers who lack context), or they swing to the opposite extreme—distributed autonomy with no coherence (teams make contradictory choices, duplicating effort, working at cross-purposes). Both fail the commons: the first because it ignores the knowledge and motivation of stewards; the second because it ignores the need for coordinated action. The deeper problem is invisibility: people do not know who decides what, why past decisions were made, or how to challenge or evolve them.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement a three-part governance structure: (1) **Authority Mapping**, (2) **Decision Rotation**, and (3) **Reasoning Documentation**.

**Authority Mapping** begins by explicitly defining decision authority by category and scale. Not all decisions are equal. Routine operational choices (which shift pattern to use today, which tool to deploy) live at the team level. Decisions affecting resource allocation or boundary conditions (who joins the stewardship circle, how much to invest in this initiative) live one level up. Decisions about fundamental purpose or values (what is this commons for, who does it serve) are held at the centre, but with mandatory consultation. The key is subsidiarity: decisions push *down* to the lowest competent level unless coordination requires elevation. This is not delegation from a centre; it is structural assumption of authority at the scale where it belongs. Map these decisions explicitly and make the map visible. When someone asks "who decides X?", the answer is documented.

**Decision Rotation** means that the power to decide specific categories shifts among stewards on a set cycle—quarterly, annually, or aligned to natural seasons of the work. The person or circle who decided budget last season does not decide it this season. This prevents authority from calcifying into a hierarchy and forces each steward to learn how others perceive the commons. Rotation also trains reserve capacity: if one steward becomes unavailable, others are already practiced at that decision.

**Reasoning Documentation** captures the *why* behind each significant decision—not just the what. When a steward decides to add a new partner, invest in a new tool, or change a governance norm, they record: What was the problem? What alternatives were considered? Why this choice? What assumption might prove wrong? This serves multiple functions: it makes implicit logic visible (enabling others to challenge it); it becomes a teaching text for whoever inherits the decision next cycle; it builds institutional memory so future stewards do not re-litigate settled questions. Document in prose, not jargon—the text should be readable to a steward five years hence who was not present.

The system works because it balances three forces: (a) Authority lives where knowledge is densest, so decisions are informed and responsive. (b) Rotation prevents authority from hardening into status, keeping the commons alive and adaptive. (c) Documentation creates continuity and accountability, so decisions are not arbitrary but reasoned, and reasoning is open to evolution. This is not consensus (which can paralyse) and not diktat (which crushes initiative). It is stewardship at scale.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with a single decision category—not everything at once. Choose one that currently creates bottlenecks: hiring decisions, product roadmap prioritization, budget allocation, customer escalation handling. Whatever your organization complains about most.

**First act: Map the knowledge.** Gather the people who actually *know* what needs deciding. Not the org chart. The real knowledge holders. Ask them: What information do you have that decision-makers upstream don't? What mistakes do you see repeated because decisions happen far from reality? Write these down. This becomes your authority map—not a policy, but a visible artifact showing where knowledge lives.

**Second act: Define the decision frame.** What makes this decision "good"? Speed? Quality? Fairness? Learning? Write three to five criteria. Be honest about tradeoffs. A fast hiring decision might sacrifice diversity; a consensus-built one might lose market timing. Naming this prevents the myth that one way serves all values.

**Third act: Designate the first decision-maker.** Give them explicit authority for the next 30 days. Not forever. Not "you're now the hiring person." You're the hiring decider *this cycle*. Tell them: "You have the authority. You must write down your reasoning before you decide. You must include perspectives from people affected." This isn't about trusting one person; it's about making one person accountable for gathering input, deciding transparently, and explaining themselves.

**Fourth act: Rotate the role.** After 30 days (or quarterly, depending on decision frequency), someone else becomes the decider. Before they step in, the previous decider writes a brief: what they learned, what surprised them, what patterns they noticed. This is not handoff documentation; it's organizational memory.

**Fifth act: Document the reasoning, not just the decision.** Create a simple template: What information did you gather? Who did you consult? What tradeoff did you choose and why? What would change your mind next time? Keep these visible. Over time, you'll see decision patterns emerge. You'll notice where the knowledge gaps actually are.

The tone matters: This is experimentation, not perfection. Decisions will sometimes be wrong. That's data, not failure.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Organizations operating at this pattern develop organizational antibodies. Frontline teams stop waiting for permission and start building muscle memory for deciding. Decision-making gets faster because it doesn't bottleneck at one desk. The organization learns faster because reasoning is visible—you can see *why* decisions were made, spot the assumptions, and adjust next time. People in rotating roles gain empathy for other perspectives; the person who decides on hiring learns why operations struggles with the candidates chosen. Decisions improve because they're made closer to the information.

**What risks emerge:** Decision quality can initially dip as new decision-makers stumble. You might see inconsistency—two hiring cycles make different calls because the decision criteria weren't clear enough. Coordination gaps can appear if decisions aren't written down; other teams don't know why something happened and make decisions that conflict. Rotation itself takes energy; people need onboarding, and some decisions are slower the first time a new person owns them. And there's a deeper risk: if reasoning documentation becomes performative (people write what they think leaders want to hear rather than actual thinking), you've just created slower, less honest centralization.

The pattern only works if the organization genuinely trusts distributed judgment. If leaders micro-review every decision in the documentation, you've signaled that rotation is theater. People sense this and stop being truthful.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Mondragon Cooperatives** (Spain) have operated over 80 years using nested decision authority. Production teams decide scheduling and work methods; elected councils decide capital allocation; general assembly decides strategy. Decisions rotate through member governance. This allows them to adapt quickly to market shifts (a team can respond to a custom order in hours) while maintaining coherence through explicit reasoning documented in council minutes. Their resilience through Spanish recessions shows the pattern's durability.

**Buurtzorg** (Netherlands), a home-care cooperative, pushed medical and scheduling decisions to 10-person neighborhood teams. Nurses decide patient care protocols; coordinators decide which cases their team takes. Central office documents the reasoning behind systemic decisions. This produced lower costs and higher patient satisfaction than hierarchical competitors—because the people closest to patients weren't waiting for authorization to solve problems.

**The Haudenosaunee Confederacy** (North America) operated for centuries with decision authority distributed across nations and clans, with rotation of speakers and explicit consensus-seeking ceremonies that documented reasoning in wampum belts and oral record. Different nations held authority over different domains (agriculture, defense, trade). Decisions affecting all required visible deliberation. This system scaled across geography and time in ways European hierarchies of the same era could not.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed authority becomes more viable and more necessary in the age of AI and rapid context-shifting. AI systems can now handle the coordination overhead that once made centralization necessary—flagging conflicts between decisions, surfacing reasoning patterns, alerting when assumptions in one domain contradict another. This is the technology's gift to polycentric governance.

But it also creates new risks. Decision-makers can now outsource reasoning to AI, creating the appearance of documented thinking while actually deferring to algorithmic judgment. The pattern requires that *humans* write the reasoning, not systems. The rotation principle becomes more important: a human who has decided, failed, and learned can evaluate whether an AI's recommendation makes sense in context. A human who has never decided is easily fooled by confidence-scored outputs.

The pattern also shifts how we think about knowledge. In the cognitive era, the knowledge that matters most lives in synthesis—connecting dots between domains, noticing when one team's decision creates problems elsewhere. This favors rotating decision-makers who can cross-pollinate understanding, and it favors reasoning documentation that's searchable and connectable. Organizations using this pattern well will develop decision graphs—maps of how decisions in hiring affected operations, how operations decisions created customer issues—visible to everyone.

The real power emerges when rotation happens *across* functions, not just within them. Marketing decides a customer campaign; operations understands why marketing chose it; finance sees the cost implications. Each role brings their knowledge to bear, not as reviewers but as decision-makers in rotation.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People outside the formal decision structure volunteer information and perspective to whoever is currently deciding, because they've seen reasoning get written down and acted on. Decision-makers refer back to previous cycles' documented reasoning, not to authority. ("Last quarter, we prioritized speed over diversity—let's see if that cost us.") New decision-makers ask good questions on day one because they've read the previous person's reasoning. Conflicts surface as clarifications to the decision frame, not as threats. The organization can point to decisions that *were* wrong and changed because the reasoning was visible. Rotation happens on schedule; people step in and out without drama.

**Signs of decay:** Reasoning documentation becomes checkbox work—vague justifications written after the fact to satisfy a process. Decision-makers start getting second-guessed frequently; trust erodes. Rotation gets skipped because "this person is too good at it" or "we need continuity." People stop volunteering perspective because they don't see it matter. Decisions start cycling—the same issue gets decided differently every rotation because reasoning isn't building on itself. Leaders begin reviewing reasoning documents and signaling disapproval, turning documentation into a surveillance tool.

**Diagnostic question:** If you handed the decision authority for something currently handled by your senior leader to a junior person with relevant knowledge, what would your organization do? Would it feel safe? If not, your system isn't ready for distributed authority—the knowledge, trust, or documentation infrastructure isn't there yet. If it would feel fine, the pattern is taking hold.
