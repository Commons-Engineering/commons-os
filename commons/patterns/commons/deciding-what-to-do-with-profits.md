> How to share earnings with stakeholders while keeping enough to grow, staying honest about what your business can actual

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Business domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

Every living system accumulates surplus—energy or resources beyond immediate survival needs. In mature enterprises, this surplus manifests as profit. The question of what happens to it is not technical; it is deeply generative. In traditional shareholder capitalism, surplus is extracted as quickly as possible. In cooperatives and stewardship-oriented firms, surplus is often hoarded, starving operations of needed investment. The commons-oriented enterprise faces a third path: deliberate circulation. This pattern draws from Orbit 1 principles of conservation (nothing is created from nothing; balance inputs and outputs), carrying capacity (every system has thresholds), feedback cycles (observe outcomes, adapt), and reciprocity (contributions matched over time). The lineage includes dividend aristocrat strategies, defined contribution frameworks, countercyclical investing, and emergency fund adequacy—all expressions of a single underlying design question: How do we steward surplus so the organism lives, not merely survives?

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Extraction (maximizing near-term distribution) vs. Regeneration (reinvesting for long-term vitality).**

The extraction-regeneration tension is real and sharpens as enterprises scale. Stakeholders—owners, workers, suppliers, communities—each hold legitimate claims on surplus. Distribute too much, and the organism starves: infrastructure decays, people leave, resilience erodes, the next crisis breaks the system. Retain too much, and stakeholders lose trust, disengage, or exit; fresh capital and energy stop flowing in. Most enterprises collapse this tension into a false binary: maximize shareholder returns OR fund growth. They miss the rhythm. Markets and operations cycle. Demand rises and falls. Seasons shift. A business that distributes in lockstep with earnings spikes and crashes, depleting reserves when conditions demand the opposite. A business that never distributes signals hoarding, triggering exit. The deeper problem: most distribution frameworks inherit outdated assumptions—linear growth, stable markets, concentrated ownership. They ignore feedback, ignore carrying capacity, ignore time horizons, and ignore fairness in who gets what.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Surplus Circulation is a pattern of deliberate, rhythmic distribution calibrated to three design parameters: carrying capacity, feedback cycles, and time horizons.

**Carrying Capacity**: Before deciding how much to distribute, diagnose what the organism actually needs to regenerate. This includes maintenance capex (roof, equipment, systems that generated the profit), growth capex (new capacity proportionate to market demand and competitive risk), liquidity buffers (emergency reserves sized to carrying capacity stress—supply chain disruption, personnel loss, regulatory shift), and stakeholder reinvestment (people development, supplier relationships, ecosystem health). Only surplus beyond these needs is available for distribution. This shifts the conversation from "How much can shareholders extract?" to "How much can we circulate while the organism remains alive?"

**Feedback Cycles**: Distribution decisions should be time-sliced and observable. Rather than locking in a fixed dividend payout ratio, establish a distribution rhythm (quarterly, annual) coupled to clear feedback signals: Are reserves adequate? Are operations underinvested? Is stakeholder commitment waning? Is the market signaling growth opportunity or contraction? Countercyclical logic: in boom years, increase reserves and selective reinvestment; in troughs, sustain baseline distributions to hold stakeholders steady. This treats distribution as adaptive, not mechanical.

**Time Horizons**: Different stakeholders hold different time horizons. Owners seeking liquidity may prefer dividends; workers care about sustained employment and capability investment; communities care about long-term presence. A commons-oriented surplus circulation acknowledges these horizons explicitly: define what portion flows to immediate stakeholders (dividend, bonus, wage increases), what portion regenerates the organism (capex, R&D, people development), what portion builds reserves for multi-year resilience, and what portion seeds the broader ecosystem (supplier relationships, community investment, knowledge commons). Make these allocations visible and reviewable.

**Governance**: Establish a stewardship council or board function explicitly tasked with surplus circulation—diagnosing carrying capacity, reading feedback, adjusting rhythms. This is not a cost centre; it is the metabolic nerve system. Document the logic; make it transparent. When stakeholders understand why distribution takes a particular shape, trust deepens.

The pattern resolves the tension by rejecting the false binary. Circulation is neither extraction nor hoarding. It is the rhythm that keeps a living system alive.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by making your regeneration budget visible—not hidden in accounting. Gather your leadership, your workers, your board, and ask: *What does this organism need to stay alive and adapt?* List it honestly. Maintenance of core assets. Reserve for seasonal dips or market shocks. Investment in the capabilities that made this profit possible (training, R&D, infrastructure). This is not ideology; it is ecology. A forest that harvests all its timber dies.

Then name your distribution tiers. First, reinvestment: the non-negotiable portion that flows back into the system's capacity. This might be 40, 50, even 60 percent, depending on your sector and maturity. Next, stakeholder distributions: wages, bonuses, supplier payments, community commitments. Make these explicit and tied to agreed principles, not whim. Finally, returns to ownership—whatever is genuinely surplus after regeneration and fair distribution.

The cultivation happens in the rhythm. Don't decide once and forget. Create an annual or quarterly review cycle where stakeholders (not just executives) examine what actually happened: Did the reinvestment land where we said? Did workers' bonuses reflect the principle we named? Did the community investment show up? This feedback tightens the system.

Use transparent metrics. Share the profit number. Share the regeneration calculations. Let people see where the money goes. This builds trust and surfaces blind spots. Someone will notice if the "infrastructure reserve" keeps growing but nothing gets fixed.

Consider profit-sharing formulas tied to roles and contribution, not seniority alone. A janitor whose work keeps the place running deserves recognition in the surplus. A supplier who took risk alongside you deserves stability.

Most important: resist the pressure to smooth distributions. A year of high profit followed by reinvestment and fair pay, then a quieter year with smaller distributions, is healthier than extracting the same amount every year while the system slowly decays. Stakeholders understand cycles if you name them honestly.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

When Surplus Circulation is well-tended, trust compounds. Workers stay. Suppliers invest in the relationship. Communities advocate for the business. The organism becomes resilient: it has reserves for crisis, capacity for innovation, relationships that hold through downturns. Profitability becomes not a spike but a rhythm—less dramatic, more durable.

Regeneration also creates optionality. A company that keeps 50 percent of profit in the system can pivot faster, invest in new capabilities, absorb a supply shock, or support workers during transition. It breathes. It learns.

The shadow is familiar: stakeholders may demand more distribution than the organism can sustain. Workers want higher wages now. Owners want maximum returns. Communities want investment they need. These tensions are real and won't disappear—but explicit circulation patterns make them discussable, bounded, and shared.

If tended poorly, Surplus Circulation decays into extraction dressed as necessity. "We need these reserves" becomes a permanent excuse. Reinvestment dries up while distributions grow hollow. Workers and suppliers lose faith and disengage. The organism becomes brittle: no reserves, no loyalty, no adaptive capacity. When crisis comes, it breaks.

Another risk: becoming so conservative in reinvestment that you calcify. Surplus that never circulates is capital imprisoned. The organism becomes a museum instead of a living thing—technically solvent but no longer generative.

The pattern also asks for honesty about what "fair" means across different stakeholders. There is no formula that will satisfy everyone. But deliberate circulation at least surfaces the values in play instead of hiding them.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**John Lewis Partnership (UK)**: The department store group has distributed profit to "Partners" (employees) for over 150 years through annual bonuses tied to profit and role. Reinvestment in stores, training, and digital capacity happens systematically before bonuses are calculated. Partners own the company, so distribution cycles align with long-term health. The pattern is so embedded it shapes culture and hiring.

**Mondragon Cooperative (Spain)**: Worker-owned industrial federation distributes surplus through guaranteed minimum wages, education funds, and reinvestment in community development alongside individual bonuses. They explicitly balance extraction and regeneration at the cooperative level, not just the firm level. Quarterly transparency reports show where profit flows.

**Grameen Bank (Bangladesh)**: Distributes profits partly as dividends to borrowers (who are also stakeholders), partly as reserves for lending capacity, partly to borrower communities for education and health. The circulation is deliberate: lending capacity must grow to serve more people, so distribution is constrained. Borrowers accept smaller immediate returns because the reinvestment grows their opportunity.

**Patagonia (USA)**: Dedicates 1 percent of sales (not profit, sales—a more radical commitment) to environmental causes, reinvests aggressively in durable product design and supply chain resilience, and pays workers above-market wages. Profit distribution is subordinate to regeneration and impact. Ownership structure (now a trust) locks this in beyond founder whim.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed sensing change how Surplus Circulation can work. Real-time data on organizational health—cashflow, asset condition, stakeholder satisfaction, supplier stability—means regeneration needs can be diagnosed faster and more accurately. A factory with sensors knows exactly when equipment needs reinvestment; it doesn't guess.

This enables more granular, responsive distribution. Instead of annual bonuses, some systems may shift toward continuous profit-sharing where workers see their earnings adjust monthly as the organism's health shifts. This requires trust and literacy—stakeholders need to understand why their share fluctuated—but it's more honest.

Algorithmic transparency becomes critical. If AI is recommending how much to distribute, stakeholders must see the logic. "The model says retain 45 percent for regeneration" is not enough; workers need to know what regeneration means, how it was defined, who defined it, and whether it tracks reality.

Distributed governance tools also matter. Blockchain-based payment systems or participatory budgeting platforms can make profit-sharing more tangible and involve more stakeholders in distribution decisions. A cooperative can make real-time decisions about bonuses across hundreds of members, surfacing disagreement and learning from it.

The risk: automation of distribution without deliberation. If profit allocation becomes a black-box process, stakeholders disengage. The pattern requires human conversation and judgment, even (or especially) when tools enable speed.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: Stakeholders can articulate the distribution logic without prompting. Workers know roughly where profit goes. Reinvestment shows up visibly (upgraded equipment, training programs, better supply relationships). Distributions feel proportionate to contribution and tied to organism health, not arbitrary. Profit dips are acknowledged and weathered; distribution isn't frantic or resentful. There is conversation about what's working and what isn't.

**Signs of decay**: Distributions hidden or explained only to insiders. Reinvestment is hypothetical—budgets set but not deployed. Workers cynical about profit announcements. Stakeholders leave quietly. Surplus grows but capacity doesn't—the organism is getting fat, not strong. Distribution becomes hostile negotiation instead of shared understanding.

**A diagnostic question to ask yourself**: *If profit dropped 30 percent next quarter, could your stakeholders articulate why, and would they trust your regeneration choices?* If the answer is no, your Surplus Circulation is unclear. The pattern only works when stakeholders understand the organism's actual needs and believe leadership is honest about them. Without that, distribution becomes extraction wearing a different mask.
