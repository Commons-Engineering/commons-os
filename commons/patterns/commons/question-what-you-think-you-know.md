> Surface and test the hidden assumptions in your plans before they become problems. A way to catch mistakes early by aski

---

### Section 1: Context

Every decision rests on a foundation of unstated beliefs — about what will happen, who people are, what matters, what's possible. In individuals, wrong assumptions compound exponentially; in groups, they calcify into collective blindness. The Orbit 1 principle of Adaptive Management teaches that optimal responses are rarely known in advance; yet most organisations proceed as though their foundational premises are already settled. Bounded Rationality reminds us that all actors work with incomplete information and cognitive limits. Truth-Seeking calls for active pursuit of accurate information about system state. A commons that cannot regularly excavate and test its own hidden assumptions becomes brittle — unable to adapt when conditions shift, unable to surface where participants' mental models have drifted apart.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Invisible assumptions guiding action vs. explicit, testable premises.**

Assumptions hide inside language, culture, and habit. A team aligns on a strategy without realising members hold incompatible mental models of success. A plan proceeds on the premise that a resource will remain constant, unexamined. A group makes a decision based on shared 'common sense' that no one has actually verified. The tension is acute: action requires some level of confidence in premises, yet premature confidence in untested assumptions leads to coordinated failure. Cheap testing early prevents expensive failure late. But testing requires naming what is usually left implicit — which feels risky, vulnerable, and often gets avoided. Groups that suppress assumption-questioning move faster in the short term but compound errors that later demand costly correction or collapse.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Assumption Excavation is a structured, recurring practice with three coupled movements: surfacing, testing, and renegotiation.

**SURFACING:** Create explicit protocols for naming hidden assumptions before decisions lock in. In groups, use facilitated dialogue to bring unconscious premises into language. Ask: What are we taking for granted here? What would have to be true for this plan to work? What are we assuming about people, resources, timing, or context? In individual work, maintain an Assumption Inventory — a living list of critical premises in your strategy, knowledge framework, or plan. Distinguish between assumptions that are critical (decision hinges on them) and those that are peripheral.

**TESTING:** Design cheap, rapid tests of the most critical and most uncertain assumptions before committing substantial resources. This is not full validation but signal-testing: Can we find evidence that contradicts this premise? What is the minimum viable experiment that would falsify it? Use counterfactual reasoning to examine baseline assumptions ("What would happen anyway without our intervention?"). Test across multiple domains — evidence, logic, lived experience, outlier cases that contradict the pattern.

**RENEGOTIATION:** When testing reveals that an assumption was false or that stakeholders held different mental models, treat this as a governance act, not a failure. Explicitly renegotiate expectations, roles, and success criteria. Surface where unspoken assumptions about labor, support, or fairness have bred silent resentment. Make the adjustment visible and documented so the commons learns from it.

This practice cycles continuously. No assumption is ever permanently settled; each is held provisionally and revisited as conditions change. The commons that can quickly excavate, test, and adapt its foundational beliefs stays coherent under pressure and responsive to emergence.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin with a simple ritual: before any significant decision, pause and name three to five foundational claims you're betting on. Write them down. Don't overthink it—the goal is to make the invisible visible, not perfect.

In groups, use the "Assumption Mapping" move. On a shared surface (wall, whiteboard, doc), list the decision at the top. Then ask: *What has to be true for this to work?* Each person contributes premises without debate. You're harvesting, not filtering. A plan to hire new staff might surface assumptions like: "We have budget," "Candidates with these skills exist in our market," "Our onboarding process is solid," "Turnover won't spike," "Remote work fits our culture." Name them all.

Next comes **testing**—the courage move. For each assumption, ask: *How do we actually know this?* Is it based on data, past success, intuition, hope? Which ones feel most fragile? This is where you separate beliefs you've verified from beliefs you're just carrying. You might discover your "solid onboarding" has never been formally measured, or your market assumption rests on one person's network.

Then **renegotiate**. For high-risk assumptions, design small experiments before full commitment. Talk to five candidates before the hire. Run a two-week remote trial before restructuring. Ask frontline staff if your cultural assumption matches their experience. Some assumptions will hold; others will shift. That's the point.

Make this a rhythm, not a one-off. Weekly team check-ins, pre-project assumption reviews, retrospectives that ask "which assumptions proved wrong?" Over time, people get better at spotting their own blindspots. The culture shifts from defending assumptions to testing them.

### Section 5: Consequences

When assumptions are regularly surfaced and tested, plans become more robust. You catch errors before they compound. Team members feel heard because their doubts get named, not dismissed. Decision-making slows down slightly but becomes more confident—you're acting on ground you've actually examined.

The practice also builds what might be called "epistemic humility": a shared understanding that everyone holds some invisible beliefs, and that's okay as long as you're willing to look at them together. This openness becomes an asset in uncertain environments where yesterday's truth dissolves quickly.

But there are risks if this pattern isn't tended carefully. **Over-excavation** can paralyze—endlessly surfacing assumptions without moving to action. Teams can become cynical if testing reveals flaws but nothing changes in response. And there's a social risk: surfacing assumptions requires psychological safety. In hierarchies where people fear being wrong, assumptions stay buried.

If the practice dies, organisations revert to tribal knowledge and unexamined certainty. Decisions made in confidence crash against hidden reality. Diverse perspectives—which might have caught the mistake—stay silent.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Wikipedia's edit-war protocols** surface conflicting assumptions about what constitutes "neutral point of view." Before articles lock, editors explicitly name the premises (about evidence, representation, significance) that different versions rest on. Testing happens through citation requirements and talk-page debate.

**Indigenous land-management practices** in Australia and North America work by continuously questioning assumptions about fire, water, and animal behaviour. The Karuk Tribe's fire management in California explicitly tests and adjusts assumptions about when to burn, based on seasonal observation and intergenerational knowledge. What works gets passed on; what doesn't gets renegotiated.

**Agile software retrospectives** at companies like Spotify embed assumption-testing into process. Teams regularly ask: "What did we assume would work? What actually happened?" The practice treats code and process like experiments, not blueprints—assumptions are expected to be refined or discarded.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI amplifies both the promise and peril of this pattern. Machine learning systems are assumption-black-boxes—opaque even to their makers. An algorithm trained on biased data will confidently make decisions based on invisible premises no human chose. Assumption Excavation becomes critical infrastructure: teams must demand not just *what* the model predicts but *what it assumes* about causation, fairness, and what matters.

Conversely, distributed intelligence systems (humans + AI + data) can excavate assumptions faster than humans alone. An AI can flag statistical anomalies that contradict your premises, or surface patterns in your reasoning you didn't see. The pattern evolves: humans ask hard questions; AI helps find the evidence that tests them.

But the risk is delegating assumption-testing to algorithms. The deepest, most consequential assumptions—about what's worth optimizing, who counts, what success means—remain profoundly human choices. The pattern strengthens when groups use AI as a mirror for their own hidden beliefs, not as a replacement for collective thinking.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** People regularly voice doubt without shame. When a decision stalls, someone says "What are we assuming here?" and others nod—it's normal, even welcome. Plans include "This assumes X; we'll test it by doing Y." Retrospectives are honest; failures become lessons, not cover-ups. Team members from different backgrounds catch each other's blindspots because the culture invites that work.

**Signs of decay:** Decisions happen in silence; assumptions stay invisible. When someone questions the plan, they're seen as obstructive. "We've always done it this way" shuts down inquiry. Plans proceed without anyone naming what they're betting on. Failures surprise everyone because hidden assumptions never surfaced.

**The diagnostic question:**

*When was the last time your team stopped before a major decision and explicitly named what you were taking for granted?*

If you can't remember, the practice has atrophied. If it happened last week or yesterday, you're tending the garden.
