### Section 1: Context

In cities across the Global South and Global North, public sanitation has become invisible commons infrastructure — present when well-maintained, catastrophic in absence. When functional, public toilets enable elders to leave home, women to work in informal sectors, homeless populations to participate in street life, disabled and chronically ill people to navigate public space without shame. They anchor informal economies (markets, street vending), enable gathering and information-sharing, and redistribute dignity across populations excluded from private infrastructure. Yet this infrastructure is systematically degraded: underfunded, privatised, removed under 'modernisation' rhetoric, or designed without attention to the bodies and contexts it must serve. The pattern emerges from lived experience: in settlements and cities where communities stewarding public sanitation collectively, where attendants maintain facilities with care, where narratives centre dignity and access, sanitation becomes commons — shared, just, and built to last. Rooted in principles of Collective Action, Reciprocity, Polycentric Governance, and Cultural Meaning.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Sanitation as private/technical problem vs. sanitation as shared dignity infrastructure.**

Sanitation access determines who belongs in public space and who is confined to margins. The core conflict: sanitation treated as technical infrastructure (pipes, efficiency, cost-recovery) vs. sanitation as relational commons (dignity, belonging, justice). When sanitation is privatised, outsourced, or under-funded, it creates cascading exclusions: elderly avoid public space to avoid incontinence shame; women cannot work informal jobs without toilet access; homeless populations are criminalized for using space without facilities; disabled and chronically ill people stay home; informal sanitation workers become invisible, uncompensated, and unseen. Design neglect compounds this: toilets lack grab bars, privacy, clear wayfinding, safety features, or attendant presence. Maintenance cycles break because responsibility is diffused — nobody owns the stewardship. Narratives remain invisible, so toilet access is never recognized as justice issue. The result: sanitation infrastructure that was meant to serve all actually serves only the privileged, while generating trauma and exclusion for the vulnerable. The pattern fails because stewardship is absent, decisions are centralized away from communities, and meaning is never named.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Sanitation as Dignity Commons requires four interlocking layers of stewardship:

**Layer 1: Permanent, Compensated, Visible Stewardship**
Establish neighbourhood sanitation attendants (or cooperatives of attendants) as permanent roles with living wages, benefits, and dignity—not precarious contract labour. These are commons caretakers, visible daily, accountable to the community they serve. They maintain facilities, repair breakdowns, ensure safety and cleanliness, and become information nodes. In South Asian and African practice, this role is proven: it sustains infrastructure, generates dignified employment, and creates human relationship to the commons.

**Layer 2: Polycentric Governance & Community Committees**
Establish neighbourhood sanitation committees (or settlement-wide committees in informal areas) with elected representatives from diverse populations: women, elderly, disabled people, informal workers, menstruating people, homeless populations. These committees make decisions on maintenance standards, fee collection (if any), facility design modifications, and attendant oversight. This embeds subsidiarity — decisions at the neighbourhood scale where information and accountability are local — and ensures that those affected by sanitation design have voice in stewardship.

**Layer 3: Integrated Infrastructure & Access Equity**
Design public sanitation as bundled commons: toilets + washing + water access + waste collection + rest areas + clear wayfinding + accessibility features (grab bars, larger stalls, changing tables, quiet rest areas). Distribute facilities throughout public space so no population is forced to travel distances that impose shame or risk. Ensure 24/7 access where street life is continuous. This removes the binary of privileged (home access) and excluded (no access).

**Layer 4: Dignity Narratives & Visibility**
Systematically document and share stories of access, dignity, and social mixing around public sanitation. Use community media, participatory documentation, and public narrative to position toilet access as justice infrastructure. Make visible the work of sanitation attendants and committee members. Challenge the shame around bodily functions by centering dignity. This layer sustains cultural meaning — the shared understanding that sanitation is not a technical problem to hide, but a commons to celebrate.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by anchoring sanitation stewardship in neighbourhood presence and compensation. Hire community members—people with roots in the area—as permanent attendants or form small cooperatives, with wages indexed to local living costs, health insurance, and paid leave. This isn't cost-cutting; it's investment in relational infrastructure. An attendant who knows Mrs. Chen has mobility issues and needs a wider stall; who notices when a teenager is living in the park; who can respond to overflow at 3 AM because they live nearby—this person is essential commons infrastructure.

Design the physical spaces with users' actual lives in mind. Consult with elderly residents, disabled people, street vendors, sex workers, unhoused neighbours—those whose dignity is most at stake. Build more stalls than official standards suggest. Include spaces for bathing, laundry, prayer, rest. Add locks that work for people with arthritic hands. Ensure lighting that feels safe. Ventilation that doesn't smell like shame.

Create neighbourhood councils—not one-off consultations, but ongoing bodies where users, attendants, and local government meet monthly. Give these councils real power: budgets, maintenance priorities, hiring decisions. A 15-year-old should sit alongside a local councillor. A waste picker should influence design. This redistributes authority from distant planners to people who actually inhabit the space.

Establish maintenance schedules that are visible and accountable. Post them. Explain them. When a toilet breaks, communicate the timeline for repair. When it's fixed, celebrate it. This sounds small, but it signals: we're tending this together. You can count on it.

Fund this through diverse revenue streams: municipal budgets (where available), reinvested service fees (modest, waived for poorest users), commercial partnerships that don't exploit users (vending space, advertising). Prevent any single funder from controlling the commons.

### Section 5: Consequences

When sanitation becomes relational commons, flourishing unfolds in ways statistics miss. Elderly people return to market life. Women in informal sectors can work longer hours without panic. Disabled and chronically ill people regain spatial autonomy—they move through neighbourhoods with less fear. Unhoused people access dignity routines: bathing, changing clothes, using a mirror. Children learn that public space is for them. The neighbourhood itself becomes more cohesive because stewardship creates repeated encounters, relationships, accountability.

Attendants become visible elders—people the neighbourhood knows, trusts, defers to on sanitation matters. Their labour is honoured rather than hidden. This shifts how sanitation work is valued culturally, upstream.

But risks emerge if not actively tended. Without real power, neighbourhood councils become theatre—generating resentment when decisions are ignored. Without adequate budgeting, attendants burn out. Without clear agreements about who decides maintenance priorities, conflicts surface (some want more privacy stalls; others want more washing stations). If attendants aren't hired locally, the relational infrastructure collapses; they become enforcers rather than neighbours.

Gentrification risk: as neighbourhoods improve, property values rise, residents are displaced. The very people the commons was built for are pushed out. This requires intentional anti-displacement measures: rent controls, community land trusts, fierce local voice in development decisions.

There's also the risk of paternalism—of stewards deciding what dignity looks like for others rather than asking. This requires constant humility: checking whether the council actually has power, whether quieter voices are being heard, whether designs serve actual needs or imagined ones.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mumbai's Sulabh Sanitation Movement (India)**: Sulabh International pioneered community-managed public toilets across Indian cities, hiring local attendants on permanent contracts and establishing user committees. Over 14,000 facilities now exist, serving millions daily. The model shifted sanitation from hidden shame to visible commons work, elevating scavenging caste communities to stewards and dignified workers. Though not without tensions (questions of inclusion, equity), it demonstrated that community-run sanitation at scale works and sustains.

**Accra's Community Toilet Centres (Ghana)**: In dense informal settlements, neighbourhood organizations manage small toilet blocks built on community land. Users pay modest daily fees; revenue funds attendants' wages and maintenance. Local youth are trained as technicians. The model emerged from residents recognizing that government wouldn't deliver, so they stewarded their own. Dignity and function coexist because attendants are neighbours, not distant contractors.

**San Francisco's Navigation Centers and Hygiene Facilities (USA)**: While US sanitation infrastructure is less obviously commons-based, SF's approach to public hygiene facilities for unhoused populations offers lessons. Facilities are staffed, welcoming, treated as entry points to services. Moving from "public toilets as punishment spaces" to "hygiene facilities as belonging infrastructure" required cultural shift—treating unhoused people as neighbourhood members, not problems to hide.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed sensing will transform how we steward sanitation commons. Real-time sensors can monitor occupancy, cleanliness, and maintenance needs—but only if this data serves communities rather than controlling them. A neighbourhood council with a simple dashboard showing usage patterns, maintenance requests, and budget allocation can make faster, smarter decisions. Attendants gain decision-making tools: predictive alerts when supplies run low, usage data informing stall configurations.

But automation risks: if algorithms decide cleaning schedules without consulting attendants' expertise, or if data systems gate access (requiring ID or digital payment from people without smartphones), the commons becomes extractive again. The cognitive era demands that communities control their own data—owning usage information, using it for their own stewardship decisions, refusing to share it with advertisers or surveillance systems.

Distributed networks of neighbourhoods can share knowledge: what maintenance strategies work, how to budget fairly, how to handle conflicts. This peer-to-peer learning—attendants in one city learning from attendants elsewhere, via digital platforms they control—accelerates commons maturation without centralizing authority.

The deepest shift: cognitive tools can surface what was invisible. Usage patterns reveal whose needs weren't being met. Maintenance logs highlight systemic inequities. When communities own this insight, they steward better. But only if the tools remain in their hands.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life**: Attendants greet users by name. The space smells clean. There's a maintenance board with handwritten notes from the council meeting. Elderly people linger—not rushing, not ashamed. Conversations happen between attendants and users about what needs fixing. A teenager helps an elder up the stairs without being asked. There's art on the walls. The budget allocation is posted and legible. An attendant mentions taking a day off last week (because they have paid leave) without apologizing.

**Signs of Decay**: Attendants are sullen, precarious, replaced frequently. No one knows who to report problems to. The council hasn't met in months. Stalls are locked, rationing access. Graffiti isn't cleaned. Elderly people avoid the space. An attendant is yelled at by a supervisor visiting from headquarters. The maintenance budget was cut because "efficiency" required outsourcing. Users are charged fees they can't afford. A woman says she doesn't go out much anymore.

**Diagnostic Question**: If you asked a random elderly resident, a street vendor, an unhoused person, and an attendant "Who decides what happens in this bathroom?" would they all name the same answer—a neighbourhood council where they have real voice? If not, the commons is drifting toward control, away from stewardship.
