> Design and maintain cooling as shared infrastructure so everyone has access to comfort during heat waves, especially tho

---

### Section 1: Context

Urban thermal justice emerges at the intersection of climate extremes, infrastructure inequality, and embodied vulnerability. In Global South cities and low-income neighbourhoods everywhere, heat is not an inconvenience—it is survival infrastructure. Yet thermal comfort has been systematically privatized: air-conditioned homes, offices, malls concentrate in wealthy districts, while heat-vulnerable populations (elderly, outdoor workers, children, unhoused) navigate peak temperatures with minimal refuge. This pattern draws lineage from Carrying Capacity (every neighbourhood has a thermal threshold beyond which health and work become impossible), Subsidiarity (cooling must be governed and maintained locally, not imposed centrally), and Polycentric Organisation (thermal justice requires nested solutions: individual shade trees, street-scale green corridors, neighbourhood anchors, citywide heat action plans).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Thermal comfort as private commodity (air-conditioned enclaves) vs. thermal comfort as shared commons right (accessible to all bodies, seasons, geographies).**

Urban heat kills. During heat waves, mortality spikes among elderly and disabled populations; outdoor workers lose income and health; children cannot concentrate in non-air-conditioned schools; informal traders must abandon street vending. Yet municipalities respond with emergency cooling centers that isolate residents, with tree-planting programmes that take 20 years to mature, or with grey infrastructure retrofits that concentrate in wealthy zones. The core conflict: thermal comfort has become a marker of wealth and privilege, not a commons right. Vulnerable populations face a cruel choice—endure heat at home, risk isolation at cooling centers, or navigate inaccessible green refuges (parks without seating, paths without water, shade networks without wayfinding for disabled bodies). Current approaches treat thermal adaptation as a technical problem (albedo coefficients, canopy coverage targets) rather than a governance and justice problem (who decides where cooling goes, who maintains it, who benefits, who decides when).

---

### Section 3: Solution

Thermal Justice as Living Commons requires five interlocked subsystems, each adaptive and locally governed:

1. EQUITY MAPPING & ACCESSIBILITY FIRST: Before any infrastructure is deployed, map the spatial distribution of thermal vulnerability (elderly, disabled, outdoor-dependent populations) and thermal resources (shade, water, cooling centers). Layer accessibility requirements (wheelchair routes, seating with armrests, public restrooms, emergency call buttons, real-time wayfinding apps). This makes justice visible and measurable—the map becomes a commons governance artifact that surfaces who is left out.

2. NESTED THERMAL ANCHORS: Position cooling infrastructure not as isolated emergency shelters but as permanent, multi-functional neighbourhood anchors—primary care clinics, libraries, food distribution hubs, digital literacy centres, markets—that serve year-round while functioning as thermal refuge during heat extremes. Govern these as cooperatives with vulnerable populations as co-designers and decision-makers. This ensures cooling infrastructure becomes woven into daily life, reducing isolation and maximizing use.

3. GREEN CORRIDOR NETWORKS: Design connected pedestrian and micro-mobility routes (tree canopy, green walls, permeable pavements, water features) that fragment the urban heat island while connecting cooling anchors, essential services, and social gathering spaces. Guarantee thermal refuge every 500m with seating, water, shade. Maintain these through community jobs and social stewardship, not contracted maintenance. This creates both immediate thermal relief and livelihood protection for outdoor workers.

4. MICROCLIMATE CHOREOGRAPHY & SEASONAL GOVERNANCE: Map thermal conditions across neighbourhoods—identifying sun-traps, shade pockets, wind channels, cool corners—and design public spaces (plazas, parks, streets) with diverse microclimates so all bodies can find comfort. Establish seasonal protocols: which shade zones serve elderly residents in summer vs. children in winter; when outdoor markets operate; how activity timing adjusts to thermal safety. Govern these protocols participatorily, treating thermal commons as alive and evolving.

5. HEAT-SENSITIVE ACTIVITY ZONING & PROTECTION: Establish planning rules that protect heat-vulnerable land uses (schools, childcare, elderly housing, healthcare, informal street work) by (a) restricting their location to cooler districts with mandatory thermal adaptation investment, and (b) allowing temporal flexibility—schools can shift to evening/nocturnal schedules during peak heat; street vendors receive dedicated shade structures and cooling access; outdoor labour receives hydration stations and rest cycles. Compensation mechanisms ensure no displacement without alternative livelihood.

Each subsystem operates at its own scale (individual trees, street corridors, neighbourhood centres, city zoning) yet remains interconnected through shared governance. Feedback loops are continuous: residents observe microclimate shifts (cool paving reinforces adoption); cooling center use data shapes anchor expansion; seasonal protocols adjust based on heat-vulnerable population input. The commons regenerates itself through visibility (maps, apps, community monitoring), through livelihoods (maintenance jobs, vendor protection, care work), and through meaning (thermal justice becomes a legible, celebrated identity of place).

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with the bodies most at risk. Map your neighbourhood: who gets hot fastest? Elderly residents in top-floor apartments. Outdoor workers. Children in non-air-conditioned schools. People experiencing homelessness. Ask them directly—not surveys, conversations—where they shelter during heat waves and what they need.

**Activate existing cool spaces.** Libraries, community centers, mosques, temples, schools already have air-conditioning. Open them explicitly as heat refuges during extreme temperatures. Stock them with water, seating for rest, quiet spaces for sensory-sensitive people. Coordinate schedules so someone can always reach a cool space within walking distance (or with free transport). Train staff to welcome people who are only there for the temperature.

**Build distributed microcooling.** Plant native shade trees in public spaces, prioritizing routes to clinics and schools. Install water features (fountains, misting stations) in gathering places—not for decoration but for evaporative cooling that works in dry climates. Create "cool zones" in markets where informal traders can rest. Paint roofs and pavements with reflective materials in low-income areas first. These compound: a shaded bus stop, a fountain, a rest area together create a thermal corridor.

**Govern cooling collectively.** Form neighbourhood thermal committees with representation from vulnerable populations (not charity boards, actual decision-makers). Meet monthly. Monitor which cool spaces stay accessible. Adjust schedules based on real usage. Plan upgrades. Pay people for this labour—it is skilled governance work.

**Establish shared protocols.** When heat exceeds danger thresholds, trigger coordinated response: extended hours at cool centers, paid heat leave for outdoor workers, delivery services for housebound elderly, check-in systems. Document what worked. Share with neighbouring communities.

**Resource continuously.** Thermal justice requires operating budget, not one-time grants. Water bills for fountains. Maintenance for trees. Staff wages. Utilities for extended cooling center hours. Advocate for municipal funding as essential infrastructure, equal to sewage and electricity.

The pattern lives in maintenance: someone checks if the cool center door opens easily; someone waters new trees in dry season; someone listens when elderly residents say the noise of the air-conditioning makes them anxious.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Elderly people who sleep through summer nights without heat-stress dreams. Children who can concentrate in school during hot seasons. Informal traders who don't have to choose between income and health. Outdoor workers who take paid heat breaks. Disabled people who can access public space comfortably. The quiet social consequence: neighborhoods develop thermal literacy—people know where safety is, know they are cared for, move with less fear.

Distributed cooling infrastructure becomes more resilient than centralized air-conditioning. If one cool space closes, others absorb the load. If electricity fails, shade and water still work. The social infrastructure—the thermal committees, the relationships—becomes the real commons: people know each other's heat vulnerabilities; young people help elderly neighbors; the community can respond faster than bureaucracy.

**What risks emerge if neglected:** Cool spaces that become segregated refuges for some while others suffer. Inequitable access disguised as availability ("the cool center is open, but it's across the city"). Cool places that are hostile to vulnerable people—unwelcoming staff, inadequate bathrooms, surveillance that makes people uncomfortable. Infrastructure that decays: trees die from lack of water; fountains break and aren't repaired; cooling centers cut hours during budget crisis.

Thermal gentrification: cooling infrastructure drives up property values in previously undesirable hot neighborhoods, displacing the very people the commons was meant to serve. The pattern becomes extractive—cooling becomes another commodity.

Without continuous community voice, thermal infrastructure becomes paternalistic: experts design "solutions" without asking what people actually need. A cool center that's too loud for trauma survivors. Shade trees that don't provide cultural gathering space. The commons becomes a top-down gift rather than a right claimed collectively.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Ahmedabad, India: Heat Action Plan with community ownership.** The Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan (launched 2013) moves beyond emergency cooling to permanent infrastructure: open cool rooms in municipal buildings and temples during summer; paid heat leave for outdoor workers; outreach teams that visit elderly residents; reflective paint on roofs in low-income areas; public fountains strategized for pedestrian routes. Critically, the plan emerged from partnerships with informal workers and elderly residents, not experts imposing solutions. The city updates protocols annually based on what residents learn. Mortality during heat waves has dropped measurably; the pattern proves that thermal justice is operationalizable at city scale.

**Los Angeles Community Gardens & Cool Corridors Network (2020s).** Community organizations mapped thermal vulnerability in South LA and East LA, then activated existing gardens and parks as "cool corridors"—shaded routes with water and rest points connecting vulnerable neighborhoods. They painted residential streets with cool pavements, prioritizing blocks where elderly residents are concentrated. Gardens became gathering places, not just food production. The network is governed by resident committees; the city funds it as essential health infrastructure.

**Dar es Salaam, Tanzania: Water Points as Thermal Commons.** In dense informal settlements where air-conditioning is economically impossible, resident groups established public water points strategized for thermal management: placed in shaded gathering spaces, accessible 24 hours, maintained collectively. During heat waves, people gather around water—for drinking, for evaporative cooling, for rest and conversation. The infrastructure costs little; the commons is the maintenance system and the governance. Similar models operate in Nairobi, Lagos, and across the Global South where cooling infrastructure must be affordable and resilient.

---

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and sensing networks can deepen—or colonize—this pattern. On the generative side: thermal sensors distributed across neighborhoods create real-time heat maps that identify vulnerability hot-spots; algorithms help committees prioritize infrastructure where risk is highest; predictive models forecast dangerous heat waves weeks ahead, allowing proactive cooling-center staffing. Communities can claim this data as their own, using it for advocacy ("show us the official proof that our neighborhood is hotter").

But risk: if thermal data becomes proprietary, if AI-driven "optimization" concentrates cooling resources by algorithmic efficiency rather than equity, if digital systems replace human relationships and local knowledge. A thermal committee guided by data should remain human-centered—data informs, residents decide.

Distributed intelligence becomes crucial: decentralized networks of neighborhood thermal monitors (people, not just sensors) who share knowledge peer-to-peer rather than reporting to a central authority. Indigenous knowledge—how communities have managed heat for centuries through architecture, scheduling, gathering patterns—can be documented and taught through digital networks without erasing its local specificity.

The cognitive shift: from heat as an individual problem (buy an air conditioner) to heat as a commons requiring distributed, adaptive intelligence (many people, many technologies, coordinated through shared governance).

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Cool spaces are genuinely crowded during hot afternoons—not because people are forced there, but because they choose to be. Elderly residents know the cool center staff by name and arrive early to sit and chat. Trees are tended by neighbors, not just municipal workers. The thermal committee includes people who initially distrusted it; they show up because something they suggested actually happened. Informal traders use cool rest areas; their income stabilizes. Schools report better student concentration during hot months. Neighborhood gardens flourish—both food and gathering place.

**Signs of decay:** Cool centers become institutional—clean but cold, unwelcoming, places you go only when desperate. Trees planted but not watered; they die and are replaced with more trees that die. Infrastructure exists but access is contingent on knowing the right person or being the "right" kind of vulnerable resident. Thermal committees dwindle to the same 5 people; younger people don't show up; knowledge leaves when people move. Cooling infrastructure becomes visible marker of class: some neighborhoods visibly greening, others still bare and hot. Check-in systems for housebound elderly become paternalistic surveillance rather than care.

**Diagnostic question:** *Who from your neighborhood can walk to a cool, welcoming place within 10 minutes, any time the temperature rises, without needing permission or documentation—and who cannot?*

If the answer is "it depends on who you are," the commons is fractured. If the answer is "everyone," but nobody actually uses it, ask why: is it truly accessible, or only nominally open?
