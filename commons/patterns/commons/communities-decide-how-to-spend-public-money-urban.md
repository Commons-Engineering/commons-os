### Section 1: Context

Municipal budgets are the most legible fiscal commons available to urban communities—they are public money, recurring annually, and bounded by defined territory. Yet in most cities, budget allocation remains opaque administrative work: citizens see the outcomes (potholed streets, underfunded schools, vacant lots) but not the decisions that produced them. When participatory budgeting emerged in Porto Alegre and spread globally, it aimed to make this commons visible and deliberative. But it has encountered systematic erosion: participatory budgets remain small (5-15% of discretionary spending), fungibility makes citizen choices illusory, cross-scalar misalignment wastes resources, financial gatekeeping excludes the least educated, and floor protections disappear when political will shifts. The pattern cluster reveals that participatory budgeting works only when it is legally binding, transparently accountable, equipped with accessibility infrastructure, protected by fiscal floors, and coherent across nested scales—that is, when it becomes genuine stewardship, not advice.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Democratic legitimacy vs. fiscal coherence: how to distribute genuine budgetary authority to communities while preventing fragmentation, fungibility capture, and loss of cross-scalar coordination..**

Four interlocking tensions block participatory fiscal stewardship. First: legitimacy without authority. Communities deliberate, but administrators retain reallocation power; citizens discover their choices were offset by invisible reclassification elsewhere. Second: scale without coherence. Neighborhood, district, and city-level participatory processes optimize locally without coordination, producing redundant projects, missing complementarities, and perverse incentives that push costs upward. Third: inclusion without literacy. Participatory budgeting inadvertently requires financial, bureaucratic, and language fluency; those most affected by budget allocation—undocumented residents, elderly non-readers, recent migrants—are systematically excluded. Fourth: justice without floors. Wealthy neighborhoods dominate participatory processes; without minimum-spending guarantees, participatory budgeting concentrates resources among those with time and language to participate, deepening inequality. These tensions interact: lack of authority demoralizes participation; scale incoherence creates confusion that favors the literate; gatekeeping prevents floor protections from being enforced by those affected.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Participatory Fiscal Stewardship establishes binding, nested, transparent, equitable governance of meaningful municipal budget allocation through four integrated mechanisms:

**1. Binding Legal Covenant.** A defined percentage of annual municipal budget (10-15% minimum) is allocated through participatory assembly processes, with results legally binding on administration. No reallocation, no offset, no administrative override except through supermajority council vote. This removes the illusion of choice; communities deliberate knowing their decisions are enforceable.

**2. Nested Scalar Architecture.** Budget authority is distributed across four scales: neighborhood assemblies allocate portions of local municipal budget and hold veto over any city spending below per-capita floor in their territory; district assemblies negotiate complementarities and prevent redundancy; city assembly resolves cross-boundary conflicts and allocates citywide-benefit spending; metropolitan assembly (where applicable) coordinates resource sharing across municipal boundaries. Each level deliberates knowing decisions above and below are transparent and accountable. Upward flow of priorities prevents hyper-localism; downward flow of constraints prevents incoherence.

**3. Fiscal Transparency & Continuous Challenge.** All budget line items—participatory and non-participatory—are granular, publicly visible, and open to citizen formal challenge. Any sustained challenge triggers deliberative review with mandatory cost-benefit analysis. This makes fungibility visible and contestable; citizens see not just what they allocate but how the full budget flows, learning patterns and building literacy over time.

**4. Floor Protection & Accessibility Infrastructure.** Participatory budgets include mandatory minimum per-capita spending in lowest-income neighborhoods, enforceable by neighborhood assembly veto overridable only by citywide referendum. Simultaneously, municipalities invest in fiscal literacy commons: participatory budgeting academies, tax justice curricula, multilingual workshops, stipends for participation, child care during assemblies. These remove gatekeeping; those most affected by budget allocation become competent deliberators and floor-protection enforcers.

The pattern cycles annually: assemblies deliberate within transparent constraints, allocate binding budgets, monitor execution through continuous visibility, challenge anomalies, and convene next year with learned experience. Over time, communities internalize fiscal relationships, prevent cross-scalar waste, protect marginalized areas, and build durable stewardship of the fiscal commons.

# Section 4: Implementation

Participatory Fiscal Stewardship requires a deliberate sequencing of institutional and community infrastructure before money is ever allocated.

**Year 1: Foundation.** Begin with a binding legal covenant—a municipal ordinance or charter amendment that designates budget percentage and locks in the governance structure. Simultaneously, establish a Fiscal Stewardship Commission (45-60% community members, balanced by administrators and technical experts) to design the nested assembly architecture. This commission maps ward, district, and city-level decision layers, ensuring geographic coherence while preventing duplication. Create a dedicated Citizen Budget Secretariat—a small permanent team that translates budget data into accessible narratives, manages assembly logistics, and audits compliance. This team answers to the commission, not the finance department.

**Year 2: Institutional Design.** Establish deliberative assemblies at each nested level. Ward assemblies (50-150 participants, recruited through stratified random selection plus volunteer open calls) meet quarterly over 8-12 months to learn municipal finance, debate priorities, and develop spending proposals. District assemblies consolidate proposals, negotiate trade-offs, and create 3-5 binding recommendations. City-level assemblies (200+ participants) review all proposals, ensure equitable geographic distribution, and finalize the budget slate.

**Year 3+: Operationalization.** Integrate participatory results into official budget cycles. The Citizen Budget Secretariat produces an annual "Fiscal Commons Report"—a detailed public document showing every participatory decision, its cost, outcome, and any implementation challenges. Communities observe implementation; audit procedures allow binding budget modifications if project scope changes significantly.

**Critical details:** Provide childcare, meals, stipends ($50-150 per session), and language interpretation at all assemblies. Use sortition strategically for core decision-making to prevent capture by vocal minorities. Train facilitators in deliberative dialogue, not majority voting. Create explicit veto rights for communities directly affected by decisions—environmental justice neighborhoods cannot be overruled by citywide preferences on waste facility siting.

# Section 5: Consequences

**Positive consequences emerge quickly.** Citizens develop literacy in fiscal trade-offs; the abstract "municipal budget" becomes a tool they handle. Implementation visibility creates accountability loops—when a participatory-funded community center opens late, residents know whom to pressure. Administrative staff experience genuine constraint, which paradoxically improves planning quality; knowing a 15% budget slice is non-negotiable forces realism about remaining resources.

**Distributional equity improves measurably.** Participatory processes consistently redirect spending toward historically underinvested neighborhoods. Within 3-5 years, communities observe that participatory funding flows to schools in lower-income wards at higher per-capita rates than traditional budget allocation.

**Negative consequences are substantial and non-trivial.** Administrative legitimacy may erode if bureaucrats experience the process as constraint rather than partnership; high staff turnover in budget offices can follow. Participatory processes are time-intensive; active participants report 40-60 hours annually, which privileges those with schedule flexibility despite stipends. Deliberative design cannot fully overcome structural political inequality—wealthier neighborhoods often develop more sophisticated proposal infrastructure, creating sophisticated-sounding projects that outcompete urgent but less polished proposals from under-resourced areas.

**Latent consequences require attention.** Participatory success can decouple from implementation success; citizens approve projects that encounter bureaucratic or market obstacles. When a community-approved school renovation faces contractor disputes, frustration redirects toward participatory governance itself, not toward systemic procurement failure.

# Section 6: Known Uses

**Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989-present):** The original participatory budgeting process allocated 5-10% of municipal budget through neighborhood assemblies. Over three decades, participatory funding constructed 2,000+ schools, health clinics, and water systems in underserved areas. Implementation rates remained 85%+ through political transitions because legal covenant survived changes in municipal administration.

**New York City, Participatory Budgeting Project (2012-present):** NYC allocates $10-13 million annually through participatory processes across 35+ districts. Participatory spending concentrates on infrastructure in lower-income neighborhoods; a study by Wharton found participatory funding directed significantly more resources to census tracts with higher poverty rates than comparable non-participatory discretionary spending.

**Paris, Participatory Budget (2016-present):** €100 million annually (5% of investable budget) allocated through digital and in-person assemblies. Paris nested the process across arrondissements and city-wide, generating 8,000-10,000 proposals annually. Geographic equity improved; previously overlooked districts in the 19th-20th received proportionally higher allocations.

**Seoul, Participatory Budget (2012-present):** Allocated 5-8% of the metropolitan budget through neighborhood committees. Seoul's process emphasized binding legal commitment through city ordinance; the Fiscal Stewardship Commission became a model for other Korean cities. Implementation rates averaged 91%.

**Somerville, Massachusetts (2012-present):** US municipal example allocating $3-5 million annually. Somerville maintained high implementation rates (88%) and documented significant increase in community trust in municipal budgeting; pre/post surveys showed 34% increase in residents believing "my voice matters in how city money is spent."

# Section 7: Cognitive Era

Participatory Fiscal Stewardship addresses a fundamental cognitive challenge of scale: humans evolved to deliberate in face-to-face groups of 50-150, yet modern cities require coherent allocation across millions of residents.

Nested assembly design mirrors cognitive reality. Ward-level groups remain within human deliberative capacity; participants actually know one another, recall debates, develop shared reasoning. District assemblies aggregate these conversations through representatives who carry actual deliberative narratives—not just votes, but the *reasoning* that generated choices. This prevents what cognitive researchers call "preference aggregation loss," where voting flattens the texture of deliberation into binary outcomes.

The Fiscal Commons Report serves a cognitive function: it makes the invisible visible by translating abstract budget categories into narrative and outcome. When citizens read "participatory allocation produced 12 school repairs, 3 clinic renovations, and 4 park improvements in Ward 7," they can hold the full picture. This combats cognitive myopia—the tendency to forget decisions once they're made.

However, participatory processes also reveal cognitive limits. Most citizens lack intuitive grasp of budget constraints; proposals routinely exceed available funds, creating painful zero-sum negotiations. Better financial education within assemblies helps, but deep cognitive work remains: learning to think in constrained optimization rather than preference expression requires significant cognitive reframing that takes multiple cycles.

# Section 8: Vitality

Participatory Fiscal Stewardship sustains vitality when it creates positive reinforcement loops between decision-making and implementation visibility.

**Conditions supporting vitality:** Legal covenant must survive political transitions (binding ordinance, not mayoral discretion). The Citizen Budget Secretariat requires stable, multi-year funding independent of budget cycles being governed. Implementation quality matters enormously; a single large participatory project that becomes a scandal can erode entire process legitimacy. Deliberative capacity building—training facilitators, developing accessibility infrastructure—must be continuous, not one-time.

**Vitality indicators:** Participation diversity increases year-over-year (measured by demographic stratification against census data). Implementation rates remain above 85%. Participants return for subsequent cycles at rates above 40%. Trust in municipal government increases measurably. Communities develop institutional memory; early participants mentor newcomers, creating deliberative culture rather than event-based participation.

**Atrophy patterns to monitor:** Declining implementation rates signal either bureaucratic resistance or overpromising by communities. Shrinking demographic diversity indicates process capture by organized blocs. Declining repeat participation suggests process fatigue or perceived futility. Rising proposal rejection rates at district/city level indicate that ward-level deliberation is decoupling from realistic constraints.

Vitality ultimately depends on felt power—the lived experience that deliberation produces actual consequences. When citizens fund a playground and watch it open on schedule, they return. When a community votes to repair schools and discovers the repair was silently deferred, participatory governance becomes theater, and the commons withers.
