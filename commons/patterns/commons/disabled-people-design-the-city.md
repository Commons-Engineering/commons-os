### Section 1: Context

Urban infrastructure—transit, sidewalks, digital services, wayfinding, buildings—has been designed by and for able-bodied, neurotypical bodies at statistical averages. Disabled and neurodivergent persons are treated as exceptions, accommodations, compliance burdens. Algorithms optimizing public space based on movement data, cost efficiency, or pedestrian counts systematically erase accessibility needs. Municipal services retrofit accessibility after launch, if at all. This creates a commons problem: decisions made without disabled persons' knowledge and authority perpetually exclude them. The lineage is clear: Collective Action (disabled persons share a resource: the city), Subsidiarity (decisions about accessibility should be made by those living with access barriers), Polycentric Organisation (power distributed to nested accessibility authorities), and Reciprocity (those who contribute their expertise and lived knowledge gain decision-making voice, not advisory status).

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Accessibility-as-afterthought (retrofitted compliance) vs. Accessibility-as-foundational (embedded in all design decisions from inception).**

The core conflict: Centralized design optimization (algorithmic, administrative, cost-driven) treats disabled people's spatial and digital needs as outliers, constraints, or afterthoughts. Accessibility gets separated from design—retrofitted, optional, expensive. Disabled persons are consulted but not empowered; they advise but do not decide. Algorithms exclude disability-access criteria from objectives. Procurement standards treat universal design as nice-to-have. Digital services launch without disabled testing. Infrastructure projects proceed despite barriers disabled communities have flagged. Result: The city systematically disables people through design choices made without their authority. Disabled persons, the primary stakeholders, have no binding voice. Non-disabled designers optimize away accessibility. Cost optimization algorithms treat accessibility as friction to be minimized. The commons fractures: disabled persons cannot access shared urban space, services, or governance itself. Trust erodes. Barriers persist because decision-makers face no material consequence for exclusion.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Distribute binding co-design and veto authority to disabled persons and accessibility experts across all urban infrastructure, digital services, and algorithmic systems. This is not advisory. This is governance.

**Structure:** Establish permanent, funded accessibility co-design authorities at the infrastructure level: Transit Co-Design Authority (disabled riders, neurodivergent users, care workers decide route design, stop placement, vehicle accessibility, real-time information access). Digital Services Accessibility Council (disabled and neurodivergent residents test and veto municipal digital services before launch; retain ongoing authority to halt services that degrade accessibility). Infrastructure Accessibility Commission (disabled persons approve all public space design, wayfinding, street furniture, curb management, building renovation; co-design reconstruction standards in post-disaster contexts). Algorithmic Audit Circle (disabled persons define accessibility criteria that must be embedded in optimization objectives; have authority to disable or rewrite algorithms that erase accessibility needs).

**Authority:** These bodies hold binding veto power. Projects do not proceed without disabled co-design completion and sign-off. Features do not launch without accessibility testing. Algorithms do not deploy without disabled persons confirming accessibility criteria are embedded. Supermajorities of these bodies must include disabled, neurodivergent, elder, and care-experienced persons. Compensation is mandatory—living wage for participation as co-designers and decision-makers, not volunteers.

**Baseline:** Universal design becomes design baseline, not retrofit or accommodation. Accessibility thresholds (ramps, curb cuts, accessible vehicles, screen reader compatibility, multiple input modalities, sensory design, cognitive access, real-time information) are infrastructure minimum standards, not optional features. No service launches, no infrastructure deploys, no algorithm activates until these thresholds are met.

**Feedback & Adaptation:** These authorities retain ongoing monitoring authority. Disabled residents conduct regular accessibility audits. Real-time accommodation protocols allow dynamic routing and service adjustments when barriers emerge. Authority has power to require redesign mid-project if implementation diverges from co-designed standards.

**Justice mechanism:** Disabled persons, historically excluded from decisions that harmed them most, gain decision-making authority. Reciprocity shifts: those with accessibility knowledge contribute to design; they gain proportional voice in governance. No longer guests in someone else's city; they are stewards.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by embedding disabled people into the decision structure before design begins—not as consultants invited midway, but as co-designers with binding authority from day one.

**Establish accessibility co-design councils.** Create permanent, paid positions on transit boards, planning departments, and digital service teams. These aren't advisory committees that can be ignored; they have veto power over projects that affect public access. Include wheelchair users, D/Deaf people, neurodivergent designers, people with chronic illness, and those with invisible disabilities. Rotate membership to prevent burnout and bring fresh perspectives. Pay them well—this is skilled work, and disabled people shouldn't subsidize cities with unpaid labor.

**Conduct access audits before design, not after.** Before a single blueprint is drawn for a new transit line or app interface, disabled people trial the *concept*. Walk the proposed route in a wheelchair. Use the interface mockup with a screen reader. Navigate the wayfinding in low light or high sensory noise. Document what breaks. Let that discovery reshape the design, not constrain it.

**Build accessibility into algorithmic objectives.** If a city is optimizing transit routes, wayfinding algorithms, or public space allocation, accessibility criteria must be hardcoded into the objective function, not bolted on. That means: shortest accessible route, not shortest route. Seating availability for people who cannot stand. Rest spaces for people with energy limitations. Quiet zones for sensory regulation.

**Create feedback loops with teeth.** Disabled people should be able to flag access failures in real time—broken elevators, confusing signage, algorithms that fail them—and see documented response. Not "we'll look into it." Weekly repair logs. Public dashboards. Consequences for repeated failures.

**Fund accessibility as infrastructure, not charity.** Stop treating accessible design as an added cost. It's part of the base budget. Universal design—curb cuts, clear signage, flexible wayfinding—benefits everyone: parents with strollers, older adults, travelers with luggage, people on bad days. Frame it as intelligent infrastructure, not kindness.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** Cities become navigable by more people, faster. A transit system designed by people with mobility disabilities often works better for elderly people, pregnant people, and anyone temporarily injured. Accessible digital interfaces are clearer for everyone—better contrast, simpler language, logical structure. Access becomes a design discipline that sharpens thinking, not a constraint that dulls it. Disabled people stop being invisible in planning and start shaping the places they inhabit daily. Trust grows between disabled communities and city institutions.

**What risks emerge:** Institutions resist sharing power. Some officials or designers will view veto authority as bureaucratic friction, delaying "efficient" projects. There's real tension between accessibility and other goods—a ramp takes space, elevators cost money, captions require labor. These are genuine tradeoffs, not imaginary ones. If veto authority exists but disabled co-designers lack real funding or are outnumbered by faster-moving departments, it becomes performative—another way to collect input without changing outcomes.

The pattern also risks co-opting disabled people into defending insufficient budgets. If the city is underfunded, accessibility councils may become the face of triage decisions, blamed for "slowing things down" rather than the system being blamed for underfunding. Without stable funding and genuine authority, this pattern can exhaust disabled volunteers while changing little.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Vienna's Accessibility Council (Austria).** Disabled people hold formal seats on planning committees for public transit and building standards. Any major transport project requires accessibility review with binding input. The result: Vienna's U-Bahn has near-universal step-free access, real-time elevator status alerts, and captions at ticket counters. It's not perfect, but the structure ensured access was baked in from line planning.

**Bogotá's Ciclovía and Disability Collective (Colombia).** When the city redesigned public space for the weekly car-free day, disabled activists insisted on inclusion. The result: ramps, accessible bathrooms, and quiet zones became standard. Neurodivergent people influenced sensory design; wheelchair users shaped surface materials and slope grades. The city didn't just add accessibility—it let disabled people redefine what "public" meant.

**Japan's Community Design Movement—Participatory Access Audits.** In several cities, disabled residents lead design walkthroughs before new stations or public buildings open. They don't just report problems; they co-design solutions on the spot with architects. The practice treats disabled knowledge as expertise, not exception-handling. Some neighborhoods have near-zero post-opening accessibility complaints because disabled people shaped the outcome.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and algorithmic systems will intensify this pattern's urgency—and its possibility.

Right now, algorithms optimizing transit, wayfinding, and urban services typically ignore accessibility. Machine learning models trained on majority movement patterns systematically learn to de-prioritize rare routes, unusual schedules, or accessibility features. Disabled people become invisible to the optimization. But if disabled co-designers write the objective function—if accessibility criteria are baked into what the algorithm is *supposed* to optimize for—AI systems can actually enhance access at scale.

Imagine a wayfinding AI trained not just on speed but on accessibility: shortest *navigable* route, fewest stairs, good lighting, rest spaces. Imagine transit algorithms that reserve capacity for wheelchairs and service animals, not treat them as edge cases. Imagine building management systems that learn and predict accessibility failures before they happen.

But this only works if disabled people help write the rules that machines learn. If algorithms are designed by able-bodied engineers without disability input, they'll be good at excluding people at machine speed. Disabled co-design authority becomes a check on algorithmic harm—a way to ensure that as cities become more automated, they don't become less accessible.

The pattern also scales: distributed veto authority is harder to centralize. If disabled people across neighborhoods hold real power over local infrastructure, it's harder for a city algorithm to override access for "efficiency." Local authority becomes a safeguard against automated erasure.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Disabled people are present in design meetings *before* decisions are made, not after. Access failures trigger visible, documented response—not just repairs, but investigation into why systems failed. Wheelchair users, D/Deaf people, neurodivergent folks, and chronically ill people move through the city visibly, using infrastructure confidently. New projects open with zero or near-zero access complaints because disabled people shaped them. Non-disabled people notice they navigate better too. Budget lines explicitly fund accessibility, not as an afterthought but as a core infrastructure category.

**Signs of decay:** Disabled co-designers are present but powerless—their input is solicited and ignored. Access failures are common and treated as individual problems, not system failures. Accessibility meetings happen after major decisions are made. Only certain kinds of disability are represented (mobility, maybe vision) while neurodiversity, chronic illness, and psychiatric disability remain invisible. Disabled people express exhaustion and burnout; the same few people are asked to solve every access problem. Budget cuts hit accessibility first. The pattern exists on paper but infrastructure keeps failing disabled people the same way.

**Diagnostic question:** When a major infrastructure project is proposed, who decides whether it goes forward—and can disabled people actually stop it? If yes, the pattern is alive. If disabled people have input but not veto, or if they're consulted after blueprints are drawn, it's not working yet.
