> Design mixed-income communities where affordable housing sits alongside job opportunities, letting residents live, work,

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★☆ (Observed)
> This pattern was derived from GraphRAG analysis of the Urban domain.

---

### Section 1: Context

In informal settlements across the Global South, the household is simultaneously a dwelling and a livelihood platform. A home contains a market stall, a workshop, a storage shed, a client waiting area—because income security and housing security are one problem, not two. Yet formal zoning, speculative property capture, and disconnected infrastructure planning treat them as separate. This pattern builds on decades of community-led settlement design (Turner, Habitat III) and the observation that informal residents are overwhelmingly economically active. It also recognises the central threat: as settlements upgrade and land values rise, residents are displaced not by lack of shelter but by inability to afford rising rents or property taxes. The covenant pattern responds by making tenure itself conditional on active livelihood participation—turning economic vitality into the mechanism that preserves community.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Housing as shelter vs. housing as livelihood platform; individual tenure security vs. collective anti-displacement governance.**

Two forces collide. First: residents need tenure security (protection from eviction, the right to invest in housing) but also need livelihood security (space and permission to earn, protection from zoning enforcement). Second: as settlements improve, land value rises, attracting speculative investors and wealthier residents who displace the original community. Formal zoning separates residential from commercial use, criminalizing the mixed-use reality of informal livelihoods. Tenure deeds protect housing but ignore livelihoods. Employment schemes exist but disconnect from housing. Mixed-income covenants exist but without economic obligation. The deeper tension: tenure treated as an individual property right enables speculative capture; tenure treated as a collective responsibility creates the conditions for both housing security and livelihood resilience.

---

### Section 3: Solution

The Livelihood-Embedded Tenure Covenant formalizes housing tenure as a reciprocal obligation: a household holds secure tenure (protected from eviction, with rights to improve dwelling) if and only if at least one resident maintains documented livelihood activity within the settlement. This is not punishment; it is stewardship. The covenant operates at three scales.

First, the household deed: documents specific livelihood activity (market trader, workshop operator, service provider, agricultural producer, waste collector), designates the space within or adjacent to the dwelling where activity occurs, and specifies basic performance standards (operational frequency, externality management, community witness verification). Non-occupancy triggers tenure review; cessation of livelihood triggers engagement with household to sustain activity or transfer tenure to an active household.

Second, the settlement-level covenant: the community collectively negotiates approved livelihood categories, basic operational guidelines, and common space arrangements (market schedules, workshop cluster rules, storage zones). This is governance by residents, not external planners. Zoning enforcement becomes peer-enforced through community witness, not municipal bureaucracy. Disputes are resolved through neighbourhood mediation, not courts.

Third, the anti-displacement mechanism: mixed-income housing remains accessible across settlement lifecycle because tenure is conditional on active residence and livelihood, not property speculation. A resident cannot hold tenure through absentee investment. A wealthier household that moves in can only hold tenure by becoming economically active in the settlement, integrating into the economic fabric rather than extracting value. Rental markets are embedded in livelihood activity (a shopkeeper can rent to the next shopkeeper), not divorced from it.

The covenant is alive because it adapts: livelihood forms change over time (artisan workshops become service hubs, agriculture becomes small manufacturing), and the community renegotiates guidelines. It is shared because residents govern it. It is just because it links tenure to participation, requiring all residents to contribute to settlement vitality. It is built to last because it makes both displacement and abandonment economically irrational.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with mapping—not the sterile kind, but the kind residents themselves conduct. Walk the neighborhood with a notebook. Where do people actually work? A tailor's corner, a phone-repair stall, a daycare run from a front room, a mechanic's shed. Document the informal economy that already exists. This is your baseline.

Next, co-design the tenure covenant with residents, not for them. Bring together a mix: long-term dwellers, younger households seeking livelihoods, elders who've watched the neighborhood change. Define "documented livelihood activity" together. It shouldn't mean formal employment or business registration—that locks people out. It means verifiable: a registered vendor, a client roster, tax stamps, photos, neighbor testimony. The documentation should be loose enough to honor dignity, tight enough to prevent abuse.

Then, physically remake the settlement to embed livelihoods. Widen certain passages to become market streets. Install water points, electricity, waste collection at nodes where vendors cluster. Designate ground-floor housing units as mixed-use: living space upstairs or in back, workspace in front. Retrofit existing structures: remove walls, add shelving, improve ventilation. This isn't gentrification—it's making visible what was already happening.

Create a neighborhood land trust or cooperative that holds title collectively. Individual households get long-term use rights (30+ years, inheritable) but not full ownership. The trust enforces the covenant and—crucially—uses any land appreciation to fund new affordable units and infrastructure. Appreciate slowly by design.

Establish a Tenure and Livelihood Board: elected residents, one staff member, a lawyer. Their job: hear disputes, adjust covenants as conditions change, track who's at risk of displacement. They meet monthly. They have teeth—the power to intervene before eviction happens.

Connect to regional job networks. Work with employers in nearby industries—manufacturing, logistics, health care—to create hiring agreements. Not charity; mutual benefit. Employers gain stable, local workforce. Residents gain wages that compound with their household earnings.

### Section 5: Consequences

What flourishes: Residents invest in their homes because they belong. A woman installs a tile floor in her unit, knowing she's building for her daughter. A carpenter expands his workshop because he's not three months from eviction. Children see their parents earning locally; education and aspiration shift. The neighborhood thickens with trust—people know each other across formal and informal ties.

Economic velocity increases. Money earned locally circulates locally: a vendor buys from a neighbor's stall, who pays the tailor, who buys from the grocer. Informal credit networks strengthen. Mutual aid deepens.

Anti-displacement takes shape without needing police. When a speculator buys a unit, the covenant transfers to them—they must maintain livelihood activity or forfeit the property back to the trust. This kills most speculation. Land values still rise, but slowly and collectively captured.

But risks emerge if attention lapses. The covenant can calcify into gatekeeping: "You don't have the *right kind* of livelihood," says a board member to a young mother whose informal care network doesn't register neatly. Bureaucracy can strangle the very informality it meant to protect. Women's unpaid care work may be excluded from "documented livelihood."

If the trust becomes corrupt or captured by wealthy residents, it becomes a tool of displacement wearing a community mask. If employers don't follow through on hiring agreements, residents remain trapped in low-wage self-employment.

The neighborhood can also become isolated, a gilded informal settlement that improves housing while leaving regional inequality untouched. Without regional job linkage and education, intergenerational mobility stalls.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Jakarta, Indonesia—RW-based Cooperatives (Informal)**: In neighborhoods like Kampung Bali, resident associations (RW) have operated de facto tenure covenants for decades. A household's right to stay depends on participation in communal work and maintenance. As formalization pressures mount, some RWs are registering their arrangements with city government—not abolishing the covenant but making it legible. The result: residents have shown higher investment in housing, lower voluntary out-migration, and stronger collective infrastructure maintenance than neighborhoods without this social contract.

**Nairobi, Kenya—Mathare Social Centre**: A housing cooperative in Mathare Valley has implemented a hybrid model where members own shares in collective land while leasing individual plots. Tenure rights are conditional on participating in income-generating activities within the cooperative (a market, a training center, a laundry). The covenant isn't written into formal law but into membership bylaws. Displacement has nearly ceased; women's income from market stalls has risen 40% over seven years because they can invest in permanent structures.

**Mexico City—Tepito Workshop Districts**: For centuries, Tepito's mixed-use neighborhoods combined residential living with jewelry-making, textile production, and trade. The municipality recently began recognizing artisan workshops as heritage uses in zoning, protecting them from removal and allowing residents to formalize their workspace without losing tenure. This isn't an explicit covenant, but it operates on the same principle: housing and livelihood are one problem, and protecting one protects the other.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI enters neighborhood governance, this pattern faces a choice: become more rigid or more resilient.

The risk: algorithms audit the covenant. A resident's "documented livelihood" gets flagged by a system trained on formal employment patterns. An AI recommendation engine suggests "optimal" land use, clustering all vendors in one zone, all housing in another—re-separating what the pattern meant to integrate. Algorithmic governance could strangle the very informality the covenant protects.

The opportunity: distributed ledgers and open-source tools can make the covenant transparent and tamper-proof without centralizing power. Residents photograph their work, timestamp it on a community blockchain, proving livelihood activity without a bureaucrat's judgment. Peer-to-peer verification replaces top-down auditing.

AI-assisted pattern recognition can also surface displacement risks early. Monitoring sudden property transfers, rent spikes, or construction near a neighborhood can trigger intervention before residents are pushed out. Residents themselves train the system, not distant data scientists.

The deeper shift: AI makes collective decision-making more feasible. A neighborhood covenant affects hundreds of households. With tools for real-time deliberation, polling, and consent-tracking, the Tenure and Livelihood Board can move from monthly meetings to continuous community input. Not algorithmic governance—algorithmic support for human collective intelligence.

But this requires that residents, not platforms, own the data and the tools. Otherwise, the cognitive era simply makes old displacement faster and more legible.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of Life**: Walk the neighborhood in the morning. Are people repairing their homes? Are tools and materials stacked outside units? Are new vendors arriving (not fleeing)? Listen for disagreement—if the Tenure Board is debating edge cases loudly, the covenant is alive, not ossified. Count how many residents can name their neighbors' livelihoods, not just their names. Ask a child what their parent does—if the answer is "works nearby," the pattern is working.

Track housing investment: renovations, expansions, improved materials. Not gentrified glossiness, but care. Track livelihood income: are people earning more or less than five years ago? Are young people staying or leaving?

**Signs of Decay**: Silence is the first warning. If the Tenure Board never convenes disputes, either the covenant is perfectly just (unlikely) or it's being ignored. If property transfers spike—outsiders buying, residents selling—the anti-displacement mechanism has failed. If vendors are disappearing, replaced by formal retailers, the livelihood embedding has been hollowed out.

Listen for who's absent from the Board. Are women represented? Young people? If the board is captured by elders or a single faction, decay has begun.

**The Diagnostic Question**: *If a longtime resident lost their livelihood tomorrow—fell ill, business failed—would the neighborhood support them in finding new work, or would the covenant treat them as expired?* 

A healthy pattern holds tenure sacred while remaining flexible about what livelihoods count. It treats the covenant as a covenant—a mutual obligation to care—not a contract to enforce. If the answer to that question is "we'd help them," the pattern is alive.
