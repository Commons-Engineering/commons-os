### Section 1: Context

Public space is a commons — a shared resource where inhabitants move, gather, work, and rest without prior permission. Yet surveillance infrastructure (cameras, facial recognition, behavioral algorithms, traffic sensors) has converted many commons into monitored territory, creating asymmetrical visibility: residents are continuously observed while observers remain invisible. This erosion accelerates in low-income and racialized neighbourhoods, where algorithmic targeting and enforcement feedback loops concentrate both surveillance density and policing. The Orbit 1 principles of Reciprocity, Subsidiarity, and Collective Action all point toward the same insight: a commons survives when those who share it can see each other and hold one another accountable. Surveillance that flows only downward — from state or corporation to citizen — violates the reciprocity that sustains trust. This pattern emerges from lineage to Foucault's panopticon, contemporary digital rights movements, neighbourhood assemblies practicing collective refusal, and the lived experience of communities reclaiming commons by making observation mutual and governance participatory.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Asymmetrical visibility (authorities watch, citizens are watched) vs. Reciprocal observation (mutual accountability and shared governance of sight).**

Surveillance systems generate a specific injury: the loss of sanctuary. Inhabitants lose the psychological and social space to exist without documentation, to gather informally, to move through public space without inducing algorithmic suspicion or generating data traces that can later be weaponized. Over time, hypervigilance erodes mental resilience. Self-censorship spreads. Youth, vendors, and people already marked by surveillance algorithms experience compound visibility: they are watched more, documented more, and subject to enforcement more. Consent for surveillance erodes once residents realise its true scope — the initial promise of 'limited, safety-focused' systems gives way to recognition of pervasive tracking, behavioral prediction, and automated enforcement. Communities respond with passive resistance (avoiding public space, wearing masks, disabling devices) but lack mechanisms to resist collectively, to set shared thresholds, or to restore reciprocal accountability. The commons withers not from resource scarcity but from visibility colonization — the appropriation of the ability to see and be seen.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Build stewardship of visibility as a commons governance practice. Establish participatory assemblies (neighbourhood, district, sector) with explicit mandate to audit all surveillance infrastructure and data collection affecting the community. This assembly becomes the legitimate authority — above city, platform, or police — for determining what observation is acceptable and what crosses the threshold of commons-erosion. The governance structure operates in iterative cycles: inventory all surveillance (cameras, sensors, algorithms, data collection); make visible to community what is being watched, by whom, with what authority; establish community thresholds for acceptable surveillance density and function; audit compliance annually; mandate removal or disabling of systems that exceed thresholds or lack community consent.

Simultaneously, design counter-surveillance commons: spaces and networks explicitly protected from technological observation. These include physical refuge commons (parks, gathering spaces, buildings) where cameras are prohibited and enforced through community monitors; digital infrastructure (platforms, communication networks) designed with privacy-by-default (encryption, anonymity options, minimal metadata, distributed servers, mandatory deletion); and reciprocal observation networks where community members (shopkeepers, caretakers, street vendors, youth) serve as informal witnesses through natural presence rather than algorithmic tracking.

Restore symmetry by making authorities visible and accountable: require public dashboards showing surveillance camera locations, data retention periods, and algorithmic functions; mandate regular public reporting on what data was collected, who accessed it, and how it was used; establish participatory audit rights where residents co-determine what collection is legitimate; create legal protections for collective refusal (opt-out zones where specific technologies are prohibited). Youth and vulnerable populations lead design of governance structures and audit teams, ensuring the most surveilled groups become stewards of the commons rather than passive data subjects. This pattern treats refusal as a collective practice — negotiated, visible, enforceable — rather than individual opt-out, rebuilding trust through reciprocal accountability.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping what watches. Walk your neighbourhood or district with neighbours and ask: Where are cameras? Who owns them? What data do they collect? What happens to it? This isn't paranoia—it's attention. You're learning the shape of observation the way a gardener learns the microclimate of a plot.

Create a participatory assembly. This can be as informal as a monthly community meeting or as structured as a formally recognized local board. The key is legitimacy born from participation, not appointment. Invite people who move through the space daily: parents, vendors, night workers, young people, elders, houseless residents. Include at least one person with technical literacy who can translate surveillance language into plain speech.

Establish a transparency protocol. Every camera, sensor, algorithm, or data system affecting public space gets documented: What is it? Who operates it? What does it record? Where does data flow? When does it get deleted—or is it kept forever? Most municipalities have public records laws; use them ruthlessly. Where records are denied, that's data too.

Create "seeing back" mechanisms. These might include: community cameras positioned toward power holders (town halls, police stations) to document official behaviour; public dashboards showing real-time traffic or air quality data that was previously hoarded; citizen audits of algorithmic decisions; regular open data releases about how surveillance is actually deployed versus how it's claimed to be deployed.

Develop consent frameworks. Which kinds of observation does your community actually want? Traffic flow monitoring to improve bus routes? Yes. Facial recognition in parks? No. Thermal imaging to count homeless people? That depends—ask them first. Make decisions visible and reversible. When new surveillance systems arrive, the assembly gets to vote before implementation, not after.

Build relationships with institutions. Police chiefs, city planners, platform managers often don't know communities are watching back. Shift from accusation to negotiation: "Here's what we need to see to trust you. Here's what we're willing to show about ourselves. Let's make those trades explicit."

### Section 5: Consequences

When reciprocal visibility takes root, trust begins to accumulate. Police become reluctant to beat someone when a dozen phone cameras are trained on them. City planners become attentive to actual community needs when residents are auditing budget data. Platforms adjust algorithms when communities publish findings about bias. The watching becomes mutual, and mutual accountability changes behaviour.

Mental space expands. When inhabitants know they've collectively set the boundaries of observation, when they know those boundaries are enforced by their neighbours rather than arbitrary authorities, surveillance feels less like suffocation. Informal gathering returns. Youth spend time in parks. Vendors set up without panic. The commons becomes habitable again.

Communities develop technical competence. People learn to read data, understand algorithmic bias, operate cameras, build dashboards. This competence spreads. It ripples into other decisions—how neighbourhoods allocate budgets, how they respond to development proposals, how they negotiate with institutions.

But risks emerge if this work isn't tended:

**Surveillance consensus can become surveillance expansion.** If the assembly votes to add cameras "for safety," you've now normalized the infrastructure and made it harder to resist when it's later weaponized. Vigilance requires ongoing dissent, not just consensus.

**Power imbalances can hide inside "participation."** If the assembly is dominated by homeowners while renters and unhoused neighbours have no real voice, you've created the appearance of reciprocal visibility while reproducing the original asymmetry. Deliberate outreach and accessibility work is non-negotiable.

**Data can leak or be stolen.** If your community builds counter-surveillance systems with sensitive information about police misconduct or algorithmic bias, that data becomes a target. Security practices matter. So does knowing what you can't safely document.

**Burnout and surveillance fatigue.** Watching the watchers requires sustained attention. Volunteers burn out. The work becomes another unpaid burden.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Santa Cruz, California (2015-present).** The Community Justice Campaign built a participatory oversight board for police surveillance systems. They mapped all cameras and license plate readers, published the data publicly, and negotiated transparency agreements. When the police department proposed facial recognition tools, the community said no, and the city listened. The practice became a model for other California cities.

**Barcelona, Decidim Platform (2016-present).** Rather than letting city surveillance expand unchecked, Barcelona's participatory budgeting and governance platform (Decidim) made surveillance decisions subject to citizen voting. Communities collectively chose which public space monitoring was acceptable, and the city was contractually bound to publish data about compliance. The platform now operates in dozens of cities globally, shifting surveillance from technical fait accompli to democratic choice.

**Reclaim the Streets / Peoples Global Action (1990s-2000s, UK and global South).** Before algorithmic surveillance, activists organized reciprocal observation of police during street protests. They assigned people to document police violence, shared video evidence, built networks to protect participants. They made clear: we're watching you watch us. The practice evolved into "cop watching" and livestreaming during direct actions, using distributed witnesses as accountability.

**Ushahidi, Kenya (2008-present).** After election violence in Kenya, citizens built a crowdsourced mapping platform to document human rights abuses—turning surveillance logic sideways. Instead of authorities watching people, people documented authorities. The platform became a model for conflict monitoring, police accountability, and community-generated evidence in over 100 countries. It shows how "watching the watchers" can be scaled across regions.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI changes the speed and scale at which authorities can watch. Algorithms can track a person through a city in real-time, predict behaviour before it happens, connect dots across years of data that humans could never manually analyze. The asymmetry has gotten harder to see—and therefore more dangerous.

But distributed intelligence—communities connected through tools, data literacy spreading, algorithms being audited by multiple eyes—creates new possibilities for reciprocal watching. A single person can't outthink a facial recognition system. But a community can. They can:

- **Crowd-source audits.** Thousands of people test an algorithm, find its biases, publish findings. The system becomes visible in ways monolithic oversight never could.
- **Build counter-algorithms.** Communities can train their own AI systems to spot bias, predict where discriminatory policing will happen, identify which algorithms are being used where. Seeing through algorithms requires algorithmic seeing.
- **Distribute the watchers.** Instead of one camera pointed down, thousands of cameras pointed up, analyzing the same feeds, cross-checking findings, making collusion harder.
- **Make data flow transparent.** APIs and data interoperability can expose who is trading information about residents, creating visibility into the invisible flows of data between institutions.

The risk: if AI surveillance becomes the norm before communities build reciprocal visibility, the asymmetry will calcify. The window for mutual accountability will close. This pattern works best when it's built *before* full algorithmic deployment, when communities still have leverage.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

- Surveillance decisions are public and contested before implementation, not justified after. People argue about whether a camera should exist, and the decision is theirs to make.
- Institutions acknowledge (sometimes grudgingly) that they're being watched back. Police departments release use-of-force data. City planners explain camera placement to residents.
- New people join the work. Teenagers learn to read data. Elders teach context. The practice becomes intergenerational, not volunteer burnout.
- The assembly develops teeth. When agreements are broken, communities have mechanisms to escalate—formal complaints, media attention, withheld cooperation, or direct action.
- Technical tools exist and are maintained. Dashboards work. Data is current. Community members can operate them without depending on any single person.

**Signs of decay:**

- Surveillance questions disappear from community conversation. The cameras are just... there. Normalized. Unquestionable.
- The assembly becomes a rubber stamp. Meetings happen but decisions are already made elsewhere. Participation becomes theatre.
- Institutions install new surveillance and apologize later, knowing community response will be too slow. They've learned they can move faster than oversight.
- The watchers get tired. Attendance drops. Data becomes stale. The documentation effort collapses into silence.
- Surveillance expands into new domains (schools, transport, health) before communities have even begun mapping existing systems. They're always catching up, never ahead.

**Diagnostic question:** 

*Can you point to something your community said no to—some camera, some algorithm, some data system—that wasn't installed because you collectively refused it?* 

If yes, reciprocal visibility is real. If no, you're still asking permission. The asymmetry remains.
