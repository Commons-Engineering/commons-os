### Section 1: Context

In both Global North and South cities, futures are typically designed by small coalitions: developers, planners, technocrats, and capital holders who invest in foresight capacity and scenario planning. Low-income residents, informal workers, Indigenous communities, and other systematically excluded populations navigate futures reactively, without voice in shaping the infrastructure, governance, and economic systems that will constrain or enable their lives. This asymmetry is not accidental—it is structural. Meanwhile, planning processes often suppress multiple temporalities, treating linear progress as the only legitimate time-horizon and erasing cyclical, ancestral, and alternative ways of understanding change. The result: urban futures that replicate extraction, inequality, and ecological degradation rather than enabling genuine alternatives. Yet distributed throughout cities and regions are communities with embedded foresight knowledge—survival strategies, cultural practices, governance experiments—that remain invisible in masterplans. Decolonial and participatory traditions point toward a different possibility: futures as a commons to be governed collectively, with decision-making distributed across scales and epistemologies.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Futures-as-imposed (designed by external powers, technocrats, capital) vs. Futures-as-stewarded (collectively imagined, governed, and inhabited by those most affected).**

Three interlocking tensions define this problem. First: **Foresight Asymmetry**. Wealthier actors invest heavily in scenario planning and have institutional capacity to imagine preferred futures; poorer populations and informal economies lack time, resources, and cultural permission to participate in visioning, yet bear the consequences of imposed futures most acutely. Second: **Temporality Erasure**. Planning processes centered on linear, progress-oriented time horizons delegitimize cyclical, ancestral, and relational understandings of change held by Indigenous and Global South communities, foreclosing whole categories of possible futures. Third: **Knowledge Monopoly**. Professional planners and technocrats claim epistemic authority to know what futures are possible and desirable, treating other ways of knowing—experiential, spiritual, artistic, craft-based—as inputs to be translated into expert language rather than legitimate knowledge systems in their own right. Together, these tensions mean that communities have little voice in shaping the futures that shape them.

---

### Section 3: Solution

**Futures Stewardship as Commons Governance**: Treat the collective right to participate in imagining and shaping urban futures as a commons—a shared, distributed, and continuously renewed resource of foresight capacity. Operationalize this through five interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Distributed Foresight Capacity Building.** Systematically build futures literacy across populations and sectors, beginning in schools and extending into informal economies, community organizations, and neighborhood institutions. This is not one-way training but reciprocal knowledge documentation—surfacing and legitimizing the scenario knowledge already embedded in street vendors, artisans, informal manufacturers, mobile workers, and other communities who navigate uncertain futures daily. Create institutional pathways (neighborhood visioning circles, trade-based foresight groups, school curricula) where this knowledge becomes visible and influential in governance decisions.

**2. Plural Temporalities Legitimation.** Explicitly acknowledge and work with multiple time horizons simultaneously: ancestral time (obligations to those who came before), relational time (seasonal, cyclical rhythms), institutional time (planning cycles), and linear time (milestone-based progress). Honor these as equally legitimate frameworks for understanding change. In scenario planning, map how different futures honor different temporal logics—some prioritizing cultural continuity across generations, others emphasizing adaptation to immediate climate shocks, others centering regenerative cycles. Name trade-offs rather than forcing false consensus.

**3. Epistemological Pluralism in Visioning.** Create methodological space for multiple knowledge systems to articulate futures: scientific data and modeling, Indigenous temporal knowledge, experiential wisdom from lived experience, artistic and spiritual framings, craft-based understanding. Use cultural translation rather than translation to common language—allowing visions to be articulated through different frameworks (storytelling, scenario portraiture, ritual enactment, technical modeling) without reducing them to a single register. This prevents the erasure of non-Western, non-linear narratives.

**4. Governance Localization & Subsidiarity.** Devolve foresight authority to the lowest competent scale: neighborhood-scale futures agencies, district-level governance councils, community-controlled infrastructure decision-making. Global-scale and city-wide scenarios still exist, but they emerge from and remain accountable to nested, semi-autonomous centers of visioning. This prevents top-down futures and enables place-specific alternatives to emerge.

**5. Futures Visioning as Ongoing Commons Act.** Treat participatory futures work not as a one-time planning exercise but as continuous stewardship—regular community gatherings (visioning circles, scenario workshops, conflict-honoring forums) where residents collectively imagine, test, refine, and adapt their preferred futures as conditions change. Make this a recognized civic right and institutional practice, not an optional add-on. Document emerging visions and feed them back into governance and investment decisions, creating accountability loops where foresight participation shapes actual outcomes.

The outcome is not consensus on a single future but a mature commons of foresight: distributed capacity to imagine alternatives, legitimate pathways for excluded voices to shape trajectories, explicit honoring of multiple temporalities and knowledge systems, and governance structures that remain accountable to those most affected by change.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by **mapping existing foresight**—the ways communities already imagine futures. This isn't absent; it's embedded in stories elders tell, in how informal economies adapt, in how families plan across generations. Recognize these as legitimate forms of futures knowledge. Convene small groups (8–15 people) in neighborhoods, not city halls. Use methods that feel native to how people actually think together: storytelling circles, photovoice, role-play, walking tours that ask "what could this corner become?"

**Build scaffolding gradually.** Begin with one neighborhood cycle: listening to existing hopes, mapping constraints and assets, creating shared visions in multiple formats (drawings, videos, written scenarios). Don't rush to consensus—honor plural futures. Document these visions in accessible forms: murals, podcasts, zines, not just reports. Pay people for their time and thinking.

**Create institutional anchors** that outlive projects. A community futures lab, housed locally (a community center, a co-working space), staffed by residents trained in facilitation and foresight methods. Partner it with one planning department office or municipality, creating legal standing for community visions in official processes. This isn't advisory—it's co-governance. Community-imagined futures should require incorporation into development plans or explicit public justification for rejecting them.

**Weave together multiple timescales.** Some people think in 5-year cycles; others in 20-year lifespans; some in ancestral or seven-generation timescales. Create spaces where these temporalities coexist. An elder's vision of land restoration, a young parent's hope for safe streets, a young person's dream of new work possibilities—all legitimate, all informing the collective future.

**Cross-pollinate visions.** Share scenarios between neighborhoods. Invite dialogue about confluences and tensions. Where do different communities' futures support or contradict each other? This builds citywide stewardship, not siloed neighborhood futures.

### Section 5: Consequences

When communities genuinely imagine their own futures, several things flourish. **Agency deepens**—people move from complaint to creation, from reactive to generative. Trust in institutions grows when visions are actually incorporated, not performed and discarded. **Local knowledge surfaces**—solutions emerge from people who live with problems daily, often more adaptive and rooted than top-down designs. **Cultural continuity** persists; futures aren't erased of older ways of knowing and being. Neighborhoods become places of genuine belonging, not just residence.

The risks demand tending. **Co-optation** is constant; institutions may accept community visions while implementing stripped-down, safe versions. Require binding commitments, not letters of intent. **Exhaustion of commons** can occur when the same people are asked to imagine endlessly without seeing results; this breeds cynicism. Deliver tangible change from first cycles before expanding. **Conflict surfacing**—genuine visioning reveals real disagreements about futures (density vs. preservation, development vs. environmental protection). This is healthy, but requires skilled facilitation and genuine willingness to negotiate, not hide behind technical "objectivity."

**Power asymmetries persist.** A community futures lab doesn't erase the developer with more capital or the municipality with final say. The pattern works only when it's backed by policy change and resource reallocation. Without that, it becomes consultation theater.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Participatory Budgeting in New York and Paris.** Residents directly shape how municipal funds are allocated—not imagining abstract futures, but making present-tense decisions that embody their values. This distributes foresight power through actual resource control.

**Favela Painting and Community Visioning in Rio de Janeiro.** Residents of informal settlements co-designed neighborhood transformations through art and dialogue, moving from external "slum clearance" narratives to self-determined futures. Visions were grounded in existing culture and aspirations, not erasure.

**Ubuntu Futures Process in South African townships.** Using Indigenous governance frameworks ("ubuntu"—I am because we are), communities imagined and stewarded urban futures through emphasis on collective wellbeing over individual property logic. Elders' knowledge and young people's energy were woven together across seven-generation thinking.

**Phoenix Futures Lab (Arizona).** Neighborhood residents from predominantly Latino and low-income areas co-created scenarios for climate adaptation, housing, and work over 10 years—with binding city commitment to incorporate community visions into official plans. Results shifted from abstract sustainability documents to lived neighborhood transformation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and distributed intelligence reshape this pattern radically. **Scenario modeling becomes faster, more accessible.** Communities can now test futures—"if we densify here, what happens to tree cover and informal economies?"—without waiting for expensive planning consultants. AI can surface patterns in community input that humans miss, revealing confluences across visions.

But new risks emerge. **Algorithmic futures** can appear neutral while encoding the biases of their designers. A tool predicting "optimal" futures might systematize extraction. Communities need literacy not just in futures-thinking but in recognizing when tools constrain imagination.

**Distributed foresight becomes genuinely distributed.** Not everyone needs to gather in one room. Digital platforms (used carefully, with offline alternatives) can enable asynchronous participation from people with caregiving duties, irregular work, or mobility constraints. This democratizes *when* and *how* people imagine.

The deeper shift: **AI becomes a commons tool if governed as such.** Scenarios, data, modeling capacity could be community-owned infrastructure—like the lab itself. Communities train their own people to use these tools, retaining sovereignty over interpretation and decision-making. Without this, AI becomes another asymmetry: the wealthy imagine futures with AI; the poor are modeled *into* them.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Communities are actively imagining—creating visions, not just responding to proposals. Diverse generations and sectors show up (not just activists; ordinary people). Visions shift over time as conditions change, showing responsiveness, not calcification. When plans are implemented, community members recognize their own ideas in them. Trust in local institutions rises. Young people stay or return because futures feel possible, shaped by people like them.

**Signs of decay:** Participation narrows to the same voices (usually more educated, more time, more cultural permission). Visions remain symbolic—displayed in reports, never resourced. Months pass with no visible change; exhaustion replaces hope. Community futures become another consultation checkbox while real decisions happen elsewhere. The lab exists but has no actual power over land use, budgets, or development approvals. Institutions listen but don't cede authority.

**The diagnostic question:** *If a community's imagined future directly conflicted with a developer's proposal or a municipality's existing plan, would that community's vision win, or would it be politely filed away?* 

If the answer is "it depends on political will" rather than "it's protected by policy and resources," the pattern is vital in intent but weak in teeth. True stewardship requires structural power—not just voice, but choice.
