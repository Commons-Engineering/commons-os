### Section 1: Context

In Global South cities, informal settlements house over 1 billion people—not as a temporary failure of formalization, but as a persistent, adaptive system of tenure, dwelling, and community. These settlements operate across a spectrum: some residents hold occupancy through long kinship ties and customary law; others through squatting and mutual recognition; still others through informal landlord-tenant relationships with no written lease. This reality exists at the boundary of formal property law, where the state simultaneously ignores and threatens informal residents—tolerating them for decades, then suddenly enforcing code or claiming land for development. The core problem is not informality itself, but the instability of that boundary. When residents face oscillating legal status, they cannot invest in their homes, accumulate assets, pass inheritance, or resist exploitation. Conversely, when formalization happens too fast—titling without community consent, service provision that triggers property tax spikes, or legalization that enables landlord extraction—it becomes a vector for displacement. This pattern draws from the lived experience of informal settlement communities across Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America, and from the 51 pattern variants that encode the same core insight: tenure security must be resident-controlled, staged, and designed to prevent both precarity and dispossession.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Tenure security vs. displacement risk; formalization as protection vs. formalization as dispossession.**

Informal residents face two equally lethal risks: precarity and formalization trap. Precarity—where tenure remains unstable, contested, or invisible—prevents investment, inheritance, disaster resilience, and collective action. Insecurity corrodes community cohesion and makes residents vulnerable to sudden eviction for development, taxation disputes, or arbitrary enforcement. But formalization, as currently designed by states and development agencies, often triggers the opposite catastrophe: once informal land is mapped, titled, and taxed, property values rise, rents escalate, and outside investors and landlords move in. Original residents—who built the settlement and stewarded it for decades—face displacement because they cannot absorb rising taxes, land values, or new rents. Formalization intended as protection becomes the mechanism of dispossession. The hidden force: every formalization process transfers value. The question is whether residents or external actors (landlords, speculators, developers, states) capture that value. Without community control over the pace, terms, and governance of formalization, tenure security becomes a Trojan horse for rent extraction and accumulation. Informal residents are trapped between two kinds of dispossession: eviction through continued informality, or displacement through forced formalization.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Implement Graduated Resident-Controlled Tenure Formalization: a staged pathway where informal residents collectively decide which formal protections they adopt, maintain governance authority throughout, and retain value capture as formalization unfolds.

**Core structure:**

**Stage 1: Enumeration & Recognition.** Conduct community-led mapping and documentation of actual residents, occupancy duration, dwelling conditions, and community governance structures. This is not state enumeration but community self-documentation, creating evidence that informal residents exist and deserve recognition. Publish findings visibly; this reverses the legitimacy pattern where informality means administrative invisibility.

**Stage 2: Collective Tenure Documentation.** Communities draft occupancy registries (names, dwelling dates, kinship ties, customary claims, informal lease agreements) in partnership with legal advocates, creating a community-authored evidence base. This is lighter than individual titling; it documents plural claims (customary, occupancy, lease) and preserves the legal plurality that informal residents navigate. These registries become negotiating tools with government and protection against eviction.

**Stage 3: Community-Negotiated Legalization Covenant.** Rather than imposing formalization, establish a governance process where residents and municipal government co-design legalization terms: tenure type offered (usufruct, leasehold, freehold, collective title), infrastructure investment schedule, property tax or community revenue sharing terms, building code flexibility pathways, and anti-displacement safeguards. Residents vote on acceptance; government commits to staged investment matching tenure stage. This is subsidiarity—decisions made at community level, not imposed from above.

**Stage 4: Phased Formal Recognition.** Implement legalization in sequence, not all at once: first, tenure security (eviction protections, inheritance rights, disaster insurance eligibility); then, infrastructure investment (water, sanitation, roads); then, optional property formalization (titling for those who choose it). Each stage is reversible if community withdraws consent.

**Stage 5: Value Capture Governance.** Establish mechanisms for residents to capture upgrade-related value: community land trusts that hold collective title, community development corporations that contract infrastructure work and distribute margins to residents, or community revenue-sharing agreements where property tax increases fund resident services rather than displacing communities. Tax bands calibrated to demonstrated income, not imputed property value, prevent asset-stripping through taxation.

**Stage 6: Adaptive Review & Exit.** Communities periodically (every 2-3 years) assess formalization impacts: Is tenure actually more secure? Are rents rising faster than incomes? Are residents being taxed into displacement? Is community cohesion maintained? Communities retain the right to pause formalization, renegotiate terms, or revert to prior stages if formalization proves extractive. Formalization is not a one-way gate but an adaptive cycle.

**Critical design rules:** Resident decision-making authority is non-delegable—communities must vote on covenant terms, not governments. Formalization is staged, not sudden; residents build formally at their own pace. Value capture is collective, not individual—individual titling is optional, not required. Legal plurality is preserved; formal title coexists with customary and occupancy claims rather than erasing them. Anti-displacement covenants are binding—tenure security is not revoked if land values rise. Community governance remains rooted; informal leaders and organisations (women's groups, savings circles, dispute resolution elders) retain authority through formalization rather than being replaced by state or market institutions.

This pattern transforms formalization from a top-down vector of dispossession into a community-steered pathway where residents upgrade their own tenure security, maintain collective governance, and preserve the adaptive strengths of informal settlement systems while gaining the formal protections those systems lack.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start where residents already are—not with blueprints, but with listening. Convene community assemblies to map who lives where, how long they've been there, what disputes exist, and what protection they actually want. This enumeration belongs to residents first; external documentation follows their priorities, not the reverse.

Create a tiered recognition system that residents control. Stage 1 might be a community-issued occupancy certificate—signed by elected settlement leaders, witnessed by neighbors, recorded in a settlement registry kept locally. This costs nothing, takes weeks, and shifts the burden of proof: the resident now holds evidence of tenure, not the burden of proving it to authorities. Many residents stop here; that's valid. They've gained internal security and inheritance clarity.

For those seeking deeper protection, Stage 2 involves negotiating with local government for *conditional* formalization: residents agree to basic infrastructure standards (drainage, fire access, sanitation) in exchange for government recognition of occupancy rights—without forced relocation or retroactive property taxes. This is not full title; it's a covenant between community and city.

Only in Stage 3, if residents choose, does formal registration occur—but now residents are negotiating from strength: they've demonstrated governance capacity, invested in improvements, and proven occupancy. They can push back on excessive fees, demand transparent valuation, and secure inheritance and lending rights without losing community authority.

Throughout: establish a resident-led tenure committee that reviews each step, holds veto power over formalization terms, and manages dispute resolution internally before state involvement. This committee becomes the institution that mediates between informal and formal systems—translating community norms into language the state recognizes, while protecting against co-optation.

Crucially, formalization happens in clusters (neighborhoods or blocks), not individual properties. Collective tenure reduces administrative burden and preserves community cohesion. Residents negotiate collectively; they benefit collectively; they enforce collectively.

### Section 5: Consequences

**What flourishes:** When residents control the pace and terms of formalization, tenure security deepens. Residents invest in homes—better materials, sanitation, structural safety—because they know they won't lose them overnight. Children inherit without dispute. Women gain collateral for loans and legal standing in family disputes. Community cohesion strengthens because formalization is chosen, not imposed.

Residents also gain negotiating power with the city. A formally recognized settlement with elected leadership can demand services—water, waste collection, emergency access—rather than beg. Disaster resilience improves because formal tenure allows residents to upgrade housing before disaster strikes.

**What risks emerge:** Formalization can become a Trojan horse for dispossession if residents lose control of the process. If external actors rush formalization—rushing residents into individual titling without understanding the consequences—residents may end up with papers but no community, or discover the state has seized land "for public use" while registering only individual plots. This happens repeatedly when NGOs or governments treat formalization as a technical fix rather than a political negotiation.

Formalized property can be seized by creditors, sold under debt pressure, or made vulnerable to property taxes residents can't pay. External investors spotting "formal" settlements may suddenly appear with buyout offers, fragmenting communities. Formal tenure also makes residents visible to authorities for tax collection, military recruitment, or police surveillance in ways informal settlements sometimes shield.

The pattern fails if residents are not genuinely in control—if committees become rubber stamps, if paperwork is imposed faster than trust builds, or if the state uses formalization as a pretext for densification and displacement.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Orangi Pilot Project (Karachi, Pakistan):** For four decades, residents of Orangi—a massive informal settlement—have led their own tenure formalization and infrastructure upgrading. Community organizations surveyed the settlement, created occupancy records, and negotiated with government for services while residents retained governance of internal disputes and land use. No individual titling; instead, residents hold community-recognized occupancy certificates and collective infrastructure rights. Residents have invested heavily in their homes; inheritance is clear; eviction is rare. The approach has been replicated across South Asia.

**Mathare Social Justice Centre (Nairobi, Kenya):** After decades of eviction threats, Mathare residents organized a community land trust model where the community collectively holds tenure rights while residents lease plots from the trust. This formalization happened on residents' terms: they documented occupancy, elected trust leadership, and negotiated government recognition as a collective entity rather than seeking individual titles. Residents maintain governance; the state recognizes the collective; individual residents are protected.

**Vila Autódromo (Rio de Janeiro, Brazil):** When threatened with demolition for Olympic development, residents formalized their settlement through a combination of community self-documentation, legal advocacy, and collective resistance. They negotiated a community land trust arrangement where the city recognized residents' collective tenure rights while upgrading infrastructure. Though partial—some residents were relocated—the pattern showed how collective formalization can be a tool of resistance against dispossession, not just accommodation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

AI and data tools can accelerate enumeration and verification—satellite imagery combined with community testimony can map settlements quickly; blockchain ledgers can record occupancy without centralized authority; predictive modeling can show which formalization pathways reduce eviction risk in specific contexts.

But the risk is clear: outsourcing tenure decisions to algorithms or centralized data systems weakens resident control. If a government uses AI to "optimize" settlement formalization—clustering residents differently than they prefer, prioritizing individual over collective tenure, or flagging "irregular" plots for clearance—technology becomes a tool of dispossession.

The pattern strengthens in the cognitive era when residents themselves use data tools. Community-controlled mapping platforms, resident-operated blockchain registries, and AI-assisted dispute resolution (trained on community norms rather than state law) can deepen tenure security while preserving resident agency. Distributed intelligence—where AI serves community decision-making rather than replacing it—allows formalization to scale without centralization.

The key: keep data and decision-making close to residents. Technology serves enumeration and record-keeping; humans and communities make tenure decisions.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Residents are actively documenting occupancy without external pressure. Community committees meet regularly and are contested—people argue, vote, change decisions. Younger residents are inheriting property without dispute or selling it deliberately, not fleeing. Women reference tenure documents in property and family disputes and are heard. Residents are investing in housing upgrades—better materials, sanitation, structural improvements—because they believe they'll be there in five years. New residents negotiate entry with existing residents, not the state. The settlement negotiates with government as a body, not individuals begging for services.

**Signs of decay:** Formalization has slowed or stopped; residents are confused about what documents mean. Tenure committees exist but don't meet; decisions are made by elders or strongmen, not collectively. Residents are reluctant to document occupancy—they fear taxation or state surveillance more than eviction risk. Young people are leaving; inheritance is contested. Women are excluded from tenure decisions or their tenure depends on male relatives. External actors (developers, NGOs, government) are pushing formalization faster than residents want it. Residents are selling land rapidly to outsiders; the settlement is becoming a market commodity rather than a community. Eviction threats have resumed despite formalization.

**Diagnostic question:** If the government announced a densification project—"turn your informal settlement into a formal neighborhood with 10 times the residents"—would residents say yes, no, or "only if we design it"? If the answer is not the last one, the pattern has weakened. Resident control has shifted to somewhere else.
