### Section 1: Context

Every living system is a network of interdependent actors with different interests, power capacities, and resource needs. Yet across domains—business ecosystems, ecological commons, urban co-governance, personal relationships—these power structures remain invisible until deliberately surfaced. The 27 Principles point toward this: Collective Action shows that hidden incentive misalignments drive irrational outcomes; Conflict Resolution warns that invisible tensions harden into destructive disputes; Reciprocity cannot exist without visibility of who contributes and who benefits; Transparency is not optional but foundational to system resilience. The pattern family in this cluster—spanning stakeholder mapping, power analysis, interest cartography, dependency visualization, and conflict-sensitive mapping—converges on a single mechanism: the deliberate, facilitated making visible of hidden relational structures through shared artifacts (maps, diagrams, matrices, narratives). This is not data collection; it is governance infrastructure.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Hidden power structures vs. transparent governance.**

In every domain, the same crisis repeats: power operates in darkness. Business stakeholders unknowingly align with vendor interests. Urban co-governance initiatives reproduce historic inequities because power asymmetries remain unmapped. Ecological commons collapse when some users' interests dominate without visibility. Personal relationships suffer when money and control flow invisibly. The problem is structural: actors lack shared understanding of who decides what, who benefits, who bears cost, what resources flow where, and where conflicts are baked into the system. Without this visibility, four failures cascade: (1) Some actors capture governance because others don't know it's happening. (2) Conflicts emerge as surprises rather than navigable design problems. (3) Reciprocity becomes impossible—how can fairness exist if nobody knows what others contribute? (4) The system degrades faster because nobody can see what's breaking until it's too late. The tension is not ignorance vs. knowledge but hidden structure vs. visible structure. The map is the intervention.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Relational Cartography & Power Visibility works through three integrated moves:

**MOVE 1: SYSTEMATIC DISCOVERY.** Conduct a structured, facilitated process to identify all actors in the system (human and non-human stakeholders). Map their interests (what they need, what they prioritize, what they benefit from). Map their power (decision authority, information control, resource access, representation capacity, veto power). Map their interdependencies (who depends on whom, where are flows concentrated, where are critical gaps). This is not a consultant's report; it is a collective learning process where actors participate in discovering the structure they are embedded in. The facilitation itself is governance work—it surfaces assumptions, reveals hidden conflicts, and builds shared language.

**MOVE 2: ARTIFACT CREATION.** Translate the discovery into a visible, updateable shared artifact: a map, matrix, diagram, or narrative document that anyone in the system can reference, understand, and challenge. The artifact encodes five elements simultaneously: (a) who the actors are, (b) what their interests/constraints are, (c) where power is concentrated, (d) where conflicts exist or could emerge, (e) where interdependencies create both vulnerability and leverage. The artifact is not neutral; it is built to surface asymmetries and creative possibilities. It becomes the "source of truth" that the system uses to navigate itself.

**MOVE 3: CYCLICAL REBALANCING.** Commit to regular (annual, bi-annual, or event-triggered) cycles of updating the cartography, assessing whether power has drifted toward concentration or capture, identifying where asymmetries exceed sustainable thresholds, and designing deliberate rebalancing mechanisms. This is not a one-time audit but an adaptive governance practice. The cartography becomes a feedback loop: visibility → negotiation → action → drift → new visibility.

**WHY THIS WORKS ACROSS DOMAINS:** The mechanism is universal because power structures follow the same relational logic everywhere. Cartography works because it externalizes what was internal, making the invisible navigable. It works because the shared artifact becomes a commons act—no longer can anyone claim "I didn't know" or "things just happened." It works because visibility enables two things simultaneously: detection of emerging domination (the problem) and identification of leverage points for rebalancing (the solution). The map is not descriptive; it is prescriptive—it says: here are the thresholds, here is where we need to act, here is where we have agency.

**COMMONS IMPLICATIONS:** This pattern serves the Commons qualities: ALIVE (by making the system's relational dynamics visible, cartography enables adaptive response rather than slow degradation). SHARED (by creating a commons artifact that all actors can reference and co-author). JUST (by making power visible and enabling deliberate negotiation toward fairness). BUILT TO LAST (by establishing feedback cycles that catch drift before capture becomes irreversible). The pattern is not a tool you apply once; it is a governance infrastructure you inhabit continuously.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with a simple question: *Who actually shapes what happens here?* Then build outward systematically.

**First, gather the actors.** Convene a diverse group—not just the official decision-makers. Include people on the edges: staff who know what really happens, end-users, suppliers, regulators, people affected by decisions they never see. Cast wide. Add non-human actors too: technologies that route decisions, natural systems that set constraints, absent stakeholders whose interests matter.

**Second, map interests explicitly.** In a facilitated session (or series of them), ask each actor: What do you need from this system? What outcomes matter most to you? Where do your interests align with others? Where do they conflict? Don't assume you know. Write it down. Make it visible on a shared surface—a wall, a digital board, a table. Name the tensions without resolving them yet. "Vendor needs cost reduction; users need customization; regulators need auditability." This is not judgment. It's clarity.

**Third, surface power truthfully.** Who decides? Who controls information? Whose absence would break the system? Who can say no? Who never gets asked? Map decision authority explicitly—not what the org chart claims, but what actually happens. Who has access to resources? Who shapes the narrative? Include informal power: reputation, relationships, knowledge monopolies. Include structural power: whose interests are built into systems, whose are bolted on afterward?

**Fourth, trace interdependencies.** Draw the flows: who depends on whom for what? Where are critical pinch points? Where could one actor's withdrawal disable everything? Where are hidden leverage points? These flows are where fairness and justice matter most—they're where silent capture happens.

**Document it as a shared artifact.** Make it beautiful enough that people want to return to it. Update it as things shift. Treat it as living cartography, not a finished map. Review it regularly with the group. Ask: What's changed? What did we miss? What surprised us?

This isn't analysis for its own sake. It's cultivation of mutual visibility—the ground condition for genuine negotiation and just decision-making.

### Section 5: Consequences

When power becomes visible, several things flourish. Trust can emerge—not the naive kind, but the mature kind built on clear-eyed seeing. Actors can negotiate openly about trade-offs instead of competing in darkness. Hidden coalitions become harder to sustain; silent capture loses its invisibility shield. Decisions that seemed inevitable reveal themselves as choices—and choices can be contested, amended, shared. The most marginalized actors gain the ability to name what they see and demand consideration.

Systems become more resilient. When you see interdependencies, you can design redundancy and mutual care into structures. When you see where power concentrates, you can deliberately distribute decision-making. When you see whose interests are structurally excluded, you can build them in. Fairness stops being aspirational and becomes buildable.

But visibility also brings risk. **Naming power can provoke defensiveness.** Actors who benefit from invisibility may resist—they'll call the process "political" or "divisive." The mapping itself becomes contested. You might surface conflicts that were manageable only because they were hidden. Relationships can fracture when people truly see the asymmetries they're embedded in.

There's also the risk of **false equivalence.** Mapping all actors and interests visibly doesn't mean all should have equal voice in decisions. Justice sometimes requires unequal power distribution. The work is not to make power disappear but to make it *accountable*—visible enough that it can be negotiated, contested, and corrected.

And there's a darker possibility: **power mapping can become a tool of management**, a way to optimize control rather than distribute it. When visibility serves the interests of those already dominant, it can deepen rather than heal inequities. The pattern only works if the mapping process itself is genuinely participatory and the maps are held in common.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**The Participatory Budgeting process in Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989–present)** began as a way to make municipal spending visible and contestable. Citizens gathered to map not just budget allocations but the interests and power behind them—which neighborhoods got infrastructure investment, whose needs shaped spending priorities, who decided in darkness. By making this power structure visible and opening it to negotiation, the city shifted resources toward historically marginalized areas. The practice has spread to hundreds of cities globally. What was hidden became shared decision-making.

**Indigenous co-management of natural commons across Canada and Australia** relies on explicitly mapping the different knowledge systems, interests, and decision-making powers of Indigenous communities, government agencies, and external stakeholders. Rather than pretending these are aligned, successful commons arrangements make power and interest differences visible through formal protocols, representation structures, and deliberative forums. The Tsilhqot'in Nation's land management, for instance, requires explicit recognition of competing claims and interests—and designs governance structures around that visibility rather than denying it.

**Stakeholder mapping in tech ethics review boards** (practiced at organizations like Mozilla, Microsoft Research, and smaller studios) involves systematically identifying who is affected by a technology, whose interests are invisible in design decisions, where power asymmetries exist between developers and users. By making this visible—often through participatory design or community advisory boards—teams can catch capture before it hardens. When power remains unmapped, "user research" becomes a substitute for power-sharing; when mapped, it becomes negotiation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Distributed intelligence systems and AI change how power hides and how it can be revealed.

**Power becomes easier to obscure.** Algorithmic decision-making embeds interests into code where they're invisible even to designers. Recommendation systems concentrate power while appearing neutral. Emergent behaviors in AI systems create power relationships no single actor intended or can easily control. The proliferation of agents (human and machine) makes the network so complex that mapping becomes technically harder.

But visibility also becomes more possible. **Tools for causal inference, network analysis, and participatory data visualization** let communities map influence and interdependence at scales previously impossible. AI can help surface hidden patterns—which actors consistently align, where power concentrates in surprising places, what interdependencies were invisible. Communities can crowdsource power cartography; AI can help synthesize and pattern-match across the map.

The key shift: **power mapping becomes an ongoing practice, not a one-time event.** In rapidly changing systems with AI agents, power structures shift too fast for static maps. Successful commons will treat power visibility as continuous calibration—regular mapping, rapid updating, collaborative surveillance of their own systems' shifting asymmetries.

The ethical question sharpens: Does the mapping serve transparency that enables collective negotiation, or does it serve optimization that increases control? AI tools for power visibility are neutral on this question—they amplify whatever intent guides their use. The pattern only works if visibility is tethered to genuine power-sharing mechanisms.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:** Actors reference the power map in real conversations. Decisions are explicitly negotiated around visible trade-offs. When someone claims a decision is inevitable or technical, others can point to the map and ask: "But whose interests shaped that inevitability?" New actors entering the system ask for an updated map before engaging. The map changes frequently because people are attending to shifting power. Most importantly: marginalized actors gain bargaining power because their interests are now visible and named.

**Signs of decay:** The map becomes a shelf document—produced once, then forgotten. Power continues to operate as if the map doesn't exist. The mapping process itself becomes controlled by dominant actors, so it obscures rather than reveals. Interests are named but decision-making doesn't shift to accommodate them. People stop updating the map because it's become "accurate enough" in a rapidly changing system. The tool becomes about compliance (we *did* the stakeholder mapping) rather than cultivation (we continuously tend visibility).

**Diagnostic question:** *If a marginalized actor in your system read the power map, would they say "Yes, that's exactly what I've been trying to name" or would they say "No, you've made me invisible in a different way"?*

If it's the latter, the pattern is serving management, not commons. Start over with different people at the center of the mapping process. Visibility without voice is just another form of control.
