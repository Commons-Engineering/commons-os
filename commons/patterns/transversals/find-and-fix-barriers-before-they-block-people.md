### Section 1: Context

This transversal emerges from O0's commitment to **shared** and **just** value creation. The commons cannot be alive or equitable if structural barriers invisibly exclude people from participation. O1 principles that power this work include Visibility as Commons Act (barriers hidden are barriers that persist), Decide at the Lowest Level (barrier removal must be designed where the exclusion actually happens), and The Commons itself (access is not a side feature but the foundation of collective life). The pattern inverts the dominant logic: rather than designing for the assumed "default" user and then retrofitting accommodations for those who don't fit, it starts with a systematic map of all barriers and designs them out proactively. This is not charity or sympathy—it is structural justice that expands the commons's productive capacity.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Invisibility of exclusion vs. systematic visibility | Retrofit accommodation vs. foundational design.**

In most systems—businesses, homes, communities, digital spaces—barriers to participation are invisible to those they don't affect. An able-bodied person never notices the stairs; a neurotypical person never encounters the cognitive load of sensory overstimulation; a monolingual person never experiences the exclusion of untranslated communication. This invisibility is not accidental; it is structural. Those with power design systems for people like themselves, and exclusion becomes the default. When barriers are finally acknowledged, they are treated as exceptions—accommodations, special requests, add-ons—rather than design failures. This approach burdens the excluded person with the work of negotiating access, often at the cost of dignity and belonging. Worse, it is inefficient: barriers removed only after complaints are costly to retrofit and signal that the community did not expect or want the excluded person's participation. Proactive barrier removal is missing.

---

### Section 3: Solution

The solution is a structured practice of **systematic barrier mapping followed by intentional removal**. This unfolds in four moves:

**1. Map all barriers simultaneously across multiple dimensions.** Rather than waiting for complaints, conduct regular audits of physical, informational, economic, sensory, cognitive, cultural, and social barriers to participation. In business: hiring processes, workspace layout, communication formats, benefits language. In domestic life: kitchen height and reach, lighting, tool complexity, storage access. In digital spaces: color contrast, keyboard navigation, alt text, plain language, caption availability. In urban design: sidewalk curbs, street crossing time, accessible transit, welcoming signage. The audit is comprehensive because barriers compound—a disabled person facing economic, physical, and informational barriers experiences triple exclusion.

**2. Make barrier data visible to everyone.** Transparency about what prevents participation is the commons act. Share audit findings publicly; this is not a private accommodation request but a community design problem. When barriers are visible, they become a collective responsibility to address.

**3. Prioritize removal by impact and feasibility.** Not all barriers have equal weight. Some affect many people; some block participation entirely while others create friction. Some are low-cost to remove; others require structural change. Sequence removal strategically—address high-impact, low-cost barriers first to build momentum and signal commitment. Kitchen accessibility might start with relocating frequently-used items to easy-reach height; digital accessibility might start with adding alt text; urban accessibility might start with tactile paving at crossings.

**4. Design for the full spectrum of human capacity from the start.** As barriers are removed, the practice shifts upstream: instead of fixing broken accessibility, design proactively for diversity. Use universal design principles—features that work for everyone. Ramps benefit not just wheelchair users but parents with strollers, movers with dolls, people with luggage. Plain language benefits not just people with cognitive disabilities but non-native speakers and people in a hurry. Multiple ways to participate (in-person, remote, asynchronous) benefit disabled people and also shift workers, caregivers, and people with unpredictable health. This is not accommodation; it is design that assumes human diversity from the start.

**5. Create feedback loops that make new barriers visible rapidly.** As access improves, new exclusions may emerge. A feedback system—regular check-ins with people who use the space, digital tools that log accessibility issues, community conversations—ensures that barriers don't harden again. This is an adaptive cycle: audit, remove, design, listen, repeat.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start with a barrier audit—but make it collaborative, not extractive. Gather people with direct experience of exclusion alongside those designing the system. A wheelchair user, a parent with care responsibilities, someone with dyslexia, a person new to your culture, someone with limited income. Ask them: *Where does this system make you feel unwelcome or unable to participate?* Listen for the small frictions that add up.

Document barriers across seven dimensions: physical (Can you move through the space?), sensory (Can you see, hear, or perceive what's happening?), cognitive (Can you process the information without exhaustion?), economic (Can you afford to participate?), cultural (Does this assume your background?), social (Do you have the networks or confidence to show up?), temporal (Do the timing and pace work for your life?).

Then prioritize ruthlessly. Not every barrier has equal weight. Ask: *Who is most blocked?* *What barrier, if removed, unblocks the most people?* *What can we fix now, not later?* A ramp costs money and time; changing a hiring deadline to accommodate caregiving costs nothing but shifts perspective.

Make removal visible and intentional. When you add alt text to images, caption videos, or translate materials, don't hide this work. Name it: "We've added captions because deaf participants matter." This signals that access is normal, not charity.

Build feedback loops. Barriers shift as contexts change and new people arrive. Create simple, low-friction ways for participants to flag what's still broken—a suggestion box, a monthly check-in, a Slack channel. Most importantly: *act on what you hear.* If you audit and ignore, you've just collected resentment.

Embed barrier removal into your regular rhythms. Before you launch anything new—a meeting, a program, a document—ask the access question: *What barriers might this create?* Make it as routine as spell-checking. This shifts the cultural weight. Inclusion becomes part of the work, not extra work.

### Section 5: Consequences

When you cultivate this practice, what flourishes: deeper participation from people who'd otherwise self-select out. More diverse thinking. Innovation that serves broader populations. Less energy spent on people explaining why they can't participate—and more energy on the work itself. A culture where people feel genuinely expected, not tolerated.

You also build resilience. Systems designed for diverse bodies, minds, and circumstances are more adaptable when conditions shift. A business that built remote work capacity for disabled employees suddenly scales easily during a crisis. A community space designed for multiple languages serves new populations without redesign.

The risks are real if this work isn't sustained. Barrier removal can become symbolic—a ramp you built but never maintained, translations that fall behind. It can also surface discomfort: people with power sometimes resist naming the barriers they've created. There's work in that friction; there's also real tension.

Without ongoing attention, barriers creep back. Captions stop being added to new videos. New hires design systems the old way. Exclusion is the default; access requires constant tending. If barrier mapping becomes a one-time audit instead of a living practice, you've created the illusion of access without the reality.

The deepest risk: barrier removal without addressing *why* the barriers existed in the first place. You can ramp the building, but if the culture still centers able-bodied experience, the ramp becomes charity. Real change requires examining power—who designed this system, for whom, and at whose expense?

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Microsoft's Inclusive Design Practice**: For the past decade, Microsoft has embedded barrier mapping into product design. They audit accessibility not as compliance but as capability. Their Xbox Adaptive Controller emerged from asking disabled gamers directly: *What keeps you out?* Rather than retrofit after launch, the team designed for multiple grip strengths, cognitive load, and one-handed play from inception. The result: a product that serves not only disabled players but aging gamers, people with injuries, and anyone playing in difficult conditions. Access became market expansion.

**The Highlander Center (Rural Tennessee, 1930s onward)**: A folk school for social change, Highlander conducted barrier mapping before the term existed. They asked: *Who wants to learn but can't?* And systematically removed obstacles. No tuition requirement. Housing provided. Childcare available. They taught in local dialect, not academic language. They welcomed people with no prior education. This wasn't abstract—it was structural. By removing barriers, they created a space where Appalachian coal miners, Black activists, and poor farmers could learn together. The civil rights movement was shaped partly by people who first learned their own power at Highlander because someone asked: *What's keeping you out?*

**Quechua Language Digital Archive (Peru/Andes)**: Rather than design systems in Spanish and retrofit for indigenous languages, this team centered Quechua speakers from the start. They mapped barriers: *Do you have internet?* (No, so they built offline-first tools.) *Can you read Spanish technical language?* (No, so they used Quechua for all interface text.) *Who has time to contribute?* (Elders and community members, not just academics.) The result: not a translation of someone else's design, but a system that could only exist as Quechua-centered. Barrier mapping led to fundamentally different architecture, not accommodation.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

As AI and distributed intelligence reshape how we work, barrier mapping becomes both easier and more necessary—and more subtle.

Easier: You can now audit at scale. Machine learning can flag inconsistencies in alt text, identify patterns in who drops out of workflows, predict where cognitive overload happens in customer journeys. You can model barriers before they're built. A neural network can test: *How would someone with dyslexia experience this interface?* at design time, not after launch.

More necessary: Complexity is accelerating. The systems that mediate participation—recommendation algorithms, AI scheduling, automated decision-making—become black boxes. A barrier isn't just a missing ramp; it's an algorithm that invisibly deprioritizes you. Distributed work means barriers are harder to see; no one gathers in one room where exclusion becomes obvious.

More subtle: This is where the tension deepens. AI can make barriers *feel* personalized ("This interface was customized for you") while actually narrowing access. An algorithm might route you to "simpler" information because it guessed your education level—excluding you from deeper understanding. Barrier removal must now include auditing the systems that create barriers, not just fixing visible ones.

The transversal becomes: *Map barriers before you deploy AI, not after.* This means asking: *Who is this system trained on?* *What populations did you exclude from training data, and what barriers does that create?* Distributed intelligence needs distributed access consciousness—people thinking about barriers at every node, not a central accessibility team catching problems after.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life**: People show up who didn't before. You hear new voices, new problems, new ideas. When someone says, "I almost didn't come, but then I noticed you'd done X," that's vitality. Barrier removal becomes normal enough that it's not remarkable—captions appear automatically, multiple languages are standard, flexible timing is expected. The work gets done faster because fewer people are stuck explaining what they need. There's a lightness: energy goes to contribution, not negotiation.

**Signs of decay**: Barriers get mapped once, then ignored. You have a diversity statement but a discriminatory hiring process. You added captions to old videos but new ones go uncaptioned. Complaints accumulate because feedback loops aren't real. Someone says, "I asked about this six months ago," and it's still not fixed. The culture drifts back to: *Access is someone else's job.* Most insidiously: you stop noticing barriers because you've become comfortable. The people who left stop asking.

**The diagnostic question**: *If someone new arrived tomorrow with a need you've never encountered before, what would happen?* Would the system resist and ask them to fit? Would you see them as a problem to accommodate, or as information about what the system is missing? Is barrier removal a muscle you exercise, or a box you checked once? Vitality lives in that answer.
