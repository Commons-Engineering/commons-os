### Section 1: Context

All living systems require circulation. Nutrients cycle through soil, plants, animals, and decomposition. Attention cycles through sensory input, processing, decision, and action. Information cycles through networks. In human economic systems, money is the primary circulation medium—it carries value, enables exchange, and should amplify community resilience when it cycles locally. Yet most institutional and urban economic designs are optimized for extraction: capital flows inward (from workers, consumers, communities) but exits outward (to distant shareholders, external investors, supply chains). The result is fiscal hemorrhage—places that generate significant economic value experience net resource depletion. This transversal describes the design principle that reverses extraction into circulation. It is rooted in the Orbit 1 principle of Channel Self-Interest: rather than shame institutions into charity, design the circulation incentives so that local retention serves everyone's financial interest. It orbits Carrying Capacity because no community can sustain itself if resources leak faster than they regenerate. And it grounds in Feedback loops because circulation velocity must be continuously measured and adjusted.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Extraction vs. Circulation — capital flowing outward to distant shareholders vs. capital recirculating locally to build community resilience.**

The default flow of capital in modern economies is extraction. A worker generates value; wages paid are spent at large retailers whose profits exit to distant corporations. A community develops land; development value is captured by external investors. A university or hospital receives tax benefits and community support; procurement flows to global supply chains. A cooperative accumulates surplus; the surplus enriches early members or external capital partners rather than funding the next cohort or community needs. A place experiences economic vitality (tourism, development, job growth); rising rents and property values displace the residents and small businesses that created that vitality. In each case, the economic energy generated locally is siphoned outward, leaving the place resource-depleted even as it generates wealth. The problem is not that resources flow out—some outflow is necessary and healthy (exports, investment in growth, external partnerships). The problem is that *inflow does not match outflow*, and the community loses agency over its own economic future. Communities experiencing this dynamic become dependent on external charity, vulnerable to disinvestment, unable to retain talent or invest in their own institutions, and increasingly unequal as wealth concentrates in those connected to extraction channels.

### Section 3: Solution

Fiscal Circulation & Local Wealth Retention operates through a four-stage mechanism:

**Stage 1: Identify Fiscal Flows**
Map the money entering a bounded place (municipality, neighbourhood, bioregion): institutional spending (anchor procurement, payroll), public investment (development, infrastructure), private capital (business revenue, real estate sales), and external flows (taxes, remittances, grants). Measure current leakage rates—what percentage exits the boundary without cycling through local actors? This is not about perfect accounting; it is about order-of-magnitude visibility.

**Stage 2: Design Circulation Governance**
Establish binding rules that require resources to recirculate locally before exit. These rules take many forms depending on domain and actor:

- **Institutional procurement mandates**: Anchor institutions (hospitals, universities, municipalities) legally commit to sourcing from local suppliers, community-rooted enterprises, and worker-owned cooperatives. The procurement power becomes a tool for local capital accumulation, not external extraction.

- **Equity-share requirements**: Development projects, cooperatives, and enterprises must issue community equity stakes or distribute surplus through community investment funds, ensuring that wealth-building opportunity accrues to local residents and institutions rather than external shareholders.

- **Revenue cycling rules**: Cooperative surplus, housing equity gains, land value appreciation, and usage fees from shared commons are automatically reinvested into community-controlled funds (CLTs, community development finance, community endowments) rather than distributed to members or extracted by external investors.

- **Temporal wealth distribution formulas**: Cooperative and community capital structures establish algorithmic formulas that distribute accumulated wealth across member cohorts and time horizons, preventing early cohorts from capturing all benefits and building intergenerational stewardship.

- **Local investment priority policies**: When community capital is deployed, governance rules prioritize local enterprises, workers, suppliers, and projects—maximizing the multiplier effect within the bounded circulation system.

**Stage 3: Measure & Monitor Circulation Velocity**
Create real-time feedback systems that track fiscal flow depth and speed:

- **Payroll circulation velocity tracking**: Monitor how wages paid by anchor institutions circulate through local spending, identifying leakage points (chain retailers, external suppliers) and intervention zones.

- **Multiplier mapping**: Document the local multiplier effect of cooperative spending, institutional procurement, and resident wealth—demonstrating how each dollar circulates through local businesses, suppliers, and service providers.

- **Hyperlocal economic circulation mapping**: Community-led documentation of which local businesses circulate earnings locally, which leak externally, and how to strengthen retention. This builds economic literacy and collective stewardship capacity.

- **Capital flow dashboards**: Visible tracking of how community capital funds are deployed, who benefits, and what returns flow back into the commons fund.

**Stage 4: Adapt Governance to Restore Circulation**
When feedback reveals leakage or circulation failure, governance structures are adapted. If anchor procurement still leaks through external supply chains, increase local supplier development investment and extend procurement lock-in contracts. If housing equity gains flow to external lenders rather than community acquisition, strengthen community purchase fund mandates and equity-share capture rules. If cooperative surplus concentrates in early members, introduce temporal wealth distribution formulas. The governance system itself becomes a feedback-responsive mechanism for deepening circulation.

**Cross-Domain Pattern Logic**

The mechanism operates identically across domains because it exploits a universal dynamic: resources naturally flow along paths of least resistance (toward existing power, external markets, institutional efficiency). To reverse that flow toward local recirculation requires intentional design at multiple leverage points simultaneously. In Business, this means cooperative governance, profit-sharing, and local procurement rules. In Urban systems, it means anchor institution compacts, community land trusts, equity-share requirements, and development value capture. In Ecology, it means regenerative resource cycling and carrying capacity thresholds that prevent export beyond sustainable rates. In Life, it means giving circles, reciprocal lending networks, and community investment that route personal wealth through local relationships rather than distant markets. Each domain instantiates the same principle: design the rules, visibility, and incentives such that capital retains its value-generating potential through local circulation rather than dissipating through extraction.

**Why This Builds Lasting Commons**

Fiscal circulation is essential to the **alive** and **built-to-last** qualities of commons value. A commons that loses resources faster than it generates them becomes dependent, vulnerable, and eventually collapses. A commons with strong internal circulation—where every actor's income, wealth, and surplus opportunity cycles through the community—develops resilience, agency, and self-determination. Circulation also serves **justice** because it redistributes wealth-building opportunity: instead of only those connected to extraction channels (external investors, global supply chains, distant shareholders) accumulating wealth, circulation ensures that residents, local suppliers, cooperative members, and community institutions share in the surplus their work and ecosystems generate. Finally, circulation is **systematic** because it is encoded in binding governance rules, feedback systems, and adaptive cycles—not dependent on individual charity or goodwill but woven into the institutions themselves.

### Section 4: Implementation

Begin by mapping your money flows. Walk through the places where capital enters your community—hospital payroll, university purchasing, government contracts, business revenue, real estate transactions—and trace where it goes next. This isn't abstract accounting; it's detective work. Interview procurement officers, business owners, residents. Ask: *Where does this money sleep tonight?* You'll likely find that 60-80% of the value generated locally exits within weeks, flowing to distant shareholders, global supply chains, and external investors.

Once you see the leakage, design interventions at three levels:

**Anchor Institution Alignment** — Universities, hospitals, and government agencies control massive budgets. Shift their procurement toward local suppliers, local employment, local real estate investment. This isn't charity; it's recognizing that these institutions are embedded in—and dependent on—your community's health. When a hospital sources food from regional farms rather than global distributors, money cycles through farm workers, equipment suppliers, and local grain mills before it reaches corporate consolidators.

**Business Formation & Ownership** — Create pathways for local wealth generation: cooperative enterprises, worker-owned businesses, community development corporations. These structures keep surplus circulating. When profits stay in community hands, they fund the next business, support local philanthropy, and build intergenerational wealth. Worker ownership means wages stay local; cooperative surplus means reinvestment happens locally.

**Intermediary Infrastructure** — Build the connective tissue: local currency systems (scrip, time banking), community investment funds, public banking initiatives, preferential purchasing networks. These aren't alternatives to conventional money; they're protocols that slow money down, make its flows visible, and create permission structures for choosing local circulation.

Implementation is gradual. Start with one anchor institution's procurement. Identify one category of goods or services currently bought externally, find (or help create) a local supplier, and shift volume. Document the difference: jobs created, tax revenue retained, relationship-building that happens when money circulates through neighbors rather than algorithms.

The deeper work is cultural: helping communities understand that keeping money local isn't parochial—it's structural repair. It's recognizing that your neighbor's paycheck is your customer base, that your community's tax dollars should build your community's capacity, not distant shareholders' wealth.

### Section 5: Consequences

When money moves locally, visible consequences emerge quickly. Employment multiplies: a dollar spent at a local business recirculates 3-5 times before leaving, compared to 1-2 times for chain retailers. Tax revenue accumulates in local funds, funding schools and infrastructure without perpetual debt. Trust deepens—you know the business owner, the farmer, the cooperatively-owned bakery. Relationships replace transactions.

Wealth-building happens across more households. When a cooperative's surplus funds the next worker-owner's equity stake, or when a community development corporation reinvests profits into affordable housing, wealth compounds locally instead of concentrating. Generations of families stay rooted because economic opportunity exists where they are.

But real risks emerge if this pattern hardens into autarky or cronyism:

**Closure Risk** — A community that obsesses over keeping money local can become insular, defensive, unable to receive external expertise, investment, or ideas. The distinction matters: circulation doesn't mean isolation. It means being intentional about what enters and leaves, not cutting yourself off from the wider world.

**Captured Allocation** — When money cycles through tight networks, it can get trapped in insider relationships. Local elites—established business owners, longtime families—can monopolize the recirculating capital, excluding newcomers, young people, or marginalized groups. You end up with local extraction instead of external extraction. Intentional governance matters: who decides how recirculated wealth gets allocated? If decisions happen in closed rooms, the pattern reproduces injustice locally.

**Reduced Competition** — Local circulation can mean less competitive pressure, higher prices, slower innovation. Communities need some external input—new ideas, competitive pricing, specialized expertise—to stay vital. The pattern works only when combined with transparency, democratic accountability, and openness to renewal.

The vitality depends on *who* the circulation benefits. Without attention to equity and inclusion, keeping money local can mean keeping power local.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Mondragon Cooperative Corporation (Basque Country, Spain)** — Since 1956, this network of worker-owned cooperatives has kept wealth circulating through the region. Worker-owners participate in surplus distribution, cooperative members fund new ventures, and profits are reinvested in education and community development rather than extracted to shareholders. The model has survived multiple economic crises because wealth generation strengthens the community's own capacity rather than enriching distant investors. When a Mondragon cooperative prospers, the region prospers; reinvestment happens locally.

**Evergreen Cooperatives (Cleveland, USA)** — Launched in 2008, this ecosystem of worker-owned green businesses (laundry, gardens, energy services) deliberately circulates revenue through anchor institution procurement (hospitals, universities) and community employment. Profits fund community development, resident wealth-building, and the creation of additional cooperatives. The model demonstrates how to align large institutional purchasing power with local ownership structures.

**The Grameen Bank & Social Business Model (Bangladesh)** — Muhammad Yunus's approach to microfinance keeps capital circulating through village economies rather than extracting it through debt service to distant lenders. Borrowers become owners; surplus funds new lending. While now commercialized, the original model showed how to structure capital flow so that wealth generation strengthens local communities and does not require perpetual extraction.

**Kin Canada & Local Purchasing Networks (Canada)** — Food-focused networks that connect institutions (schools, hospitals) directly with regional producers, ensuring food dollars cycle through farm economies rather than commodity chains. The practice has spread across North America and Europe, each adapting the basic principle: make procurement decisions visible, choose circulation, build relationships with suppliers.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In an era of distributed intelligence and AI-mediated systems, fiscal circulation patterns face both amplification and disruption.

**Amplification**: AI can make money flows visible in real time. Mapping software can show, continuously, where capital enters a community and where it exits—a kind of financial transparency that was impossible before. Communities can run rapid scenario modeling: *If we shift 10% of hospital procurement to local suppliers, what multipliers activate? Which sectors strengthen? Where do new employment opportunities emerge?* This computational clarity enables faster, more confident decision-making about circulation design.

Blockchain and distributed ledger systems can enable local currency and time-banking at scales previously impossible. Smart contracts can automate preferential purchasing—when a procurement officer initiates a purchase order, the system flags local suppliers automatically, making circulation-aligned choices the path of least resistance.

**Disruption**: Algorithmic commerce (Amazon, Alibaba, platform networks) makes it harder for communities to retain visibility into their own fiscal flows. Money moves through systems optimized for extraction; individuals have less agency. Gig economy platforms extract value from local labor while concentrating ownership globally.

**Adaptive response**: The pattern becomes more necessary, not less. Communities will need to build their own digital infrastructure—cooperative platforms, local transaction systems, community data cooperatives that track and visualize fiscal flows. The work shifts from individual purchasing choices (too fragmented to matter) to structural redesign of platforms and institutions. This requires literacy about algorithms, data governance, and the politics of infrastructure design—skills that become as essential as financial literacy.

The cognitive shift: from thinking of the economy as a black box to understanding it as a designed system that can be redesigned. AI makes this visible; communities must learn to see it clearly enough to act.

### Section 8: Vitality

A community where money moves locally shows visible signs of life:

**Signs of Vitality**: Small businesses proliferate and succeed—not because they're insulated from competition, but because they have reliable local demand and access to community capital. Newcomers and young people can start enterprises because community investment funds exist. Employment is stable and rooted; people don't have to leave to find work. Tax revenue accumulates in public coffers, funding schools and infrastructure without perpetual austerity. Anchor institutions (hospitals, universities) are known for supporting local suppliers; procurement officers have relationships with vendors. Community members invest in each other—through cooperatives, time banks, local funds—and see their neighbors' prosperity as their own. Wealth-building happens across many households, not concentrated in a few. Resilience is visible: during economic downturns, communities with strong local circulation weather the storm better because they're less dependent on distant markets.

**Signs of Decay**: Money exits faster than it enters. Small businesses close while chains expand. Young people leave because economic opportunity requires moving away. Tax revenue is captured externally; public infrastructure decays. Institutions (hospitals, universities) procure globally; procurement decisions happen in distant headquarters. Community members have no say in how capital circulates. Wealth concentrates in a few hands—often old money, often excluding newcomers and marginalized groups. When economic crises hit, the community has no resilience because it's deeply dependent on external markets and distant decision-makers. Real estate becomes an extraction vehicle: outsiders buy property, extract rent, leave.

**Diagnostic Question**: *If a major employer closed tomorrow, would your community have the capital and relationships to create new economic opportunity locally, or would people have to leave?*

This single question reveals whether circulation is real or performative. If the answer is "we'd struggle," fiscal circulation design is not yet embedded. If the answer is "we have cooperative enterprises, community investment, local business networks, and the relationships to mobilize them"—you're building genuine local resilience.
