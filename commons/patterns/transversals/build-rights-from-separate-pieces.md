### Section 1: Context

The Commons Engineering corpus recognizes that ownership is not monolithic—it is a bundle of separable claims over time, space, and decision authority. Classical commons theory (Ostrom, Schlager & Ostrom) identifies five distinct rights: access (entry), withdrawal (extraction), management (decision), exclusion (boundary), and alienation (sale). Yet most real systems collapse these rights into single categories (owner/non-owner, member/non-member), creating two pathological outcomes: either diffuse rights produce tragedy-of-commons (each actor extracts as much as possible, depleting the resource); or concentrated rights produce enclosure (a single holder monopolizes benefits and decisions, excluding stewards and beneficiaries). The O1 principles of Subsidiarity, Polycentric Organisation, Collective Action, and Reciprocity all point toward a third possibility: GRANULAR RIGHTS ARCHITECTURE, where each claim is separable, visible, and bundled according to stewardship capacity and vulnerability.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Monolithic control vs. granular participation; open-access depletion vs. exclusive appropriation.**

Across all domains, we observe a recurring pattern: systems that attempt to protect commons resources either (A) distribute rights so broadly that no one is accountable for stewardship, leading to depletion and abandonment; or (B) concentrate rights in a single authority (state, corporation, or landowner) to ensure management, but this sacrifices democratic voice, local adaptation, and justice for those who depend on the resource but lack exit options. A household with shared finances but no role definition experiences resentment and breakdown. A forest managed by distant state forestry loses local knowledge and adaptive capacity. An urban park with open-access rules faces vandalism and decay. A platform with distributed governance but no distinction between casual users and core stewards faces tyranny-of-the-majority and founder capture. The problem is architectural: rights and obligations are bundled too coarsely, making it impossible to align stewardship with decision-making while protecting those who have no alternatives.

---

### Section 3: Solution

Rights Decomposition & Stewardship Bundling operates through five interlocking mechanisms:

**1. SEPARABLE CLAIMS ARCHITECTURE**: Break the monolithic claim (ownership, membership, access) into distinct, transferable dimensions. In ecology: separate fruit-harvesting rights from timber-harvesting rights, from water-access rights, from governance votes. In urban commons: separate access-rights (can I enter?) from use-rights (can I extract value?) from management-rights (can I decide rules?) from alienation-rights (can I sell this?). In business: separate royalty obligations from usage permissions from governance seats. In households: separate kitchen-access from food-purchase authority from meal-planning votes from cleanup obligation. Each dimension can be held by different actors and bundled according to context.

**2. STEWARDSHIP-CAPACITY ASSIGNMENT**: Assign each right-dimension to the actor(s) closest to the feedback loop and most capable of adaptive response. Those who harvest the forest know seasonal variation; grant them management rights over extraction timing and methods. Those who live daily in the neighborhood know maintenance needs; grant them management votes over park allocation. Those who contribute intellectual labor to a community of practice hold governance seats for resource allocation. This ensures that decision-making authority and local knowledge converge, preventing both bureaucratic rigidity and tragedy-of-commons indifference.

**3. LOCK-IN PROTECTION & VETO RIGHTS**: Identify stakeholders with low exit substitutability—those who depend on the commons with no viable alternative. Household members cannot exit to another home; grant them veto rights over major decisions affecting their subsistence. Urban residents with no alternative housing cannot exit; grant them priority-access rights and blocking coalitions. Employees of mission-driven enterprises cannot easily exit; grant them governance votes. This prevents tyranny-of-majority and ensures reciprocal fairness: those who depend on the commons are protected by design.

**4. THRESHOLD TRIGGERS & AUTOMATIC REDISTRIBUTION**: Establish explicit concentration thresholds. If any single actor accumulates decision authority over X% of the resource, or captures Y% of benefits, automatic redistribution is triggered: voting power spreads, benefit streams rebalance, or new governance seats are created. In urban commons, anti-monopoly statutes specify that no entity can control more than a defined share of extraction or governance. In forests, tenure rules prevent any individual from claiming all high-value products. In households, income concentration triggers renegotiation of financial decision-making. These thresholds are not rigid laws—they are clear signals that concentration is unsustainable, triggering procedural adjustment before crisis.

**5. VISIBLE, AMENDABLE RULE SETS**: Codify all rights-bundles and bundling rules in written charters or governance protocols (Commons Charter, Municipal Ordinance, Household Covenant, Community of Practice Charter). Make rules explicit rather than tacit, enabling transparency, accountability, and amendment. Establish lightweight pathways for members to propose rule clarifications and amendments—acknowledging that initial rules are incomplete and must evolve as context changes (Incompleteness & Rule Amendment Pathways principle). Regular charter reviews (e.g., four-year cycles with rotating delegate composition) ensure that rule-making power itself rotates.

**OUTCOME**: Rights Decomposition & Stewardship Bundling prevents both tragedy-of-commons and enclosure by making each stakeholder's claim granular, visible, and accountable. Stewardship capacity aligns with decision authority. Vulnerability is protected through veto. Concentration triggers redistribution. Rules remain transparent and amendable. The commons becomes a living, adaptive system rather than either a depletion-spiral or a monopoly.

### Section 4: Implementation

Start by mapping what your resource actually *is*—not as a unified thing, but as a layered set of possible claims. A forest isn't one entity; it's fruit availability, timber rotations, water flows, hunting seasons, ceremonial access, and fire management decisions happening in different rhythms. A data commons isn't one database; it's who can read, who can write, who trains models, who sets quality standards, who profits from derived products.

For each layer, ask: **Who is closest to stewarding this well?** Not ideologically—practically. Fruit harvesters understand seasonal ripeness better than distant regulators. Local fishers sense ecosystem shifts before data arrives at headquarters. A teenager knows what makes a digital space safe for teens. Proximity to consequence should guide who holds that specific right.

Create **threshold triggers**—points where rights automatically bundle or unbundle based on measurable conditions. If extraction falls below 20% of capacity, perhaps decision rights migrate to a broader council. If one group consistently violates stewardship norms, their withdrawal rights suspend while management rights expand elsewhere. These aren't punitive; they're adaptive. The system recalibrates itself when contexts change.

Build in **veto gates** at critical junctures. In Indonesia's community forestry models, individual harvesters hold withdrawal rights, but the village assembly holds alienation rights—preventing any single actor from selling the whole forest. In shared housing, residents control daily use; a supermajority controls structural changes; an external board prevents converting it to condos. Each gate protects something irreversible.

Document everything **plainly and locally**. Not in legal language that requires lawyers to interpret, but in a form the actual stewards can read, contest, and amend. Printed. Visible. Updated when conditions change. This prevents rights from becoming theoretical—they stay connected to practice.

Test your decomposition with an **inversion question**: "If this person lost this right tomorrow, what would break?" If nothing breaks, the right wasn't real. If everything breaks, you've given one person too much.

### Section 5: Consequences

When rights decomposition works, remarkable things flourish. **Accountability becomes concrete.** You can't blame "the commons" for depletion; you can ask why the person holding withdrawal rights didn't enforce their stewardship covenant. **Local knowledge gets captured.** A beekeeper's management decisions improve pollination outcomes better than a distant regulator's rules. **Minorities don't disappear.** Fractional rights mean a small group of users can hold a veto on decisions that would harm them—they can't be voted out entirely. **Systems become repairable.** When one right fails, others can compensate. When contexts change, you adjust one layer without dismantling the whole structure.

But unattended, certain risks metastasize. **Proliferation of petty conflicts.** When rights are granular, disputes multiply—not fewer, more. Whose harvesting season trumps whose water intake? This requires **constant negotiation infrastructure.** Without it, the system bogs down in argument. **Vulnerability to capture at scale.** A sophisticated actor can quietly accumulate fragments—buying withdrawal rights here, securing management appointment there—until they've reassembled a de facto monopoly. You need **transparency mechanisms and re-aggregation checks** that stop this silently.

**Coordination failures under stress.** When a crisis hits (drought, invasion, collapse), a fractured rights system can paralyze. Nobody moves without their piece. This is why **emergency protocols**—temporary right consolidation under strict time limits—must be built in beforehand, not improvised during panic.

**Hidden injustice in initial allocation.** If you decompose rights from an already-unjust starting state, you just crystallize inequality into smaller fragments. The decomposition only works if it follows or enables a **rebalancing of who holds what**.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Indonesia's Community Forestry Rights (Hutan Kemasyarakatan).** Since the 1990s, villages have held bundle rights over state forests: withdrawal rights to harvest timber and non-timber forest products on annual rotation; management rights to set harvesting rules and enforce boundaries; but explicitly *not* alienation rights (can't sell the concession). This decomposition has stabilized forests better than either state-only or open-access models. Village elders hold different pieces—harvesters, mediators, recorders—creating distributed stewardship. Disputes flare, but they're contained because no single actor can override the whole system.

**Swiss Alpine Pasture Commons (Alpen Allmend).** For seven centuries, mountain villages have separated the right to *graze* animals from the right to *decide herd size* (held by a village assembly), from the right to *manage hay production* (held by specialized commons administrators), from the right to *exclude outsiders* (negotiated seasonally). Each right holder has skin in the game and specialized knowledge. This system has survived climate swings, economic pressures, and demographic shifts because it distributes risk and adaptation across multiple hands.

**Kenyan Pastoralist Rangeland Agreements.** Semi-arid pastoral systems in northern Kenya have begun formalizing fragmented rights: a Samburu clan holds seasonal access to water points; the Rendille hold grazing rights during dry season; decisions about herd size go to a joint council; but *no one* can permanently degrade the land (an implicit community veto). These are newer, more fragile than the Alpine example, but they've prevented the tragedy-of-the-commons collapse that afflicted entirely open rangelands while avoiding the pastoral displacement caused by state conservation reserves.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In worlds of scarce information and slow decision cycles, monolithic rights made sense—one authority could gather data, decide, and enforce. The cost of decomposition exceeded its benefit.

AI and distributed intelligence flip this. **Sensors and algorithms can now monitor granular rights in real time.** A forest concession-holder's exact timber extraction can be tracked via satellite; water withdrawal can be metered; management decisions can be logged. The *cost of transparency* collapses. This makes decomposition not just viable but *economic*—you can track who's stearding what without bureaucratic overhead.

**But new risks emerge.** When rights are visible and granular, automated systems can aggregate them faster than humans can resist. An algorithmic buyer can scan thousands of fractional rights, identify which combinations yield monopoly, and assemble them through markets before anyone notices. Rights decomposition in the AI era requires **active re-aggregation detection**—systems that alert when any actor crosses a threshold of accumulated authority.

**Distributed intelligence also enables better coordination.** Smart contracts can enforce veto gates automatically. Blockchain records can make rights transfer transparent and tamper-evident. But these tools are morally neutral—they can equally entrench injustice if the initial allocation is unjust. The *decomposition itself* must be designed with equity built in, not assumed to emerge from technical elegance.

**Collective AI systems** (models trained by distributed communities, not centralized firms) make decomposition more natural. If a model is trained by a thousand stewards, each contributing data and decisions, each must hold some piece of governance. Monolithic AI-ownership becomes harder to justify. Decomposed rights become the default.

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**
- Stewards can articulate *why* they hold their specific right and *what* they're accountable for
- When a problem emerges, people know which right-holder to engage—accountability is traceable, not diffuse
- Rights *move* when contexts change (a poor harvest season shifts decision authority, a new steward takes on a role) without requiring constitutional collapse
- Minorities *invoke* their veto gates and the system *honors* those invocations, even when inconvenient
- New entrants can gradually acquire rights through proven stewardship, not wait for permission from gatekeepers
- Conflicts happen frequently but resolve locally and quickly because authority is clear and limited

**Signs of decay:**
- People refer to "the commons" or "the owners" as unified actors—rights have re-consolidated invisibly
- Stewards can't explain why they hold their right or what they're responsible for—it's become symbolic or ceremonial
- A single actor vetoes changes repeatedly, regardless of context—one fragment has become a choke point
- New stewards can't enter, or can only after proving themselves to gatekeepers who have no formal authority
- Disputes escalate to outside arbiters because local right-holders lack power to resolve them
- The rules are written but never enforced; stewards know they can ignore them without consequence

**Diagnostic question: "If I wanted to change one decision about how this resource is used—say, harvest timing or access rules—which three people would I actually need to convince? If I can't name three, or if the list keeps growing, your rights decomposition is either failing or hasn't actually happened."**
